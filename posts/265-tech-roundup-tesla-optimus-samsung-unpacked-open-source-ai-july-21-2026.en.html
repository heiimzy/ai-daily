Tesla Optimus Gen 3: 1,000+ Units Working 24/7 at Fremont

More than 1,000 Tesla Optimus Gen 3 humanoid robots are now deployed across the Fremont factory and Giga Texas, logging 24/7 shifts in what Tesla describes as a "learning and data collection" phase. This marks the first time humanoid robots have entered real factory environments at scale as a clustered workforce — not as a one-off demonstration.

22-DOF Hands: The Critical Leap from Demo to Productivity

The headline upgrade is the hands. Each Gen 3 unit sports 22 degrees of freedom per hand — double the 11-DOF design of Gen 2 — powered by 25 actuators per forearm through a tendon-driven system, totaling 50 actuators across both arms. This biomimetic design enables the robot to execute over 3,000 discrete manipulation tasks, a sixfold increase from Gen 2's roughly 500. Battery cell sorting and parts kitting are already running fully autonomously with no human in the loop.

The onboard AI5 chip delivers approximately 5x the data bandwidth of its predecessor, allowing the robot to process tactile feedback, visual input, and motor commands simultaneously without the bottlenecks that plagued earlier generations. Walking speed has improved to 5 mph (8 km/h), sufficient for navigating factory floors between workstations.

Production Timeline and Commercial Prospects

Tesla is targeting full body production of Gen 3 units beginning in late July to August 2026, with a stated goal of 50,000 or more units produced by the end of the year — which would make Optimus one of the largest humanoid robot fleets ever assembled. The Fremont line targets an eventual capacity of 1 million units per year, while a second Optimus factory at Giga Texas is under construction with a long-term goal of 10 million units annually.

First business-to-business sales are expected in late 2026, with large manufacturers and logistics operators likely to be early customers. Musk has previously suggested a long-term target price below $20,000 per unit — less than a year's salary for a factory worker in most developed markets.

The academic robotics community has taken notice. Most competitors like Figure and Apptronik top out at 12-16 DOF for hand dexterity. Tesla's jump to 22 DOF per hand with 50 actuators represents a genuine engineering lead rather than a marketing claim.

Samsung Galaxy Unpacked Tomorrow: Z Fold 8 Wider Redesign, Smart Glasses Expected

Samsung's Galaxy Unpacked event takes place July 22 in London, streaming globally at 2pm BST / 9am EDT. The teaser tagline "A New Shape Unfolds" all but confirms that the Galaxy Z Fold 8 will feature the most radical design change since the original foldable launched in 2019.

Z Fold 8: Entering the Wide-Screen Era

Multiple leaks suggest the Z Fold 8 will abandon the tall, narrow cover screen design entirely in favor of a wider, shorter form factor. The unfolded inner display is rumored to approach a 4:3 aspect ratio, behaving more like a true tablet. Reports indicate Samsung renamed its foldable lineup late in development, with the wider redesigned phone taking the "Galaxy Z Fold 8" name while a model retaining the tall narrow shape moves upmarket as the "Galaxy Z Fold 8 Ultra."

Chip selection follows a regional split. Buyers in the US and most of Asia are expected to get the Snapdragon 8 Elite Gen 5, while European and South Korean units switch to Samsung's own Exynos 2600 built on a 2nm process. Pricing is expected around $1,099 for the 256GB model.

Z Flip 8, Watch 9, and Smart Glasses

The Galaxy Z Flip 8 is expected to feature a 6.9-inch 120Hz inner display alongside a significantly larger 4.1-inch cover screen, with folded thickness dropping to 13.2mm and weight around 180 grams. The Galaxy Watch 9 and Watch Ultra 2 are also expected, with battery gains of approximately 23% and 40% respectively, leaning further into AI-driven health tracking.

The biggest potential surprise is Samsung's first smart glasses. Early reports describe a Snapdragon AR1 chip running Google's Gemini AI, styled closer to normal eyewear like Meta's Ray-Ban partnership than a bulky AR headset. Rumored pricing sits between $379 and $499, positioning it competitively for consumer adoption.

Mozilla Report: Open Source AI Closes the Gap at 1/6 the Cost

Mozilla's 2026 State of Open Source AI report, based on a survey of 1,494 developers and analysis of OpenRouter's 100-trillion-token dataset, delivers a clear verdict: open models now match closed systems on coding, instruction-following, and general knowledge tasks.

Key Findings: Converging Performance, Diverging Costs

Closed models still lead on reasoning and complex agentic workloads. On the demanding Terminal-Bench 2.1 benchmark, GPT-5.5 scores 83.4% against the best open model's 67.9%. However, on coding, instruction-following, and general knowledge, the gap has essentially disappeared.

The cost differential is staggering. GPT-4-class inference now costs $0.40 per million tokens, down from $20 three years ago — a 50-fold drop Mozilla attributes largely to open weight model releases. At comparable performance levels, closed models still cost approximately six times more per call. A Linux Foundation analysis cited in the report estimates annualized global savings from this gap at $24.8 billion.

China's Open Source AI Ecosystem Accelerates

The report highlights that Greater China and East Asia lead global open source AI adoption at 89%, versus 70% in Western Europe. Chinese-built open weight models now account for more than 60% of traffic among the ten most-used models on OpenRouter. In February 2026, Alibaba's Qwen model family surpassed Meta's Llama as the most downloaded on Hugging Face — 942 million downloads against 476 million.

Mozilla describes this as deliberate industrial policy: China's State Council AI-Plus directive explicitly promotes open weight releases as a strategy for driving global adoption while reducing dependence on US-built closed platforms.

Deployment Gap Remains a Challenge

Despite clear economic advantages, open model production deployment still lags. While 79% of developers use open models, only 51% have deployed them in production, compared to 63% for closed models. In large enterprises, open model production rates rise from 53% to just 57%, while closed model rates climb from 54% to 73%. Mozilla identifies integration challenges, maintenance requirements, and operational complexity as the most common reasons for abandoning open models — not performance.

The report also flags AI agent governance as an under-appreciated emerging risk — only 21% of companies report mature governance frameworks. The Model Context Protocol has grown from 2 million to 97 million monthly SDK downloads in just 16 months, with over 10,000 active servers in production. However, Mozilla warns that authorization — precisely defining what an agent is permitted to do unattended — remains an unsolved standardization problem.
