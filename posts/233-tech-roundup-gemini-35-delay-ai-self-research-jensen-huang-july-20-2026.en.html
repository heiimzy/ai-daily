⚡ July 20 Tech Roundup: Google Gemini 3.5 Pro Delayed on Coding Flaws, AI Self-Improvement Sparks Researcher Anxiety, Jensen Huang Praises China's AI Ecosystem

Three major tech stories broke in the early hours of July 20. Google's flagship AI model Gemini 3.5 Pro faces indefinite delay due to coding performance shortfalls, sending Alphabet shares down 15% for the week. At ICML 2026 in Seoul, OpenAI's Chief Research Officer warned that AI spending could soon rival human researcher budgets as the era of recursive self-improvement arrives. Meanwhile, NVIDIA founder Jensen Huang sat down with China's CCTV for a rare in-depth interview, praising China's AI market as "truly unique" and naming his distinguished Chinese friends in the tech ecosystem.

Google Gemini 3.5 Pro Delayed Indefinitely as Coding Bugs Persist

Google's next-generation AI model, Gemini 3.5 Pro, has been delayed indefinitely as it continues to fall short of internal performance benchmarks for coding capabilities, Bloomberg reported July 19. Originally targeting a June release, the product is now weeks—potentially months—behind schedule.

A late-June attempt to refresh the model's training data failed to patch coding gaps, pushing the release timeline further into uncertainty. A Google spokesperson confirmed the company is actively testing Gemini 3.5 Pro and an upgraded 3.5 Flash model with private partners, but declined to commit to a revised public release date.

The news sent Alphabet shares down approximately 4% intraday, capping a difficult week that saw the stock slide 15% from its $408.61 peak. The pressure intensifies ahead of Alphabet's Q2 earnings report on Wednesday, July 22, with analysts projecting 2026 AI infrastructure capital expenditure as high as $190 billion.

Internal frustration is mounting, with Google engineers reportedly concerned about losing ground in the developer ecosystem to faster-moving rivals. OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol delivers a 54% improvement in token efficiency for agentic coding tasks, while Anthropic has launched Claude Fable 5 and Mythos 5, intensifying competitive pressure on Google's enterprise developer appeal.

Google CEO Sundar Pichai has previously acknowledged the company's lag in agentic coding, attributing it to the lack of a dedicated developer-facing coding product that would naturally generate the high-quality training data its competitors enjoy.

The Era of Recursive Self-Improvement: AI Researchers Face Existential Anxiety

At the International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML) in Seoul on July 9, a lecture titled "What Will Be Left for Us to Work On?" struck a nerve across the AI research community.

Princeton professor Arvind Narayanan declared: "AI is replacing researchers' experiments and execution." In a panel discussion, when asked how close the field is to AI developing AI, OpenAI's Chief Research Officer Mark Chen replied: "In the future, we could spend as much on Codex as we currently spend on hiring researchers." The implication is stark—money will soon be spent on AI instead of humans for AI research.

The era of "Recursive Self-Improvement"—where AI autonomously improves itself without human intervention—has arrived. Anthropic recently revealed that "Claude writes 80% of its own code," and OpenAI has entered this phase as well. Former OpenAI chief scientist Jakub Pachocki set a target last year: AI achieving research capabilities equivalent to an AI research intern by September 2026 and a full-time AI researcher by March 2028.

At this year's ICML, experiments showed GPT-5.5, Anthropic's Fable 5, and Zhipu AI's GLM-5.2 directly enhancing open-source AI models with significant performance improvements. Researchers project that "by the end of this year, AI will approach human researcher levels in post-training."

The anxiety is well-founded. According to CNBC, approximately 140,000 people in the U.S. tech industry have lost their jobs this year—the largest layoff scale across all sectors. AI is cited as a key reason in about 23% of all announced layoff plans. Meta has conducted at least three restructuring rounds this year, while Amazon and Microsoft laid off hundreds to thousands of employees.

Even those with big tech careers are struggling to find re-employment. A former Amazon engineer told CNBC: "I applied to over 250 companies but received responses from only four, with 200–300 people applying as soon as job postings appear."

Jensen Huang on CCTV: China's AI Market is 'Truly Unique'

In a rare extensive interview with China's CCTV program "Face to Face" airing July 20 evening, NVIDIA founder and CEO Jensen Huang delivered a strong vote of confidence in China's AI ecosystem.

"China is not just one among many markets—China is a truly unique market," Huang said. "The vitality, innovation capability, development momentum, and the speed of industrial development here are matchless."

Huang specifically named his "distinguished friends" in China: Alibaba, Meituan, Tencent, Baidu, Xiaomi, and BYD. "We came to China 30 years ago, when there were only two tech companies—Lenovo and Great Wall. Many of my old friends grew up together with me in the PC industry."

Regarding his meeting with Xiaomi founder Lei Jun during this visit, Huang revealed a long-standing partnership: "We built phones together, and now we're jointly developing AI, autonomous driving software, and many more projects."

Addressing concerns about whether the H20 chip meets China's market needs, Huang said China's innovation pace cannot be blocked, expressing confidence that NVIDIA can still make important contributions. He praised DeepSeek as highly innovative and expressed respect for Huawei, noting that "Huawei is much larger than NVIDIA in scale, breadth, and depth of technical capabilities."

On his third trip to China this year, Huang attended the China International Supply Chain Expo, announced U.S. approval for H20 chip sales to China, and delivered a keynote in Chinese. NVIDIA's CUDA ecosystem now includes approximately 4.3 million developers, of which 1.5 million—over 30%—are from China.

"This is a unique period, unprecedented in our time. The vitality, innovation capability, and development speed of China's market are truly one of a kind." — Jensen Huang, CCTV Face to Face interview
