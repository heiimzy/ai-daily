⚡ July 16 Financial Roundup: Bank of Korea Hikes to 2.75% as KOSPI Crashes 6%, IBM Plunges 25% in Worst Drop in 58 Years, China's ChiNext Falls 3%, CXMT Launches Record IPO

Global financial markets faced a barrage of shocks on Thursday, July 16. South Korea's central bank raised rates for the first time in 3.5 years to combat inflation, triggering a cascade selloff that sent the KOSPI crashing over 6%. IBM suffered its worst single-day decline since 1968 after flagging weak AI infrastructure sales. Chinese equities closed broadly lower with the ChiNext falling 3%, while the STAR Board saw its largest-ever IPO subscription from memory chip maker CXMT. Here is a comprehensive roundup.

Bank of Korea Hikes Rate to 2.75%, KOSPI Crashes 6% Triggering Circuit Breaker

The Bank of Korea raised its benchmark interest rate by 25 basis points to 2.75% on Thursday — the first rate hike since January 2023 — in a move widely anticipated by markets. The decision was driven by intensifying inflationary pressure from the Middle East conflict, which has pushed global oil and commodity prices higher. South Korea's June CPI rose 3.2% year-over-year, a three-year high well above the central bank's 2% target.

BOK Governor Rhee Chang-yong had signaled a hawkish shift in recent weeks. The central bank said in its statement that "inflation is projected to remain elevated for some time as the impact of the rise in energy prices feeds through with a time lag." The bank now projects 2026 headline inflation at 2.7%, with core inflation running "somewhat higher" than its previous 2.4% forecast.

"The Korean economy is seeing strong recovery, but the won remains weak, inflation is far above target, and financial imbalances are growing. Further rate hikes at appropriate times will be necessary." — BOK Governor

However, the rate hike coincided with severe market turbulence. The KOSPI index opened 4.45% lower and quickly plunged over 6%, triggering a sell-side sidecar (temporary halt of program sell orders) — the 19th such event this year and the second time this week. The index has fallen approximately 25% from its June peak, entering bear market territory.

SK Hynix tumbled over 11% while Samsung Electronics dropped more than 9%. The Financial Services Commission announced it would soon unveil supplementary measures for single-stock leveraged ETFs, which President Lee Jae-myung blamed for amplifying market volatility.

IBM Crashes 25% in Worst Single-Day Drop Since 1968, Wiping Out $67 Billion

IBM shares plunged 25.21% in a single trading day on Wednesday — the steepest one-day decline in 58 years — erasing approximately $67 billion in market capitalization. The company issued a preliminary Q2 revenue warning of just $17.2 billion, falling short of the Wall Street consensus of $17.8 billion.

The core issue: weak sales of IBM's flagship AI infrastructure product, the z17. Enterprise customers are redirecting IT budgets toward securing servers, storage, and memory amid a "chipflation" environment — where semiconductor prices are soaring due to AI-driven demand — deprioritizing software and AI hardware purchases. IBM CEO Arvind Krishna acknowledged that the impact of capital expenditure reprioritization "exceeded expected levels."

The selloff cascaded across the enterprise software sector: ServiceNow fell 5.76%, Adobe dropped 4.26%, Workday declined 3.49%, and Salesforce slipped 2.14%. Analysts noted this reflects a broader shift in AI spending from software to hardware infrastructure — companies are buying GPUs and servers instead of software licenses.

China's ChiNext Falls 3% as Semiconductor Rout Deepens; CXMT Launches Record IPO

China's A-share markets closed broadly lower on Thursday. The Shanghai Composite fell 0.96%, the Shenzhen Component dropped 1.08%, while the ChiNext (Growth Enterprise Market) plunged 2.01%. The STAR 50 index briefly declined over 4% before paring losses to close flat.

Semiconductor stocks led the decline. Memory chip concepts extended their correction, with Shenzhen Desay (德明利) hitting its daily downside limit for a second consecutive session. Montage Technology (澜起科技) fell nearly 10% after news broke that its South Korea office was raided by prosecutors investigating alleged semiconductor component price collusion with Renesas Electronics and Rambus.

Hong Kong markets told a different story. The Hang Seng Index rose 2.04%, while the Hang Seng Tech Index surged 3.54%, with Alibaba, Meituan, Tencent, and JD.com all rallying in sympathy with overnight US-listed Chinese stocks.

CXMT Launches Record $66.6 Billion STAR Board IPO

The day's biggest highlight was the launch of CXMT's (ChangXin Memory Technologies, 688825.SH) IPO subscription — the largest-ever on Shanghai's STAR Board and China's biggest IPO of 2026. Priced at 8.66 yuan per share with 6.688 billion shares offered, the total raise could reach 66.6 billion yuan ($8.55 billion) if the over-allotment option is fully exercised. This makes it the second-largest IPO in STAR Board history after SMIC's 53.2 billion yuan raise.

Analysts project a lottery winning rate of 0.3% to 0.7%, significantly higher than typical new listings, with potential profits of ~20,000 yuan per 500-share lot. As China's only volume producer of mainstream DRAM products, CXMT's listing is expected to provide a new valuation anchor for the A-share semiconductor sector.

US Dollar Hits One-Month Low as Fed's Cook Flags Inflation Risks

The US Dollar Index fell to 100.47, its lowest since June 18, after June PPI unexpectedly declined 0.3% month-over-month — the largest drop in 14 months — reinforcing expectations that the Fed can remain patient on rate hikes. Market-implied odds of a July rate hike collapsed from 45% a week ago to just 11%, according to CME FedWatch.

The euro rose to a one-month high of $1.1472, while sterling held near a two-month high at $1.354. The dollar slipped 0.1% against the yen to 162.075.

However, Fed Governor Lisa Cook struck a hawkish tone in a speech at the Washington Financial Club. Cook stated she is "prepared to act" if inflation does not cool quickly, citing AI investment, tariffs, and the Middle East conflict as persistent upside risks to prices. "The high inflation we are seeing could push future inflation expectations higher," she warned, noting that the risk balance has shifted from labor markets toward inflation.

Oil prices extended gains for a fourth consecutive session. Brent crude rose 0.4% to $85.28 per barrel, while WTI crude gained 0.5% to $80.02. The US-Iran conflict, now in its fifth consecutive day of back-and-forth strikes, continues to threaten shipping through the Strait of Hormuz — which normally carries about one-fifth of global oil and LNG trade. Goldman Sachs warned Brent could climb above $110 per barrel in Q4 if Gulf export recovery remains delayed.
