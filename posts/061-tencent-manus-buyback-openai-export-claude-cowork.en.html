⚡ July 12 AI News: Tencent Leads $2B Manus Buyback, OpenAI Sold AI to Blacklisted Chinese Firms, Claude Cowork Goes Mobile

⚡ Tencent leads a $2 billion buyback of Manus as Beijing classifies autonomous AI agents as sovereign assets; an FT investigation reveals OpenAI and Google supplied AI models to Pentagon-blacklisted Chinese companies via Singapore subsidiaries; Anthropic launches Claude Cowork on mobile and web — usage data reveals 90%+ of sessions aren't coding; OpenAI resets Codex and ChatGPT Work limits twice in one day as traffic surges at an unprecedented rate.

🏛️ Tencent Leads $2B Manus Buyback: China Declares Autonomous AI Agents Sovereign Assets

The Financial Times reported on July 10 that Tencent is leading a consortium to buy back AI agent startup Manus from Meta at a $2 billion valuation. This follows Beijing's unprecedented use of the Foreign Investment Security Review (FISR) mechanism in April to force the unwinding of the completed cross-border acquisition. China's NDRC ruled that agentic AI — with its persistent tool access and autonomous action capability — qualifies as "important information technology" that cannot leave the country.

The ruling establishes several landmark precedents: offshore incorporation no longer provides safe harbor; completed, integrated acquisitions can be ordered reversed; and China applies a "substance over form" principle — AI technology with Chinese origin remains within Beijing's regulatory perimeter regardless of redomiciliation. Tencent, ZhenFund, and Sequoia Capital China (HSG) are leading the buyback. The chilling effect is already measurable: voluntary withdrawal rates for foreign tech M&A involving algorithms and data reached 40% in Q1 2026, compared to just 5% for manufacturing.

🛃 OpenAI and Google Provided AI Access to Blacklisted Chinese Firms

An FT investigation has revealed that OpenAI and Google supplied AI model access to Chinese companies on the Pentagon's Section 1260H blacklist — entities the DoD believes are connected to China's military-industrial complex — through their Singapore subsidiaries. While inclusion on the list doesn't automatically trigger sanctions, the revelation has reignited debate over the effectiveness of AI export controls.

Unlike conventional defense technologies that move through physical supply chains, AI models can be delivered through cloud-based APIs, commercial partnerships, or intermediary services. This makes frontier AI systems extraordinarily difficult to restrict once they become commercially available. If frontier models continue reaching military-linked Chinese enterprises despite existing controls, lawmakers could seek mandatory KYC checks for AI API users or direct limits on model provision to strategic rivals — measures that could fundamentally alter the revenue models of leading AI developers.

📱 Anthropic Launches Claude Cowork on Mobile: 90%+ Usage Isn't Coding

On July 7, Anthropic launched Claude Cowork on iOS, Android, and web for the first time, freeing its AI agent from the desktop. Alongside the launch, Anthropic published usage data from 1.2 million anonymized Cowork sessions across 600,000+ organizations. The numbers paint a surprising picture: business operations account for 33.4% of usage, content creation 16.4% — and over 90% of Cowork sessions have nothing to do with coding.

This data fundamentally shifts the narrative around AI agents. The technology is rapidly moving beyond developer tools into everyday knowledge work. Cowork sessions now run in the cloud by default, persisting even when your laptop is closed. Scheduled tasks execute autonomously, with push notifications for human approval when needed. Anthropic has extended doubled usage limits through August 5 to drive adoption. The launch puts Claude Cowork in direct competition with OpenAI's ChatGPT Work and Microsoft's Copilot Cowork — all three sharing a remarkably similar architecture: persistent cloud-based AI agents with MCP plugin ecosystems and cross-device access.

🔌 OpenAI Resets Codex and ChatGPT Work Limits Twice in One Day

The launch of GPT-5.6 and ChatGPT Work has driven traffic far beyond expectations. Codex engineering lead Thibault Sottiaux admitted the team has "never seen traffic increase so quickly," resetting usage limits twice on July 10-11 to keep users online.

Sottiaux identified four problem areas: high-compute settings were too easily triggered, exhausting usage budgets rapidly; the desktop app overhaul made familiar features like chats and projects harder to find; multi-agent workflows regressed; and plugin submission bugs appeared. "We didn't get everything quite right," he acknowledged. A major update is promised next week, including restored sidebar chats and projects, clearer usage metrics, and better default compute tiering. The incident highlights a broader industry challenge: AI agent capabilities are outstripping infrastructure capacity, forcing developers to organize work around quotas rather than deadlines.

📌 Bottom Line: Today's stories converge on one theme — AI has entered a triple stress test of geopolitics, infrastructure, and scale. The Manus case rewrites the risk framework for cross-border AI acquisitions; the OpenAI export loophole reveals the fundamental uncontrollability of cloud-delivered AI; and the simultaneous explosion of Claude Cowork and ChatGPT Work confirms that AI agents are crossing the chasm from "can do something" to "used every day."
