⚡ July 17 Tech Roundup: Apple Intelligence Approved in China, Microsoft Patches Record 570 Flaws, OpenAI Employees Fund Rival PAC Against Their Own President, WAIC 2026 Opens in Shanghai

🍎 Apple Intelligence Finally Gets China Approval with Alibaba's Qwen AI and Baidu

After a two-year wait, Apple's generative AI service, Apple Intelligence, has finally received the green light from Chinese regulators. China's Cyberspace Administration (CAC) published a notice on July 15, officially listing Apple's AI services among newly approved providers alongside local companies like Huawei and Xiaomi.

According to reports from TechCrunch and Reuters, Apple Intelligence's China rollout will rely on Alibaba Group's Qwen AI models and Baidu's large language models. An Alibaba spokesperson confirmed that Qwen models will be integrated into Apple Intelligence across iOS, iPadOS, macOS, and visionOS, providing text and image understanding and generation capabilities.

A Baidu spokesperson similarly confirmed its involvement in developing Apple Intelligence features for Chinese users. Apple had reportedly explored partnerships with DeepSeek and ByteDance before settling on Alibaba and Baidu as official technology partners.

The approval carries enormous significance for Apple. In Q2 2026, Apple generated $20.5 billion in sales in Greater China, up 28% year-over-year, and recently regained its No. 2 position in China's smartphone market. Counterpoint Research senior analyst Ivan Lam noted that while CAC approval is a crucial step, the service likely won't be available to Chinese users until late 2026 or early 2027.

Apple Intelligence originally launched globally in 2024, first in the United States, then Europe in 2025. China's strict regulations on foreign AI tools forced Apple to partner with local firms. Notably, outgoing CEO Tim Cook — set to hand over to John Ternus in September — joined President Trump's business delegation to Beijing earlier this year, further strengthening ties with Chinese authorities.

🔐 Microsoft Ships Record 570 Security Patches in July Patch Tuesday

Microsoft released a staggering 570 security fixes in its July 2026 Patch Tuesday — nearly triple last month's record-breaking count. The company attributes the surge to AI-assisted vulnerability discovery, which is dramatically accelerating the pace of finding and fixing security holes.

Nearly 60 of the patched vulnerabilities earned a "Critical" severity rating, meaning attackers could remotely seize control of Windows devices with little or no user interaction. Microsoft also addressed three zero-day flaws, two of which are already being exploited in the wild.

The two exploited zero-days are elevation-of-privilege bugs: CVE-2026-56155 (Active Directory Federation Services) and CVE-2026-56164 (Microsoft SharePoint). The third, CVE-2026-50661, is a BitLocker security feature bypass allowing attackers with physical access to access encrypted data. Microsoft says this flaw has been publicly disclosed but not actively exploited.

"The pace of vulnerability discovery is changing with advances in AI making it possible to find more issues, faster, across more code," wrote Microsoft EVP Pavan Davuluri in a July 9 blog post.

Security experts note that while the sheer volume of patches is unprecedented, it reflects Microsoft's growing investment in AI-powered security detection. However, the massive patch count also creates significant management challenges for enterprise IT teams, who must prioritize the most critical and actively exploited vulnerabilities.

💰 OpenAI Employees Quietly Fund Pro-Regulation PAC Against Their Own President

A extraordinary internal political rift has emerged at OpenAI. According to an exclusive report by Wired, multiple OpenAI employees have quietly donated more than $215,000 of their own money to Guardrails Alliance — a super PAC pushing for stricter oversight of frontier AI labs, directly opposing their own president, Greg Brockman.

Brockman and his wife Anna have pledged $50 million to Leading the Future, a pro-industry super PAC with a $100 million-plus war chest that advocates for lighter AI regulation. Guardrails Alliance launched last month with just $5 million, aiming to push for tougher oversight.

Remarkably, nearly all of the employee donations came from a single person: Juan Felipe Cerón Uribe, a research engineer who joined OpenAI in 2022, who donated $200,000. His job for the past four years has been reducing AI harms — work he believes is wasted without regulatory backing. "Tech billionaires, such as Greg Brockman, funded the super PAC Leading the Future to keep AI unregulated," Cerón Uribe told Wired.

Other donors include safety researcher Gabriel Wu ($5,000) and alignment researchers Julie Steele and Jason Wolfe ($5,000 each). While their contributions pale compared to Brockman's $50 million, Guardrails Alliance co-founder Shaunna Thomas argues her strategy isn't about matching dollars — it's about exposing what the spending is actually for.

OpenAI has sought to distance itself from the controversy. A company spokesperson pointed to a June blog post calling Brockman's involvement personal, not corporate, and noting employees are free to support causes on their own time. The episode exposes a growing internal divide at OpenAI between leadership fighting regulation and rank-and-file safety researchers advocating for it.

Guardrails Alliance is not alone. Public First Action, a super PAC seeded with $20 million from Anthropic, is working the same side. Both groups previously backed Alex Bores, author of New York's landmark AI safety law, who ultimately lost his primary race in a contest that drew $27 million in combined spending.

🌐 WAIC 2026 Opens in Shanghai, Nubia Debuts World's First AI Agent Phone

The 2026 World Artificial Intelligence Conference (WAIC) officially opens today in Shanghai, running through July 20. The conference spans four venues — Shanghai World Expo Exhibition Hall, Zhangjiang Science Hall, and West Bund International Convention Center — with a total exhibition area exceeding 100,000 square meters. Over 1,100 enterprises are showcasing more than 3,000 cutting-edge technologies, with over 300 AI products making their global debut.

One of the most anticipated exhibits is Nubia's NaviX Ultra, touted as the world's first AI agent phone. The device has completed China's AI model registration and features a dedicated Doubao voice assistant. Unlike existing "AI phones" that merely stack features, the NaviX Ultra integrates a proprietary AI agent engine at the Android system level, capable of understanding complex commands and autonomously executing cross-app tasks.

Nubia president Ni Fei stated that the next phase of AI smartphones will move toward "native agent" experiences where phones proactively understand user needs rather than requiring users to adapt to the phone's logic. Also on display are Nubia's AI companion robot iMoochi and ZTE's TOPFLOW live-streaming神器.

On the global governance front, Pakistan's Deputy Prime Minister and Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar will sign the founding agreement for the World Artificial Intelligence Cooperation Organization (WAICO) during the conference, making Pakistan a founding member of the new global AI governance body.
