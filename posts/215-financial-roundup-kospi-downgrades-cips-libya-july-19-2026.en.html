KOSPI Plunges to 6,800 Range: Brokerage Target-Price Cuts Outpace Upgrades for First Time in 2026

South Korea's KOSPI index has collapsed from an all-time intraday high of 9,385.59 on June 19 to the 6,800 range in just 17 trading sessions — a decline of over 20% that officially places it in a technical bear market. In this extreme environment, Korean brokerages have reversed course dramatically: target-price downgrade reports have surpassed upgrade reports for the first time in 2026.

According to financial data provider FnGuide, domestic brokerages issued 323 reports lowering target prices between July 1 and July 16, compared with just 247 upgrade reports — a margin of 76 more downgrades. This marks a stunning reversal from the first half of the year.

In January, upgrade reports outnumbered downgrades 4.1 to 1; by February the ratio had widened to an extraordinary 9.7 to 1. March (5.9x), April (3.9x), and May (4.0x) continued the optimistic trend. The turning point came in June, when the ratio narrowed to 2.2x, and July finally saw downgrades overtake upgrades for the first time this year.

"Analysts tend to issue optimistic outlooks to support their firm's brokerage and investment banking revenue or maintain smooth relationships with listed companies," said Kim Jun-seok, senior research fellow at the Korea Capital Market Institute. "The fact that downgrades now outpace upgrades signals that brokerages have turned markedly more conservative."

The wave of target-price cuts has swept across virtually every sector — semiconductors, autos, defense, shipbuilding, biotech, and secondary batteries. Companies affected include Hanwha Aerospace, Hyundai Motor, Hyundai Mobis, LG Energy Solution, Samsung SDI, and HD Korea Shipbuilding & Offshore Engineering. Even within the semiconductor space, analyst views have diverged wildly: target prices for SK Hynix range from a high of 4.2 million won (KB Securities) to a low of 1.85 million won (BNK Investment & Securities) — a two-fold valuation gap for the same stock.

All eyes are now on Alphabet's Q2 earnings and AI capex guidance on Monday (U.S. time), followed by SK Hynix's earnings on Tuesday. These results could determine whether the downgrade trend accelerates or reverses.

Libya Signs Agreement to Join China's CIPS Yuan Payment System

On July 19, the Governor of the Central Bank of Libya, Naji Mohammed Issa, and the Governor of the People's Bank of China, Pan Gongsheng, formally agreed in Beijing to connect Libyan commercial banks to China's Cross-Border Interbank Payment System (CIPS). The move will enable Libyan banks to send and receive payments directly in Chinese yuan, bypassing the need for dollar-based intermediary transactions.

CIPS, launched by the People's Bank of China in 2015, provides dedicated infrastructure for clearing and settling cross-border transactions in Chinese renminbi (RMB). The system has steadily expanded its reach across Africa, the Middle East, and Asia, positioning itself as an alternative to the SWIFT network for yuan-denominated transactions.

According to the Central Bank of Libya's statement, implementation will begin with direct money transfers to China — a crucial step for small-scale traders who have long navigated a cumbersome informal financial corridor. The two sides also agreed to allow letters of credit to be opened directly through Chinese banks, and to launch the first Libya-China Banking Forum in early 2027.

Libya's accession to CIPS is particularly significant as the country is a major North African oil producer. It marks the first time a petro-economy with close ties to OPEC has formally integrated with China's yuan payment infrastructure, potentially accelerating the broader trend of oil trade settlement in yuan.

Global 'Rate Reset' Hits Japan: 10-Year JGB Yield at 30-Year High

Global bond markets are undergoing a profound repricing — and Japan is the most extreme case. The 10-year Japanese Government Bond (JGB) yield touched 2.900% on July 14, its highest level in nearly 30 years. Meanwhile, the yen weakened to around 162 against the dollar, its lowest since 1986.

The Bank of Japan raised its policy rate by 25 basis points to 1% on June 16, and markets expect at least one more hike by year-end. BNP Paribas projects that the BOJ will maintain a gradual tightening pace — one hike every four to five months — taking the policy rate to 2.50% by late 2028.

This has profound global implications. The yen has long been the centerpiece of global carry trades, where investors borrow yen at near-zero rates to invest in higher-yielding assets. BlackRock strategists have warned that "increasingly crowded yen short positions warrant close attention" — any trigger for a short squeeze could cause violent FX reversals.

The JGB yield surge is also transmitting to global markets: the U.S. 10-year Treasury yield has risen in sympathy to 4.62%, reflecting the accelerating end of the cheap-money era worldwide.

Circle Wins Federal Trust Bank Charter; Banks Warn Stablecoins Could Drain $500B From Deposits

On July 10, USDC issuer Circle received final approval from the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) to open Circle National Trust Bank — a federally regulated trust bank. While not a traditional retail deposit bank, this milestone signals Washington's fundamental shift in attitude toward regulating stablecoins.

However, the banking industry has expressed serious concern. Standard Chartered warned in January that stablecoins could drain approximately $500 billion from U.S. bank deposits by the end of 2028. A December 2025 FEDS Note estimated that widespread stablecoin adoption could reduce bank lending by $65 billion to $1.26 trillion, depending on adoption scenarios.

As of July 13, USDC had $72.95 billion in circulation against reserves of approximately $73.15 billion. Of these reserves, roughly 84.2% is invested in overnight reverse repo agreements and short-term Treasury bills, with only 15.8% held in bank deposits. While this structure maximizes liquidity and safety, it also channels most of the funds away from traditional bank balance sheets — exactly the trend that lenders are worried about.
