⚡ July 17 International Politics Roundup: US Strikes Reach Tehran Area, Iran Vows to Crush Regional Infrastructure, Zelenskyy Fires Defense Minister Triggering Mass Protests

1. US Airstrikes Reach Tehran Vicinity for First Time; Iran Retaliates Against Three US Bases

The US-Iran conflict escalated dramatically on July 15-16. US Central Command (CENTCOM) announced a new wave of large-scale airstrikes completed at 9 PM ET Wednesday — the heaviest since the interim ceasefire memorandum was signed last month.

The 90-minute operation targeted the southern port city of Bandar Abbas, a key naval hub overlooking the Strait of Hormuz, as well as coastal defense and cruise missile sites on Greater Tunb Island. Notably, US strikes reached around Iran's capital Tehran for the first time in this round of fighting.

Iranian state media reported strikes in Semnan province, home to Iran's ballistic missile production facilities and space program. Explosions were also reported in Bandar Abbas, Qeshm Island, Chabahar, Konarak, Rask, Khondab, Khorramabad, and other locations. Iran activated air defenses over Tehran, and the IRGC shot down a US MQ-9 drone over Andimeshk in Khuzestan province.

Iran's Health Ministry reports at least 35 killed and over 300 wounded since fighting reignited last week.

Hellfire Missile Strikes Oil Tanker Defying Blockade

CENTCOM also announced that US forces fired on a Curacao-flagged oil tanker, the M/T Belma, as it attempted to sail toward Iran's Kharg Island — the country's main oil export terminal — ignoring multiple warnings. A US aircraft disabled the vessel by firing Hellfire missiles into its smokestack. This was the first forcible interdiction since the US reimposed its port blockade on Tuesday.

Iran Strikes Back: Missiles and Drones Hit US Bases in Kuwait, Bahrain, Jordan

In retaliation, Iran's IRGC launched missile and drone attacks early Thursday against US military assets in three Gulf nations:

  • Kuwait: Missiles struck Patriot missile batteries and fuel tanks at Ali Al Salem Air Base. Kuwait said it intercepted 4 cruise missiles and 21 drones.
  • Bahrain: Drones targeted Super Hawk radar systems and Patriot installations at Sheikh Isa Air Base.
  • Jordan: Khyber-Shakan ballistic missiles hit the US fighter jet storage ramp and command center at Al-Azraq Air Base. Jordan intercepted 8 Iranian missiles.

Iranian army spokesman Brigadier General Mohammad Akraminia warned that attacks "will spread to new areas" if US strikes continue, adding that "a significant portion of the armed forces' capabilities have not yet been demonstrated."

2. Iran Vows to 'Crush' All Regional Infrastructure; Houthis Ordered to Close Bab el-Mandeb

Colonel Ebrahim Zolfaqari, spokesperson for Iran's Khatam al-Anbiya Central Headquarters, declared the Strait of Hormuz an "inviolable red line" and warned that if Trump follows through on threats to strike Iranian power plants and bridges, Iran would "crush all infrastructure across the region that has so far remained intact solely because of Iran's restraint."

He emphasized that Iran's response would not be "proportional" but "superior" — "more severe, more extensive, and more devastating than ever before."

"Under no circumstances and in no way will we allow America, as a foreign and extraregional country, to interfere in the Strait of Hormuz. This is Iran's invincible red line." — Colonel Ebrahim Zolfaqari

Houthis Poised to Close Red Sea Route

According to sources cited by Reuters and multiple news outlets, Iran has asked its Houthi proxies in Yemen to prepare to close the Bab el-Mandeb Strait at the mouth of the Red Sea if the US strikes Iranian infrastructure. This would effectively cut off the second most important global energy shipping chokepoint.

With both the Strait of Hormuz and potentially the Bab el-Mandeb Strait under threat, global energy markets face a supply crisis. Brent crude traded above $85 per barrel on Thursday, up more than 15% since the conflict reignited.

3. Zelenskyy Fires Defense Minister Fedorov, Triggering Mass Protests and Drone Commander's Resignation

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy's dismissal of Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov has sparked rare public protests and a political crisis, ABC News reported at 2:06 AM on Friday. More than 1,000 demonstrators gathered outside Zelenskyy's office in Kyiv chanting "Shame!" and carrying signs reading "For what?" and "The Russians are celebrating."

Fedorov, 35, a tech expert who previously served as Minister of Digital Transformation, was widely credited with modernizing Ukraine's military through digital reforms, cutting bureaucracy, and scaling up drone warfare. After his dismissal, he launched a blistering attack on Commander-in-Chief Oleksandr Syrskyi, accusing the 60-year-old general of "not thinking about how to defeat Russia, but figuring out how to split the country."

Colonel Pavlo Yelizarov, deputy commander of Ukraine's Air Force and a key leader in drone warfare, resigned in protest, calling Fedorov's removal "a great evil" for Ukraine's defense. His drone units had been instrumental in striking Russian oil infrastructure and military logistics.

Zelenskyy called for calm, saying a president "is not supposed to pick sides" in wartime, but acknowledged that "the sides did not find unity." Prime Minister Sergii Koretskyi's new government was approved by parliament on Thursday, but defense and foreign minister appointments were postponed to the next session on August 18.

4. Russia Deploys 'Doomsday Plane' to Tehran to Protect Bushehr Nuclear Plant

Flightradar24 tracking data shows a Russian Tu-214PU "Doomsday Command Plane" departed Moscow's Vnukovo Airport, stopped over in Beijing, and flew on to Tehran. The aircraft is one of Russia's highest-protection airborne command platforms, equipped with data relay systems and strategic encrypted communications capable of maintaining nationwide military and government command chains even in nuclear war scenarios.

Military analyst Alexander Perendzhiev said Moscow's primary mission is protecting the Bushehr Nuclear Power Plant — Iran's only civilian nuclear facility, built by Russia. With two additional reactor units under construction totaling 2,100 MW capacity and a project cost of $10 billion, Russia has decades of investment at stake.

Iran has stockpiled its enriched uranium at Bushehr, making it a potential US target. Russia's deployment of the doomsday plane signals that Moscow will not abandon its strategic and economic interests in the Middle East, while also undertaking responsibility for securing the International North-South Transport Corridor (INSTC).

5. US Pressures China to Rein in Iran Ahead of Trump Visit

Hours before Trump's planned visit to China, US Secretary of State Marco Rubio and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent jointly pressured Beijing to use its influence over Iran. Rubio argued that Iran's Strait of Hormuz blockade harms China more than the US — noting China imports nearly half its crude oil and a third of its natural gas from the Middle East. Bessent baselessly claimed China purchases 90% of Iran's energy exports, "financing state terrorism."

Analysts say the US move reveals Washington's anxiety before the summit, trying to force Beijing to shoulder the burden of resolving the Iran crisis. China has maintained strategic composure, pursuing independent foreign policy while accelerating energy diversification and expanding yuan-denominated oil trade.
