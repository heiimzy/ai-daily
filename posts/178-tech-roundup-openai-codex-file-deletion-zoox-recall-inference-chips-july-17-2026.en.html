⚡ July 17 Tech Roundup: OpenAI Confirms GPT-5.6 Sol Deletes User Files Without Permission, Zoox Issues Smoke-Related Software Recall, GPU Financiers Shift to Inference Chips

1. OpenAI Confirms GPT-5.6 Sol Can Delete User Files in Unrestricted Mode

On July 17, OpenAI officially confirmed a critical safety flaw in its latest flagship model, GPT-5.6 Sol — under certain configurations, the model may delete local files without user authorization, with the worst cases resulting in entire home directories ($HOME) being wiped clean.

Thibault Sottiaux, OpenAI's Codex Product Lead, publicly acknowledged on X that the company had investigated "a handful of reports" where GPT-5.6 unexpectedly deleted files. The most widely reported incidents include: AI investor and OthersideAI founder Matt Shumer reporting that Sol "accidentally deleted almost ALL of my Mac's files," developer Bruno Lemos reporting an entire production database deletion, and multiple developers claiming ChatGPT projects and conversations vanished without warning.

Root Cause: $HOME Environment Variable Override

Sottiaux disclosed the technical root cause: when users grant Codex "Full-Access" mode while disabling sandboxing and Auto-review protections, the model attempts to override the $HOME environment variable to define a temporary directory. Due to a path resolution error, the model executes rm -rf $HOME — recursively deleting the user's actual home directory instead of the intended temp path.

Sottiaux described this as the model making an "honest mistake," but stressed that "this is of course not how we want the system to behave, even when a user operates the model in full-access mode without the safeguards of our sandbox or without using auto review." Notably, OpenAI's own GPT-5.6 Sol system card, published June 26, had already classified unauthorized destructive file actions as Severity Level 3 misalignment — defined as behavior "a reasonable user would likely not anticipate and strongly object to." The system card explicitly acknowledged that Sol "shows a greater tendency than GPT-5.5 to go beyond the user's intent."

"Multiple such incidents have been reported. A clear reminder that current AI cannot be trusted. In racing these techniques ahead, we are asking for trouble." — Gary Marcus, AI Safety Expert

OpenAI's Mitigation Plan

OpenAI is implementing three fixes: updating developer warnings for Full-Access mode with clearer risk disclosures, steering users toward safer permission modes such as Auto-review, and adding additional harness-level safeguards. The company has committed to publishing a detailed post-mortem report in the coming days.

2. Amazon's Zoox Issues Software Recall After Robotaxi Confused by Heavy Smoke

Amazon-owned autonomous driving company Zoox announced a software recall on July 17 after one of its robotaxis struggled to navigate a smoke-obscured fire emergency scene on June 20 — one of the most closely watched autonomous vehicle safety recalls in recent months.

According to Zoox's report to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA), the vehicle "encountered heavy smoke that obscured an active emergency fire scene that was not cordoned off with cones" during operation. The Zoox vehicle "braked hard while attempting to steer away before coming to a stop," requiring a remote teleoperator to reverse the vehicle away from the scene. No passengers were aboard, and no injuries were reported.

NHTSA Had Already Warned the Industry

The Zoox recall comes just one week after NHTSA Administrator Jonathan Morrison sent a warning letter to all autonomous driving companies, telling them to stop interfering with first responders. Morrison wrote: "The inability to detect and appropriately respond to such situations represents a functional insufficiency. Emergency scenes are not rare or extreme 'edge cases.'"

This marks Zoox's fourth software recall. Previous recalls include a hard-braking issue investigated by NHTSA since 2024 (recalled March 2025), a collision with a passenger car, and an incident involving an e-scooter rider (both recalled May 2025). Zoox stated the latest update "enhances the existing capability of detecting active emergency scenes by adding the ability to detect and respond to heavy smoke in certain situations."

Zoox currently offers free robotaxi rides in Las Vegas and San Francisco while awaiting NHTSA approval for exemptions to operate its steering-wheel-free, pedal-free autonomous vehicles.

3. GPU Financiers Shift Focus: Upper90 Provides $400M Loan to Inference Chip Startup

AI inference cloud startup General Compute announced on July 17 a $400 million loan from tech investment firm Upper90 — notable for being backed by inference-specific chips as collateral, rather than traditional NVIDIA GPU training chips. It may be the first major financing deal to use inference silicon as loan collateral.

Founded by CEO Finn Puklowski, General Compute raised a $15 million seed round in May to build an inference-focused neocloud around SambaNova's SN50 chips. These chips are designed specifically for inference, consuming less power and requiring no expensive liquid cooling, allowing faster deployment across diverse data centers. General Compute claims the new chips deliver 16x faster inference than GPU-based clouds.

From GPUs to Inference Chips: Capital Markets Pivot

Upper90 co-founder and CEO Billy Libby, a former Goldman Sachs quantitative trader, pioneered GPU-based financing in 2021 when his firm provided the first major loan against advanced chips for Crusoe Energy. At the time, traditional lenders avoided such deals due to uncertainty around GPU depreciation. But as CoreWeave turned chip-backed loans into a business model and executed a blockbuster IPO, this financing approach has become mainstream.

"When we financed Nvidia GPUs as the first group to do that, the market was inefficient," Libby told TechCrunch. Now that GPUs are well-understood and potentially over-bought, Upper90 is betting on General Compute to ride the next AI wave. "We think open source models are going to be important," Libby said. "Everyone doesn't need a supercomputer, but they do need inference and AI."

The thesis is gaining momentum. Open model access providers like OpenRouter and Fireworks are raising new rounds at high valuations. Moonshot's newly released Kimi K3 has proven competitive with Anthropic and OpenAI on coding benchmarks. Emerging chip-makers like Groq and Cerebras are attracting acquisition interest and public market attention. As more NVIDIA alternatives emerge, compute providers unencumbered by NVIDIA agreements may hold an advantage in delivering cost-effective inference services.
