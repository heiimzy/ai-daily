⚡ July 22 International Politics Roundup: Zelensky Fires Ukraine's Army Chief Syrskyi, Russia-Ukraine Talks Set for Istanbul, Trump Threatens Houthis and Iran's Nuclear Site

1. Zelensky Fires Commander-in-Chief Syrskyi, Appoints Drapatyi as Replacement

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky announced on the evening of July 21 that he had dismissed Oleksandr Syrskyi as Commander-in-Chief of the Armed Forces of Ukraine, replacing him with Joint Forces Commander Major General Mykhailo Drapatyi. This marks the most significant military leadership shakeup in Ukraine since the start of Russia's full-scale invasion in 2022.

In his evening address, Zelensky stated that he, Drapatyi, acting SBU head Yevhen Khmara, and Deputy Head of the Presidential Office Pavlo Palisa had determined a series of accompanying personnel changes at the General Staff. He thanked Syrskyi for Ukraine's steadfast defense on the battlefield and noted that Drapatyi "shares these views."

"I have decided that the new Commander-in-Chief of the Armed Forces of Ukraine will be Mykhailo Drapatyi." — Volodymyr Zelensky on Telegram

Syrskyi, 60, who had served as commander-in-chief since 2024, was one of Kyiv's most experienced commanders, having led the defense of the capital at the start of Russia's 2022 invasion. His dismissal follows days of mass protests that began on July 15 after Zelensky fired popular Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov. Thousands of demonstrators gathered outside the presidential office chanting "Syrskyi out," holding him responsible for Fedorov's ouster and accusing him of obstructing military reforms.

The new commander, Mykhailo Drapatyi, approximately 46 years old, rose from a tank lieutenant to senior army command through fierce frontline battles. Zelensky appointed him Ground Forces commander in 2024. He resigned on June 1, 2025, after a Russian Iskander ballistic missile attack on a training ground killed 12 Ukrainian soldiers. Drapatyi thanked Zelensky for entrusting him with the role, calling it "a new stage of service" and pledging to lead "with responsibility." Zelensky said he met with Drapatyi twice over the past two days, along with multiple top unit commanders, to discuss reforms.

2. Russia-Ukraine Third Round Talks Confirmed for July 24 in Istanbul

Russia and Ukraine's third round of direct negotiations will take place on July 24 in Istanbul, Turkey, according to sources cited by TASS on July 22. Delegations from both sides may arrive as early as July 23. The talks are expected to be held at the Saray Palace, the same venue as the previous round.

Zelensky stated that Ukraine is prepared to discuss the repatriation of prisoners and evacuated children, as well as preparations for a potential meeting between the two countries' leaders. "This is a difficult area. We all understand that truly productive negotiations can only happen at the leadership level," he said. Former Defense Minister Rustem Umerov will continue to lead Ukraine's delegation.

Russia's delegation will again be led by Presidential Aide Vladimir Medinsky. Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov confirmed that "the Russian negotiating team remains unchanged." Russian sources indicated that the third round could focus on exchanging memoranda drafts on peaceful resolution visions and discussing humanitarian issues including prisoner exchanges and repatriation of remains.

NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte had previously dismissed Medinsky as a "historian" leading negotiations, calling it "not serious." The Kremlin countered that Medinsky outranks Russian federal ministers. Ukrainian Foreign Minister Andrii Sybiha stressed that Russia's delegation should be authorized to discuss critical issues including ceasefire and cessation of hostilities.

3. Trump Says US Will 'Take Care of' Houthis, Threatens Iran's 'Pickaxe Mountain' Nuclear Site

President Donald Trump said Tuesday the United States would "take care of" the Iran-backed Houthi militia if it proceeds with a Red Sea blockade against Saudi Arabia, as concerns grow that another major waterway could be disrupted amid the ongoing Strait of Hormuz crisis.

"We will see what happens. So far, it hasn't happened. (It) might happen, but we take care of things," Trump said during a press availability with Lebanese President Joseph Aoun at the White House. "If something like that happens, we (will) take care of it." He cited previous U.S. military operations against the Houthis, saying the U.S. has "had no problem with the Houthis" since then.

On Monday (July 20), the Houthi militia announced an immediate maritime blockade against Saudi Arabia — a response to what it called the kingdom's "unjust blockade on Yemenis for over 10 years." A Houthi official said the Bab al-Mandeb strait at the southern end of the Red Sea — through which roughly 12% of global trade passes — would be closed to Saudi vessels. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively shut, Saudi Arabia had been relying on Red Sea alternative routes to export oil.

Trump also announced that the U.S. military will conduct strikes on "Pickaxe Mountain" — an underground Iranian site suspected of housing covert nuclear operations. "We'll be hitting that area pretty soon, and very heavily," he said. He claimed Iran "desperately" wants to meet for nuclear talks but said the U.S. has "no interest in meeting" until Iran is ready to engage "in a meaningful way."

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth warned that Iran has built its conventional military capabilities "the same way North Korea did before developing its nuclear arms" and said the U.S. would hit Iran "10 times harder" if it attacks commercial ships in the Strait of Hormuz.

4. US and Iran Trade 10th Consecutive Night of Strikes; Iran Hits Tanker in Hormuz

The Middle East conflict continues to escalate. The U.S. military carried out its 10th consecutive night of airstrikes on Iran on July 21, targeting Iranian military command centers, maritime capabilities, missile and drone launch sites, and air defense systems. Iranian state media reported explosions in Fars, Hormozgan, Ilam, Kerman, and Sistan-Baluchestan provinces.

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps retaliated forcefully, announcing an attack on a tanker in the Strait of Hormuz that forced its crew to abandon the vessel. The United Kingdom Maritime Trade Operations (UKMTO) confirmed the attack. Iran also claimed drone and missile strikes on U.S. bases in Bahrain and Kuwait. Three U.S. service members have been killed in the latest round of exchanges.

The conflict is increasingly drawing in civilian infrastructure. The U.S. bombed a water desalination plant in Iran's southern Hormozgan province, cutting off drinking water for approximately 10,000 residents across 20 villages. Iran in turn struck a power and desalination plant in Kuwait, which relies on desalination for 90% of its drinking water. Gulf state water infrastructure is emerging as a critical vulnerability in the conflict.

Notably, the U.S. Air Force has withdrawn its contingent of F-22 Raptor stealth fighters from Israel's Ovda Air Base, with the aircraft arriving at RAF Fairford in the United Kingdom on July 10. Analysts interpret this as a potential signal of shifting U.S. military posture in the region.

5. Lebanese Army Deploys in Pilot Zone After Israeli Withdrawal

Under a U.S.-brokered agreement, Israeli troops have begun pulling out of a small part of southern Lebanon occupied during their conflict with Hezbollah, and the Lebanese army has started deploying troops in a designated "pilot zone," BBC reported on July 22. This marks the first substantive military disengagement between the two sides since hostilities reignited on March 2.

The United Nations peacekeeping force UNIFIL confirmed it has not detected any missile trajectories from either side since Saturday. Analysts view this localized withdrawal as a potential step toward a broader ceasefire agreement. However, Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu faces parliamentary elections in October, and his government remains divided over the pace and scope of the withdrawal.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi has emphasized that the Lebanon front is the "first real test" of the U.S.-Iran nuclear negotiations. If the Israel-Hezbollah front can be stabilized, it could open a diplomatic window for broader regional de-escalation.
