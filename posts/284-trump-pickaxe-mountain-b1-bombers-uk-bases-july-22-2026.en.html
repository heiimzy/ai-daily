⚡ July 22 International Politics: Trump Threatens Strike on Iran's Pickaxe Mountain Nuclear Bunker, B-1 Bombers Depart UK, Burnham Approves UK Bases

1. Trump Targets Pickaxe Mountain: 'A Nice Big Fat Shot Right in the Front Door'

President Donald Trump, in a July 21 interview on The Hugh Hewitt Show, explicitly named Iran's Pickaxe Mountain (Kūh-e Kolang Gaz La) underground nuclear facility as a target for the first time, vowing to deliver "a nice big fat shot right in the front door" and stating the strike would happen "pretty soon."

Pickaxe Mountain is located approximately 2 km south of the Natanz enrichment facility in Iran's Isfahan province, roughly 220 km south of Tehran. According to the latest satellite imagery analysis by the Institute for Science and International Security (ISIS), the facility features two separate tunnel networks — one originally built around 2007 and another begun around 2020 — all buried 90 to 137 meters deep beneath solid granite rock. Analysts assess that the main caverns lie beyond the reach of the largest conventional bunker-busting bomb in the U.S. arsenal, the 30,000-pound GBU-57/B Massive Ordnance Penetrator (MOP).

"They don't have it because we have eyes with Space Force, and everything else. We have a lot of eyes on it [Pickaxe Mountain]. But Pickaxe is a possible target for a nice big fat shot right near the front door, and I think that maybe you'll see that." — Donald Trump on The Hugh Hewitt Show

Notably, Trump has previously declared Iran's nuclear program "obliterated" — the June 2025 Operation Midnight Hammer saw U.S. B-2 bombers drop MOPs on the Fordow and Natanz facilities, while March 2026's Operation Epic Fury targeted the Shahid Boroujerdi underground site. Pickaxe Mountain was left untouched in both campaigns. This new点名 suggests U.S. intelligence believes the facility may be nearing operational capability for nuclear-related activities.

2. B-1 Bombers Depart UK for the Middle East

In the early hours of Wednesday, July 22 (Beijing time), multiple U.S. Air Force B-1B Lancer strategic bombers reportedly departed UK bases, heading toward the Middle East. Meanwhile, KC-135 Stratotanker aerial refueling aircraft launched from Romania, consistent with supporting the bomber formation en route.

The B-1B is a supersonic strategic bomber capable of carrying over 34 tons of precision-guided munitions and penetrating bombs. Analysts note that launching B-1s from UK bases rather than direct from the U.S. significantly reduces transit distance and increases payload capacity for missions against deep targets in Iran.

The Wall Street Journal previously reported that Iran has moved thousands of centrifuges into the tunnels beneath Pickaxe Mountain. Israeli intelligence has also confirmed that Iran has been steadily transferring key nuclear program components to the facility since fall 2025, raising alarm in both Washington and Jerusalem.

3. New UK PM Burnham Approves US Use of British Bases

New British Prime Minister Andy Burnham made his first major foreign policy decision within 48 hours of taking office: approving continued U.S. use of British military bases for "defensive strikes" against Iran, maintaining the policy established by his predecessor Keir Starmer.

The bases involved include:

  • RAF Fairford, Gloucestershire — Forward operating base for B-1, B-2 and other strategic bombers
  • Diego Garcia, Indian Ocean — America's key strategic hub in the Indian Ocean

Bloomberg reported that Burnham was briefed on the decision immediately after being formally appointed as PM on Monday (July 21) and subsequently spoke with President Trump by phone. A Downing Street statement said: "The prime minister outlined the UK's commitment to securing the movement of shipping in the Strait of Hormuz."

The decision is expected to draw criticism from the left-wing Green Party and Liberal Democrats, who argue it makes Britain complicit in war crimes. However, the Burnham government maintains that "defensive strikes" comply with international law given Iran's ongoing attacks on commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz.

4. US-Iran Conflict: 10th Consecutive Night of Strikes

As of July 22, U.S. forces have completed their 10th consecutive night of airstrikes on Iran. U.S. Central Command said the strikes targeted Iranian military command centers, naval capabilities, missile and drone launch sites, and air defense systems "to degrade Iran's ability to continue attacking commercial vessels flowing through the Strait of Hormuz."

Iran has retaliated forcefully. On July 21, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) announced strikes on Amazon infrastructure in Bahrain in retaliation for U.S. attacks on Iranian nuclear facilities. Iran also launched missile and drone attacks on U.S. bases in Kuwait and Jordan. The Pentagon confirmed at least three U.S. service members killed and nearly 100 wounded in recent attacks.

Meanwhile, Yemen's Iran-aligned Houthis declared a naval blockade on Saudi Arabia, threatening the Bab el-Mandeb Strait — through which approximately 7% of global oil supply transits. The Saudi-led coalition has vowed to respond with force.

5. Diplomatic Window: 10-Day Ceasefire Proposal Still on Table

Despite escalating military operations, diplomatic channels remain partially open. According to Axios, regional mediators including Qatar, Egypt, and Pakistan have presented both the U.S. and Iran with a 10-day ceasefire proposal aimed at reopening the Strait of Hormuz and creating space for longer-term negotiations. However, the Trump administration remains divided, with some officials arguing that accepting a ceasefire after three U.S. deaths would signal weakness.

Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesperson Esmail Baghaei confirmed receiving messages from the U.S. via mediators. Secretary of State Marco Rubio acknowledged that Iran "continues to send signals that it wants dialogue and negotiations" but emphasized the U.S. would respond to actions, not words.

Analysts note that Trump faces a difficult dilemma: accepting a 10-day ceasefire could be seen as weakness, while escalating further risks entangling the U.S. in a prolonged war with rising oil prices — American gas prices have already crossed $4 per gallon.
