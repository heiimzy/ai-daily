⚡ Gold Defies Geopolitics, Stalls at $4,120; September Rate Hike Odds Cross 50%; Morgan Stanley Flags 3 Risks to Summer Rally

Global financial markets are entering a period of heightened uncertainty. The escalating US-Iran conflict and near-total halt of tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz should, by traditional logic, be gold's moment to shine. Instead, spot gold slipped to $4,120.67 per ounce on Friday, extending its weekly decline. The culprit? Oil-driven inflation fears that pushed September Fed rate hike probability above 50% for the first time this cycle, completely overwhelming gold's safe-haven bid.

The Gold Paradox: Inflation Fears Trump Geopolitics

Spot gold settled at $4,120.67 on Friday, down $3.12 or -0.08%. As analysts at FXEmpire noted, this was a session where "gold should be running" — Iranian forces hit US military facilities across Gulf states, explosions were reported near Iran's nuclear infrastructure, and Strait of Hormuz tanker traffic stayed near a standstill. Instead, gold finished lower.

"Oil's rally reset the inflation outlook, September rate hike odds crossed 50% for the first time, and the yield market repriced before gold had a chance to catch a geopolitical bid." — FXEmpire

The 10-year US Treasury yield rose more than 2 basis points to 4.561%. The 2-year climbed more than 4 basis points to 4.208%. The 30-year added less than 1 basis point to 5.062%. Crucially, the 2-year yield moving faster than the 30-year on a day with no major economic data told the bond market was pricing in a policy change — not merely reacting to noise.

Diverging Institutional Views on Gold

Institutions remain sharply divided. JPMorgan maintains a bullish medium-to-long-term outlook, projecting gold could reach $4,500 per ounce by late 2026, driven by persistent inflation concerns and geopolitical risks. In contrast, Bloomberg Intelligence Senior Macro Strategist Mike McGlone issued a cautionary note on July 11, suggesting gold's rally may be losing steam. He pointed to the formation of a large red annual candlestick and forecast a sustainable peak for 2026 after gold's record highs near $5,500 earlier this year.

Morgan Stanley: Three Risks That Could Shatter the Summer Rally

Andrew Sheets, global head of fixed income research at Morgan Stanley, warned on the bank's "Thoughts on the Market" podcast that despite July historically being one of the best months for equities — the S&P 500 has gained every July since 2014 — the market is showing cracks. The Nasdaq 100 is roughly flat since the start of the second half, and a rotation trade has roiled the sector as investors take profits in high-flying chip and memory stocks.

Risk 1: Iran War Reignites

Morgan Stanley's base case relies on the assumption that maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz will eventually normalize, with Brent crude falling back to at least $75 a barrel within 12 months. However, the US Strategic Petroleum Reserve has been drawn down to its lowest-ever levels, reducing the ability to absorb supply shocks. Higher oil prices push up the cost of other goods, amplifying inflationary pressures throughout the economy.

"The US has already drawn down its Strategic Petroleum Reserve to its lowest-ever levels, potentially reducing some ability to absorb shocks if the conflict re-escalates." — Andrew Sheets, Morgan Stanley

Risk 2: The Fed Hikes Rates

Markets are pricing in an 82% chance the Fed will hike rates at least once by year-end, per the CME FedWatch tool. The June FOMC minutes revealed that several officials wanted another rate increase before the committee decided to hold. New Fed Chair Kevin Warsh testifies before Congress on July 14-15, and markets will scrutinize his remarks for clues on policy direction. The Cleveland Fed's Nowcast models CPI at 3.71% and core PCE at 3.84% — well above the Fed's 2% target.

With nominal rates at 3.50-3.75% and inflation above 3.5%, real yields remain negative — which has been supportive for equities. But if Warsh signals a hawkish pivot, the entire rate repricing could accelerate rapidly.

Risk 3: AI Capex Spending Cools

AI investment has been the cornerstone of the 2026 equity rally. Morgan Stanley estimates global Big Tech AI capex at ~$800 billion in 2026, rising to $1.2 trillion in 2027. But if Q2 earnings reveal hesitation to spend — particularly given the recent underperformance of large tech stocks — sentiment could shift quickly. The Magnificent Seven stocks dropped 13% from their May peak through their June trough. Some traders are already punishing the biggest AI spenders, signaling growing anxiety over AI investment ROI.

Oil Markets: Supply Healing Faster Than Demand

WTI crude settled at $70.98 per barrel on Friday, fading from an intraday spike to $72.83. The IEA's July monthly report revealed that global oil and refined products demand is set to decline by 1 million barrels per day in 2026 — the first annual drop since 2020. Supply rebounded by 4.1 million bpd in June to 98.8 million bpd, though output remains 9.4 million bpd below pre-war levels.

The IEA's forward projections are worse for the bullish case: the balance swings back to surplus toward year-end, with a significant overhang building in 2027 if transit volumes keep recovering. De-escalation fast-forwards the glut; renewed escalation deepens demand destruction. Only a full, sustained loss of the Strait of Hormuz would genuinely re-price supply — and traders have now watched two shutdowns of the waterway fail to stick.

  • Tuesday (July 14): June CPI report (Cleveland Fed Nowcast: 3.71% YoY); Fed Chair Warsh testifies before House Financial Services Committee; JPMorgan, Wells Fargo, Citigroup kick off Q2 earnings season
  • Wednesday (July 15): Warsh continues Senate testimony; Bank of America, Goldman Sachs, Johnson & Johnson, ASML report
  • Thursday (July 16): June retail sales; weekly jobless claims; TSMC, Netflix, Morgan Stanley, United Airlines report
  • Friday (July 17): June housing starts and building permits; Baker Hughes rig count

Analysts expect S&P 500 companies to report Q2 net profit growth of 24% year-over-year, with technology leading. The S&P 500 trades at roughly 20x forward earnings, down from 21.3x in late May, and is up 10.7% year-to-date.

Conversely, cooler-than-expected data could push September hike odds back below 40%, giving both gold and equities a reprieve.
