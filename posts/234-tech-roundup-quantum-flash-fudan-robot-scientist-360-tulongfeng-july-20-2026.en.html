Fudan University Invents "Quantum Flash Memory" Achieving Room-Temperature Single-Electron Storage at Theoretical Limit

The physical limits of memory chips have been pushed to their theoretical maximum by a Chinese research team. On July 17, the team led by Zhou Peng and Liu Chunsen at Fudan University published their groundbreaking results in the journal Science, unveiling a "quantum flash" memory technology called "Guiyi" — a device with the world's largest non-volatile quantum storage window that achieves stable single-electron storage at room temperature (27°C) for the first time.

In theory, "one electron, one bit" represents the physical limit of storage density — storing 1 bit of information requires just 1 electron. But current mainstream DRAM consumes approximately 200,000 electrons to store a single bit. If we think of a storage device as a reservoir and electrons as individual droplets, observing single-electron storage is like trying to detect the ripple of a single drop in a vast lake.

Based on fundamental quantum mechanics principles and leveraging the atomic-level electron confinement advantages of 2D semiconductors, the team designed the "Guiyi" quantum flash device. A single injected electron creates a 0.5-volt storage window that meets commercial-grade standards, reaching the theoretical peak of charge storage. The device can fundamentally reduce computing power consumption and represents a next-generation storage core suited for artificial general intelligence (AGI) development.

The team also introduced an innovative "Density-of-States Scissors" theory, revealing an unprecedented anomalous quantum storage behavior — using an invisible "quantum scissors" in energy space to precisely "cut" and make specific quantum states disappear. This achievement not only pioneers a complete theoretical framework for single-electron quantum storage but also fills a critical gap in the path toward engineering applications.

Shenzhen Builds "Robot Scientist" Dr.Mind, Accelerating Critical Materials Breakthroughs

At the Materials AI Laboratory of the Shenzhen Institute of Advanced Technology (SIAT), Chinese Academy of Sciences, a two-armed robot named "Dr.Mind" independently performs a complete centrifuge experiment — opening lids, picking up test tubes, balancing samples, and resetting — with zero human intervention. This is China's first embodied intelligent robot scientist system for materials research, developed by the team led by Professor Yu Xuefeng at SIAT's Materials AI Research Center.

The team built a dual-mode architecture: the "brain" is a materials science large language model that reviews literature, derives research approaches, and designs experimental protocols, while the "cerebellum" is a proprietary embodied intelligence algorithm that manages autonomous movement, dexterous grasping, visual and tactile perception. This creates a complete "perception-decision-execution-feedback" loop: the AI outputs process plans, the robot executes experiments, real-time data flows back to refine the model, continuously optimizing the research pipeline.

Key material breakthroughs include:

  • Magnetic microspheres: Less than one-third the diameter of a human hair, these are essential consumables for in-vitro diagnostics and biopharma — listed as a "bottleneck" technology for over a decade. The robot's high-throughput autonomous screening improved experimental efficiency by 10x, producing domestic microspheres matching imported quality.
  • Lithium iron phosphate (LFP) cathode material: The intelligent system completed full-process optimization, helping a partner company build a 300,000-ton LFP production line with 541% annual business growth.
  • Black phosphorus and quantum dots: The system dramatically compressed parameter optimization time, reducing early-stage commercialization costs.

In terms of operational precision, the robot's adaptive force control keeps the coefficient of variation below 0.5%. For multi-day experiments like single-crystal growth, the robot monitors microscopic changes around the clock, automatically adjusting temperature and parameters to consistently grow large, high-quality single crystals. The fully automated centrifuge loading task achieves 92% success stability after multiple simulation training rounds.

Over the past five years, the team has undertaken over 250 million yuan ($36.8M) in commissioned enterprise R&D projects, holds 120 invention patents, and has commercialized 29 core technologies worth over 40 million yuan. The team has led the construction of the first batch of national-level manufacturing pilot platforms, bridging the gap from lab research to production line.

360's AI Vulnerability Hunting Agent "Tulongfeng" Opens for Public Trial

On July 20, 360 Group officially opened "Tulongfeng" (Dragon Blade), its AI vulnerability hunting agent, for public trial — with first priority given to enterprise users, critical industries, and national infrastructure operators. Dubbed "China's Mythos," the agent leverages 360's two decades of offensive-defensive cybersecurity experience, combining large language model capabilities, security expert knowledge, and vulnerability databases into a collaborative multi-agent system.

Unlike traditional static scanning tools with added AI, or systems that generate a flood of "suspicious alerts" for manual triage, Tulongfeng uses a multi-agent architecture where specialized agents handle project profiling, task scheduling, attack surface analysis, static auditing, dynamic verification, targeted vulnerability mining, and report generation. The system first understands project structure, then decomposes audit tasks and performs parallel analysis around high-risk entry points, with agents sharing real-time updates through a shared state board.

Tulongfeng's four core capabilities include:

  • Multi-agent collaboration: Simulates a professional security team working in concert rather than a single-point scanner
  • Attack surface analysis: Automatically identifies externally reachable entry points across HTTP interfaces, system calls, file operations, and mobile components
  • Intelligent auditing: Dual-engine approach combining pattern matching and semantic reasoning — not just identifying "code that looks vulnerable" but determining "whether a real attack path exists"
  • Verification closure: Reads source code, traces call chains, generates attack scripts, and tests in sandbox environments — delivering verifiable, reproducible, and fixable vulnerability conclusions

To date, Tulongfeng has discovered over 4,000 vulnerabilities, with more than 100 high-value bugs confirmed by authoritative bodies. Its track record includes Microsoft acknowledgments for Windows kernel, Office, and Excel zero-days, as well as vulnerabilities in the Openclaw and Flowise platforms. 360 founder Zhou Hongyi has warned that China's large-scale reliance on open-source software and complex architectures could trigger a concentrated wave of vulnerabilities, making proactive detection a strategic necessity.
