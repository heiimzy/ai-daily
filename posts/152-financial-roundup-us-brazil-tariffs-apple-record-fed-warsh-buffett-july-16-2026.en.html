⚡ July 16 Financial Roundup: US Slaps 25% Tariffs on Brazil, Apple Hits Record High on AI Chip Acquisition Buzz, Fed Chair Warsh Testifies, Buffett Warns of Gambling Market

I. US Imposes 25% Tariffs on Brazilian Goods, Effective July 22

The Office of the U.S. Trade Representative (USTR) announced on Wednesday (July 15) that it will impose 25% tariffs on most imports from Brazil effective July 22, concluding a yearlong Section 301 investigation into what Washington calls unfair trade practices. The investigation covered digital trade policies, electronic payment services, intellectual property protection, ethanol market access, and illegal deforestation measures.

Exempted products include coffee, beef, oranges and orange juice, certain oil and gas energy products, and aerospace parts and components — items either not produced in the U.S. or whose disruption could harm supply chains. USTR Ambassador Jamieson Greer stated: "Brazil's unfair trading practices have prevented US workers and producers from accessing this important market. We remain open to continuing negotiations with Brazil to bring about long-needed changes."

The tariffs mark a strategic shift by the Trump administration following a Supreme Court ruling in February that struck down sweeping 50% tariffs imposed under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA). The administration has turned to Section 301 authority, which enables tariff imposition without additional congressional approval following an unfair trade practices finding. A separate forced-labor investigation could add another 12.5% duty on Brazilian goods, with a decision expected next week.

Analysts note the tariffs have become entangled in Brazil's upcoming October presidential election politics. Brazilian President Lula visited the White House in May, raising hopes of a compromise, but negotiations ultimately collapsed. The U.S. does not expect immediate retaliation from Brazil but has warned that any pushback could invite further countermeasures.

II. Apple Surges 4% to Record High, Market Cap Nears $5T on AI Chip Acquisition Report

Apple (AAPL) shares surged 4.01% to close at $327.50 on Wednesday, hitting an all-time high with an intraday peak of $328.73. The rally was fueled by a trifecta of positive catalysts: cooling inflation data, a major AI regulatory milestone in China, and blockbuster acquisition news.

The June U.S. Producer Price Index (PPI) fell 0.3% month-over-month — the steepest one-month decline in six years — marking the second consecutive day of below-consensus inflation data. This dramatically reduced the probability of a Fed rate hike this month to just 10.2%, according to CME FedWatch. Meanwhile, Apple and Alibaba's AI feature partnership received regulatory approval in China, removing a key hurdle for the domestic launch of next-generation iPhone AI capabilities. Alibaba shares also jumped 4.8% on the news.

The most transformative story, however, came from The Information's exclusive report that Apple is actively hunting for AI chip company acquisitions. Apple has held discussions with multiple banks about potential deals in recent months and has directly approached semiconductor startups about acquisition interest, according to sources. The company is reportedly preparing for multibillion-dollar acquisitions — a rare strategic shift for a company whose biggest deal ever was the $3 billion Beats purchase in 2014.

The driving force behind Apple's urgency: its self-designed M2 Ultra server chips have proven inadequate for large AI model inference workloads. Apple's new Siri — powered by Google's Gemini — effectively runs on Nvidia GPUs inside Google Cloud. Apple's in-house server chip, codenamed "Baltra," was due this year but has slipped, with Bloomberg reporting that a chip competitive with Nvidia may not arrive until 2029. An M5 Ultra upgrade is planned as an interim solution.

Apple CFO Kevan Parekh recently signaled the company would abandon its long-held conservative financial target of holding cash equal to debt, freeing up its $45.6 billion cash hoard for potential acquisitions. The company also just extended a $30 billion chip partnership with Broadcom through 2031 while separately seeking immediate acquisition targets.

III. Fed Chair Warsh's Senate Debut: Inflation Not Defeated, Independence Affirmed

Federal Reserve Chairman Kevin Warsh completed his first congressional testimony before the Senate Banking Committee this week, delivering a carefully calibrated message. Asked about the Trump administration's influence on monetary policy, Warsh acknowledged meeting "often" with the administration but stressed that Fed decisions remain independent — "the president has never asked me to do anything improper."

On inflation, Warsh struck a cautious tone. While acknowledging recent improvements — June CPI dropping to 3.5% (a four-year low) and PPI unexpectedly falling 0.3% — he emphasized that "inflation has not been fully defeated." He drew a distinction between AI-driven chip price increases and energy-driven inflation from the Middle East conflict, arguing they have fundamentally different implications for monetary policy. Warsh also revealed that five working groups he established are approaching the Fed's inflation framework "from a blank sheet of paper."

Following two consecutive days of below-consensus inflation data, market pricing for a July rate hike collapsed from 31% to 10.2%, according to CME FedWatch. President Trump reiterated his desire for rate cuts, saying "the Fed standing still is better than raising rates." The 10-year Treasury yield fell to 4.55%, the 2-year to 4.13%, and the U.S. Dollar Index dropped to a four-week low.

Fed Governor Lisa Cook, speaking separately, expressed concern about potential inflation pressures from tariffs, Middle East conflict, and AI investment, signaling readiness to act if inflation persists. The differing emphasis between Warsh and Cook reveals that the FOMC has not yet formed a consensus on the timing of any rate cuts.

IV. Buffett Slams Speculative Market: "Tough to Find Values When Everybody Is Gambling"

Warren Buffett issued a rare and sharp critique of current market dynamics this week. In an interview, the Berkshire Hathaway chairman said the stock market is increasingly driven by speculative trading rather than long-term investing, declaring: "It's tough to find values when everybody is preferring gambling."

Buffett's comments come as the S&P 500 sits within 0.5% of its all-time high, powered by two consecutive days of gains following cooling inflation data. While acknowledging that economic fundamentals and corporate earnings remain solid, Buffett suggested that market sentiment has clearly diverged from value-investing principles. Notably, Berkshire Hathaway has been steadily reducing its equity holdings and building cash reserves, which surpassed $200 billion in Q1 2026 — a record high.

On the same day, Citigroup revised its 12-month Bitcoin price target downward from $112,000 to $82,000, citing June's heavy ETF outflows and geopolitical risks from the Middle East. Bitcoin traded around $64,734, having briefly touched $65,507 overnight before retreating. Ethereum outperformed, breaking above $1,900 for the first time since February.

V. Wall Street Close: Three Major Indexes Edge Higher, Tech Leads, Memory Chips Plunge

U.S. stocks closed modestly higher on Wednesday (July 15). The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 150.37 points (+0.29%) to 52,658.64, the S&P 500 gained 28.83 points (+0.38%) to 7,572.40, and the Nasdaq Composite climbed 162.22 points (+0.62%) to 26,269.23.

Big Tech was the clear leader: Apple (+4.01%) led the Dow, followed by Alphabet (+3.17%), Meta (+3.07%), Amazon (+3.02%), and Microsoft (+2.78%). Nvidia recovered from an intraday dip of nearly 3% to close up 0.33%. Tesla was the only Magnificent Seven stock to decline, falling 0.43%. Morgan Stanley hit a new all-time high, and BlackRock surged 6.6% after earnings.

The memory chip sector was the day's biggest loser, suffering a coordinated selloff. SanDisk fell 8.12%, SK Hynix ADR dropped 9%, Western Digital lost 8.7%, Micron declined 8%, and Seagate fell 5.7%. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index dropped 2.08%. Analysts attributed the selloff to profit-taking after massive gains earlier this week — SK Hynix had surged 27% in a single session just two days prior. Market confidence in the memory super-cycle appears to be wavering, with investors questioning whether the AI-driven demand surge is sustainable at current valuation levels.

ASML reported better-than-expected Q2 earnings and raised its full-year guidance, citing continued AI chip demand, but the positive news failed to reverse the broader chip sector weakness. WTI crude rose 0.33% to $79.60/barrel, and Brent crude gained 0.26% to $84.95/barrel — both hitting one-month highs as the U.S.-Iran conflict continues to support energy prices.
