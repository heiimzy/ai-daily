⚡ July 16 International Politics: Iran Strikes US Bases in 3 Countries, Trump Threatens Power Plants, Russia Bombards Kyiv, UK PM Starmer's Final Kyiv Visit

1. Iran Launches Retaliatory Strikes on US Bases in Bahrain, Kuwait, Jordan

The US-Iran conflict escalated dramatically on July 16 as Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) and the Iranian army launched large-scale retaliatory operations against US military bases in Bahrain, Kuwait, and Jordan, firing ballistic missiles and drones in response to the fifth consecutive day of American airstrikes that had expanded to the outskirts of Tehran.

In the eighth wave of its Operation Nasr-2 (Victory 2), the IRGC's Navy and Aerospace Force struck Kuwait's Ali Al Salem Air Base, targeting the C-RAM early warning radar system and what it described as "a gathering point of US troops." The IRGC claimed the operation dealt a "crushing blow" to US forces. Simultaneously, the Iranian army conducted the ninth phase of Operation Saeqeh (Lightning), launching loitering munitions against the fixed radar site, communications systems, and fuel depots at Jordan's al-Azraq Airbase — described as "one of the most important strategic and command centers for US forces operating in West Asia."

Air raid sirens sounded across Bahrain, home to the US Fifth Fleet. Kuwait confirmed its air defenses intercepted 4 missiles and 21 drones from Iran, reporting material damage but no casualties. Jordan's armed forces said they shot down 3 missiles from Iran. In Iraq's Kurdistan region, the US-led coalition downed 8 explosive-laden drones over Erbil, with explosions heard near the US consulate.

US Daylight Strikes on Iran Mark New Escalation

On July 15, the US military conducted two waves of precision strikes against Iran. The first wave at 6:00 AM EDT lasted 90 minutes, targeting coastal defense systems and cruise missile storage and launch sites on Greater Tunb Island. Nine hours later, CENTCOM announced a second wave of strikes hitting Iranian command centers, air defense sites, missile and drone capabilities, and coastal surveillance facilities in multiple locations including Bandar Abbas.

This marked the first time the US conducted daylight strikes on Iran since the conflict began, signaling a significant increase in operational tempo. Three US officials told Reuters the strikes were designed not only to force open the Strait of Hormuz but also to degrade Iranian military capabilities ahead of potential more complex operations — including ground troop deployment to seize Kharg Island and bombing the underground nuclear facility known as "Pickaxe Mountain."

The IRGC also announced it had shot down a US MQ-9 drone over the southwestern city of Andimeshk using an advanced air defense system, and separately downed a US LUCAS drone over Bandar Abbas.

Iran Declares 'Permanent Control' Over Strait of Hormuz

Iranian military headquarters issued a statement on July 16 declaring that Iran has "permanent sovereignty and control" over the Strait of Hormuz. Brigadier-General Ebrahim Zolfaghari stated that the strait has been a "red line" since the US-Israel war began on February 28. Tehran warned that if the US attacks Iranian infrastructure, Iran will target infrastructure across the entire region.

"We are in an essential and existential war with America," said Parliament Speaker and chief negotiator Mohammad Baqer Ghalibaf. "Iran's security depends on maintaining Iranian arrangements in the strait." Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmail Baghaei confirmed there are currently "no plans for negotiations" and the country is focused on national defense.

2. Trump Issues Ultimatum: Power Plants and Bridges Next

President Donald Trump warned Iran in a Fox News interview on July 15 that if no deal is reached by next week, the US will expand strikes to Iranian power plants and bridges. "Next week it gets really bad for them," Trump said. "Next week we're going to hit their power plants, and bridges. Unless they come to the negotiating table, we'll destroy all their power plants and bridges."

Speaking at the Pennsylvania Defense and Innovation Summit, Trump claimed Iran "wants to settle so badly," adding: "They don't like what we're doing, and they do want to settle. We'll find out whether or not we settle with them, or we just finish it off."

In a possible sign of goodwill, Trump confirmed that Iran had allowed American citizen Dena Karari — who had been prevented from leaving Iran since December 2024 — to depart the country. "The United States of America appreciates this gesture of Goodwill by Iran," Trump wrote on Truth Social.

3. US Approves $1.96B Arms Sale to Saudi Arabia

The US State Department announced on July 15 the approval of a potential $1.96 billion arms sale to Saudi Arabia to strengthen its air defense capabilities amid escalating Middle East conflict. The package includes up to 20,000 Advanced Precision Kill Weapon Systems (APKWS-II) with warheads for both air-to-air and air-to-ground configurations. The principal contractor is BAE Systems of Nashua, New Hampshire.

A separate $484 million Foreign Military Sale to Kuwait for C-17 Sustainment and related equipment was also approved. "This proposed sale will support the foreign policy and national security objectives of the United States by enhancing the security of a major non-NATO ally," the State Department said.

Brent crude oil closed at $84.95/barrel on July 15, a one-month high with weekly gains of 13%. On July 16, prices eased slightly to $84.51/barrel. Maritime tracker Kpler reported only 21 transits through the Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday, with war risk insurance premiums reaching 7% of vessel value.

4. Russia Strikes Kyiv with Ballistic Missiles, 2 Killed

Russian forces launched a ballistic missile attack on Kyiv in the early hours of July 16, killing at least 2 people and injuring 6 others, including a 16-year-old boy. This marks the sixth ballistic missile attack on the Ukrainian capital this month.

Ukraine's Air Force warned of a ballistic missile threat at 12:46 AM, followed by multiple explosions across the city. The strikes ignited fires at warehouse facilities in the Sviatoshynskyi and Darnytskyi districts, with falling debris also striking a non-residential development area in Darnytsia. Three of the wounded were hospitalized.

Ukraine's Air Force reported that 8 ballistic missiles were fired in two waves targeting Kyiv. Russia's Defense Ministry claimed it hit military and industrial facilities in the capital involved in drone production and storage, as well as port infrastructure in Odesa and Pivdennyi.

Earlier on July 15, Russian daylight bombardments across Ukraine killed 13 people and wounded approximately 50 others. The UN Human Rights Monitoring Mission recorded at least 293 civilians killed and 1,990 wounded in June 2026 — the highest monthly toll since April 2022.

5. EU Signs Drone Production Deal with Ukraine

Hours before the missile attack, European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen arrived in Kyiv and announced a landmark "drone deal" between the European Union and Ukraine during the nation's Statehood Day ceremony. The agreement combines Ukraine's battlefield expertise with EU industrial capacity to establish joint projects and rapidly scale up drone production.

"This deal will bring together Ukrainian ingenuity and Europe's industrial scale," von der Leyen said, emphasizing the EU's advantages of "huge technological and industrial capacity" alongside "safe and secure production sites."

Ukraine's military also announced on July 16 that it struck 6 Russian tankers and 2 tugboats in the Black Sea and Sea of Azov overnight using naval drones. Ukraine's Security Service (SBU) confirmed that "Mamai" naval drones successfully hit the shadow fleet tankers "Louise 1" and "Banda." On July 15, Ukrainian Unmanned Systems Forces Commander Robert "Madyar" Brovdi announced that Operation "MoLoChKa" had expanded from the Sea of Azov to the Black Sea, striking a total of 17 oil tankers, 2 LNG carriers, and 1 tugboat in its first phase.

6. Outgoing UK PM Starmer Makes Final Kyiv Visit

Outgoing British Prime Minister Keir Starmer made a farewell visit to Kyiv on July 16, meeting President Volodymyr Zelenskyy to reaffirm the UK's "cast-iron" commitment to Ukraine. Starmer will formally step down on Monday, July 20, with Andy Burnham succeeding him as Labour leader and Prime Minister.

"Throughout this conflict, I have seen the incredible fortitude of the Ukrainian people and the iron will of a nation that refuses to be cowed," Starmer said during the high-security visit. He emphasized that Britain's support would continue under his successor, benefiting not just Ukraine and European security but also "families in Britain who have felt the cost of this war through rising prices."

Starmer's premiership saw the signing of a 100-year partnership between the UK and Ukraine. He becomes the fifth British Prime Minister since Russia's 2022 invasion, reflecting the turbulent nature of UK politics during wartime.

7. Over 500 Feared Dead in Myanmar Boat Capsizing

In a humanitarian disaster off the coast of Myanmar, two boats capsized with more than 500 people feared dead, most believed to be Rohingya. The United Nations has raised alarms, with rescue operations underway but hampered by severe sea conditions.
