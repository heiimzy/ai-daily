⚡ July 12 Financial Update: Iran Missile Strikes on Multiple US Bases Send Oil Surging 2.5%+ in Dark Trading, BofA Warns 'Everyone Long' Signals Danger, Global Markets Enter 'Turbulent Summer'

🛢️ Iran's Third-Wave Retaliation: Ballistic Missiles Hit US Bases Across the Gulf, Hormuz Shut Indefinitely

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched a sweeping third-wave retaliation on July 12, striking multiple US military installations across the Middle East with ballistic missiles and attack drones. According to CCTV News, targets included the US aircraft carrier logistics center and refueling platform at Oman's Duqm Port, the fighter jet maintenance and command center at Qatar's Al Udeid Air Base, Patriot missile systems and ammunition depots in Kuwait, communications and radar stations in Bahrain, and an air base and MQ-9 drone hangars in Jordan.

Simultaneously, the IRGC announced the Strait of Hormuz would remain "closed indefinitely" after disabling a second "violating vessel" in the waterway. Shipping data shows daily transits through the strait have collapsed from over 130 vessels before the conflict to just 22, with freight and insurance costs soaring.

In response, WTI and Brent crude both surged over 2.5% in dark trading, briefly touching 3% gains. Brent closed the week near $76 a barrel, roughly 5% above pre-conflict levels. Analysts warn that if the closure persists, oil prices could see a sharp rally when markets open Monday, reigniting inflation expectations and putting pressure on tech stock valuations.

"Iran has once again demonstrated its ability to sway global energy markets through actions in the Strait of Hormuz. While oil remains well below the wartime peak of nearly $120, the timing and intensity of this escalation cannot be ignored." — Analyst report cited by BlockBeats

⚠️ BofA Issues 'Everyone Long' Danger Signal: Bull & Bear Indicator Holds at 9.5 Extreme Optimism

Bank of America's latest The Flow Show report published July 12 issued a stark warning. Its Bull & Bear Indicator remains at 9.5 — deep in extreme optimism territory — well above the 8-point sell signal threshold. The sell signal has been flashing for multiple consecutive weeks. According to 24 years of historical data, the indicator has triggered 17 sell signals, with the global ACWI index averaging 2%-3% declines within 2-3 months, and extreme cases showing losses of 15%-20%.

The report reveals multiple extreme readings: fund manager equity holdings are at the 100th percentile (a historic first); hedge fund equity positions at the 81st percentile; global equity fund flows at the 88th percentile; bond fund flows at the 84th percentile. BofA's global flow trading model has also been flashing warnings for 8 consecutive weeks.

Yet money continues pouring into risk assets. Global equity funds saw net inflows of $56.6 billion in the week ending July 8 — the fourth-largest weekly total this year. Tech funds alone absorbed $18.8 billion, on track for a record $183 billion annual inflow.

💴 Yen Breaks 162, Hitting 40-Year Low as Carry Trade Risks Mount

The Japanese yen has once again become a global market flashpoint. USD/JPY broke through the 162 level, pushing the yen to its weakest point in nearly 40 years. Market analysts highlight two key systemic risk channels.

First, if Japanese authorities intervene to stabilize the yen, they may need to sell massive amounts of dollar assets — particularly US Treasuries — potentially triggering ripple effects across global bond markets. Second, a vast pool of yen carry trade positions (borrowing cheap yen to buy global assets) remains on the books, with the Bank of England warning that "leveraged capital has grown rapidly." A sharp yen rebound could force massive unwinding of these positions, with shockwaves spreading to unpredictable corners of the market.

The BoE specifically noted that leveraged capital has been a key driver of recent global equity strength — a finding that has sparked market unease.

📊 Global Markets Enter 'Turbulent Summer': Deep Fractures Beneath Calm VIX

Despite the VIX remaining low, internal market pressure has quietly climbed to multi-year highs. UBS derivatives strategy team's "Turbu-lens" market fragility gauge currently reads 0.9 (on a scale of -1 to 1) — the highest level since mid-September 2025. Historically, such readings have preceded sharp VIX spikes.

Barclays strategist Emmanuel Cau characterizes the current phase as a "dangerous summer window." UBS strategist Maxwell Grinacoff warns that single-stock volatility now exceeds index volatility by more than three times — a gap likely to narrow during the summer months. If systematic strategies add further leverage, the fragility reading "could truly hit +1."

State Street Investment Management CEO Yie-Hsin Hung told the Financial Times that new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's deliberate reduction in forward guidance "will introduce volatility and uncertainty." Bond yields are approaching 4.6%, adding further valuation pressure on equities.

📈 Q2 Earnings Season Opens: 24% Growth Expectations Become a Double-Edged Sword

Against this backdrop of accumulating macro risks, analysts expect S&P 500 Q2 earnings to grow 24% year-over-year, with the Euro Stoxx 600 also expected to deliver 12% growth. Unlike previous seasons, analysts have been raising forecasts throughout the pre-reporting period — the very confidence that means disappointment could trigger sharper selloffs.

The tech sector deserves special attention. Barclays estimates that Apple, Meta, Amazon, Alphabet, Microsoft, and Nvidia have collectively lost approximately $2 trillion in market value since October 2025. Nvidia, now worth $5 trillion, trades at a P/E ratio similar to Hershey — a striking sign of cooling enthusiasm.

Key events ahead: the US CPI report on July 14 and Fed Chair Warsh's congressional testimony on July 15. Bitcoin is trading near $63,850 with the Fear & Greed Index at 26 (fear). US spot Bitcoin ETFs recorded their first net inflow in eight weeks on July 10 at $90.44 million, but market sentiment remains cautious.
