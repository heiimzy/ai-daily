⚡ July 13 World Politics Roundup: US Launches 4th Wave of Strikes on Iran, Hormuz Strait Re-Closed, Oil Surges 4.5%, Senator Graham Dies at 71, Paris Wildfire Burns 800 Hectares

July 13, 2026 — A dramatic escalation in global geopolitical tensions unfolded over the weekend. The United States launched its fourth wave of airstrikes against Iran in a single week, striking over 300 military targets in total. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) retaliated with missile and drone attacks targeting US military facilities across five Gulf nations and declared the Strait of Hormuz closed indefinitely. Brent crude surged 4.5% as shipping through the critical waterway nearly ground to a halt. In Washington, Senator Lindsey Graham — a close Trump ally and foreign policy hawk — died suddenly at 71 from an aortic dissection. Meanwhile, a massive wildfire of "exceptional scale" raged through the Fontainebleau forest near Paris, burning 800 hectares and disrupting major transportation arteries.

1. US Launches 4th Wave of Strikes on Iran: 300+ Targets Hit

At 5 p.m. ET on Sunday, July 12 (12:30 a.m. Tehran time, July 13), US Central Command (CENTCOM) announced a new wave of offensive strikes against Iran — the fourth in a week. "At the direction of the Commander in Chief, U.S. Central Command forces have started conducting additional strikes against Iran to further degrade their ability to threaten freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz," CENTCOM said in a statement.

The strikes employed US fighter aircraft, naval vessels, one-way attack aerial drones, and one-way attack sea drones used in combat for the first time. Targets included Iranian military air defense systems, coastal radar sites, missile and drone capabilities, and small boats.

On July 11, CENTCOM had already struck approximately 140 Iranian military targets — including missile and drone launch positions, naval equipment, ammunition storage, communications networks, and coastal surveillance sites. Over three consecutive nights, more than 300 Iranian targets were hit. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth stated: "Iran made a bad choice. Now they are paying the price."

President Trump told Reuters: "We're beating them up." On NBC's Meet the Press, he added: "We bombed the hell out of them last night."

Iranian media reported explosions across multiple cities in southern Hormozgan province — Bandar Abbas, Sirik, Jask, and Qeshm Island. A military base in Nain (Isfahan province) was hit, killing at least one and wounding seven. An agricultural water pumping station in Mahshahr (Khuzestan province) was also struck, killing one and wounding four. Over the past four days, US airstrikes have killed at least 19 people in Iran and wounded more than 100, according to Iranian state media.

2. Iran Strikes Back: Missiles Rain on Five Gulf Nations, Hormuz Closed Indefinitely

The IRGC launched a massive retaliatory operation. According to Iran's official IRNA news agency, the IRGC struck targets in five countries simultaneously:

  • Jordan: Ballistic missiles and drones struck Prince Hassan Air Base, hitting a US command and control center and drone hangars, setting fuel depots and ammunition storage facilities ablaze;
  • Kuwait: The Ali Al Salem Air Base and border outposts were targeted, and an offshore oil drilling platform was hit by a drone, wounding one worker;
  • Bahrain: The US base at Sheikh Isa and the 5th Fleet headquarters were attacked, triggering air raid sirens three times across the island nation;
  • Oman: IRGC claimed to have destroyed US aircraft carrier logistics hubs and refueling platforms at Port of Duqm, as well as long-range FPS radar and ship-detection radar systems;
  • Qatar: Al Udeid Air Base was struck by missiles, with three people — including a child — injured by falling shrapnel.

Iran's Persian Gulf Strait Authority declared: "Passage through the Strait of Hormuz is currently not possible due to recent illegal movements of the United States military forces in the region. Permits will be issued as soon as stability and calm are restored." Iran's top negotiator Mohammad Baqer Qalibaf posted on X: "The era of one-sided deals is OVER. We told you: keep your word or pay the price. Reality is knocking."

The US dismissed the closure claim. CENTCOM responded: "Iran does not control the strait. Traffic is flowing." The US Navy-led Joint Maritime Information Center confirmed that an expanded southern route near Oman remained available for two-way traffic.

3. Oil Prices Surge 4.5% as Hormuz Shipping Grinds to a Halt

The renewed conflict immediately rattled global energy markets. Brent crude futures surged 4.5% to $78.69 per barrel in early Asian trading on Monday, while WTI jumped to above $74 — the largest single-day gains since the June ceasefire agreement.

Shipping data from Kpler showed that only six vessels transited the Strait of Hormuz on Sunday — the lowest in five weeks, compared with pre-war daily averages of 130 ships carrying approximately 20 million barrels of oil (20% of global petroleum trade) and 80 million tonnes per annum of LNG. Windward, a maritime intelligence platform, raised the threat level at the chokepoint to "severe."

Wood Mackenzie warned that in a worst-case extended closure scenario, Brent could approach $200 per barrel by year-end, with diesel near $300 per barrel in key refining hubs, potentially contracting global GDP by approximately 0.4%.

Asian stock markets tumbled. Japan's Nikkei 225 closed nearly 2% lower, while South Korea's KOSPI plunged 9% as SK Hynix tanked 15%. Hong Kong's Hang Seng Index edged up 0.2%, bucking the regional trend.

4. Senator Lindsey Graham Dies Suddenly at 71: Aortic Dissection

The US political landscape was shaken by the sudden death of Senator Lindsey Graham (R-South Carolina) on Saturday evening, July 11 — just two days after his 71st birthday. Preliminary findings from the medical examiner indicated the cause was an aortic dissection (a tear in the inner wall of the aorta), related to arterial hardening.

Graham had just returned from a trip to Kyiv, where he met Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky on July 10. No health concerns were apparent during his travels. His office initially reported a "brief and sudden illness."

A former Air Force lawyer who served in Congress for more than three decades, Graham was one of the Senate's most influential foreign policy hawks — a staunch advocate for arming Ukraine, a fierce supporter of Israel, and a leading China hawk. His death deprives President Trump of a key congressional ally at a critical moment. Another senior Republican, 84-year-old Mitch McConnell of Kentucky, has also been hospitalized for weeks with an undisclosed medical condition, raising concerns about a leadership vacuum in the Republican caucus.

Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesperson Esmaeil Baghaei described Graham as "malicious," stating that "his entire being was aggressive and intimidating" — a remark that drew diplomatic controversy.

5. Major Wildfire Rages in Fontainebleau Forest Near Paris: 800 Hectares Burned

A massive wildfire erupted on the afternoon of July 12 in the Fontainebleau Forest, approximately 60km southeast of Paris. By early July 13, the fire had consumed 800 hectares of woodland. Interior Minister Laurent Nunez described it as a fire of "exceptional scale."

Some 400 firefighters and two aerial firefighting aircraft were deployed. The blaze forced the partial closure of the A6 motorway, France's main north-south artery, and disrupted high-speed TGV rail lines — just ahead of the July 14 national holiday and the first major summer travel weekend. Around 15 homes in the village of Vaudoue were evacuated. Firefighting aircraft suspended operations overnight due to poor visibility.

France is experiencing its third heatwave of the year. Data shows that approximately 25,000 hectares have burned across France in 2026 — double the area at the same point last year — with officials attributing the increase to climate change-driven drought and extreme temperatures.
