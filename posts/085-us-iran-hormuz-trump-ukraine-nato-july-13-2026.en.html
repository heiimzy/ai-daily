⚡ July 13 World Politics Roundup: US-Iran All-Out Clash — 140 Targets Hit, Hormuz Closed, Trump Pivots to Arm Ukraine with Patriots

1. US-Iran Conflict Explodes: Third Wave of Massive Airstrikes

The U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) launched its third and largest wave of airstrikes against Iran in a week early Sunday morning, striking approximately 140 military targets across southern Iran. The operation was framed as a direct response to Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) attacking a civilian container ship in the Strait of Hormuz.

According to CENTCOM's official statement, the targeted sites included missile and drone launch positions, naval capabilities, ammunition storage facilities, communication networks, and coastal surveillance locations. Over three nights this week, more than 300 targets have been struck "to degrade Iran's ability to attack civilian mariners and commercial vessels freely transiting the strait." Iran's Ministry of Health reported 17 fatalities and 115 injuries across six Iranian cities from the U.S. strikes.

"Iran does not control the strait. Traffic is flowing." — CENTCOM, refuting Iran's Hormuz closure claim

The immediate trigger was the IRGC's missile strike on the Cyprus-flagged M/V GFS Galaxy, a container ship transiting the Strait of Hormuz. The vessel suffered severe engine room damage, was set ablaze, and its crew was forced to abandon ship. One Indian crew member remains missing. India's Ministry of External Affairs confirmed the crew member's nationality and called for "free and unimpeded" navigation through the strait.

Iran's Retaliation: Five Gulf Nations Targeted

In response, the IRGC announced the "indefinite closure" of the Strait of Hormuz and launched coordinated ballistic missile and drone strikes targeting multiple countries hosting U.S. military forces:

  • Qatar: Ballistic missiles targeted Al Udeid Airbase. Qatari air defense intercepted incoming fire, but three people — including one child — were wounded by falling shrapnel. Qatar condemned the attack as a "flagrant violation of sovereignty and territorial integrity" and a "blatant breach of international law."
  • Kuwait: Iranian drones and missiles struck a Patriot air defense system, ammunition depot, and radar site. Kuwaiti military confirmed interception of "hostile aerial targets."
  • Bahrain: Home to the U.S. Navy's 5th Fleet, Bahrain activated air raid sirens as Iranian drones targeted U.S. communications and radar sites.
  • Jordan: Eight Iranian ballistic missiles were fired toward Jordanian territory, all intercepted by Jordanian air defense systems. Missile fragments scattered across multiple locations with no casualties reported.
  • Oman: Iran claimed a "heavy and surprise" attack on logistics support centers and refueling platforms used by U.S. aircraft carriers at the port of Duqm. Oman formally summoned Iran's ambassador in protest.

2. Strait of Hormuz: Global Energy Artery Under Siege

The Strait of Hormuz handles approximately 20% of the world's oil and liquefied natural gas supply. The IRGC declared the waterway closed "until further notice and until the end of American interference in this region." However, CENTCOM insisted the strait remains open to commercial shipping, stating it had shepherded over 800 commercial vessels carrying 400 million barrels of crude through the waterway since early May.

An advisor to Iran's Supreme Leader declared: "This strategic passage is more important than dozens of atomic bombs, and the Islamic Republic of Iran will protect it." Meanwhile, a Greek shipping magnate told reporters: "The strait is not closed by law, but it is closed by risk. I will not send my tankers and my men into a shooting gallery."

War risk insurance premiums for vessels operating in the Persian Gulf have surged to effectively uninsurable levels. Energy analysts warn that a sustained Hormuz closure exceeding 30 days would represent one of the most severe energy supply disruptions in modern history. Brent crude prices spiked further following the latest wave of strikes.

On the diplomatic front, Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi met with Omani officials in Muscat on Saturday to discuss strait management. Oman proposed a dual-channel system using both the southern Omani waters and northern Iranian waters, but Iran requested to "take the proposal back to Tehran for further consultations." Hours later, diplomacy collapsed into the largest military escalation since the ceasefire.

3. Trump Confirms Patriot Missiles and Offensive Weapons for Ukraine

In a dramatic policy shift, U.S. President Donald Trump confirmed on Sunday at Joint Base Andrews that the United States will provide Ukraine with Patriot air defense systems and "various pieces of very sophisticated military equipment," potentially including offensive long-range missiles capable of striking deep inside Russian territory.

"We will send them Patriots, which they desperately need," Trump said. "Putin really surprised a lot of people. He talks nice and then bombs everybody in the evening. But there's a little bit of a problem there. I don't like it." He emphasized that NATO and European partners would reimburse the U.S. at 100% of the cost.

Senator Lindsey Graham told CBS News' Face the Nation: "The game, regarding Putin's invasion of Ukraine, is about to change. I expect, in the coming days, you will see weapons flowing at a record level." Graham revealed that Trump may exercise the Presidential Drawdown Authority (PDA) to release approximately $3.85 billion in remaining Biden-era funds, and potentially tap about $5 billion in frozen Russian assets.

Axios reported that Trump is preparing a "major announcement" on July 14 that could include the first new funding for Ukraine since his term began — potentially including offensive weapons such as long-range missiles. This marks a complete reversal from his previous stance of withholding new aid to pressure Kyiv into negotiations.

NATO Chief to Visit Washington July 14

NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte announced he will visit Washington on July 14-15, coinciding with Trump's planned "major statement" on Russia. Trump told NBC News: "I'm disappointed in Russia, but we'll see what happens over the next couple of weeks."

The policy reversal was driven by multiple factors. During a July 3 phone call, Putin reportedly informed Trump of plans to escalate military operations in eastern Ukraine within 60 days. Additionally, Trump has been deeply impressed by Ukraine's long-range drone campaign, including recent strikes on the Syzran oil refinery and eight Russian tankers in the Sea of Azov. Zelensky declared at the Ankara NATO summit: "We have completely destroyed the very idea that Russia has a strategic rear."

Meanwhile, North Korean leader Kim Jong Un pledged on July 12 to "unconditionally" support Russia's war in Ukraine, with Pyongyang preparing to deploy an additional 25,000-30,000 troops. The Ankara NATO summit committed $80 billion in defense aid to Ukraine for 2026.

4. Ukraine Battlefield Update

President Zelensky said Sunday that Russia's summer offensive has "fallen far short" of expectations, with Ukrainian active defense thwarting Russian advances in Donetsk and Sumy oblasts. The Economist estimates approximately 31,000 Russian soldiers have been killed in the summer offensive alone.

Russia launched 123 attack drones and multiple ballistic missiles against Ukrainian cities in the past 24 hours, targeting Kyiv, Kharkiv, Dnipro, and Odesa. Ukraine's Air Force reported intercepting 111 drones and 2 ballistic missiles, but warned that the majority of Russian ballistic missiles still reached their targets. Two civilians were killed in Odesa.

German Major General Christian Freuding confirmed that hundreds of German-funded long-range weapon systems will arrive in Ukraine by late July. The agreement covers domestically produced Ukrainian long-range systems financed by Germany.

Ukrainian drones struck the Syzran oil refinery in Russia's Samara region and an oil tanker in the Don-Azov Canal early Sunday. Ukraine's General Staff also reported strikes on the Belgorod airfield oil depot, two military-industrial complex enterprises, and multiple bridges.
