⚡ July 13 Geopolitics Roundup: Trump Pivots to Arm Ukraine with Patriots, US-Iran Conflict Escalates, 14 Nations Reaffirm South China Sea Ruling on 10th Anniversary

I. Trump Confirms Patriots and Offensive Weapons for Ukraine in Major Policy Shift

President Donald Trump confirmed on July 13 that the United States will provide Ukraine with Patriot air defense systems and other sophisticated military equipment, marking a sharp departure from his previous stance. Speaking to reporters at Joint Base Andrews, Trump said the weapons will be delivered under a new arrangement where NATO and European partners will fully reimburse the U.S.

"We will send them Patriots, which they desperately need," Trump said. "Putin really surprised a lot of people. He talks nice and then bombs everybody in the evening. But there's a little bit of a problem there. I don't like it."

This marks the first time Trump has approved fresh weapons shipments to Kyiv since the beginning of his second term. Ukraine had been receiving military support solely from funds left over from the Biden administration. Trump had previously argued that new aid would "harden Putin's position" and obstruct efforts to reach a negotiated settlement.

According to Axios, Trump is preparing to announce a new military aid package on July 14 that may include offensive capabilities, including long-range missiles capable of striking deep inside Russian territory. The shift toward offensive weapons represents a significant departure from Trump's earlier policy emphasizing strictly defensive aid.

What Triggered the Policy Shift

Trump's position toward Russia has hardened noticeably in recent weeks, particularly after a crucial July 3 phone call with Putin. During the call, the Russian leader reportedly informed the U.S. president of plans to escalate military operations in eastern Ukraine within 60 days.

Senator Lindsey Graham told Axios: "Trump is really pissed at Putin. His announcement tomorrow is going to be very aggressive." Graham and Democratic Senator Richard Blumenthal both said it is time to begin tapping the $300 billion in blocked Russian state funds held by G7 nations.

President Zelensky expressed optimism following diplomatic engagements in Rome, saying those talks provided "clear political commitments" for the resumption of arms deliveries. NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte is set to meet Trump on July 14 to discuss Ukraine and broader alliance matters.

II. US-Iran Conflict Escalates: Fresh US Strikes, Tehran Says Diplomacy 'Rendered Futile'

The US-Iran conflict sharply escalated over July 12-13. U.S. Central Command launched fresh strikes Sunday evening targeting Iranian military facilities along the Strait of Hormuz, following Saturday's massive strikes hitting approximately 140 Iranian targets. CENTCOM stated the strikes aimed to "further degrade Iran's ability to threaten freedom of navigation."

Sentinel-2 satellite imagery confirmed severe damage to the IRGC Navy base in southern Iran — hangars, piers, and the shiplift dock all showed indications of strike damage. Explosions were reported across multiple Iranian provinces including Bushehr, Hormozgan, and Chabahar.

Iran's Retaliation

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps announced the indefinite closure of the Strait of Hormuz and launched missiles and drones at five Gulf states including Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Oman, and Jordan. Iranian media reported 8 military personnel killed in the U.S. strikes.

Iran's Foreign Ministry said the latest U.S. strikes had "rendered recent diplomatic efforts futile," accusing Washington of violating Article 1 of the MoU — the commitment to cease military operations. Iran Parliament Speaker Qalibaf warned there is "no alternative" to accepting Iran's 14-point proposal, adding that "the longer it drags on, the more the American taxpayers will pay."

Qatar reported 3 wounded including a child, Kuwait's air defense intercepted 15 Iranian missiles and drones, and air raid sirens sounded across Bahrain.

III. Iranian Newspaper Publishes Revenge List of 13 Foreign Leaders

Iran's conservative Hamshahri newspaper — published by Tehran authorities — released an infographic on July 11 featuring photos of 13 foreign leaders as targets for revenge over the killing of late Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. The list includes U.S. President Donald Trump, Israeli PM Benjamin Netanyahu, UK PM Keir Starmer, French President Emmanuel Macron, Italian PM Giorgia Meloni, and German Chancellor Friedrich Merz, among others.

New Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei vowed in his first statement: "Vengeance is the will of our nation and must inevitably be carried out. These criminals, whose names appear on a list, will take to their graves the wish of a peaceful death in their beds." However, there was no indication the newspaper's list was officially endorsed by Iran's leadership.

IV. Ukraine Strikes 90 Russian Shadow Fleet Vessels in One Week

Ukraine's Unmanned Systems Forces (USF) Commander Robert "Madyar" Brovdi announced on July 12 that Ukrainian drone operators struck 90 Russian vessels in the Sea of Azov between July 6-12 — including tankers, tugboats, ferries, and dry cargo ships — averaging one target every 112 minutes.

The Ukrainian General Staff reported that on the night of July 11-12 alone, forces struck 10 Russian tankers and 4 ferries. These tankers are used to transport Russian oil and petroleum products to bypass international sanctions, while the ferries facilitate military logistics for the Russian army.

Additionally, Ukrainian forces struck the Syzran Oil Refinery in Russia's Samara region, which has a design capacity of 8.5 million tons of oil per year, producing gasoline, diesel, and aviation fuel used by the Russian Armed Forces.

V. 10th Anniversary of South China Sea Ruling: 14 Nations + EU Reaffirm Legally Binding Award

On July 12, marking the 10th anniversary of the 2016 South China Sea arbitration ruling, 14 countries led by the United States issued a joint statement reaffirming that China's expansive maritime claims "have no legal basis under international law." The statement was signed by the U.S., UK, Japan, Australia, Philippines, Canada, Germany, Italy, New Zealand, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Romania, and Slovenia.

The 27-member European Union issued a separate statement describing the ruling as a "landmark" decision in the peaceful settlement of maritime disputes. The 2016 tribunal found that China's historic rights claim over most of the South China Sea under its so-called "nine-dash line" had no legal basis under UNCLOS.

China's Foreign Ministry reiterated that the award is "null and void" and that Beijing "neither accepts nor recognizes" the tribunal's decision.
