World's First Million-Level Atomic Optical Tweezer Array Chip Launches, Ushering in a "Foundation Revolution" for Quantum Computing

On July 19, Shanghai-based Xuanshang Technology, in partnership with neutral-atom quantum computing company Zhongqi Wuliang, announced the successful development of the world's first metasurface chip capable of generating a million-level atomic optical tweezer array. This breakthrough pushes the scale of optical tweezer sites for neutral-atom quantum computing from tens of thousands directly into the millions, providing critical foundational hardware for large-scale quantum computers.

Conventional approaches rely on spatial light modulators or acousto-optic deflectors to generate optical tweezer arrays, with a top-tier spatial light modulator limited to approximately 50,000 high-uniformity trapping sites. Xuanshang's solution employs a metasurface chip that integrates hundreds of millions of nanoscale optical elements in an area the size of a fingertip, converting a single laser beam directly into a million-level optical tweezer array. For context, in September 2025, a Caltech team used two spatial light modulators to generate approximately 12,000 optical tweezer sites, ultimately trapping 6,100 neutral atoms — a figure now surpassed by two orders of magnitude with a single chip from a Chinese team.

The number of optical tweezer sites directly determines how many qubits a neutral-atom quantum computer can simultaneously control. A million-level optical tweezer array theoretically supports a million-qubit scale — a magnitude that superconducting and ion trap approaches cannot match in the near term. Industry observers describe this as a classic "curve overtaking" moment: while the global quantum computing community was still wrestling with how to generate more tweezer sites using traditional optics, a Chinese team deployed metasurface technology to redefine the playing field entirely.

However, significant engineering challenges remain, including quantum gate fidelity and readout precision. Another Shanghai-based neutral-atom company, Taiyi Liangsheng, has already begun using the chip series in its experiments, accelerating the path to industrialization.

Apple Files Lawsuit Against OpenAI, Alleges Two Former Employees Stole Hardware Trade Secrets

Apple filed a lawsuit on July 10 in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California, accusing OpenAI, its hardware division io Products, and two former Apple employees of misappropriating trade secrets to accelerate OpenAI's consumer AI hardware development plans.

The two former employees named in the suit are Chang Liu, an Apple senior system electrical engineer of eight years who joined OpenAI in January 2026, and Tang Yew Tan, who spent more than 24 years at Apple as vice-president of product design for iPhone and Apple Watch before becoming OpenAI's chief hardware officer. Apple alleges that Tan emailed supplier information to himself before leaving the company, used internal Apple project names during OpenAI interviews, and asked job applicants to bring physical components for "show and tell" presentations.

Apple is seeking a jury trial, monetary damages, and court injunctions to prevent OpenAI from using or disclosing its trade secrets. The company also demands the return of relevant materials and preservation of evidence. A broad injunction could force OpenAI to restructure parts of its hardware operations, separating certain staff, designs, or supplier relationships while the case proceeds.

OpenAI responded that it has "no interest in other companies' trade secrets" and remains focused on developing technology that benefits users. No court has ruled on Apple's claims. The lawsuit highlights the transformation of the Apple-OpenAI relationship from AI partnership to competitive rivalry. Since announcing their partnership in 2024 to integrate ChatGPT into Apple experiences like Siri, OpenAI has aggressively expanded its hardware footprint through io Products and former Apple design talent — a push Apple now treats as a direct competitive threat.

Waymo Pauses San Francisco Robotaxi Service After PG&E Blackout

On July 19, Alphabet-owned Waymo suspended all robotaxi services in San Francisco after a massive PG&E power outage left approximately 7,000 customers without electricity. The company informed riders it was making "temporary adjustments" and would resume normal operations as soon as possible. Freeway routes were also suspended.

This is not the first time Waymo has been crippled by a blackout. In December 2025, Waymo vehicles became stuck during another power outage, and the company previously experienced traffic jams following July 4 fireworks displays. With these recurring problems, San Francisco's mayor is pushing for stricter regulations governing driverless vehicles during emergencies to ensure smooth city operations when unexpected events occur.

The incident has reignited debate about the reliability of autonomous vehicles under non-standard conditions — whether fully automated transportation systems can make sound decisions and ensure passenger safety when power fails, communications networks are disrupted, or natural disasters strike.
