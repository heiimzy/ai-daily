Trump Sets Abraham Accords as Condition for US-Saudi Nuclear Deal

President Donald Trump on Thursday posted on Truth Social that the landmark civilian nuclear cooperation agreement between the United States and Saudi Arabia is "totally subject" to the Gulf kingdom joining the Abraham Accords — the US-brokered normalization framework between Israel and Arab nations. Trump further clarified that the deal permits "no enrichment of material."

"The Civil Nuclear Deal (There will be no enrichment of material!) being made between the United States Department of Energy and Saudi Arabia, which pertains only to non-military use such as the ones that Iran and UAE (and others) already have, will be approved, but is totally subject to Saudi Arabia joining the very respected and successful Abraham Accords," Trump wrote.

The announcement immediately reshaped the diplomatic landscape. The US Department of Energy and Saudi Energy Minister Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman signed the so-called "123 Agreement" on Wednesday, allowing the kingdom to develop civilian nuclear power using American technology over 30 years. However, Trump's new conditions directly link the deal's approval to Saudi-Israeli normalization — a position at odds with Riyadh's longstanding stance that it will not establish ties with Israel without a credible pathway to Palestinian statehood.

Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu welcomed the prospect, calling Saudi Arabia joining the Abraham Accords "an historic leap forward for peace in the Middle East." The UAE, Bahrain, Sudan, Morocco, and Kazakhstan are current signatories to the accords.

Saudi Arabia has not publicly responded to Trump's conditions. Analysts noted confusion between Trump's post and the original agreement text, which reportedly permits Saudi domestic uranium enrichment (below weapons-grade) and does not explicitly condition the deal on the Abraham Accords. The pact now heads to Congress, which has 90 legislative days to object.

Iran's IRGC Intercepts Three Oil Tankers at Strait of Hormuz

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) announced Thursday that its naval forces intercepted three oil tankers attempting to transit the Strait of Hormuz, blocking their passage into the Persian Gulf. The move represents the latest escalation in Iran's campaign to assert control over the strategic waterway.

The Strait of Hormuz — which in peacetime saw approximately 138 vessels per day carrying about 20% of the world's oil and gas — has seen traffic plummet to single digits since the US-Iran war escalated on February 28. Iran claims the right to manage traffic and potentially charge fees for passage, a position Washington firmly rejects.

US Central Command (CENTCOM) responded by condemning Iran's actions as a violation of international law, reiterating the US commitment to freedom of navigation. CENTCOM also confirmed completing its 13th consecutive night of strikes against Iranian military targets.

US 13th Night Strikes Hit Power Station Near Bushehr Nuclear Plant

In the early hours of July 23, US forces conducted their 13th consecutive night of airstrikes across southwestern and southern Iran. Iranian state media reported that a US missile struck a power station near the Bushehr nuclear power plant, causing a power outage in a nearby village that lasted approximately two hours. Bushehr Governor Mohammad Mozaffari confirmed the attack and stated there were no casualties.

Explosions were also reported in the southwestern cities of Ahvaz, Ramshir, and Andimeshk in Khuzestan province, as well as near Bandar Mahshahr. The semi-official Fars News Agency reported two explosions heard near the city of Bushehr itself. Although the nuclear plant was not directly hit, the targeting of critical infrastructure near a nuclear facility raised international concerns about escalation dynamics.

US Central Command stated that strikes began at 5:30 PM ET on July 22, targeting Iranian maritime capabilities, missile and drone storage facilities, coastal surveillance sites, and air defense assets, to "further degrade Iran's ability to threaten civilian mariners and commercial vessels transiting regional waters."

Kuwait Intercepts Iranian Drones at Al-Abdali Border Crossing

Kuwait's Ministry of Defence announced Thursday that its air defense systems intercepted hostile drones targeting the Al-Abdali border crossing with Iraq. The military confirmed the interception was successful with no casualties reported, urging residents to remain calm and follow official safety instructions.

The drone attack followed earlier Iranian strikes on Kuwait's Ali al-Salem military base, where the IRGC claimed to have destroyed ammunition depots and logistics equipment. Iran has also asserted it destroyed a Patriot air defense system in Kuwait and a THAAD radar in Jordan during the latest round of overnight attacks.

The United Arab Emirates Foreign Ministry "strongly condemned the renewed Iranian aggression" against Bahrain, Kuwait, and Jordan, describing the attacks as "a blatant violation of the sovereignty of these brotherly nations and a threat to their security and stability."

Iran Condemns UK for Allowing US Use of British Military Bases

Iran's Foreign Ministry issued a sharp condemnation Thursday of the United Kingdom's decision to allow the United States to use British bases and facilities for military strikes against Iran, calling it a violation of the UN Charter and international law.

"Such action could constitute participation in an act of aggression against Iran," the ministry said, adding that assisting US-Israeli attacks amounts to "complicity in aggression and war crimes." The statement accused the UK of siding with aggressors rather than condemning them, and cited past British actions against Iran including the 1953 coup. Iran warned that any country aiding attacks on its sovereignty will bear the consequences.

UK Prime Minister Burnham recently authorized US forces to use RAF Fairford and Diego Garcia bases for what he termed "defensive strikes" against Iran. Multiple US B-1B Lancer strategic bombers departed from UK bases earlier this week en route to the Middle East.

Brent Crude Surges Toward $100 as Dual Chokepoint Crisis Intensifies

Brent crude oil surged toward the $100/barrel milestone on Thursday, reaching an intraday high of $98.80 before settling near $99 — a seven-week high. West Texas Intermediate (WTI) broke above $91. The rally was driven by the unprecedented dual-threat scenario: the Strait of Hormuz remains largely closed, while Yemen's Houthi rebels — Iran-backed proxies — have imposed a maritime blockade on Saudi Arabia in the Red Sea, threatening the Bab el-Mandeb strait through which all Saudi Petroline exports must pass.

Goldman Sachs has warned that if Hormuz remains substantially shut, Brent could average $120 per barrel in Q3 2026. US average gasoline prices have climbed back above $4 per gallon, approaching the war-era peak of $4.56 set in May. BMI Research projects that sustained oil above $100 could drag global GDP growth below 2%, a level consistent with a technical global recession.
