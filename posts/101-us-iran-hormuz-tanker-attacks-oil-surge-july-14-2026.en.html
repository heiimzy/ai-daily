⚡ July 14 World Politics Roundup: US Strikes Iran for 3rd Night, UAE Tankers Hit in Hormuz (1 Dead, 8 Injured), Trump Reimposes Blockade with 20% Cargo Toll

July 14, 2026 — The Middle East witnessed a dramatic escalation over the past 24 hours. The United States struck Iran for a third consecutive night, Iran retaliated by hitting two UAE oil tankers in the Strait of Hormuz (killing one Indian sailor and wounding eight others), and President Donald Trump reimposed a naval blockade on Iran while declaring the U.S. the "Guardian of the Strait of Hormuz" with a controversial 20% cargo toll.

1. U.S. Strikes Iran for Third Consecutive Night

U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) confirmed it completed the latest wave of airstrikes against Iranian military targets on the night of July 13, marking the third consecutive night of operations since July 11 and effectively shattering last month's interim ceasefire agreement.

The five-hour mission struck coastal defense systems, missile sites, drone bases, and maritime military facilities across Bandar Abbas, Bushehr, Jask, Konarak, Abu Musa Island, Kish Island, and Qeshm Island. CENTCOM stated the operation used precision munitions aimed at "further degrading Iran's ability to threaten commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz."

Iran's state-run IRNA news agency reported five explosions in western Bandar Abbas, six blasts on Kish Island, and additional strikes on Qeshm. Four people were injured in the southwestern city of Omidiyeh in Khuzestan province. Nour News reported three boats caught fire at Kish Port following the attacks.

"These strikes will continue imposing a heavy cost on Iranian forces and degrade their ability to attack innocent civilians and commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz." — CENTCOM statement

Earlier, Trump told conservative radio host Hugh Hewitt: "We're going to hit them very hard tonight, and we're going to hit them hard tomorrow. And there's not a damn thing they can do about it." He also threatened to strike "Pickaxe Mountain" (Mount Kolang Gaz La) — a heavily fortified underground nuclear facility near Natanz that experts say is beyond the reach of the most powerful bunker buster bombs in the U.S. arsenal.

2. Iran Hits UAE Tankers: 1 Dead, 8 Wounded

In retaliation for U.S. strikes, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched two cruise missiles at the southern shipping lane of the Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday morning, striking two UAE-flagged very large crude carriers (VLCCs) — the Mombasa and Al Bahiyah — owned by ADNOC L&S.

The UAE Defense Ministry confirmed the attack occurred in Omani territorial waters. One Indian crew member aboard the Mombasa was killed, and eight others were wounded (four seriously), including six Indian and two Ukrainian nationals. Fires broke out aboard both vessels but were later brought under control, though both ships sustained "significant damage."

The UAE condemned the strike as a "blatant attack" and said it "reserves its full right to respond to this escalation." The UAE Foreign Ministry warned that "using the Strait of Hormuz as a tool of economic coercion or blackmail constitutes an act of piracy."

India's Ministry of External Affairs summoned Iran's Deputy Chief of Mission in New Delhi to lodge a strong protest over the killing of an Indian seafarer, calling for an "immediate cessation of hostilities" and urging restoration of free navigation. "The targeting of commercial shipping and civilian infrastructure must cease," the ministry said in a statement.

The IRGC claimed that U.S. forces had guided the two tankers through an "illegal route" and that the vessels had turned off their navigation systems and "ignored repeated warnings" from the Strait of Hormuz Security Control Center. The IRGC warned that "cooperation with the aggressor enemy will only result in regret, damage and delays in reopening the Strait of Hormuz."

3. Trump Reimposes Blockade, Announces 20% Cargo Toll

President Donald Trump announced a dramatic policy shift on Truth Social on Monday: the U.S. would reimpose a naval blockade on Iran and declare itself the "Guardian of the Strait of Hormuz," levying a 20% fee on all cargo shipped through the strategic waterway.

"The Hormuz Strait is OPEN, and will remain OPEN, with or without Iran. We are reinstating THE IRANIAN BLOCKADE. The U.S.A. will be, from this point forward, known as 'THE GUARDIAN OF THE HORMUZ STRAIT', but as such, and as a matter of FAIRNESS, will be reimbursed, at the rate of 20% on all cargo shipped." — President Donald Trump on Truth Social

The U.S. Navy-led Joint Maritime Information Center (JMIC) said the blockade would take effect at 2000 GMT on July 14, applying to all vessel traffic regardless of flag, covering the entire Iranian coastline including ports and oil terminals. Neutral transit passage to or from non-Iranian destinations would not be impeded, and humanitarian shipments would be permitted subject to inspection.

Before the conflict began in February, roughly one-fifth of the world's oil and gas (over 15 million barrels per day, worth at least $1.2 billion) passed through Hormuz. At a 20% fee rate, the U.S. could collect approximately $250 million per day. Trump singled out Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, and Kuwait, stating that the U.S. was protecting these nations and should be compensated.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araqchi responded sarcastically on X: "POTUS is absolutely right. Whoever guarantees safe passage should be compensated. Iran has always been the guardian of the strait and will remain so forever. 20% is of course too much. We will be fair." Iran's top military command stated that the U.S. has no role in determining the future of the waterway and will not be allowed to intervene.

The International Maritime Organization (IMO) passed a resolution reaffirming that "transit passage through straits shall remain free of any tolls and other charges under international law." Brazilian President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva said Trump's plan would turn the U.S. into a "pirate state."

4. Bahrain Under Attack, Gulf States Drawn Into Conflict

Bahrain activated missile warning sirens multiple times on Tuesday morning — the third such alert since dawn — as Iran retaliated following U.S. airstrikes. Bahrain's military said air defense systems intercepted several Iranian missiles and drones.

The IRGC claimed its second wave of "Operation Nasr-2" destroyed a Patriot radar system, the U.S. Fifth Fleet's air control radar, and a C-RAM early warning radar at the Juffair base in Bahrain, while also striking U.S. fuel depots, communications facilities, and control towers in Kuwait. Iran's army separately announced it had targeted a "hostile" U.S. vessel with cruise missiles and hit U.S. facilities in Kuwait with drones.

The Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) Secretary-General Jasem Mohamed Albudaiwi condemned Iran's attacks on UAE tankers as "an explicit violation of UN Security Council Resolution 2817." Kuwait denounced the attacks as "reprehensible."

Omani Foreign Minister Badr Al Busaidi wrote in Le Monde that "complex talks have begun to shape a lasting framework guaranteeing freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz," noting Oman's "particular responsibility" due to its territorial waters bordering the strait.

5. Global Reactions: India Protests, China Urges Restraint

India summoned Iranian diplomats and demanded an immediate end to hostilities and restoration of shipping freedom. The Ministry of External Affairs said it would "focus all efforts on ensuring the safety of Indian crew members and freedom of passage through the strait."

China called on both the U.S. and Iran to restore safe passage through the Strait of Hormuz, with Beijing pledging "unremitting efforts to help de-escalate the situation."

Lebanon and Israel held new negotiations in Rome under U.S. auspices to advance their June 26 framework agreement. The Lebanese presidency instructed its delegation to "demand the immediate start of Israeli forces' withdrawal from the two pilot zones before any further discussion." Hezbollah continues to reject the disarmament clause.

Iraqi Prime Minister Ali al-Zaidi is scheduled to meet President Trump in Washington today, as the U.S. piles pressure on Baghdad to curb Iranian influence.

Qatar declared four days of national mourning and suspended public sector work following the passing of former Emir Sheikh Hamad bin Khalifa Al Thani at age 74.

6. Oil Markets in Turmoil: Brent Breaks $85

The sharp escalation in the Middle East sent shockwaves through global energy markets. Brent crude rose 2% to $85 per barrel on Tuesday, while U.S. West Texas Intermediate (WTI) gained 2.3% to $80, both hitting one-month highs. On Monday, oil prices had already surged more than 9%, with Brent posting its biggest single-day dollar gain since April 2.

Shipping data platform Kpler reported that confirmed transits through the Strait of Hormuz declined approximately 52% week-on-week from July 10-12. An increasing number of vessels are resorting to "dark shipping" — turning off transponders to avoid detection — while visible traffic along the Omani IMO-authorized route has completely ceased since July 8.

Lloyd's List Intelligence warned that war risk premiums are expected to rise sharply as shipowners and charterers pause transit decisions. German shipping company Hapag-Lloyd stated that charging fees for passage through international waters "would be fundamentally wrong."

Adding to energy supply concerns, Yemen's Houthi movement fired missiles at Saudi Arabia on Monday, accusing the kingdom of bombing an airport under its control and breaking a four-year truce between Riyadh and the Iran-aligned group.
