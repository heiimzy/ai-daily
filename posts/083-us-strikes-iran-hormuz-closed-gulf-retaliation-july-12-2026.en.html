⚡ Middle East Escalates: US Strikes 140 Iranian Targets, Iran Retaliates Against 5 Nations, Closes Hormuz

July 12, 2026 — The Middle East conflict escalated dramatically on Sunday as U.S. Central Command launched its third and largest wave of airstrikes against Iran this week, hitting approximately 140 military targets, and Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps retaliated by declaring the Strait of Hormuz closed and launching missiles and drones against five Gulf states.

Third Wave of US Strikes: 140 Targets Hit

CENTCOM said it launched the strikes at 7:15 p.m. ET on Saturday, targeting Iranian missile and drone launch sites, naval capabilities, ammunition storage facilities, communication networks, and coastal surveillance locations across Iran's southern provinces including Bushehr, Hormozgan, Khuzestan, and Sistan-Baluchestan. In total, more than 300 targets have been struck across three nights of operations this week.

President Donald Trump told CNN by telephone on Sunday: "We hit them very hard last night." Trump had declared the 60-day interim ceasefire "over" on Wednesday after Iran attacked commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz earlier in the week.

Iranian state media reported that Navy Lieutenant Hamidreza Dehghani was killed in the overnight strikes at the port of Jask.

Iran's Retaliation: Missiles and Drones Strike Multiple Nations

The IRGC responded by launching ballistic missiles and explosive drones targeting U.S. military facilities across the region:

  • Qatar: Al Udeid airbase was struck by ballistic missiles. The IRGC claimed to have destroyed a fighter jet maintenance center and a command-and-control facility. Qatar's military intercepted incoming fire, but three people — including one child — were wounded by falling shrapnel. Qatar's Foreign Ministry issued a "strongest possible condemnation," calling the attacks a "dangerous escalation" and a "flagrant violation of sovereignty."
  • UAE: Air defense systems engaged Iranian missiles and drones. The National Emergency Crisis Authority later said missile threats detected were "outside the country's borders."
  • Kuwait: The IRGC claimed to have used explosive drones to destroy a Patriot air defense system, ammunition depot, and radar site. Kuwait's military confirmed it was intercepting "hostile aerial targets."
  • Bahrain: Home to the U.S. Navy's 5th Fleet, air raid sirens sounded multiple times. The IRGC claimed strikes on U.S. communications systems and radar installations.
  • Oman: Drones struck sites in the Musandam governorate on the Strait of Hormuz. Oman summoned the Iranian ambassador and issued a formal protest, state media reported, a rare public rebuke from a nation that has been a key mediator.
  • Jordan: Three Iranian missiles landed inside Jordanian territory, causing minor property damage and no casualties.

The Strait of Hormuz: Closed vs Open

The IRGC Navy announced early Sunday that the Strait of Hormuz would be closed "until further notice," saying no vessels would be allowed to pass. The Force said several vessels attempted to use unauthorized routes despite repeated warnings, and one — the Cyprus-flagged container ship M/V GFS Galaxy — was struck by warning fire and brought to a halt with significant engine room damage.

CENTCOM directly contradicted Iran's claim, stating: "Iran does not control the strait. Traffic is flowing." The U.S. military said its forces were "positioned and prepared" to ensure freedom of navigation, and advised vessels to use an expanded southern route near Omani territorial waters.

India's Ministry of External Affairs confirmed that 10 of the 11 Indian nationals aboard the GFS Galaxy had been rescued, with one crew member still missing. Search-and-rescue operations are ongoing off the coast of Oman.

The IRGC claimed to have struck a second vessel in the strait on Sunday, though no further details were provided.

Diplomacy in Crisis

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi met Omani counterpart Sayyid Badr Albusaidi on Saturday to discuss safe passage mechanisms through the Strait of Hormuz. Oman had proposed a draft framework allowing free navigation through a southern corridor in Omani waters while requiring prior approval for transit through the northern corridor in Iranian waters.

However, after Iranian drones struck Omani territory, the sultanate summoned Iran's ambassador and issued a formal protest — a significant diplomatic blow given Oman's role as a key mediator.

The 60-day interim ceasefire brokered by Pakistan on June 17 now appears effectively dead. Iran's new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei issued his first formal statement since the war began, vowing revenge for his father's killing: "Revenge is the will of our nation and must certainly be carried out."

Trump responded on Truth Social, warning he had ordered the U.S. military to be prepared to launch thousands of missiles at Iran if the regime attempted to assassinate him, following intelligence reports of an Iranian assassination plot shared by Israel.

Global Economic Shockwaves

Before the war, approximately one-fifth of the world's oil and LNG supply transited through the Strait of Hormuz. Iran's effective blockade — combined with repeated attacks on commercial vessels — has sent energy prices soaring and fueled global inflation. Analysts warn that Brent crude could breach $200 per barrel if the blockade persists.

Iran's top negotiator Mohammad Baqer Qalibaf posted on X: "The era of one-sided deals is OVER. We told you: keep your word or pay the price. Reality is knocking."

As of press time, CENTCOM confirmed that U.S. forces remain at high readiness in the region, while insisting the southern corridor of the strait remains available for commercial transit. But with Iran striking the homelands of U.S. allied Gulf states for the first time in this conflict, the war that began on February 28 has entered a far more dangerous phase.
