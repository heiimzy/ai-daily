⚡ July 17 Financial Evening: Oil Surges 13% Weekly Past $86, Fed Hawks Circle, Korea 350K Retail Accounts Liquidated

1. Oil Surges 13% Weekly: Strait of Hormuz Paralyzed, Kuwait Power Plant Struck

The escalating US-Iran conflict sent crude oil prices sharply higher on Friday. Brent crude futures advanced about 2% to trade at $86.03 per barrel, while WTI crude rose 2.6% to $80.99. Both benchmarks were on track for weekly gains of nearly 13% — the largest since April. Brent heads for a third consecutive weekly gain.

The US Central Command announced a sixth consecutive night of massive airstrikes against Iran on Thursday, targeting military logistics infrastructure and maritime capabilities. More than 50,000 US service members are operating across the Middle East, Central Command said.

Iran retaliated with missile and drone attacks on US targets in Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, and Syria, according to state outlet PressTV. Kuwait's Ministry of Electricity, Water and Renewable Energy confirmed that a power generation and water desalination station was hit by an Iranian attack, sparking a fire that damaged a large number of its electricity generating units. Qatar's defense ministry said a child was injured by shrapnel from intercepted missiles.

International Energy Agency Executive Director Fatih Birol warned at a Council on Foreign Relations event: "Oil security is still a critical issue. We should be worried, and I am worried, if the situation does not improve in the next few weeks."

Shipping Near Standstill in Hormuz, Red Sea Faces Second Blockade

The Strait of Hormuz, which handles roughly 20% of global oil traffic, has seen shipping traffic collapse. Iran has repeatedly attacked tankers in an attempt to force commercial vessels through its waters. The United Kingdom Maritime Trade Operations Center reported a tanker was hit by a projectile off the coast of Oman, causing minor damage.

More alarmingly, Reuters reported that Iran has instructed Yemen's Houthi movement to prepare to close the Bab el-Mandeb Strait — the Red Sea gateway for Saudi oil exports — if the US strikes Iranian power infrastructure. Commerzbank analysts noted that Red Sea transit has already increased significantly since the Iran war began, as Saudi oil exports were rerouted away from Hormuz. "Should a blockade of the Bab el-Mandab occur, oil prices are likely to rise further," they warned.

Goldman Sachs said Brent could exceed $110 per barrel in Q4 if Gulf exports remain delayed, but could fall back to the $60s by year-end if geopolitical tensions ease. Even with an immediate ceasefire, JM Financial's Pranav Mer said Brent would not fall below $70.

2. Fed Hawks Circle: Logan First to Explicitly Call for Rate Hike, September Odds at 46%

The debate within the Federal Reserve over rate hikes is intensifying. Dallas Fed President Lorie Logan became the first FOMC voter in this cycle to explicitly call for rate increases. Speaking in Houston on Thursday, she said: "I currently believe that modestly higher interest rates would better balance the outlook and risks facing the FOMC's dual mandate."

"One month of improvement is far from enough. It is time to finish the job of restoring price stability," Logan said, warning that if high inflation becomes entrenched, the Fed may need to tighten more aggressively later. "Modest tightening now is better than being forced into aggressive tightening later."

Fed Vice Chair Philip Jefferson, speaking at Stanford University, also signaled openness to rate hikes if inflation doesn't cool soon, though he described current policy as "well-positioned" — publicly distancing himself from Logan's immediate-action view.

Kansas City Fed President Jeff Schmid said inflation is "too hot and has been above target for too long," warning that price pressures extend beyond energy to food and broad services categories.

CME FedWatch: September Hike Probability Approaches 50%

The CME FedWatch tool shows the July 28-29 meeting is nearly priced for a hold (88.8% probability), but September odds for a 25bp hike have risen to 46.2%, with October emerging as the most likely window at 57%. Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's congressional testimony earlier this week, where he expressed "zero tolerance" for inflation, reinforced the hawkish shift.

The Bank of Korea also raised rates by 25bp to 2.75% on July 16, its first hike since January 2023, signaling the global tightening wave extends beyond the US.

3. KOSPI Crashes 6.37% Triggering Circuit Breaker: 350,000 Retail Accounts Liquidated

The global semiconductor sell-off triggered a devastating chain reaction in South Korea. The KOSPI index plunged 6.37% to 6,820.60 on Thursday, triggering a circuit breaker for the second time in two weeks. SK Hynix crashed 11.5%, and Samsung Electronics fell nearly 9%.

According to a Goldman Sachs desk note, the sudden crash triggered margin calls on over 1.2 million South Korean retail accounts — locally known as "the ants." Brokerages forcibly liquidated approximately 350,000 accounts to cover outstanding debts, impacting roughly 3% of the country's adult population. Retail brokerage deposits evaporated by roughly 30 trillion won during the sell-off.

The Financial Services Commission (FSC) swiftly intervened, announcing sweeping restrictions on single-stock leveraged ETFs, effective August 5: minimum cash deposit tripled from 1 million won to 30 million won (~$20,300); substitute securities (stocks, bonds) no longer accepted as basic margin collateral; single-stock leveraged ETF trades capped at 20 shares per order; all new single-stock leveraged product listings suspended.

The forced selling in Seoul immediately spilled over to Japan and Taiwan. The Nikkei 225 extended losses on Friday, closing down 2.79% at 66,835.54, now more than 10% below its June peak — entering technical correction territory. Kioxia cratered 15%, SoftBank plunged over 9%, and Tokyo Electron fell over 8%. Taiwan's Weighted Index collapsed more than 6%, with TSMC falling 7.29% despite reporting a record Q2 net profit surging 77% to $22 billion. China's Shanghai Composite fell 1.85% to 3,882.

4. Netflix Plunges 9% After Hours to 52-Week Low on Weak Q3 Guidance

Streaming giant Netflix reported mixed Q2 results after Thursday's close. Revenue of $12.56 billion narrowly missed analyst expectations of $12.58 billion, while EPS of $0.80 beat the $0.79 consensus. Net income rose to $3.4 billion, up from $3.13 billion a year ago.

But the market's focus was on Q3 guidance: Netflix projected $12.86 billion in revenue (11.7% growth), well below analyst estimates of approximately $13 billion, and EPS of $0.82 versus $0.84 expected. The 11.7% growth rate would be Netflix's slowest since Q3 2023.

Shares tumbled over 9% in after-hours trading to around $67.90 — a 52-week low. The company also announced it will reduce the frequency of engagement data reporting from quarterly to annual, further clouding visibility into subscriber trends. Netflix shares are now down roughly 21% year-to-date.

The weak Netflix outlook compounded tech-sector woes, following Alphabet's 4.4% drop on Thursday (delayed Gemini 3.5 Pro launch) and IBM's earnings miss earlier this week.

5. Market Recap: Tech Crashes but Equal-Weight S&P 500 Hits Record High

Thursday's US market close revealed dramatic structural divergence beneath the surface. The Nasdaq Composite fell 1.47% to 25,881.95, while the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index plunged 4.29% into technical bear territory (down over 22% from its June peak). Memory chipmakers were hammered: SanDisk -12.6%, Western Digital -9%, Seagate -10%, Micron -5.65% (losing $1 trillion in market cap).

Yet a striking contrary signal emerged: the S&P 500 Equal-Weight Index hit an all-time high. Eight of eleven S&P sectors closed higher — Healthcare (XLV) surged 2.22% (UnitedHealth strong Q2 beat and raised guidance), Energy (XLE) rose 0.92% on the oil surge, and Financials (XLF) added 0.34%. The S&P 500 itself fell only 0.51%, with over 70% of individual stocks actually rising.

This signals a massive sector rotation in progress — capital flowing out of overvalued AI/semiconductor stocks into healthcare, energy, and financials. The 10-year Treasury yield edged up to 4.549%, while the 30-year yield breached 5% to 5.083%.

This week's softer-than-expected June CPI (3.5% YoY) and PPI data had briefly lifted hopes, but the oil price surge has reignited inflation fears. US June retail sales rose just 0.2% (five-month low), while initial jobless claims fell to 208,000 — painting a "Goldilocks" picture of resilient employment alongside cooling consumption. All eyes now turn to the July 28-29 FOMC meeting for the clearest forward guidance yet from Chair Warsh's Fed.
