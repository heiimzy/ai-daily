⚡ July 17 International Politics Evening: Iran's 15-Wave Retaliation Hits Qatar's Al Udeid Base, Kuwait Power Plant Destroyed, Iran Strikes Syria for First Time, Houthis Poised to Block Red Sea

1. Iran's 15th Wave of 'Operation Lightning': Strikes on Qatar's Al Udeid Air Base Destroy US Radar and Refueling Aircraft

The US-Iran conflict escalated dramatically on July 17 as Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched its 15th wave of 'Operation Lightning', striking the Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar — a key US military hub — for the first time. The IRGC said the attack destroyed a long-range radar system and multiple US military refueling aircraft, warning of "more crushing" retaliation if US strikes continue.

Qatar's Ministry of Defence confirmed its armed forces intercepted a missile attack targeting the country. Qatar's Interior Ministry later reported that a child was injured by falling shrapnel from the interception. This was the second time this week that Qatar — a key mediator in US-Iran peace efforts — has come under direct Iranian missile threat, severely undermining diplomatic prospects.

Iranian military spokesman Brigadier General Mohammad Akraminia said Iran also struck the US base at Azraq in Jordan, the Sheikh Isa Air Base in Bahrain, and the Ali Al Salem Air Base in Kuwait. India TV and Iranian state media reported at least three ballistic missiles struck the US Fifth Fleet base in Bahrain, triggering a large fire.

Iran Strikes US Radar Stations in Oman for the First Time

The IRGC further announced it had struck two US radar stations in Oman — marking the first time the conflict has expanded into Omani territory, which had previously been considered a neutral safe zone. The IRGC said the radar stations "were being used to guide US drone strikes against Iranian targets."

Iran Hits US Base in Syria for the First Time

In perhaps the most significant escalation, Iran's Revolutionary Guards announced they had attacked the US Special Operations Command Center at al-Tanf in eastern Syria — the first known Iranian strike on Syrian territory since the regional war erupted on February 28. Iran's state broadcaster Tasnim said the attack was in "retaliation for the blood of martyred Iranian soldiers in Iranshahr."

However, a Syrian military source told Reuters that the Iranian strike near al-Tanf did not hit the base itself and caused no casualties or material damage. The US military said in February it had completed its withdrawal from al-Tanf, which sits at the tri-border confluence of Syria, Jordan, and Iraq.

Syrian President Ahmed al-Sharaa stated in March that Syria would stay out of any conflict unless attacked. Syria also announced it had intercepted a shipment of missiles and advanced weapons allegedly destined for Hezbollah after being smuggled across the Iraqi border.

2. Kuwait Power Station Destroyed: Iran's Retaliation Hits Critical Civilian Infrastructure

Kuwait's Ministry of Electricity, Water and Renewable Energy confirmed on July 17 that an Iranian attack had struck a power generation and water desalination station, igniting a fire and severely damaging "a large number of electricity generation units." This marks the first time Iran has successfully struck critical civilian infrastructure in Kuwait in this round of fighting.

The Kuwait General Staff of the Army said the country's air defense systems were actively intercepting "hostile missile and drone attacks" and that any explosions heard were the result of successful interceptions. Kuwait, a desert nation, is heavily dependent on desalination for its water supply, making the damage strategically significant.

On the same day, Iran's Energy Ministry urged citizens to conserve electricity for the first time since the war began, citing US attacks on southern Iran's power infrastructure "amid extreme heat and attacks on electricity supply facilities."

3. US Sixth Consecutive Night of Strikes: Bridges, Ports, and Railways Targeted

US Central Command (CENTCOM) announced it had completed its sixth consecutive night of massive strikes against Iran on July 17, lasting nearly 8 hours. The statement said "US forces, including fighter jets, aerial drones, and warships, launched precision munitions that hit dozens of Iranian military targets such as coastal surveillance and air defense sites, military logistics infrastructure, and maritime capabilities."

Iran's state news agency IRNA reported that US forces struck at least six bridges in Hormozgan province, with the attack on Bandar Khamir's bridges killing at least seven people. Explosions were reported in Bushehr province — home to Iran's only civilian nuclear power plant. US strikes also hit Iranshahr Airport in southeastern Iran, the railway junction at Bandar Abbas, and Chabahar Port.

Chabahar Port Surveillance Tower Collapses

US Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth shared an image on social media showing a collapsed surveillance tower at Iran's Chabahar Port on the Gulf of Oman, surrounded by a plume of thick grey smoke. Iranian state media acknowledged a third round of strikes on the facility but did not respond to the tower's collapse. Chabahar is a strategic port on the Gulf of Oman — a key trade route for Afghanistan and a major Indian investment.

Hellfire Missile Disables Oil Tanker

US forces also fired Hellfire missiles at the empty oil tanker M/T Belma sailing toward Iran's main oil export terminal at Kharg Island, after the ship "ignored multiple warnings." CENTCOM said three additional vessels attempting to breach the blockade had been redirected, and one had been boarded for inspection. Over 50,000 US service members remain deployed across the Middle East.

4. Houthis Poised to Block Bab al-Mandeb: Two Key Oil Chokepoints at Risk

Three sources told Reuters on July 16 that Iran has instructed Yemen's Houthi rebels to be ready to close the Red Sea's Bab al-Mandeb Strait — a vital energy chokepoint — if the US follows through on President Trump's threats to strike Iranian power infrastructure. A source close to the Houthis said the group "has completed preparations: deploying missiles and drones around Bab al-Mandeb and in Yemen's highlands, and is awaiting the order to act."

If implemented, both of the Middle East's most important oil transit chokepoints — the Strait of Hormuz and the Bab al-Mandeb — would be simultaneously blocked. Shipping through the Strait of Hormuz has already plummeted to roughly one-tenth of pre-war levels (Kpler data: only 13 ships passed on July 16). Qatar says maritime trade through Bab al-Mandeb accounts for 11% of global seaborne trade. Meanwhile, Saudi Arabia and the Houthis have recently clashed again, with the Houthis warning they could target the Yanbu port.

International Energy Agency (IEA) Executive Director Fatih Birol warned at the Council on Foreign Relations in Washington: "Oil security remains a critical issue. If the situation does not improve in the coming weeks, we should be worried — and I am worried."

5. Iran Reports 38 Dead, Over 400 Wounded

Iran's Health Ministry spokesman Hossein Kermanpour announced on July 17 that at least 38 people have been killed and more than 400 wounded since fighting with the US resumed on June 22 over control of the Strait of Hormuz. State news agency IRNA later updated the overnight death toll to eight, with 20 wounded.

Government spokeswoman Fatemeh Mohajerani previously said US strikes on southern Iran had killed at least 30 civilians in recent days. Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baqaei condemned a US attack near a children's cancer hospital in Ahvaz that forced the evacuation of 211 pediatric chemotherapy patients as a "barbaric war crime."

6. Trump: 'We're Winning Big in Iran'

President Donald Trump publicly declared the United States was "winning big in Iran" and promised "you will see the fruits of that labor very, very shortly." White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt said Trump "remains always open to diplomacy" but "will not sit by and allow Iran to fire on ships in the Strait without ensuring they pay a consequence."

Leavitt revealed that indirect communications between Washington and Tehran continue. But Iran's chief negotiator and parliamentary speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf warned that the memorandum of understanding "only has meaning when its clauses are valid and being implemented," adding that Iran is in an "existential war."

China and Pakistan jointly called for an "immediate ceasefire and return to dialogue" after their foreign ministers met in Shanghai on July 17. Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi and Pakistani Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar "expressed concern over the deterioration of the current situation."

Brent crude futures rose approximately 1.82% to $85.76/barrel on Friday, with WTI crude up 2.14% to $80.64/barrel. Both benchmarks have gained nearly 13% this week, heading for a third consecutive weekly gain.
