1. Historic Breakthrough: GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra Proves 50-Year-Old Math Conjecture in One Hour

OpenAI announced today a result that has stunned both the mathematics and AI communities — its flagship GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra model successfully generated a complete proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture in less than one hour. This conjecture, independently posed by mathematician George Szekeres in 1973 and Paul Seymour in 1979, has remained one of graph theory's most important unsolved problems for over 50 years.

The conjecture asks: for any bridgeless graph, does there always exist a set of cycles such that every edge appears in exactly two cycles? Despite its simple formulation, countless mathematicians have attempted and failed to prove it over the past half-century.

64 Subagents Working in Parallel

OpenAI researcher Ethan Knight announced the achievement on X. According to the prompt disclosed by OpenAI, GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra was instructed to utilize up to 64 parallel subagents, dynamically manage their work, maintain diverse research approaches in early stages, and deploy dedicated "adversarial agents" to find flaws and edge cases.

The system was allocated 8 hours of compute time but completed the proof in just one hour. Industry estimates put the computational cost at approximately $275 to $485 at OpenAI's official Sol pricing.

Mathematicians' Initial Assessment

University of Manchester mathematician Thomas Bloom was one of the first to publicly evaluate the proof. "This is a very beautiful proof," he said. "It's concise, fundamental, uses methods that aren't complicated, and if someone had thought of it, it could have been proven in the 1980s." Bloom noted that AI's biggest advantage isn't generating entirely new mathematical ideas, but its superhuman persistence in trying countless variations.

However, he pointed out a significant issue: the proof contains no references to existing literature. For instance, the seminal 1983 paper by Bermond, Jackson, and Jaeger should have been cited but was not. The proof also has not yet undergone peer review or formal verification using tools like Lean.

If validated by the mathematics community, this would mark the first time a large language model has independently solved a problem listed on Wikipedia's "Unsolved Problems in Mathematics" — a milestone breakthrough in AI reasoning capabilities.

2. ByteDance Launches China's First AI IDE: Trae Domestic Edition

ByteDance today announced the official launch of Trae, described as "China's first AI-native integrated development environment (AI IDE)." The domestic edition is equipped with the Doubao-1.5-pro model and supports switching to the full-capability DeepSeek R1 and V3 models.

Trae is positioned as an "intelligent collaborative AI IDE" covering code completion, code understanding, bug repair, and natural language-based code generation. The standout feature is the new Builder mode: developers simply describe requirements in natural language, and Trae rapidly builds a project framework, continuously refining it until usable code is produced.

Trae was previously released overseas with built-in GPT-4o and Claude-3.5-Sonnet models. The domestic edition has been localized for Chinese development scenarios and habits, with model customization support coming soon, allowing users to connect their preferred large model APIs.

The IDE is now available for Mac (macOS 10.15+) and Windows, with a Linux version to follow. This launch marks a new chapter in China's AI development tool ecosystem.

3. Google Unveils Magic Pointer AI App for Googlebook Computers

Google has revealed Magic Pointer, an AI-powered application designed for its upcoming Googlebook computers. The app briefly appeared on the Play Store, offering a glimpse into how Google plans to integrate its Gemini AI assistant directly into the desktop workspace environment.

Magic Pointer leverages Gemini AI to enable smarter cursor control, contextual assistance, and more responsive desktop management. The core vision is to elevate AI from an isolated tool to the underlying capability of the desktop operating system, redefining how users interact with their computers.

While detailed features, official launch timelines, and pricing remain undisclosed, the app's presence on the Play Store signals that Google is aggressively building toward AI-driven next-generation personal computing. This move intensifies competition with Apple and Microsoft in the AI PC space, as Gemini AI progressively permeates hardware and operating system layers.

4. Meta Suspends Instagram AI Image Generation Amid Privacy Backlash

Meta recently announced the suspension of an AI feature that generated images using publicly available Instagram content. The feature drew widespread criticism over user privacy, content licensing, and intellectual property concerns.

Critics argued that using public content for AI image generation without explicit user permission crossed ethical boundaries and risked infringing on creators' rights. Facing mounting pressure, Meta decided to pause the feature and reassess its AI content usage policies.

This incident highlights the ethical dilemmas AI companies face when leveraging social media data to train and run AI models. As AI capabilities continue to advance, finding the right balance between innovation and privacy protection has become an urgent challenge for the entire industry. Meta, one of the most aggressive players in generative AI, is learning that rapid AI product deployment requires careful consideration of regulatory and user acceptance factors.
