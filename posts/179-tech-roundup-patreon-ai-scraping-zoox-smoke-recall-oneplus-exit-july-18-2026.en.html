⚡ July 18 Tech Roundup: Patreon Blocks AI Crawlers, Zoox Issues Smoke Recall, OnePlus Leaves US/EU

1. Patreon Partners with Cloudflare to Actively Block AI Training Crawlers

Patreon, the membership platform for creators, announced Thursday it is working with Cloudflare to directly block AI training crawlers at the network level. The move upgrades Patreon's strategy from passive robots.txt requests to active enforcement — reducing individual AI training crawlers' weekly access attempts from "thousands to zero," according to the company.

"Consent shouldn't depend on whether a scraper chooses to behave," a Patreon blog post explains. Product chief Drew Rowny remarked: "As AI agents become increasingly powerful and popular, creators deserve a meaningful say in how their work is used by AI companies. On most of the Internet, creators have to accept AI training on their work just to reach and grow an audience. Patreon has a different vision: creators should be able to grow their audience and control how their work is used."

The strengthened measures were necessary because AI scraping has become more sophisticated. Cloudflare data shows that 57.4% of all internet requests now originate from automated bots. Cloudflare recently introduced tools allowing websites to restrict AI bots, including a "Pay Per Crawl" marketplace, and starting in September, training and agent bots will be blocked by default on ad-supported pages. Patreon CEO Jack Conte posted on Instagram: "Creators deserve credit, compensation, and consent. If that's not on the table, the crawlers can stay the f*** off Patreon."

2. Zoox Issues Software Recall After Robotaxi Confused by Heavy Smoke

Amazon-owned Zoox issued a software recall Friday after one of its robotaxis struggled to navigate a smoke-obscured emergency fire scene in June. The recall covers all 105 vehicles in Zoox's fleet and marks the company's fourth recall since 2024.

According to Zoox's report to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA), on June 20, a Zoox robotaxi "encountered heavy smoke that obscured an active emergency fire scene that was not cordoned off with cones." The vehicle "braked hard while attempting to steer away before coming to a stop." A Zoox teleoperator reversed the vehicle away from the scene, allowing first responders to place traffic cones. No one was on board, and Zoox said it is not aware of any injuries associated with the problem.

The software update "enhances the existing capability of detecting active emergency scenes by adding the ability to detect and respond to heavy smoke in certain situations." The recall comes just a week after NHTSA administrator Jonathan Morrison sent a letter to autonomous vehicle companies warning them about interfering with first responders, calling such failures a "functional insufficiency." Waymo has also had repeated run-ins with first responders — at least six incidents as of March 2026 where responders had to physically move robotaxis from emergency scenes.

Zoox is currently offering free robotaxi rides in Las Vegas and San Francisco ahead of a planned commercial launch, which depends on NHTSA granting an exemption from certain safety standards for vehicles without steering wheels or pedals.

3. OnePlus Officially Confirms Exit from US and EU Markets

OnePlus confirmed Friday it is exiting the US and EU markets, ending six months of speculation and marking a major strategic pivot for the Chinese smartphone brand. The company will refocus on markets where it sees stronger growth potential.

Existing OnePlus devices will continue to receive software updates, security patches, and warranty support. Users with eligible phones can voluntarily upgrade to ColorOS from OxygenOS. However, the US OnePlus Community forum will shut down on August 16, 2026 at 11:59 PM EST.

In an interview with Android Authority, OnePlus said the decision "wasn't dictated by OPPO nor made unilaterally by OnePlus" but came after a long strategic assessment. The exit reflects growing cost pressures in the smartphone market. With AI server demand driving up memory chip prices, IDC projects average smartphone prices will reach a record $523 in 2026, up 14% year-over-year. Apple CEO Tim Cook recently described the memory crunch as "unlike anything I've seen in over 40 years."
