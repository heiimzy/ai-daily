⚡ July 21 Financial Roundup: China and Hong Kong Indices Hit Multi-Year Highs, Oil Holds Above $88, Korea Leverage Crackdown

🇨🇳 China A-Shares: All Three Major Indices Hit Yearly Highs on Infrastructure Boom

China's A-share markets surged on July 21, with all three major indices reaching new year-to-date highs. The Shanghai Composite Index closed up 0.72% at 3,559.79, the Shenzhen Component Index rose 0.86% to 11,007.49, and the ChiNext Index gained 0.87% to 2,296.88. Total turnover on the Shanghai and Shenzhen exchanges exceeded 1.7 trillion yuan ($238 billion), with over 4,000 stocks rising and more than 100 hitting the daily涨停 (10% upside) limit.

The infrastructure sector was the day's standout performer. Cement and building materials stocks led the charge, with 20+ stocks including Conch Cement and Tibet Tianlu hitting涨停. The ultra-high voltage (UHV) transmission sector extended its rally, with China XD Electric and XJ Electric among the leaders. The construction machinery sector also saw strong gains, with Zhongda Lide hitting涨停.

The catalyst: On July 19, the State-owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission formally established China Yarlung Tsangpo Group Co., Ltd., and simultaneously commenced construction of the lower Yarlung Tsangpo River hydropower project in Nyingchi, Tibet — a mega-investment of approximately 1.2 trillion yuan ($168 billion) involving five cascade power stations. Analysts estimate the project will generate 800,000 tons of new cement demand and over 12 million tons of steel consumption.

The semiconductor sector also staged a sharp rebound, with the STAR 50 Index surging 5.63% at midday. SoChip Micro, Zhenbao Technology and other chip stocks hit涨停. Orient Securities noted that AI-driven domestic computing power expansion is accelerating demand for advanced process and packaging equipment.

🇭🇰 Hong Kong: Hang Seng Breaks 25,000 for First Time Since 2022, Dongfang "Fat-Finger" Trade Sparks Frenzy

Hong Kong stocks also posted strong gains, with the Hang Seng Index closing up 0.68% at 25,143.05 — breaching the 25,000 level for the first time since February 2022. The Hang Seng Tech Index rose 0.84%, and the State-owned Enterprises Index gained 3.0%. Total turnover reached HK$306.4 billion ($39.2 billion). Southbound inflows remained robust at over HK$4.3 billion for the session.

Infrastructure and resource stocks dominated the rally. Huaxin Cement soared 85.63% in a session that saw it briefly double in early trade. Dongfang Electric (Dongfang) saw one of the most extraordinary trades in Hong Kong market history: 400 shares changed hands at HK$119.90 during the opening auction — a 520% premium to its previous close of HK$19.32 — before rapidly settling around HK$24. The trade was widely attributed to a "fat-finger" error by a trader. Harbin Electric rose 28.64%.

Chinese broker stocks extended their recent rally, with Orient Securities up 7.53% and China International Capital Corp up 5.71%. Oil stocks rallied on elevated crude prices, with Sinopec up 5.53% and PetroChina up 3.61%.

The Hang Seng Index is now up over 24% year-to-date, making it one of the best performing major indices globally. Analysts attribute the strength to sustained southbound capital inflows and improving China-US relations.

🛢️ Oil Markets: Brent Holds Above $88 as Hormuz Crisis Enters 10th Night

Crude oil prices remained elevated on July 21, with Brent crude trading at $88.87 per barrel (down 0.4% on the day) and WTI at $82.16. Prices have rallied sharply this month as the US-Iran conflict entered its 10th consecutive night of US airstrikes. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps reported that two oil tankers were hit by explosions while attempting to transit the Strait of Hormuz via a southern route, with crews abandoning one vessel.

Tensions remain extremely high. Yemen's Houthi militants announced a comprehensive maritime embargo against Saudi Arabia, threatening Red Sea shipping routes. The Strait of Hormuz — which carries approximately 20% of global oil consumption — has seen traffic plunge to its lowest levels in two months. Brent crude has gained more than $10 per barrel since early July.

Goldman Sachs had previously warned that if Hormuz remains largely closed, Brent could average above $100 for the rest of 2026, potentially reaching $120 in the third quarter and $130 by late 2026 in an extended disruption scenario. In the US, the national average gasoline price has climbed to approximately $4.00 per gallon, while diesel has broken above $5.00 per gallon, stoking supply-chain inflation fears.

🇰🇷 South Korea: President Lee Says Market Needs Time, Leverage ETF Rules Tightened

South Korean President Lee Jae-myung acknowledged Wednesday that the domestic stock market needs "time and volatility to stabilize" after experiencing "unprecedented rapid surges" in a short period. Earlier this month, the KOSPI index fell 20% from its June peak, technically entering a bear market, though it remains up roughly 75% year-to-date.

The core controversy centers on single-stock leveraged ETFs tracking SK Hynix and Samsung Electronics. These products require daily rebalancing of 700 billion to 2.1 trillion won ($525 million to $1.58 billion) in stock transactions, and have been accused of amplifying market volatility. The Korea Financial Investment Association disclosed that 10 major asset managers are discussing raising the minimum deposit requirement for leveraged ETFs from the current 10 million won ($6,714).

President Lee has directed the Financial Supervisory Service and Korea Exchange to address the issue promptly. The opposition People Power Party criticized the Lee administration for overlooking mounting leverage risks while pursuing ambitious stock market targets. To stabilize markets, the Financial Services Commission announced expanding the National Growth Fund from 150 trillion won to 200 trillion won and plans to establish a "Korea Strategic Technology Partner Company" for long-term tech investments.

💎 Market Outlook

Global financial markets on July 21 painted a picture of sharp divergence. China and Hong Kong markets surged on domestic policy tailwinds and the massive Yarlung Tsangpo infrastructure project, while South Korea continued to digest the aftershocks of leverage unwinding, and oil markets priced in persistent geopolitical risk. Going forward, three key factors will shape market direction: policy stimulus (China's growth stabilization), geopolitics (US-Iran conflict trajectory), and capital flows (global deleveraging). The $168 billion Tibet hydropower project has opened a multi-year growth runway for China's infrastructure supply chain, while Korea's leveraged ETF regulatory overhaul will provide an important case study for emerging-market financial innovation risk management.
