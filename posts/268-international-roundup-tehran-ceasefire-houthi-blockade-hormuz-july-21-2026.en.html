⚡ July 21 International Politics: Iran Initiates 10-Day Ceasefire Push, Houthis Blockade Saudi Arabia, US Bombs Iran for 10th Straight Night

1. Tehran Initiated the 10-Day Ceasefire Proposal: A Sign of Urgency

In an exclusive report on July 21, The Jerusalem Post revealed that the 10-day ceasefire proposal currently being advanced by mediators was actually initiated by Tehran itself — underscoring how urgently Iran is seeking a diplomatic off-ramp as U.S. strikes expand and President Donald Trump weighs a return to full-scale war.

Two sources familiar with the negotiations told the Post that the purpose of the proposed ceasefire is to carve out space for a solution on the Strait of Hormuz — the issue that triggered the collapse of last month's Memorandum of Understanding (MoU). Mediators including senior Qatari, Egyptian, Omani, and Pakistani officials have presented the proposal to Washington, adding elements such as a "middle corridor" through the Strait of Hormuz, located between Omani-controlled and Iranian-controlled waters, for safe commercial passage.

However, the U.S. is demanding a longer ceasefire. Washington insists on reaching at least partial understandings on freedom of navigation through the Strait before any truce takes effect, with details to be finalized during the 10-day pause. One source said at least some Trump administration officials have described the Iranian proposal as "absurd" and "illogical."

A U.S. official told the Post that Trump is focused on making Iran "pay a price" for violations of the MoU and for the deaths of American service members. "The president will also ensure that Iran pays for the recent deaths of U.S. soldiers. These devastating blows will continue until the president decides otherwise, but talks between our countries are continuing," the official said.

2. Houthis Declare Maritime Blockade on Saudi Arabia: Bab el-Mandeb at Risk

On Monday July 20, Yemen's Iran-aligned Houthi movement (Ansar Allah) declared an immediate "maritime embargo" against Saudi Arabia, marking a major escalation in the regional conflict that threatens another critical global energy chokepoint — the Bab el-Mandeb Strait.

Houthi military spokesman Yahya Sarea said the decision was "based on the equation of an eye for an eye," in retaliation for what the group called Saudi Arabia's "unjust and oppressive siege for nearly 12 years." Deputy head of the Houthi media office Nasruddin Amer clarified on X that the group is "closing Bab al-Mandab to Saudi enemy ships."

The Bab el-Mandeb Strait — just 20 miles (32 km) wide at its narrowest — connects the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden and serves as the southern gateway to the Suez Canal. Approximately 12% of global trade and 7% of global oil output (7.4 million barrels per day as of June 2026) passes through this chokepoint, according to Kpler data.

Saudi Arabia has been heavily reliant on the Bab el-Mandeb route since Iran effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz in late February. The kingdom has diverted more than 70% of its crude exports to the Red Sea port of Yanbu, with shipments surging from 973,000 barrels per day a year ago to 4 million bpd in recent weeks. A simultaneous closure of both Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb would cut off nearly all of Saudi Arabia's oil export capacity.

Saudi Arabia's foreign ministry vowed to "take all necessary measures to protect its ships in accordance with international law." But analysts warn that even the announcement alone will disrupt shipping and create uncertainty for Saudi ports.

Goldman Sachs warned that Brent crude could break $120/barrel in Q4 2026 if the Strait of Hormuz disruption continues. U.S. regular gasoline prices have already climbed to an average of $4/gallon, putting political pressure on Republicans ahead of the November midterm elections.

3. CENTCOM Completes 10th Consecutive Night of Airstrikes on Iran

U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) completed its 10th consecutive night of strikes on Iran at 9 p.m. ET on July 20 (01:00 UTC July 21). The strikes targeted Iranian military command centers, maritime capabilities, missile and drone launch sites, and air defense systems — all aimed at "further degrading Iran's ability to continue attacking commercial vessels flowing through the Strait of Hormuz."

According to CENTCOM, the U.S. has helped facilitate the transit of approximately 900 commercial vessels and 450 million barrels of crude oil through the Strait since early May. In retaliation, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) struck a U.S. radar system in Muharraq, Bahrain, and a Patriot air defense system in Riffa, Bahrain, while Iranian ground forces hit U.S. HIMARS systems at Camp Arifjan, Kuwait with surface-to-surface missiles. Kuwaiti air defenses engaged incoming fire, and Jordan's armed forces shot down three Iranian missiles.

The Pentagon confirmed the identities of two U.S. soldiers killed in Jordan, with a third still missing. Another U.S. service member died in Iraq when a downed Iranian drone was "controlled detonated" on Saturday. The total U.S. military death toll since the latest escalation has reached at least 16.

President Trump is scheduled to attend a Dignified Transfer ceremony on Tuesday evening at Dover Air Force Base, Delaware, for the fallen service members. Multiple senior U.S. officials say Trump is expected to decide within days whether to expand military operations and return to full-scale war against Iran.

The State Department issued a "Worldwide Caution" alert, warning American citizens abroad — including outside the Middle East — to maintain heightened vigilance, as U.S. diplomatic facilities could face threats.

4. Global Energy Market Fallout

The dual threat to both the Strait of Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb poses an unprecedented risk to global energy supplies. Brent crude has already broken above $90/barrel, with weekly gains of 15.5% last week alone.

Goldman Sachs said the risk of further escalation in the Middle East creates clear upside risks to its Q4 2026 forecasts of $80/barrel for Brent and $75/barrel for WTI. If Hormuz remains disrupted, Brent could exceed $120/barrel in Q4, the bank warned.

On Monday, Pakistan and Qatar formally presented a joint de-escalation proposal urging both sides to return to their pre-July 9 positions "as a first step" — with Iran reopening the Strait of Hormuz and the U.S. ending its blockade of Iranian ports and lifting sanctions on Iranian oil sales — to enable a two-week ceasefire and resumed talks.

Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei confirmed Monday that Tehran had received the mediators' proposals and was reviewing them, reaffirming Iran's commitment to diplomacy while reserving the right to respond to U.S. attacks. Analysts say Tehran is showing willingness to negotiate under the dual pressure of soaring domestic gasoline prices and intensifying military strikes, but the Trump administration remains deeply divided between hardliners pushing for full escalation and those open to de-escalation. The president's decision after today's dignified transfer ceremony may determine whether the Middle East war moves toward de-escalation or all-out conflict.
