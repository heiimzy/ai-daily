PrismML Bonsai 27B: First 27B-Parameter Model to Run on a Phone, Apple in Talks

On July 14, PrismML released Bonsai 27B — a low-bit quantization of Alibaba's Qwen 3.6 27B — marking the first time a 27-billion-parameter AI model can run natively on a smartphone. In a CNBC interview the same day, PrismML CEO Babak Hassibi revealed that Apple is evaluating the company's technology.

Bonsai 27B ships in two variants: Ternary ({-1, 0, +1} weights, 1.71 bits per weight, 5.9GB, retains 94.6% of FP16 performance) and 1-bit binary ({-1, +1} weights, 1.125 bits per weight, 3.9GB, retains 89.5% performance). This compresses what was a 54GB model by over 13x while still supporting multi-step reasoning, structured tool calling, vision tasks, and computer-use agentic workflows.

The phone constraint is far tighter than storage numbers suggest: a 12GB iPhone exposes only ~6GB per app, far below even a conventional 4-bit build (18GB). At 3.9GB, the 1-bit Bonsai 27B fits with room to spare, achieving 11 tok/s on iPhone 17 Pro Max and 672 tokens per 1% battery. PrismML also released DSpark speculative decoding, boosting H100 inference by 37% to 143.8 tok/s.

Hassibi told CNBC that Apple and other companies have been measuring PrismML's models for speed, energy efficiency, and on-device performance. "Things are progressing nicely," he said, describing the discussions as early-stage but positive. The disclosure suggests future iPhones could run 27B-parameter models locally rather than sending data to the cloud.

Bonsai 27B is available now under Apache 2.0 license, supporting MLX (Apple devices) and CUDA (NVIDIA GPUs), with a free limited-time developer preview API.

Google SensorFM: Health Foundation Model Trained on Trillion Minutes of Wearable Data

Google Research unveiled SensorFM on July 14 — a foundation model for wearable health trained on over one trillion minutes of sensor data from five million consenting participants across more than 100 countries and 20+ Fitbit/Pixel Watch models (September 2024–September 2025).

Applying the foundation-model approach to physiological data, SensorFM learns a general-purpose representation of human physiology from unlabeled data rather than building separate labeled models for each condition. It can adapt to more than 35 health-related measures spanning cardiovascular health, metabolic risk, mental health, sleep, and lifestyle factors. The model beat engineered-feature baselines on 34 of 35 tasks.

The most striking result: when SensorFM's predictions were fed into an AI health coach and blindly rated by clinicians, the SensorFM-grounded summaries matched those grounded in actual clinical lab measurements — with no statistically significant difference. For this test, the model's estimates served the coach about as well as real lab results.

Caveats apply: SensorFM is a research model, not an approved medical device, with no consumer or clinical deployment. It was trained on Fitbit/Pixel Watch data; transferability to other wearables remains unverified.

World Bank Makes First AI Chip Investment: IFC Leads $46M Quadric Series C

On July 15, AI chip licensor Quadric announced the second close of its Series C round at $46 million, bringing total funding to $90 million. The round was led by the International Finance Corporation (IFC), the private-sector investment arm of the World Bank Group — marking the IFC's first-ever investment in an AI chip company.

Quadric's programmable chip architecture enables AI to run on-device rather than in the cloud, particularly targeting small and medium enterprises in emerging markets. The company has secured design wins with automotive giant Denso and autonomous-driving leader Tier IV. New funding will expand automotive and AI PC customers while pursuing humanoid robotics, wearables, and advanced networking.

Quadric's product revenue more than tripled in the year leading to the first close, and the company reached profitability — a rare milestone for a hardware startup. Existing investors Pear VC, Uncork Capital, and BEENEXT all doubled down, joined by new investor Offline Ventures, co-founded by Facebook Platform creator Dave Morin.

Google's AI Finds Android Bugs Faster Than Anyone Can Patch Them

According to the GrapheneOS project, Google has informed Android device manufacturers that it will sharply reduce security backports to older Android versions — and the reason is that its own AI is discovering vulnerabilities faster than engineers can fix them.

Under the new policy, only Critical-severity issues deemed an "imminent risk" will be backported, and only to the two most recent releases (Android 16 and 17). When Android 18 arrives, it is expected to be the only version receiving current High and Critical patches. Previously, fixes were backported to roughly three major releases.

The "vast majority" of new vulnerabilities are now found internally by Google's AI models, producing what GrapheneOS calls a "massive torrent." The discovery machine runs fast, but the repair machine is still made of people — and there are fewer of them after successive layoffs. Hundreds of millions of perfectly functional devices will now carry known, catalogued, unfixed flaws. And because the same AI tooling isn't exclusive to Google, a documented but unpatched vulnerability is a published attack surface.

The news lands in a week of cascading AI-security events: xAI's Grok Build was caught silently uploading entire code repositories, and Langflow became the first AI Agent orchestration platform added to CISA's KEV catalog. AI is simultaneously becoming the vulnerability finder, the vulnerability vector, and the bypass tool.
