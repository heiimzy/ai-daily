⚡ July 17 International Politics Evening: US Hits Bridges for First Time in 6th Night of Iran Strikes — 7 Killed, Chabahar Tower Collapses, Iran Strikes US Base in Syria, China & Pakistan Call for Ceasefire

Sixth Consecutive Night: US Bombs Iranian Bridges for the First Time

The United States launched its sixth consecutive wave of massive airstrikes against Iran on Thursday at 2:00 PM ET (early Friday Beijing time), expanding its target set for the first time to include infrastructure — specifically bridges. According to Iran's state-run IRNA news agency, US airstrikes on bridges in Bandar Khamir in southern Hormozgan province killed at least 7 people and wounded 9 others. This marks the first time since the US-Iran war began on February 28 that the US military has targeted transportation infrastructure such as bridges.

US Central Command (CENTCOM) said in a statement that US forces — including fighter jets, aerial drones, and warships — launched precision munitions that hit "dozens of Iranian military targets" including coastal surveillance and air defense sites, military logistics infrastructure, and maritime capabilities. CENTCOM stated the operation "further degrades Iranian military capabilities" and "holds Iran accountable for recent attacks on commercial shipping."

Iranian media also reported that the Chabahar port surveillance tower collapsed in the strikes. Chabahar is Iran's key port on the Gulf of Oman and a critical trade route for Afghanistan. US Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth shared a photo of the collapsing tower on social media as a symbol of American efforts to assert control over the Strait of Hormuz.

This round of strikes is among the largest since the June ceasefire memorandum collapsed, covering Qeshm Island, Bandar Abbas, Ahvaz, Bushehr, and multiple other cities and provinces. Iranian media reported that strikes in Bandar Abbas caused partial power outages and hit a communications tower. The US also struck Iranshahr Airport in southeastern Iran's Sistan-Baluchestan province.

Iran Launches Unprecedented Retaliation: First Strike on Syria Base

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched one of its largest retaliatory operations since the war began on Friday, expanding its strikes to Syrian territory for the first time. The IRGC announced a "surprise attack" on the US Special Operations Command Center at al-Tanf in Syria, in retaliation for the killing of Iranian soldiers in Iranshahr.

Iranian state media reported the IRGC used missiles and drones to target the "new command-and-control center" at al-Tanf. A Syrian military source told Reuters that Iran carried out a strike near Tanf but did not directly hit the base itself, with no casualties or material damage reported. Notably, the US military completed its withdrawal from al-Tanf in February of this year.

Simultaneously, Iran launched massive strikes across multiple Middle Eastern nations:

  • Kuwait: Iran struck a power generation and water desalination station, igniting a fire and damaging "a large number of electricity generation units," according to Kuwait's Ministry of Electricity, Water and Renewable Energy. Kuwait's military said it intercepted 32 Iranian drones.
  • Qatar: Multiple explosions were heard in Doha as the government issued safety alerts urging residents to take shelter. Qatar's Defense Ministry confirmed its armed forces "intercepted a missile attack," and a child was injured by falling debris.
  • Bahrain: Iran claimed drone attacks on US helicopters and P-8 reconnaissance aircraft deployment areas at Sheikh Isa Air Base, using "Arash" suicide drones.
  • Jordan: Jordan's military said it intercepted three Iranian missiles that entered its airspace.
  • Oman: Iran also struck US radar facilities in Oman.

Iran Reports 38 Dead, 400+ Wounded; Houthis on Standby for Red Sea

Iran's Health Ministry spokesman Hossein Kermanpour announced Friday that at least 38 people have been killed and more than 400 wounded since fighting resumed on June 22. Iranian Parliament Speaker and chief negotiator Mohammad Bagher Qalibaf previously stated that the country is engaged in an "existential war."

An IRGC spokesperson said Iran's current operations are focused on destroying US "offensive infrastructure" in the region, warning that "the enemy should not think it can continue the current battle situation and drag the war into a war of attrition."

Reuters reported, citing three sources, that Iran has asked Yemen's Houthi rebels to be ready to disrupt shipping through the Red Sea if the US attacks Iranian power infrastructure. A source close to the Houthis said the group has positioned drones and missiles near the Bab el-Mandeb Strait and is awaiting orders. If the Bab el-Mandeb is blocked, two of the Middle East's most important oil export routes would be simultaneously disrupted, further worsening the global energy crisis.

China and Pakistan Jointly Call for Ceasefire; White House Says Diplomacy Still Possible

Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi and his Pakistani counterpart Ishaq Dar issued a joint statement after meeting in Shanghai, "expressing concern over the deterioration of the current situation, calling on the involved parties to immediately cease hostilities and return to dialogue." This marks the first joint ceasefire call by China and Pakistan since the US-Iran conflict escalated.

Despite the intensifying conflict, White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt said at a briefing that President Trump is "always open to diplomacy" and revealed that Iran "has expressed they still want to make a deal." She emphasized, however, that the president "will not allow them to fire on ships in the Strait without paying a consequence."

Brigadier General Mohammad Akraminia, spokesman for Iran's military, stated: "Americans think that by attacking some of our southern coastal bases, they can control the Strait of Hormuz. However, Iran has the ability to control the Strait of Hormuz from any point on its territory. The Strait of Hormuz is Iran's invincible red line."

Strait of Hormuz Shipping Nearly Paralyzed

With the sharp escalation of the conflict, shipping through the Strait of Hormuz has come to a near standstill. Maritime intelligence firm Kpler data shows only 3 commercial ships transited the strait in the past 24 hours, compared to a pre-war daily average of approximately 110. CENTCOM said US forces have redirected 3 commercial vessels trying to run the blockade, disabled 1 that refused to comply, and boarded 1 to "ensure full compliance" with the blockade.

Brent crude edged up to around $84.30/barrel on Friday, gaining nearly 12% this week. US diesel prices surpassed $5/gallon for the first time since June. Analysts warn that if the Houthis blockade the Bab el-Mandeb, oil prices could spike further.

The US also disabled an empty oil tanker heading toward Iran's Kharg Island oil export terminal using a Hellfire missile — the first such action since the naval blockade was reimposed, and occurring deep in the Persian Gulf far from the Strait of Hormuz, potentially signaling an expanding blockade zone.

Ukraine: Defense Minister Firing Sparks 15-City Protests, Drone Commander Resigns

The political crisis triggered by the dismissal of Ukrainian Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov continues to escalate. At least 15 Ukrainian cities saw protests on Friday — the largest public demonstrations since Zelenskyy took office. Deputy Air Force Commander Pavlo Yelizarov formally submitted his resignation, publicly stating he could not accept Fedorov's dismissal, calling it a "great evil" for Ukraine's defense.

After his dismissal, Fedorov launched a scathing attack on Armed Forces Chief General Oleksandr Syrskyi, accusing the military leadership of obstructing defense reforms. Zelenskyy called for public unity and said he was still considering candidates for defense minister.

Ukraine's parliament voted Thursday to approve energy executive Serhii Koretskyi as the new prime minister. Meanwhile, Russia struck Kyiv with 8 ballistic missiles overnight, killing 2 and wounding 6.
