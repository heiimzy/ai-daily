⚡ July 15 International Politics: US Reimposes Full Blockade, Launches Fourth Day of Strikes; Iran Retaliates Against US Bases in Three Gulf States; Trump Threatens to Hit Power Plants and Bridges Next Week

The US-Iran conflict has dramatically escalated this week after a brief ceasefire. The United States reimposed a naval blockade on Iranian ports Tuesday (July 14) and launched a fourth consecutive day of massive airstrikes targeting Iranian military installations along the Strait of Hormuz. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) retaliated with missile and drone strikes against US military bases in Bahrain, Kuwait, and Jordan. President Donald Trump warned in a Fox News interview that if Tehran does not return to negotiations, "next week comes the power plants, next week comes the bridges."

US Reimposes Naval Blockade and Launches Rare Daylight Strikes

The US Central Command (CENTCOM) announced that the naval blockade of Iranian ports and coastal areas took effect at 4:00 PM ET (20:00 GMT) Tuesday, barring all vessels traveling to or from Iranian ports. Simultaneously, US forces conducted seven consecutive hours of airstrikes overnight Tuesday, deploying fighter aircraft, drones, and naval vessels to launch precision munitions against Iranian missile sites, drone positions, naval capabilities, and coastal defense systems, striking "dozens of military targets."

On Wednesday morning (July 15) at 6:00 AM ET (10:00 GMT), US forces conducted an unprecedented daylight strike — a 90-minute attack on Iran's Greater Tunb Island in the Strait of Hormuz. CENTCOM stated the operation destroyed Iranian coastal defense systems and cruise missile storage and launch sites, "further degrading Iran's ability to attack commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz."

"US fighter aircraft, drones, and naval vessels launched precision munitions against Iranian missile and drone sites, naval capabilities, and coastal defense systems during the seven-hour wave to further degrade Iran's ability to threaten commercial shipping and civilian crews." — US Central Command statement

This marks the first time the US has conducted large-scale daylight airstrikes since the US-Israeli joint offensive against Iran began on February 28, signaling a significant acceleration in operational tempo. More than 21 US Navy vessels and hundreds of military aircraft are currently deployed across the Middle East.

Iran Strikes Back: Hits US Bases in Bahrain, Kuwait, and Jordan

In retaliation for American strikes, the IRGC claimed responsibility for attacks on US military targets in three Gulf nations on Wednesday:

  • Bahrain: The IRGC said it struck command-and-control centers, logistics facilities, fuel depots, and military equipment installations at the US Fifth Fleet headquarters in the Juffair area. Air raid sirens sounded across Bahrain, and the military said it had intercepted multiple aerial attacks from Iran.
  • Kuwait: Iranian forces targeted the major US logistics hub at Mina Abdullah. A Kuwaiti Navy vessel was hit, wounding four crew members. Kuwait's air force said it successfully intercepted 33 drones, 5 cruise missiles, and 1 ballistic missile.
  • Jordan: Iran's army launched multiple drone attacks on the Azraq air base. Jordan's military said its air defenses intercepted and shot down three Iranian ballistic missiles.

Iran also released satellite imagery showing damage to the US Fifth Fleet Drone Center in Bahrain. The IRGC vowed to "continue attacks against US military assets in the region."

Trump: Power Plants and Bridges Next Week, Ground Troops Not Ruled Out

President Trump indicated in a Tuesday night Fox News interview that strikes would only intensify:

"We're going to hit them very hard tonight. We're going to hit them hard tomorrow night. We're going to hit them really hard the night after. And next week, it gets really bad for them, because next week comes the power plants. Next week comes the bridges. We're going to knock out all their power plants. We're going to knock out all their bridges unless they get to the table and negotiate.""I'll save the energy targets for last, but ultimately we'll hit energy targets.""They'll continue until I say that's enough."

Trump also revealed that US negotiators had been in contact with Iranian officials as recently as an hour before the interview, telling them: "You better make a deal, or you're not going to have anything left." For the first time, he did not rule out deploying ground troops to Iran.

On Truth Social, Trump announced he was scrapping the previously proposed 20% fee on cargo shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, replacing it with "massive trade and investment deals" with Gulf states.

However, Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesperson responded that Tehran currently has "no plans for negotiations" and is "focused on defense." Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi declared the June Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) with the US "no longer valid."

Heavy Casualties: 7 Soldiers Killed, 260+ Wounded, 30+ Civilians Dead

Iran reported severe casualties from the latest US strikes:

  • Iran's army said 13 US missiles struck the 388th Mechanized Infantry Brigade barracks at Bampur in southeastern Sistan and Baluchestan province, killing 7 active-duty and conscript soldiers. The attack targeted accommodation blocks, guard posts, and a guesthouse.
  • Health Ministry spokesperson Hossein Kermanpour said overnight strikes alone wounded more than 260 people, with 222 discharged after treatment. This figure far exceeds any previous round of recent US-Iran violence.
  • Government spokesperson Fatemeh Mohajerani said more than 30 civilians had been killed in recent US attacks across southern Iran.

Civilian Facilities Hit

Iran reported that a bottled water production facility in Dehloran, Ilam province was hit by three US projectiles; explosions near the Kish Island power plant caused technical anomalies; and an environmental protection station in Hormozgan province was struck, killing three family members of a ranger. The US military denied striking civilian facilities, saying all targets were military.

Oil Markets in Turmoil: Brent Surges to $86.19

The escalating Hormuz crisis is roiling global energy markets:

  • Brent crude climbed to $86.19 per barrel in early Wednesday trading, up more than 23% from around $70 before the latest escalation.
  • Murban crude surged nearly 5% in a single day.
  • The International Monetary Fund (IMF) warned that the global oil spare capacity buffer has been "substantially depleted," and "unless inventories are replenished, the world will start from a weaker position when the next shock comes."
  • In peacetime, roughly one-fifth of global oil and gas trade passes through the Strait of Hormuz.

Multiple shipping companies have suspended transits through the Strait of Hormuz. Greek security firm Diaplous advised ships to pause until Saturday. Iran has attacked seven commercial vessels in the past week, leaving nearly a dozen crew members killed, missing, or injured.

IRGC Threatens to Halt All Middle East Energy Exports

The IRGC issued its strongest warning yet on Wednesday, threatening to completely disrupt Middle East energy exports if the US continues to control maritime routes:

"The export of oil and gas from the region will be either for everyone or for no one." — IRGC statement

The IRGC also signaled it could activate its Houthi allies in Yemen to blockade the Bab el-Mandeb Strait — another global energy artery linking the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden. Analysts warn that if both chokepoints are blocked simultaneously, oil prices could skyrocket to $200 per barrel.

Separately, the US Treasury announced it had frozen over $130 million by sanctioning cryptocurrency wallets linked to Iran's central bank. The State Department imposed new sanctions targeting 50 individuals, entities, and vessels allegedly involved in a sanctions-evasion network facilitating Iranian oil exports.

Trump Holds Situation Room Meeting on "Massive Offensive"

According to Axios, citing three sources familiar with the matter, Trump held an emergency Situation Room meeting Tuesday to discuss plans for a "massive offensive" against Iran. The meeting reportedly focused on "a new plan for devastating strikes on Iran's strategic targets," far broader in scale than current operations around the Strait of Hormuz.

Meanwhile, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth met with Iraqi Prime Minister Ali al-Zaidi at the Pentagon, demanding Baghdad "assert its sovereignty and disarm Iran-aligned militias" as a condition for deeper US-Iraq partnership.

Iran's UN Ambassador Amir Saeid Iravani wrote to Secretary-General Antonio Guterres: "America is an aggressor, not a victim." He accused Washington of refusing to implement its commitments under the MoU "almost immediately" after signing it.

What's Next: All-Out War or Return to Diplomacy?

Analysts identify four potential paths forward:

  • Full escalation: If Trump follows through on threats to target civilian infrastructure, Iran has signaled readiness for a "two-to-three-year protracted war." Prominent Iranian politician Mohammad-Javad Larijani called on state television for the nation to "prepare for a war economy."
  • Diplomatic breakthrough: Gulf states are working behind the scenes to bring both sides back to the table, with Oman and Pakistan continuing mediation efforts.
  • Protracted attrition: The battle for control of the Strait of Hormuz could evolve into a long-term conflict similar to the Ukraine war.
  • Proxy expansion: Iran could widen the conflict to the Red Sea, Bab el-Mandeb, and beyond, drawing more actors into the fray.

Whatever the outcome, the blockade of the Strait of Hormuz has become the biggest black swan event for the global economy in 2026. The world is holding its breath for the next escalation.
