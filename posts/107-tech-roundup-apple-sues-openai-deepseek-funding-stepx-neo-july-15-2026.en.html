⚡ July 15 Tech Roundup: Apple Sues OpenAI Over Trade Secret Theft Involving 400+ Employees, DeepSeek Closes $7.4B Funding at $50B+ Valuation, Stepfun Debuts World's First LLM-Native Agent Phone

1. Apple Files 41-Page Lawsuit Against OpenAI Over 'Systematic Trade Secret Theft'

On July 10, Apple filed a sweeping 41-page lawsuit in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California, accusing OpenAI and its hardware subsidiary io Products of orchestrating a coordinated campaign to steal Apple's trade secrets to accelerate its own AI hardware development. The lawsuit marks a dramatic escalation between two companies that were once close partners.

The complaint centers on two key figures: Tang Yew Tan, OpenAI's Chief Hardware Officer and former Apple VP of Product Design for iPhone and Apple Watch, and Chang Liu, a former Apple senior systems electrical engineer who joined OpenAI in January 2026. Apple alleges Tan emailed himself Apple supplier information before leaving and instructed Apple candidates interviewing at OpenAI to bring "actual parts" — batteries, motherboards, prototypes — for "show and tell" sessions. Most alarmingly, Tan allegedly possessed an Apple internal "Need to Know" security review document, which OpenAI then shared with departing Apple employees to coach them on how to bypass exit security protocols and extend internal network access.

Chang Liu's case is more direct: after leaving Apple, he kept his company-issued laptop and exploited a previously unknown zero-day authentication vulnerability to download dozens of confidential hardware files over several weeks, including information about unreleased products, engineering presentations, and technical specifications. Apple's server logs confirmed only Liu exploited this vulnerability. After discovering he could still remotely access Apple's systems, Liu messaged colleague Yu-Ting Peng: "LOL, I found out I can access the [network storage], so funny."

Apple revealed that OpenAI now employs more than 400 former Apple employees, with at least 25 senior design, manufacturing, and engineering hires in 2025 alone. "OpenAI's nascent hardware business now rests on the shakiest of foundations, rotten to its core by its illegal reliance on misappropriated trade secrets," Apple wrote in the complaint. The company is seeking an injunction, destruction of all misappropriated materials, financial damages, and a court order requiring OpenAI to redesign all affected products.

OpenAI responded that it has "no interest in other companies' trade secrets." Elon Musk quickly joined the fray, taunting Sam Altman on X for "taking scamming to a whole new level," sparking a public exchange. Analysts broadly predict a conditional settlement similar to the Waymo vs. Uber resolution.

2. DeepSeek Closes $7.4B Funding Round; Liang Wenfeng Becomes World's Richest AI Founder

Chinese AI unicorn DeepSeek has completed its first-ever external funding round, raising over $7.4 billion at a valuation exceeding $50 billion (some estimates place it as high as $59 billion), making it China's most valuable AI startup by a wide margin.

The round's most distinctive feature is founder Liang Wenfeng's iron grip on control. Through High-Flyer, the quantitative hedge fund he co-founded in 2016, Liang personally contributed approximately $3 billion — about 40% of the total raise. Outside investors face a five-year lock-up period with zero voting rights. Liang retains full strategic and operational control, particularly over research direction toward artificial general intelligence (AGI).

According to the Bloomberg Billionaires Index, Liang's personal net worth has reached approximately $16.7 billion, surpassing multiple Silicon Valley AI founders to become the world's wealthiest AI model creator. This milestone marks a significant elevation of Chinese AI power in global capital markets.

DeepSeek was founded in July 2023, spun out of High-Flyer's internal AI research. The company has historically self-funded through High-Flyer's trading profits, focusing on open-source AI models and research, producing models competitive with GPT-4 at significantly lower computational costs. This pivot to external funding represents a major strategic shift from self-sufficiency to scaled expansion. Liang plans to use the capital to expand compute infrastructure, recruit top talent, and accelerate AGI research.

Analysts note that the five-year lock-up with zero voting rights would typically make institutional investors walk away — but the round was oversubscribed, proving that when the technology is compelling enough, capital accepts almost any terms.

3. Stepfun Launches World's First LLM-Native Agent Phone: STEPX Neo

On the evening of July 13, Chinese AI company Stepfun unveiled the world's first large language model-native agentic smartphone, STEPX Neo, alongside the STEPX brand, Step AOS agent-native operating system, and the Amoo personal AI agent. This marks the first time a Chinese AI company has achieved full-stack capability across "model — system — hardware."

STEPX Neo's core innovation lies in its radically reimagined interaction paradigm: instead of navigating through menus and switching between app interfaces, users simply issue natural language commands. The Amoo agent interprets intent, decomposes tasks, and orchestrates system resources to execute them automatically. This shift from "manual system control" to "human-agent collaboration" represents a fundamental reinvention of the smartphone interaction model.

To enable on-device AI capabilities, Stepfun launched the Step Edge on-device model family on July 12, comprising four products: Base, Audio, GUI, and Gen — purpose-built for phones, vehicles, and other edge scenarios. "I don't think China's large model commercialization path is Coding (programming)," said Stepfun Chairman Yin Qi at a media briefing. "Whether software-hardware integration is the way, we don't know. But it's the path we believe in, so we'll try it."

On the ecosystem front, Stepfun announced initial partnerships with Alipay, Baidu, Ctrip, DiDi, Meituan, JD.com, WPS, and Jianying as launch partners. On the funding side, the company recently closed approximately $2.5 billion in new funding, with supply-chain capital — including Huaqin Technology, Longcheer, OmniVision, and ZTE — participating across the board. Stepfun is also reportedly preparing for a Hong Kong IPO, which would make it the third Chinese LLM startup to go public after Zhipu AI and MiniMax.

Industry analysts view STEPX Neo as a "showroom" piece — demonstrating what AI agents can achieve. The real test lies in mass production pricing and actual user experience. But one thing is clear: the AI competition has fully escalated from the software layer to the hardware layer.
