⚡ July 20 Financial Closing Bell: A-Share Extreme Divergence, Brent Surges Past $91, KOSPI Enters Bear Market as China National Team Mounts $84B Rescue

📊 A-Shares in Extreme Divergence: Shanghai Composite Rises 0.85% as 200+ Tech Stocks Hit Limit-Down

China's A-share market experienced one of the most extreme divergence sessions in recent memory on July 20. The Shanghai Composite Index closed up 0.85% at 3,796.28, while the CSI 300 (Shanghai 50) surged over 3%. However, the Shenzhen Component Index fell 0.71% to 13,610.23, and the STAR 50 (Kezhuang 50) dropped 2.28%. More than 3,700 stocks declined across the market, with over 200 tech stocks hitting their daily limit-down, even as coal, power, and oil-and-gas sectors saw a wave of limit-up rallies.

The market presented a stark tale of two halves. Coal stocks exploded higher — Yanzhou Coal, China Coal Energy, Dayou Energy, Zhengzhou Coal, and Haohua Energy all hit limit-up, as thermal coal prices climbed to 821 yuan/ton. The power sector surged with Huayin Electric and Leshan Electric recording back-to-back limit-ups, while Fuling Electric and Jinkong Power also hit limit-up. The oil-and-gas sector was propelled by surging international crude prices, with CNOOC, Zhongman Petroleum, and Bomai Energy all hitting limit-up, and PetroChina rising over 5%. Baijiu (Chinese liquor) stocks also strengthened, with Gujing Gongjiu recording its fourth limit-up in five days and Kweichow Moutai rising over 5%.

On the downside, the computing hardware supply chain suffered severe losses — PCB, lithography, and CPO sectors led the decline, with memory chip concepts broadly falling. Demaili hit limit-down for a fourth consecutive session. The semiconductor sector recorded net capital outflows of 11.49 billion yuan, the highest among all industries.

Total trading volume across the Shanghai, Shenzhen, and Beijing exchanges reached 2.7183 trillion yuan, up 46.4 billion from the prior session. Multiple broad-based ETFs saw massive buying activity near the close — the STAR 50 ETF (ChinaAMC) recorded 23.4 billion yuan in turnover, the ChiNext ETF (E Fund) exceeded 19.4 billion yuan, and the CSI 300 ETF (Huatai-PineBridge) surpassed 18.5 billion yuan, widely attributed to "national team" capital inflows.

🏛️ National Team + SOEs Mount Coordinated Defense: CSRC Holds Emergency Symposium

In the face of extreme volatility, Chinese regulators and state capital launched one of the most aggressive joint market stabilization operations in recent years.

On the evening of July 19, two state-owned capital operating companies simultaneously issued major statements. China Guoxin Corporation announced it has used over 50 billion yuan ($6.9B) in stock buyback relending facilities and matching funds to stabilize the capital market. China Chengtong Group disclosed it has accumulated nearly 10 billion yuan ($1.4B) in Chinese equity purchases. Both institutions explicitly committed to further purchases.

On July 20, six SOE-controlled listed companies — CRRC, Aluminum Corp of China (Chalco), NARI Technology, China Coal Energy, SDIC Power, and China Shenhua Energy — issued buyback or shareholder increase announcements. Chalco's controlling shareholder plans to purchase 1-2 billion yuan in shares, NARI's chairman proposed a 500 million to 1 billion yuan buyback, and CRRC plans 150-300 million yuan in share purchases.

The China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC) held an investor symposium on July 20 morning to gather opinions on stabilizing the capital market. Three securities firms — Hua'an Securities, Guolian Minsheng, and Guojin Securities — announced buyback plans totaling up to 700 million yuan. Quantitative hedge fund Lingjun Investment announced 200 million yuan in self-purchases, while Pingfanghe Investment announced 100 million yuan. Boshi Fund committed 50 million yuan to invest in its own equity funds.

CICC believes a "second buying opportunity of the year" may have arrived, with positive factors accumulating. China Merchants Securities noted that margin liquidation has entered its later stages, and heavy ETF inflows signal panic selling is being absorbed, limiting further downside.

🛢️ Oil Surges Past $91 as Strait of Hormuz Closes

International oil prices gapped sharply higher at the Asia-Pacific open on July 20, becoming the epicenter of global financial market volatility. Brent crude surpassed $90 for a second consecutive day, peaking at $91.40 per barrel — the highest since June 11. WTI crude jumped over 3% to $84.59 per barrel.

The trigger was a dramatic escalation in the Strait of Hormuz. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) declared on July 19 that transit through the strait had dropped to zero, claiming two oil tankers had struck mines and exploded. The IRGC warned the waterway "will not be safe for oil and petrochemical transit" as long as US military operations continue. US Central Command completed its ninth consecutive night of airstrikes targeting Iran's ability to attack commercial shipping.

Energy analysts warned that a prolonged closure could rapidly widen the global supply gap. Amrita Sen, founder of Energy Aspects, said depleted global inventories and slowing traffic could push oil past $100 per barrel. US gasoline prices have returned to $4 per gallon, reigniting supply-chain inflation fears.

In a notable diplomatic signal, Iranian Foreign Ministry Spokesman Esmail Baghaei said Monday that negotiations with the US "could be pursued" based on national interests. This caused Brent to partially retreat from intraday highs, settling in the $88-89 range. Nonetheless, markets see little prospect for near-term de-escalation.

🇰🇷 KOSPI Crashes Into Bear Market, Leveraged ETF Meltdown Spreads

South Korea's stock market was the hardest hit in Asia on July 20. The KOSPI index plunged over 4% at the open, triggering its 38th "Sidecar" trading halt mechanism of the year. The index hit an intraday low of 6,498 points, more than 25% below its June peak, officially confirming a technical bear market. The KOSPI partially recovered to close down approximately 3.53% at 6,580.

Blue chips were hammered: Samsung Electronics fell 3.24% to 246,500 won, while SK Hynix dropped 2.66% to 1,793,000 won. Foreign investors and institutions were net sellers of 133.7 billion won and 36.9 billion won respectively. According to reports, over 300,000 retail accounts have been fully force-liquidated by Korean brokerages. CNBC reported that Korean retail investors had poured a net 14 trillion won ($9.4 billion) into single-stock leveraged ETFs since their launch on May 27, with the KODEX SK Hynix Single Stock Leveraged ETF collapsing approximately 70% from its June peak.

The Bank of Korea had previously warned that leveraged stock investment had climbed to a record high. KB Financial Group noted that leveraged ETFs increasingly resemble speculative trading vehicles rather than long-term investment products. Despite regulators raising the minimum cash threshold for leveraged ETF trading from 3 million won to 30 million won on July 16, the measures failed to stem the crash.

🌍 Global Market Crosscurrents: Gold Breaks Below $4,000, Bitcoin Holds, US Futures Rebound

Global markets displayed unusual divergence on July 20.

Gold failed to benefit from its traditional safe-haven status amid the geopolitical turmoil, breaking below the $4,000 level to trade in the $3,989-4,014 range. Analysts pointed to surging oil prices stoking inflation fears and Fed rate hike expectations, which raise the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold. The Kitco survey showed 79% of Wall Street analysts bearish on gold this week, and HSBC has lowered its year-end gold forecast to $4,560.

Bitcoin demonstrated resilience amid macro uncertainty, holding steady in the $64,500-64,800 range with total crypto market capitalization at approximately $2.28 trillion. Market analysis noted bitcoin's growing role as a "shadow market" asset that trades continuously through weekends when traditional markets are closed. However, oil above $90 remains a significant headwind for risk assets. CME bitcoin futures were last at $64,785.

US equity futures pointed to a positive open: Nasdaq 100 futures rose 0.94% to 29,039, S&P 500 futures gained 0.46% to 7,529, and Dow futures added 0.20% to 52,482. This week brings critical Q2 earnings from Magnificent Seven members Alphabet and Tesla, with markets focused on any signs of AI capex moderation. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index has confirmed a technical bear market, down over 20% from its June peak, with global chip stocks losing $3.3 trillion in market value over three weeks.

The US Dollar Index firmed near 100.8, while the 10-year Treasury yield held steady. Japanese markets were closed for a public holiday, reducing Asia-Pacific trading volumes.

Looking ahead, markets will focus on three key variables: the trajectory of the Strait of Hormuz situation (determining the oil price ceiling), the Fed's September rate hike probability (currently ~58%), Q2 earnings guidance from megacap tech on AI capex sustainability, and the follow-through on China's policy support. The consensus view is that elevated volatility will persist in the near term.
