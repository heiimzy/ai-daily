GPT-5.6 Sol Autonomously Post-Trains Luna: A Self-Improvement Milestone

OpenAI revealed that its flagship GPT-5.6 Sol model can independently optimize smaller models. A researcher gave Sol a "fairly underspecified prompt" through Codex, asking it to find training configurations, select GPUs, launch the training script, and verify it was running correctly. Sol autonomously completed the post-training for the Luna model — a task that would have taken two staff researchers roughly two weeks, according to OpenAI employee Jason Liu.

On a new internal benchmark measuring Recursive Self-Improvement (RSI), Sol scored 16.2 points higher than its predecessor GPT-5.5. Average daily token output per active researcher more than doubled during internal testing, and pull requests and experiments per researcher also increased. Computing allocated to internal coding inference grew 100x over the past six months, while agent-based token usage jumped about 22x.

Sol Cracks 50-Year Math Problem with 64 Subagents in Under 1 Hour

OpenAI's Ethan Knight announced on X that GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra, just one day after general availability, produced a proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture — a graph theory problem that had sat unresolved since independently posed by George Szekeres in 1973 and Paul Seymour in 1979.

OpenAI published both the proof and the full prompt. The instructions tell the model to run up to 64 concurrent subagents managed "aggressively and dynamically," keeping early rounds diverse so the search doesn't collapse onto one incomplete idea too early. Agents that hit walls get marked as blocked; adversarial agents specifically hunt edge cases. The prompt demands at least eight hours before giving up — but the entire run completed in under one hour.

The announcement hit Hacker News's front page within its first hour and has already been added to the conjecture's Wikipedia entry. However, the conjecture has attracted multiple previous "proofs" later found to have gaps, so mathematicians are closely reviewing the claim.

UK AISI Finds Universal Jailbreaks in GPT-5.6 Sol

The UK AI Security Institute (AISI) discovered "universal jailbreaks" in GPT-5.6 Sol during pre-release evaluations, unlocking autonomous long-form agentic cyber task completion including vulnerability discovery and exploit development. AISI said the jailbreaks were "often developed within hours," allowing testers to bypass safeguards and make the model autonomously hack into systems.

The finding immediately drew comparisons to Anthropic's Fable 5, whose similar vulnerabilities led to US export controls in June (later lifted July 1). AISI characterized Sol's jailbreaks as "universal" and potentially more severe, unlocking a wider range of cyber functions. OpenAI said it had worked to "reproduce and mitigate the specific jailbreaks" but AISI warned it "expects further red teaming to surface similar jailbreaks." The Trump administration has not imposed export controls on GPT-5.6 so far.

OpenAI Launches ChatGPT Work: Persistent Cloud AI Agent Platform

OpenAI today launched ChatGPT Work, a persistent cloud-based AI agent embedded inside ChatGPT that aims to transform it from a Q&A tool into an autonomous work platform. Powered by GPT-5.6, ChatGPT Work can execute complex multi-step tasks across users' email, calendars, code repositories, and messaging apps, producing finished documents, spreadsheets, presentations, reports, and websites.

The product runs on a persistent cloud virtual machine on OpenAI's servers — users don't need their local device to stay powered on. It supports MCP-based plugins connecting to Gmail, Google Calendar, Slack, and GitHub. Rolling out to Pro, Enterprise, and Edu users now, with Plus and Business expansion over the coming days. This directly competes with Anthropic's Claude Cowork and Microsoft's Copilot Cowork, forming a three-way battle for enterprise AI agent dominance.

OpenAI confidentially filed its S-1 registration in June with valuations between $730B and $852B and annualized revenue exceeding $25 billion. ChatGPT Work's enterprise traction will be critical to the IPO narrative.

Blackstone Plans $30 Billion Investment in Japan AI Data Centers

Blackstone, the world's largest alternative asset manager, plans to invest $30 billion in AI data centers in Japan over the next three to five years, president and COO Jonathan Gray told Nikkei. Blackstone has already developed over 500MW of data center capacity in Japan and is in talks for projects exceeding 1GW — equivalent to a nuclear reactor's output.

Gray said the greater risk lies in "underbuilding computing capacity" rather than overinvesting due to AI bubble fears. Blackstone recently closed its Asia private equity fund at $13.1 billion, above its $10 billion target. Japan is the second-largest data center market among developed economies after the US, generating $23.4 billion in revenue in 2024 and projected to reach $33.4 billion by 2030.
