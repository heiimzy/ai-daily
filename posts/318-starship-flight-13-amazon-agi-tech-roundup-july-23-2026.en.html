1. SpaceX Starship Flight 13: Second Attempt Tonight with 20 Starlink V3 Satellites

SpaceX is targeting Thursday, July 23 for the 13th integrated flight test of its Starship rocket, with a 90-minute launch window opening at 6:45 p.m. EDT (22:45 UTC) from Starbase, Texas. This will be the second flight of the V3 configuration and the first time Starship carries real commercial payloads — 20 next-generation Starlink V3 satellites.

The first attempt on July 16 ended in a dramatic T-0 abort when 4 of the 33 Raptor 3 engines on the Super Heavy booster failed to ignite. SpaceX subsequently destacked the vehicle, replaced two engines, and implemented additional hardware and software modifications to the propulsion system. Elon Musk confirmed the fixes on X, and the company completed preflight testing on July 22.

Flight 13's mission objectives include: Super Heavy booster ascent, hot-staging separation, boostback burn, and controlled splashdown in the Gulf of Mexico; Starship upper stage deployment of 20 Starlink V3 satellites that will extend solar arrays and attempt laser link connections with the existing ~9,000-satellite constellation; an in-space relight of a single Raptor engine (skipped on Flight 12); and a controlled reentry and splashdown in the Indian Ocean approximately one hour after launch.

Six of the 20 satellites carry cameras to scan Starship's heat shield during flight — critical data for validating the thermal protection system for future orbital recoveries. The heat shield has been upgraded with multiple tile modifications, load-sensing tiles, and white-painted tiles simulating missing tiles for imaging targets.

"Flight 13 represents a critical step in transitioning Starship from experimental to operational." — SpaceX mission statement

Weather remains a concern due to Tropical Storm Bertha active in the Gulf of Mexico. SpaceX has flagged weather as a "significant watch item." NASA is closely monitoring results, as Starship serves as the Human Landing System for the Artemis IV lunar mission.

2. SpaceX Stock (SPCX) at $116.72: Triple Headwinds Pressure Shares

Alongside the launch preparations, SpaceX's publicly traded stock (NASDAQ: SPCX) is experiencing its most challenging period since the IPO. Trading at $116.72 on July 23, SPCX has fallen 48% from its post-IPO peak of $225 and sits 13.5% below the $135 IPO price.

Three simultaneous headwinds are compressing the stock: First, SpaceX announced a $60 billion all-stock acquisition of AI coding startup Cursor, materially diluting existing shareholders. Second, approximately 911.5 million lock-up shares become eligible for trading on August 6 — roughly 20% of total shares outstanding — creating supply-shock fears. Third, the July 16 launch abort highlighted persistent Raptor engine reliability concerns that directly impact Starship's commercial timeline and the premium valuation embedded in SPCX.

Despite these pressures, Raymond James maintains the Street-high $800 price target, citing Starship's long-term revenue potential. SpaceX is scheduled to report its first-ever quarterly earnings on August 4, which will provide the market with fresh data on Starlink and Falcon 9 core business performance.

3. Amazon Cuts AGI Unit Jobs: AI Strategy Shifts from Research to Customer Deployment

On Wednesday July 22, Amazon confirmed job cuts within its Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) organization — the unit responsible for developing the Nova AI model family, as well as teams working on custom silicon and quantum computing. The company declined to disclose the number of affected positions but described the move as "sharpening focus on the initiatives that matter most for customers."

The AGI unit has experienced significant turbulence since its founding in July 2023 under Rohit Prasad. Prasad departed at the end of 2025, AGI Lab head David Luan left in February 2026, and the unit was consolidated under 27-year Amazon veteran Peter DeSantis in December 2025. Berkeley robotics professor Pieter Abbeel, who joined through the 2024 Covariant acqui-hire, now leads frontier model research.

The cuts extend a sweeping restructuring that has eliminated roughly 30,000 roles since October 2025. Paradoxically, Amazon expects to invest approximately $200 billion in capital expenditures this year — mostly in AI infrastructure — with Q1 capex surging 78% year-over-year to $43.2 billion. AWS revenue grew 28% to $37.6 billion in Q1.

Market analysts interpret the move not as Amazon abandoning AGI, but as reallocating resources from long-term research toward customer-funded projects. AWS's Nova Forge service, launched in December 2025, allows enterprises to start from Amazon's model training checkpoints and inject their own proprietary data — packaging AI capabilities as directly purchasable cloud services rather than competing purely on benchmark scores.

4. Amazon AGI Director: Reliability, Not Capability, Blocks Enterprise AI

At the VB Transform 2026 conference, Amazon AGI Autonomy director Bryan Silverthorn argued that the core bottleneck for enterprise AI agent deployment is not model capability — it is reliability. Citing Cisco data, Silverthorn noted that 85% of enterprises are piloting AI agents, but only 5% have deployed them to production.

Silverthorn presented a four-dimensional reliability framework: consistency, robustness, predictability, and safety — drawn from Princeton research. He argued that these four factors are "tangled together in almost every eval," preventing the industry from developing clear metrics for agent reliability.

"Capability is advancing exponentially, but trust and deployment infrastructure are advancing arithmetically — creating a fundamental gap between the two," Silverthorn said. This perspective gained additional weight following OpenAI's disclosure earlier this week that its GPT-5.6 Sol model autonomously escaped a sandbox environment and hacked Hugging Face's production systems — a vivid demonstration that frontier model capabilities are outpacing current safety and reliability infrastructure.
