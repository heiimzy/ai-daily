⚡ July 21 International Politics Depth: Houthis Declare Maritime Blockade of Saudi Arabia, Opening a Second Global Energy Front; Trump Vows 'Many Times Over' Retribution; US Bombs Tabriz in Northern Iran for First Time

1. Houthis Declare Comprehensive Naval Blockade on Saudi Arabia: A New Red Sea Front Opens

Yemen's Iran-aligned Houthi movement (Ansar Allah) announced a "comprehensive maritime blockade" against Saudi Arabia on July 20, effective immediately. Houthi military spokesman Yahya Saree declared the embargo was based on an "eye for an eye" principle, in response to what the group described as Saudi Arabia's 12-year siege on Yemen and last week's airstrike on Sanaa International Airport.

Nasruddin Amer, deputy head of the Houthi media office, confirmed on X that the group's forces were "closing Bab al-Mandab to Saudi enemy ships." The Bab al-Mandab Strait — the southern gateway connecting the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden — is one of the world's most critical maritime chokepoints. Total petroleum volumes transiting Bab al-Mandab reached 7.4 million barrels per day in June 2026, or about 7% of global oil output. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed since late February, Saudi Arabia has diverted over 70% of its crude exports to the Red Sea port of Yanbu, making the Bab al-Mandab route a vital lifeline.

Analysts warn that if the Houthis enforce their blockade of Bab al-Mandab on top of the Hormuz closure, up to 25% of the world's oil and gas supply could be cut off — the most severe energy supply disruption since the 1973 oil crisis.

The Houthis warned that any "foolish" Saudi act would trigger a "comprehensive and harsh response" and expressed "complete readiness for all options." The group previously disrupted global commerce by attacking Red Sea shipping from November 2023 until the Gaza ceasefire in October 2025, forcing major shipping lines like Maersk and Hapag-Lloyd to divert around Africa's Cape of Good Hope. This new declaration threatens to reignite that crisis at a time when global energy markets are already reeling from the Hormuz closure.

2. Trump Vows Iran Will 'Pay Many Times Over' as Mediators Push 10-Day Truce

President Donald Trump issued a stark warning on July 20, vowing that Iran would "pay" for killing three US soldiers in recent days. "Every time Iran kills an American Soldier they will pay for that killing many times over!" Trump wrote on Truth Social. White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt confirmed the president will attend a dignified transfer ceremony for "our fallen heroes" on the evening of July 21.

Meanwhile, mediators are working to de-escalate tensions. Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmail Baghaei confirmed that "ideas from some mediators have been conveyed" to Tehran. Qatar and Pakistan, the main intermediaries, propose returning to pre-July 9 positions as a first step. Reuters reported, citing an unidentified senior Iranian official, that one proposal calls for a 10-day ceasefire to find ways to revive last month's memorandum of understanding. Iran's interior minister has departed for Pakistan.

Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said Iran would not abandon diplomacy but also would not be forced back to the negotiating table while under military attack: "Iran's actions in the Strait of Hormuz and Persian Gulf had global consequences and we became a global player," he said, underscoring Tehran's view of the strait as leverage over the US.

Oil swung between gains and losses on July 20, with Brent crude closing about 1% higher at $88.93 per barrel. US gasoline prices have climbed back above $4 per gallon, potentially hurting Trump and Republicans ahead of November's midterm elections.

3. Ninth Night of US Strikes Hits Tabriz in Northern Iran for First Time

The United States conducted its ninth consecutive night of airstrikes on Iran on July 20-21, expanding the target zone to include northern Iran for the first time. Iranian state media reported that US forces struck a military area in the southwestern part of Tabriz, the capital of East Azarbaijan province in northwestern Iran. Majid Farshi, director general of crisis management at East Azarbaijan province, confirmed at least one person was killed and several others injured.

The previous eight nights of strikes were concentrated in southern Iran's coastal areas. US Central Command said the latest wave targeted maritime surveillance sites, weapon launch facilities, and air defense positions to degrade Iran's ability to control shipping in the Strait of Hormuz. Iran has continued retaliatory strikes against US bases in Kuwait, Jordan, Bahrain, and Iraq. Kuwait's state energy company reported an unspecified site sustained "significant material losses" with multiple injuries.

Iranian Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf posted on X: "The Americans keep bringing new military equipment into the region while claiming they want to stop the war. We have become experts at recognising these American tactics and have prepared accordingly."

4. Ukraine Strikes Moscow Oil Depot and 6 Russian Vessels in Black Sea

Ukraine launched a major counter-strike on July 20, hitting an oil depot and logistics facilities in Russia's Moscow Oblast, as well as six Russian vessels in the Black Sea. President Volodymyr Zelensky confirmed the strikes, saying they targeted "two shadow fleet tankers and four cargo ships." The operation was described as retaliation for what Zelensky called "one of the largest Russian ballistic missile attacks to date" on Kyiv.

Russia, meanwhile, was accused of attacking civilian shipping. On the evening of July 19, Russian forces struck the MV Golden Leo — a Guinea-Bissau-flagged, Turkish-owned civilian bulk carrier carrying grain — with three Kh-59/Kh-69 cruise missiles as it departed the port of Odesa. Four of the five Indian crew members on board were killed, with one critically injured. India's Ministry of External Affairs strongly condemned the attack, calling it "deplorable" to target commercial shipping and endanger innocent civilian crew members. The Ukrainian Navy characterized the strike as "an act of terror against peaceful shipping."

Ukraine's domestic political turmoil continues. President Zelensky's dismissal of Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov last week has sparked days of mass protests — only the second such unrest since Russia's full-scale invasion began in 2022. The New York Times reported that Zelensky is also facing pressure to replace Ukraine's top military commander, General Oleksandr Syrskyi, and is consulting with senior security officials.

5. Israel Convenes Security Cabinet on Full Alert

Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu convened a security cabinet meeting on the evening of July 20 to discuss Iran tensions. Security Cabinet member Eli Cohen warned: "If Iran makes the mistake of attacking the State of Israel, we'll respond with a might surpassing that of the last campaign." Israeli air defense systems have been activated repeatedly in recent days, intercepting missile debris from Iranian strikes on Jordan that spilled into Israeli airspace.

US Secretary of State Marco Rubio said Iran continues to send signals it wants to negotiate, reiterating the US view that the Iranian government is split between those seeking a peace agreement and hardliners wanting to continue fighting.
