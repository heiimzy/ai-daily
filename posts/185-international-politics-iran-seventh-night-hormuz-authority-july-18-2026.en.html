⚡ July 18 International Politics Roundup: US Launches 7th Night of Iran Strikes, Trump Halts 'Major' Strike, Iran Establishes Strait Authority

1. Seventh Night of Strikes: CENTCOM Confirms New Wave, IRGC Claims Bahrain AI Center Destroyed

US Central Command launched its seventh consecutive night of airstrikes against southern Iran on Thursday evening, starting at 18:00 GMT July 17. While this round was described as less intense relative to previous nights, explosions were reported across multiple strategic locations including Sirik — a city overseeing the Strait of Hormuz — Qeshm Island, and Bushehr province.

The Iranian Students' News Agency (ISNA) reported that the area around Sirik in Hormozgan province was targeted. State-affiliated Fars news agency confirmed some explosions were caused by Iranian air defenses intercepting incoming US missiles and drones.

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) claimed in a statement early Friday that it had destroyed a US drone depot and "the main artificial intelligence center" in Bahrain, alleging the facility was used by the US to identify targets in Iran and commit "war crimes." The IRGC warned it would "henceforth destroy the most important industrial, information technology, and artificial intelligence assets of companies with American shareholders in the region, regardless of the country they are in." This marks the first time the IRGC has explicitly designated AI infrastructure as a military target since the war began on February 28.

Iran's Foreign Ministry Spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei condemned the US strikes on Iranian civilian infrastructure as "another blatant war crime," noting that the bombing of bridges in Hormozgan province had killed at least seven civilians and injured nine.

2. Trump Postpones 'Major' Strike After Gulf Leaders Intervene

President Donald Trump revealed on July 18 that the US had planned a "very significant" military strike against Iran for July 19, but decided to delay it by "two or three days" following urgent requests from the leaders of Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates. The White House informed Israel and other Middle Eastern allies of the postponement. Trump stated he "doesn't want to act" but "may have no choice."

Earlier in the day, Trump assessed Iran's latest proposal as "disappointing" and ruled out making any concessions. A senior US official characterized Iran's latest draft as containing only "cosmetic adjustments" with no substantive nuclear concessions, warning that talks are at a "very serious" juncture and the US "may have to continue negotiations through bombs" if Iran does not change its position.

Iran confirmed it had transmitted its latest negotiating text to Washington through Pakistani mediators. Foreign Ministry Spokesman Baghaei said Iran would not compromise on matters concerning its national rights. Sources indicated serious disagreements remain on compensation amounts, the linkage between negotiations and nuclear issues, and the scope of sanctions relief. Iran insists on war reparations with "grave seriousness."

3. Iran Establishes 'Persian Gulf Strait Authority' — Targets Undersea Cables for Fees

On July 18, Iran officially established a new body to manage the Strait of Hormuz — the "Persian Gulf Strait Authority" — launching an official social media account the same day. The IRGC Navy retweeted the announcement.

In a more dramatic move, the IRGC announced that "all fiber optic cables passing through this waterway must obtain permits, submit to regulation, and pay sovereign fees." Citing the 1982 UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), the IRGC claimed Iran's sovereignty over the seabed and subsoil of its territorial waters entitles it to license, regulate, and charge for submarine cables transiting the Strait of Hormuz.

This represents a significant expansion of Iran's leverage over the strait — from traditional shipping tolls to digital infrastructure. Multiple critical submarine cables connecting the Middle East to South Asia, East Africa, and Europe traverse the strait, carrying a substantial portion of global internet traffic. If enforced, this policy could have far-reaching consequences for global digital communications.

The US Treasury had previously sanctioned an earlier Iranian body attempting similar management of the strait in May. The Trump administration has consistently opposed any "monetization" of the Strait of Hormuz by any party. Analysts view this as an asymmetric countermeasure by Tehran — using legal and jurisdictional tools to consolidate control over the strategic waterway while outmatched militarily.

4. Iran Proposes Conditional Uranium Transfer to Russia: New Nuclear Talks Twist

Saudi Arabia's Al Arabiya television reported on July 18, citing sources, that Iran's revised peace proposal includes a conditional plan to transfer enriched uranium to Russia rather than to the US or a third country as Washington had demanded. According to the obtained text, Iran has agreed to a long-term freeze of its nuclear program but not complete dismantlement of nuclear facilities.

A senior Iranian source confirmed that the US showed flexibility regarding Iran maintaining limited peaceful nuclear activities under IAEA supervision. However, the source acknowledged that nuclear and uranium enrichment issues remain the most intractable parts of negotiations and have been deferred to subsequent rounds.

Iran's Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei reiterated through social media on July 18 his earlier stance on opening new battlefronts in areas "where the enemy has minimal experience and is extremely vulnerable," claiming that "research on opening other fronts has been completed." President Masoud Pezeshkian stated: "Negotiation does not mean surrender. We will negotiate on the basis of preserving dignity, strength, and national rights."

5. Ukraine Drones Hit 8 Russian Oil Tankers in Sea of Azov; Houthis Threaten Bab al-Mandab

Ukrainian forces launched a coordinated drone strike on July 18 against eight Russian "shadow fleet" oil tankers in the Sea of Azov, including the Venera-3, Sanar-1, Sanar-17, Klymena, Teti, Alexey Savrasov, and Penelope. Ukraine's General Staff said the operation aimed to further degrade Russia's fuel supply chain and war economy.

Meanwhile, Russian aviation continued launching glide bombs at Kharkiv, hitting the Shevchenkivskyi district, killing at least one person and wounding 13. In Dnipropetrovsk Oblast, Russia launched 123 attack drones overnight; Ukraine's Air Force said most were intercepted.

Ukraine has intensified its campaign against Russian energy infrastructure in recent months. Last month, Ukraine's 1st Assault Army struck a secret fuel depot in Novoandriivka, Donetsk, and simultaneously hit the Sisra refinery inside Russia. Russian front-line fuel supplies are critically strained — some soldiers reportedly walk 30 km to the front line. Russia lost nearly 40 tanks in June due to logistical issues. At current advance rates, analysts estimate it would take 14 years for Russian forces to capture the entirety of Donetsk, far exceeding Putin's year-end 2026 target.

In Yemen, the Houthi movement (Ansarallah) threatened on July 18 to block the Bab al-Mandab strait to Saudi Arabian shipping. A Houthi politburo member stated the strait is a "legitimate leverage tool" to enforce a "blockade-for-blockade" equation. Houthi leader Abdulmalek al-Houthi declared that "all Saudi oil facilities and vital installations" were targets. If the Houthis enforce a blockade, Saudi Arabia's alternative oil export route via the Red Sea would also be severed, placing immense additional strain on global energy supply chains already disrupted by the Hormuz crisis.
