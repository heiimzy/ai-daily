⚡ July 13 Tech Roundup: Jensen Huang Refutes Rubin Ultra Delay at Morgan Stanley Roadshow, TSMC 2nm Mass Production Begins with Google First, SK Hynix Says Memory Is No Longer Cyclical

July 13 marks a milestone day for the semiconductor and AI chip industry across three fronts. Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang personally attended a Morgan Stanley roadshow, forcefully pushing back against four major market doubts and confirming that Rubin Ultra will ship on schedule next year. TSMC's 2nm process has officially entered mass production, with Google's Tensor G6 chip securing the first customer slot a full month ahead of Apple. And SK Hynix Chairman Chey Tae-won declared after the company's historic Nasdaq listing that memory is no longer a cyclical industry, as AI inference-driven KV caching demand fundamentally reshapes the market structure.

1. Jensen Huang Crushes Four Bearish Narratives: Rubin Ultra on Track for 2027, Anthropic Shifts to Nvidia Compute

Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang, joined by CFO Colette Kress and other senior executives, attended a Morgan Stanley non-deal roadshow (NDR) in California, directly confronting the four major doubts that have been plaguing the market. Morgan Stanley analyst Joseph Moore described the meeting as "positive in tone," with management repeatedly emphasizing a core message: even with quarterly revenue approaching the $100 billion mark, the company's growth curve continues to accelerate.

Rubin Ultra Delay Rumors Dismissed

An industry rumor circulating before the roadshow claimed that Nvidia's next-generation flagship Rubin Ultra architecture could be delayed to 2028. Huang directly denied this at the meeting, clearly stating that Rubin Ultra will ship on schedule next year with no delays whatsoever. Moore added that some rack-level design adjustments are indeed underway for Rubin systems — the original Kyber rack design is being replaced by a new optimized solution to accommodate larger-scale AI computing clusters. However, this is purely a system architecture optimization and has no impact on product delivery timelines. Core technologies including the 800V power delivery system and rack-level optical interconnect are all progressing according to original plans.

ASIC Cannibalization Concern Reversed

Addressing investor anxiety about cloud providers' custom ASIC chips eating into Nvidia's market share, Huang revealed compelling customer data: a representative frontier model that previously relied primarily on ASIC development — with minimal Nvidia involvement — now sees Nvidia's computing share approach 50%. Market consensus suggests this points to Anthropic, the AI startup partnered with AWS and its in-house Trainium chips, which is now significantly increasing its Nvidia GPU procurement.

Nvidia management presented a new industry thesis: ASIC expansion and Nvidia's growth are not a zero-sum game. They can develop in parallel. The reason is that customers evaluate computing on total cost per token across the full training and inference workflow, not on individual chip price tags. Leveraging its complete CUDA software-hardware stack advantage, Nvidia offers superior per-token cost in most commercial scenarios. Data shows that from 2024 to 2026, Nvidia's global AI computing market share has actually increased rather than decreased.

Three Growth Engines Fire Simultaneously

Nvidia outlined three differentiated growth tracks during the roadshow. The first is AI labs, currently contributing about 20% of total demand, with OpenAI maintaining deep engagement and even previously ASIC-preferring clients like Anthropic increasing GPU deployment. The second is traditional hyperscale cloud providers — Microsoft, Meta, Amazon, and Google collectively contribute roughly half of revenue, and Nvidia is expanding from GPU-only supply to full-stack offerings including Vera CPUs and high-speed networking equipment. The third and fastest-growing track is sovereign AI, emerging AI cloud platforms, and industrial enterprise markets, driven by geopolitical concerns and data security demands.

Additionally, Nvidia reiterated its CPU business target of $20 billion for the current fiscal year, with nearly half of that revenue expected from standalone CPU racks. This means Vera CPU has officially entered the general-purpose server market, competing directly with x86 architecture. Morgan Stanley maintains its "Overweight" rating on Nvidia with a $288 price target, representing 42% upside from current levels.

2. TSMC 2nm Mass Production Begins: Google Tensor G6 Takes First Slot, Pixel 11 Launches in August

July 13 marks a milestone for the semiconductor industry. According to Taiwan's Economic Daily News and multiple authoritative reports, TSMC's 2nm (N2) process has officially entered mass production. Google has become the first smartphone chip customer for TSMC's 2nm process, breaking Apple's long-standing tradition of being the first to adopt TSMC's newest manufacturing technology.

TSMC's 2nm (N2) process is the company's first node to adopt Gate-All-Around (GAA) nanosheet transistor technology. Compared to the existing N3E process, N2 delivers 10-15% higher performance at the same power consumption, 25-30% lower power at the same performance, and 15% higher transistor density. This represents another transistor architecture revolution following FinFET, marking the semiconductor manufacturing industry's official entry into the GAA era.

Google's Tensor G6 chip will be the first to leverage TSMC's 2nm process, featuring a 7-core CPU architecture with one 4.11GHz ARM C1-Ultra super core, four 3.38GHz C1-Pro performance cores, and two 2.65GHz efficiency cores. The communication baseband has been switched to a MediaTek solution to improve signal stability. The Google Pixel 11 series, powered by Tensor G6, is scheduled for an August launch — a full month ahead of Apple's iPhone 17 series with the A20 chip.

Analysts note that while Pixel 11's market share won't rival the iPhone, Google's Tensor strategy has always been about controlling the underlying capabilities for on-device AI computing. The shift from Samsung's 5nm to TSMC's 2nm represents both a generational leap in process technology and a renewed declaration of chip independence. Notably, the disclosed mass production timeline is slightly ahead of earlier market expectations, reflecting strong AI computing demand pulling advanced process technologies forward.

3. SK Hynix Chairman: Memory No Longer Cyclical as KV Caching Reshapes Demand

Following SK Hynix's historic ADR listing on Nasdaq, SK Group Chairman Chey Tae-won shared major insights at a New York press conference. He directly pushed back against concerns that the memory chip market has peaked, arguing that the industry's cyclical structure has fundamentally changed. Production capacity constraints and sustained AI demand growth are reshaping the supply-demand dynamics of the memory market.

KV Caching: The New Demand Explosion Point for the AI Inference Era

Chey specifically highlighted the growing importance of Key-Value (KV) caching technology. During large language model inference, KV caching stores intermediate states generated by the model, avoiding recalculation of full historical context when generating each new token. As AI inference workloads explode, KV caching demand for high-bandwidth memory is rising exponentially. SK Hynix's roughly 60% global share of the HBM market positions it as an irreplaceable player in AI infrastructure.

Chey also floated the novel concept of "Memory as a Service" (MaaS) as a potential future business model. As AI inference continues to demand ever-greater memory capacity and bandwidth, offering memory as a service to customers could become a significant growth vector.

HBM4 Prices Could Double Next Year

According to industry reports, HBM4 prices are expected to surge from approximately $2 per gigabit in the second half of this year to $4-5 or more next year. HBM3E, currently priced at around $1.5-1.6 per gigabit, is also expected to rise in tandem. Nvidia's next-generation Vera Rubin platform, equipped with HBM4, will ship next year, further driving HBM procurement demand. HBM manufacturing consumes approximately three times the wafer capacity of general-purpose DRAM, and HBM4's production cycle is up to twice as long as DDR5 with initially low yields, creating even greater upward price pressure.

Chey also warned that excessive memory price increases could negatively affect traditional industries including retail and automotive. He noted that Chinese semiconductor companies are turning from losses to profits and building up capital expenditure capacity, and the industry must prepare for rapid competitive pursuit.

SK Hynix's Nasdaq listing raised approximately $26.5 billion, surpassing Alibaba's 2014 record of $25 billion to become the largest IPO by a foreign company in US history. Proceeds will fund the Yongin Semiconductor Cluster, the Cheongju advanced packaging facility, and ASML EUV lithography equipment procurement — key components of SK Hynix's AI memory capacity expansion strategy.

Summary

Today's three major tech stories converge on a single core theme: AI computing demand is profoundly impacting the entire semiconductor value chain — from chip design and manufacturing process to memory supply. Nvidia's roadshow reinforces market confidence in sustained AI infrastructure investment, TSMC's 2nm mass production provides more powerful hardware for on-device AI, and SK Hynix's structural thesis on the memory market signals that the long-cycle logic of AI infrastructure investment is gaining widespread acceptance.
