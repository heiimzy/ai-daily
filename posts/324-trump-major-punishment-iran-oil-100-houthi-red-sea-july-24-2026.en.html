⚡ July 24 International Politics: Trump Considers Restarting Large-Scale Iran Combat; Brent Breaks $100; Houthi Red Sea Attacks Open Second Front

🚨 Trump Says He Is 'Seriously Considering' Restarting Large-Scale Combat Operations Against Iran

In a bombshell interview with Axios on Wednesday, President Donald Trump revealed he is actively considering a return to large-scale combat operations against Iran. "I'm considering launching a massive attack — unprecedented in scale. I'm close to a decision. We're fully prepared for it," Trump said. He added that Iran "wants to negotiate" but is "not ready for a deal yet," claiming "they haven't learned enough of a lesson."

Trump stated that if he requested it, Israel would "join in two minutes," but added that the U.S. does not "need anyone's help" for operations against Iran. He warned that Israeli participation would have "consequences," suggesting Iran would retaliate against Israel. Earlier Wednesday, Trump posted on Truth Social that any further Houthi attacks on Saudi vessels would trigger "major military punishment" against Iran, holding Tehran accountable for its Yemeni proxies. According to Xinhua News Agency, the U.S. military has now conducted 13 consecutive nights of strikes on Iranian territory since July 11, with Iran responding with missile and drone attacks on U.S. facilities across the Middle East. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth disclosed that total war expenditures have reached approximately $37.5 billion and continue to rise by hundreds of millions per day.

🔥 Brent Crude Surges Past $100 as Houthi Red Sea Tanker Attacks Trigger Global Energy Panic

Brent crude futures breached the $100 per barrel mark in early Asian trading on July 24 for the first time since May, surging more than 7% to an intraday high of $100.70. WTI crude climbed 6% to $92.25. The sharpest single-day spike since the June ceasefire collapse was triggered by the opening of a second maritime front — Houthi militants attacking Saudi tankers in the Red Sea.

Houthi military spokesman Yahya Sarea announced that the group struck two Saudi oil tankers — the MV Encelia and the MV Layla — in the Red Sea using ballistic missiles, cruise missiles, and drones, scoring direct hits that ignited fires on both vessels. Approximately 10 ships were forced to reverse course. This marks the first confirmed armed attack since the Houthis declared a full maritime blockade against Saudi Arabia last week, shutting the Bab el-Mandeb Strait and threatening the ports of Jeddah, Jizan, and Yanbu. The Houthis warned that any vessel loading cargo at Saudi ports could be targeted.

Goldman Sachs commodities analyst Daan Struyven warned that if Strait of Hormuz disruption persists through 2027, Brent could exceed $120 per barrel in Q4 2026, with further upside risk if the Bab el-Mandeb Strait and Suez Canal also suffer sustained disruption. Global oil inventories are at multi-year lows after months of conflict, OPEC+ spare capacity is nearly exhausted, and the energy supply structure is extremely fragile. Analysts note that the "dual chokepoint" scenario puts approximately 25% of global crude supply at risk, with war risk insurance for a single tanker transit soaring from $250,000 to $10 million.

⚔️ US Completes 13th Night of Strikes; Iran Hits Qeshm Island; IRGC Declares Hormuz 'Completely Closed'

U.S. Central Command confirmed that at 5:30 p.m. ET on Wednesday, U.S. forces began "launching more strikes against Iranian military targets at the Commander in Chief's direction," marking the 13th consecutive night of operations against Iran. Targets included Iranian military operations centers, maritime capabilities, aircraft hangars, drone storage facilities, and logistics infrastructure, designed to "further degrade Iran's ability to threaten commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz."

Iran responded swiftly. Iranian state media reported that a missile struck Qeshm Island, the largest island in the Persian Gulf, located strategically in the Strait of Hormuz. Multiple explosions were reported across the island. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) subsequently declared the southern route of the Strait of Hormuz mined and "completely closed," warning that no tanker would be allowed to enter or leave without coordination with Tehran. The IRGC claimed one oil tanker struck a mine south of Qeshm and caught fire, while two others turned back.

Simultaneously, Iran launched drone attacks on U.S. facilities at Camp Doha in western Kuwait, targeting ammunition depots and logistics equipment. The IRGC Aerospace Force struck King Faisal Air Base and Prince Hassan Air Base in Jordan with missiles and drones, claiming destruction of eight MQ-9 Reaper drones. Iran also targeted Amazon's central data infrastructure in Bahrain. Jordan's armed forces confirmed intercepting 4 Iranian missiles and 4 drones. President Trump attended a dignified transfer ceremony at Dover Air Force Base for four U.S. service members killed in Iran. U.S. military death toll has now reached 18 since the war began on February 28.

🌍 Dual Energy Chokepoint Crisis: 25% of Global Oil Supply at Risk

The Middle East is facing a rare "dual blockade" crisis. The Strait of Hormuz normally carries approximately 20 million barrels of oil per day (20% of global supply), while the Red Sea's Bab el-Mandeb Strait has become Saudi Arabia's lifeline export route since Hormuz was effectively shut by Iran. Saudi Arabia has been relying on its East-West pipeline to transfer crude across the peninsula to the Red Sea port of Yanbu, which currently exports over 4.5 million barrels per day.

The Houthi blockade directly threatens this alternative route. Approximately 3 million barrels per day of Saudi crude exports to Asia via Yanbu face immediate disruption. Tankers forced to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope face at least an additional month of voyage time. Combined with the near-standstill at Hormuz — just 15 transits recorded on July 19 versus a pre-crisis baseline of 88 per day — global energy markets face a supply shock of unprecedented scope.

Market reactions have been severe. The average U.S. gasoline price has surpassed $4 per gallon, reaching $5-6 on the West Coast. Strategic fuel reserves have dropped to approximately 210 million barrels, the lowest in nearly 15 years and just 20 million barrels above the critical threshold of 190 million barrels. Analysts warn that a prolonged "dual lock" scenario would hit Asian energy importers hardest, further elevate global oil prices, force major central banks to delay rate cuts, and intensify financial market volatility. World Bank models suggest global economic growth could fall to 1.3% if the conflict continues to escalate.

🤝 Diplomacy in Parallel: 10-Day Ceasefire Proposal Hangs in the Balance

Despite the military escalation, diplomatic channels remain partially open. Mediators Pakistan and Qatar continue pushing a 10-day ceasefire proposal, though the Trump administration remains deeply divided. Some officials dismiss the proposal as "absurd," while others advocate using a cooling-off period to restart the June memorandum of understanding framework.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio stated at the ASEAN foreign ministers' meeting that the U.S. remains "willing to engage, negotiate, and settle differences with Iran," but asserted that Tehran is "not serious" about talks. Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei confirmed that diplomatic contact through mediators continues. Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi reiterated Iran's "eye-for-an-eye" defense doctrine on social media, warning that "any aggression against Iran, including attacks on our infrastructure, will be met with a powerful and decisive response." Iranian Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf went further, declaring that the Strait of Hormuz situation "will not return to pre-war conditions" — warning that if Iran's oil exports are blocked, other countries' exports will also be affected.

The World Bank has issued an economic warning: if the US-Iran conflict continues to escalate, global economic growth could fall to 1.3%. Rising U.S. gasoline prices are becoming a pivotal political issue ahead of midterm congressional elections, with Trump facing growing pressure to end the conflict. Analysts believe that "fight while talking" remains the dominant pattern of US-Iran relations, as neither side has the economic or political capacity to sustain a prolonged all-out war.
