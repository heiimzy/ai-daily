⚡ July 22 International Politics: Trump Vows to Bomb a Bridge or Power Plant for Every Ship Strike, Iran Warns of 'Powerful Attack', Ukraine's New General Pledges Counteroffensive

The international political landscape continued to shift dramatically on Wednesday, July 22, 2026. U.S. President Donald Trump issued his most explicit escalation threat yet on Truth Social — for every commercial vessel Iran strikes in the Strait of Hormuz, the U.S. will destroy an Iranian bridge or power plant, including those in or near Tehran. Iran's military command warned that any strike on the "Pickaxe Mountain" underground nuclear facility would be treated as an expansion of war, triggering a "powerful assault" on all U.S. and allied interests. In Ukraine, newly appointed Commander-in-Chief Mykhailo Drapatyi pledged on his first day to intensify counteroffensive operations. The Philippines and China held their first face-to-face foreign ministerial talks in two years on the sidelines of the ASEAN meeting in Manila. Here is the full report.

1. Trump's Ultimatum: One Ship Struck, One Bridge or Power Plant Destroyed

President Donald Trump posted a statement on his Truth Social platform Wednesday that sent shockwaves through the international community, announcing a new "tit-for-tat" escalation policy:

"From this point forward, any time the Islamic Republic of Iran shoots at a ship in the Strait of Hormuz, whether it be by Missile, Rocket, Drone, or any other device or weapon, the United States will bomb and destroy ONE BRIDGE OR POWER PLANT, including those located next to, or in, the Capital City of Tehran."

The verbal escalation marks a dangerous new phase in the U.S.-Iran conflict — expanding from military targets to explicit threats against civilian infrastructure. Multiple international law experts warned that deliberately attacking civilian bridges and power plants could constitute war crimes under the Geneva Conventions. Paul Musgrave, professor at Georgetown University in Qatar, said Washington is "burning diplomatic bridges" and making the situation even more intractable.

Trump had earlier laid out the escalation roadmap in a Fox News interview Tuesday night: "We're going to hit them very hard tonight. We're going to hit them very hard tomorrow night. We're going to hit them very hard the night after, and then next week it gets really bad for them because next week comes the power plants. Next week comes the bridges." He added that he would save energy targets for last.

Trump also confirmed he would attend a "dignified transfer" ceremony Wednesday at Dover Air Force Base in Delaware for four U.S. service members killed in the Iran war. U.S. military fatalities have reached 18 since the war began in late February, with more than 450 personnel wounded.

2. Iran Fires Back: 'Powerful Attack' Against All U.S. Targets If Nuclear Sites Hit

Iran's Khatam Al-Anbiya Central Headquarters issued a forceful statement Wednesday in response to Trump's threats. The military command warned that if the U.S. strikes Iran's nuclear facilities or sensitive centers — especially the "Pickaxe Mountain" (Kuh-e Kolang) underground nuclear complex south of Natanz — Iran will treat it as "an expansion of war in the region" and launch a "powerful assault" covering all interests of America, its allies, and supporters.

"America, its allies, and the supporters of that rogue and evil country will be targeted by a powerful assault from Iran's armed forces," the statement said.

"Pickaxe Mountain" is a fortified underground facility near Iran's Natanz nuclear enrichment site. Western intelligence agencies suspect Iran is building an undeclared uranium enrichment facility there. Israeli sources believe Iran moved advanced centrifuges and some highly enriched uranium stockpiles to this facility after the 12-day Israel-Iran conflict in June 2025. Trump told reporters Tuesday at the White House: "We'll be hitting that area pretty soon, and very heavily."

Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei responded on X, saying the U.S. threats against Iran's nuclear facilities constitute "a flagrant violation of the core principles of the UN Charter, international law, and the relevant resolutions of the IAEA." The Iranian people, he said, "stand resolute and united, ready to confront with full strength any act of hostility or violation of sovereignty."

3. War Bill Hits $37.5 Billion as Hegseth Testifies Before Angry Senate Panel

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth testified before the Senate Appropriations Committee Tuesday, revealing that the total cost of the Iran war has reached $37.5 billion — up sharply from the $29 billion estimate in mid-May. Hegseth is seeking approximately $67 billion in supplemental Pentagon funding.

The hearing was repeatedly interrupted by anti-war protesters who shouted that Hegseth and other officials had "blood on their hands" and demanded an immediate end to the "illegal war against Iran." Nearly 100 U.S. military personnel have been wounded since July 7 alone. Democratic senators harshly criticized Hegseth as a "failed leader."

U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) confirmed it completed its 11th consecutive night of strikes against military targets across Iran. Unlike the previous 10 nights, this round of strikes expanded to cover the entire country — from Tabriz in the northwest to Bushehr in the south, from Kermanshah in the west to Chabahar in the southeast. Iranian state media reported that air defense systems opened fire simultaneously in eastern, western, and northeastern Tehran — the first time the capital's full air defense network was activated since the conflict began.

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) announced phases 21 and 22 of "Operation Thunderbolt": suicide drone strikes against ammunition depots and logistics facilities at Camp Doha in Kuwait, accommodation buildings at Al Azraq Air Base in Jordan, and equipment warehouses at Sheikh Isa Air Base in Bahrain.

4. Ukraine Shakeup: New Commander-in-Chief Drapatyi Vows to Step Up Counteroffensives

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky dismissed Commander-in-Chief General Oleksandr Syrskyi late Tuesday and appointed 43-year-old Major General Mykhailo Drapatyi as his replacement — the biggest shakeup in Ukraine's military high command since Russia's full-scale invasion began in 2022.

In his first statement on Wednesday, Drapatyi said the president had set clear objectives: "to continue and intensify offensive operations in all domains, to plan new operations behind enemy lines, to develop our military and its technologies, and to build up the capabilities of the Armed Forces of Ukraine." He emphasized that Ukraine would continue conducting "deep strikes" against targets deep inside Russia.

Before his departure, Syrskyi said Ukrainian forces had recaptured 700 square kilometers of territory from Russian forces this year. In a Telegram statement, he said: "I am handing over to my successor an army that is not only holding the defense but is also on the offensive."

The leadership change was triggered by Zelensky's decision last week to dismiss the popular Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov, the 35-year-old credited with modernizing Ukraine's drone warfare capabilities. The firing sparked nationwide protests. Zelensky has since offered Fedorov a "respected government position" to unify the country's technology sector, though it remains unclear whether Fedorov accepted.

Russian military bloggers greeted Ukraine's internal turmoil with glee, predicting the leadership infighting would weaken Ukraine's combat effectiveness. Russia launched a massive overnight attack against Ukraine on July 22, firing 1 Iskander-M ballistic missile, 3 Kh-59/69 cruise missiles, and 216 attack drones. Ukrainian air defense forces shot down 204 drones and 3 missiles, but 1 ballistic missile and 12 drones struck 12 locations. Russia's defense ministry claimed its forces captured two settlements in Ukraine's Kharkiv and Zaporizhzhia regions.

Ukraine, meanwhile, expanded its strikes deep inside Russian territory: drones hit warehouses belonging to Russia's largest online retailer Wildberries in Krasnodar and Nevinnomyssk, injuring 10 people. Ukrainian forces also struck 13 Russian vessels in the Black Sea and Sea of Azov. Zelensky said Ukraine had targeted "logistics hubs" in Russia's Krasnodar and Stavropol regions that supply the Russian military with drone components and navigation equipment.

5. Philippines vs. China: Diplomatic Showdown at ASEAN Over Ayungin Shoal Clash

On the sidelines of the ASEAN Foreign Ministers' Meeting in Manila, Philippine Foreign Secretary Maria Theresa Lazaro held her first bilateral talks with Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi in two years. Lazaro lodged a "strong protest" over what Manila called China Coast Guard violence at Ayungin (Second Thomas) Shoal.

On July 20, Philippine Navy personnel conducting a rotation and resupply mission to the grounded BRP Sierra Madre warship clashed with Chinese Coast Guard vessels. The Philippine military said a sailor was struck in the head with a baton, sustaining a 7-8 cm laceration. The Philippine Coast Guard then conducted a medical evacuation mission, which it claimed was obstructed by a Chinese Coast Guard rigid-hull inflatable boat.

Lazaro also condemned a July 10 China Daily video that depicted Filipinos as monkeys, calling it "deeply offensive, distressing and unacceptable." Wang Yi reiterated China's position on the South China Sea, while Chinese Ambassador Jing Quan separately argued that the Philippine personnel had violated a 2024 provisional agreement by conducting "unauthorized construction work" on the Sierra Madre.

Despite the deep disagreements, both sides agreed to maintain open communication channels, strengthen confidence-building measures, and push forward negotiations on the ASEAN-China Code of Conduct (COC) in the South China Sea, with the goal of concluding it this year. The Philippines, as this year's ASEAN chair, has made the legally binding COC its top priority.

U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio also addressed the ASEAN meeting, warning that allowing Iran to control the Strait of Hormuz and collect tolls would set a "dangerous precedent." He said Washington remains open to diplomacy but believes Tehran is "not serious" about negotiations.

6. EASA Adds Jordan to No-Fly List as Regional Security Crisis Spreads

The European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA) issued a warning Wednesday advising airlines to avoid Jordanian airspace, adding Jordan to its existing no-fly recommendations covering Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, the UAE, Iran, Iraq, and Lebanon. EASA stated: "Since the middle of July 2026, the security situation has deteriorated rapidly, with a sustained increase in kinetic military activity affecting Jordanian airspace."

Jordan's military announced it had intercepted 6 Iranian missiles on Wednesday, with two falling in remote, uninhabited areas. The Israeli military detected an attack on Aqaba — the Red Sea resort adjacent to Eilat — but did not intervene as the missile did not threaten Israeli territory.

World Bank Chief Economist Indermit Gill warned Wednesday that if U.S.-Iran hostilities last six months or more, global headline inflation could reach 4.5% and global growth could fall to as low as 1.3%. Oil prices continued to climb in Asian trading, with Brent crude breaking above $94 per barrel as the risk of a global "stagflation" scenario intensified.
