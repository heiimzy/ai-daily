🌍 July 24 International Politics Roundup: Trump 'Seriously Considering' Resuming Large-Scale Iran Strikes, Houthis Blockade Bab el-Mandeb, ASEAN Summit Sees US-Russia Diplomatic Clash

1. Trump Says He Is 'Seriously Considering' Resuming Large-Scale Military Operations Against Iran

President Donald Trump said on Thursday he is "seriously considering" launching a new wave of large-scale combat operations against Iran, according to an interview with Axios news site reported by Xinhua News Agency from Washington on July 23.

"I'm thinking about launching a massive strike — unprecedented in scale. I'm close to making a decision. We are fully prepared for it," Trump said, adding that Iran "wants to negotiate" but is not ready to reach a deal yet because "they haven't been taught a harsh enough lesson."

Trump further warned that if Yemen's Houthi rebels attack Saudi vessels again, the United States would hold Iran responsible and impose "major military punishment" on both Iran and the Houthis. He also hinted that Israel would join "within two minutes" if requested, but emphasized that the U.S. does not need assistance to carry out the strikes.

Since the interim ceasefire collapsed on July 11, U.S. forces have conducted 12 consecutive nights of airstrikes on Iranian territory. Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth told the Senate that the war has cost the U.S. $37.5 billion so far, with 18 U.S. service members killed and over 450 wounded. Iran's Health Ministry reports 53 civilian deaths and 592 injured since late last month.

2. Houthi Blockade of Bab el-Mandeb: Global Oil Routes Face a 'Two-Strait' Crisis

As the U.S. and Iran remain locked in a standoff over the Strait of Hormuz, Iran-backed Houthi rebels in Yemen have opened a new front in the Red Sea. On July 23, the Houthis announced they had struck two Saudi oil tankers — the Encelia and the Layla — using drones, ballistic missiles, and cruise missiles in the Bab el-Mandeb Strait.

According to Reuters, the Encelia sent a distress signal late Tuesday near the Saudi port of Jizan after being hit by a missile on its starboard side, causing a fire in the bow. The Houthis threatened to ban all vessels from loading or discharging cargo at Saudi ports, effectively imposing a full maritime blockade on the kingdom.

The move triggered a cascade of panic in international shipping. Data shows that five oil tankers changed course in the Red Sea on Wednesday, with two signaling the Suez Canal as their new destination. On Tuesday, three tankers loaded with Saudi crude bound for China and India also made U-turns. The Houthi blockade threatens to cut off approximately 7% of global oil supply.

"This means the U.S. military will have to split its already strained regional resources between two simultaneously active fronts," warned Jason Campbell, a former Pentagon official now at the Middle East Institute.

Former Deputy Assistant Secretary of Defense Michael Mulroy added that the Houthis have "repeatedly demonstrated their capability and willingness to disrupt Red Sea and Bab el-Mandeb shipping." With both the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed and Bab el-Mandeb now under threat, the Middle East faces an unprecedented simultaneous closure of both oil export chokepoints.

Brent crude surged past $93 per barrel on Wednesday, briefly touching $95 — a six-week high. U.S. gasoline prices climbed back above $4 per gallon for the first time in months.

3. 59th ASEAN Foreign Ministers' Meeting Concludes: US-Russia Talks in Manila

The 59th ASEAN Foreign Ministers' Meeting (AMM-59) was held July 20-24 at the Philippine International Convention Center (PICC) in Manila, hosted by ASEAN Chair Philippines under the theme "Unity for a Shared Future." The meeting drew top diplomats from China, the U.S., Russia, Japan, South Korea, India, Australia, and Canada.

US-Russia Talks: Lavrov Warns Rubio on Ukraine

On July 23, U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio and Russian Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov held their first face-to-face meeting since the UN General Assembly in New York in September 2025. According to AFP, Lavrov warned that continued arms supplies to Ukraine were "unacceptable" and condemned what he called "destabilizing policies" by European nations. The U.S. State Department said the two "discussed U.S.-Russia relations and the need to end the war in Ukraine."

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy revealed on the same day that he had spoken with Trump envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner about "reviving diplomacy and bringing peace closer." However, analysts note that with Washington's strategic focus shifting to the Iran war, Ukraine peace talks have stalled.

US-China Talks: Paving the Way for September Summit

On July 22, Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi and Secretary Rubio held a 90-minute closed-door meeting. Both sides agreed to implement the consensus reached by their heads of state and prepare for the next phase of high-level exchanges. Media reports widely interpreted the meeting as laying the groundwork for Chinese President Xi Jinping's expected visit to the U.S. in September.

Rubio told reporters afterward that the two discussed Taiwan, trade differences, and the China-U.S. Investment Council. "We need to clarify areas of cooperation for the September summit," he said. "We had a full exchange on that."

South China Sea and Multilateral Dynamics

Divergences over the South China Sea resurfaced at the meeting. The U.S., joined by the Quad allies (Australia, Japan, India), expressed "concern" over the situation, while China insisted on maintaining dominance in the South China Sea and opposed the involvement of external forces. ASEAN's joint statement reaffirmed the importance of international law and peaceful dispute resolution.

The draft ASEAN joint statement also includes explicit language on the "right of passage through straits" — a direct response to Iran's blockade of the Strait of Hormuz. The ASEAN+3 (China, Japan, South Korea) foreign ministers' meeting focused on regional energy security, critical mineral supply chains, and combating cross-border cyber fraud.

Notably, Lavrov stated at the meeting that Russia would no longer support calls for a nuclear-free Korean Peninsula given what he called "joint pressure" on North Korea. This comes after North Korean leader Kim Jong-un claimed his navy would be equipped with nuclear weapons and larger warships, adding further uncertainty to Northeast Asian security.

4. Outlook: Can Diplomacy Still Prevail?

Despite escalating rhetoric on both sides, diplomatic channels remain technically open. Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesman Baghaei confirmed that Tehran is communicating with Washington through mediators. Iran's Interior Minister Eskandar Momeni recently visited Pakistan to urge Islamabad to continue mediation efforts. Secretary Rubio also stated at the ASEAN meeting that Washington remains "willing to resolve the issue through diplomatic negotiations."

But Trump's stance is growing increasingly hardline — from threatening "one bridge per ship" to hinting at strikes on Iran's underground nuclear facility "Pickaxe Mountain," and now "seriously considering" a massive new campaign. With U.S. forces stretched thin and war costs reaching $37.5 billion, internal pressure may also limit further escalation.

The Middle East has descended into a two-front quagmire. Both the Strait of Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb are in crisis, and global energy markets are on edge. As the ASEAN foreign ministers' meeting concludes on July 24, the chessboard of great-power geopolitics is far from settled.
