⚡ July 12 Chip Sector Roundup: SK Hynix's Historic ADR Debut Surges 12.8%, AMD Confirms Zen 6 Venice for July 22, Nvidia Reclaims $5T

SK Hynix Lists on Nasdaq in Largest ADR in History, Surges 12.8%

South Korean memory giant SK Hynix began trading on the Nasdaq on July 10 under ticker SKHY, completing a $28-29 billion ADR offering priced at $149-166 per share — surpassing Alibaba's 2014 record of $21.8 billion to become the largest ADR listing in history. The stock surged 12.8% on debut day, pushing its market cap well past $100 billion.

The landmark IPO is powered by AI-era demand for High Bandwidth Memory (HBM). SK Hynix commands roughly 60% of the global HBM market and serves as Nvidia's primary memory supplier for AI GPUs. The company posted Q1 2026 revenue of $35.55 billion with an eye-popping 72% operating margin — numbers that would have seemed like science fiction for a memory company just three years ago.

However, on the very same day as the listing, CEO Kwak Noh-jung issued a stark warning: the global chip industry could face its worst supply crisis in history by 2027. Customer demand is growing at a breakneck pace while production capacity remains severely constrained. Despite aggressive expansion efforts, memory chip demand is projected to far exceed SK Hynix's production capabilities well into 2030.

To address supply chain challenges, SK Hynix is evaluating overseas fab locations in the US, Japan, and Southeast Asia. It is investing $4 billion in a chip packaging facility in Indiana and $10 billion to develop an AI solutions business there.

AMD Confirms Zen 6 EPYC Venice for July 22-23: 256 Cores on 2nm

AMD CTO Mark Papermaster has officially confirmed that the "Venice" Zen 6 EPYC server CPU will launch at the "Advancing AI 2026" event on July 22-23. This is AMD's first high-performance computing processor built on TSMC's 2nm process — the first HPC chip to reach production on this node.

The flagship Venice configuration packs up to 256 Zen 6 cores, a 33% increase over the current 192-core EPYC Turin (Zen 5). AMD claims over 70% higher performance and efficiency versus the Zen 5 predecessor. On the platform side, Venice moves to the new SP7 socket with 16-channel memory delivering up to 1.6 TB/s of bandwidth and adopts PCIe Gen 6 for improved CPU-to-GPU communication critical for AI accelerator workloads.

The CPUs will be paired with new AMD Instinct MI455 GPUs in the Helios rack-scale system — AMD's first direct challenge to Nvidia's DGX architecture. Analysts note that both Meta and OpenAI have each committed to 6-gigawatt Instinct GPU deployments, providing multi-year revenue visibility.

"Every enterprise conversation I'm having now comes up. Enterprises have decades of running x86. They're not going to move that install base." — Mark Papermaster, AMD CTO

Nvidia Reclaims $5 Trillion: Chipmakers Win the Quarter

Nvidia shares rose 2.3% on July 10, pushing its market capitalization back above the $5 trillion mark. The week capped a period in which semiconductor names broadly outperformed the AI software companies they supply. Meta surged over 7% on its compute expansion memo. The quarter's scoreboard is unambiguous: chipmakers won it.

Nvidia officially unveiled the Rubin platform on July 8, featuring six new GPU and system designs shipping to production in H2 2026. The flagship GB200 Grace Blackwell Ultra packs 301 billion transistors with 2.3kW per GPU, and the NVL72 liquid-cooled pod hits 230kW total rack power. AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, Oracle Cloud, CoreWeave, Lambda Labs, and others have all committed to Rubin instances — the first time Nvidia has achieved simultaneous hyperscaler deployment.

Industry analysts note that the binding constraint has shifted from GPU supply to power grid capacity. Rubin's 600kW racks can only be supported by approximately 400 data centers globally. The PJM grid has issued emergency curtailment notices for AI data center loads, Virginia enacted a data center consumption tax, and Texas is reopening coal plants specifically to feed AI demand.

The Big Picture: AI-Driven Structural Memory Shortage

Micron raised its US manufacturing commitment from $200B to $250B on July 9, targeting 40% of DRAM output on American soil by 2035, alongside a 10-year wafer supply deal with GlobalWafers. Apple has already passed rising memory costs through to Mac, iPad, and Vision Pro pricing — the shortage has moved from the AI data center to the consumer shopping cart.

Samsung and SK Hynix have jointly committed $880 billion to capacity expansion, but new capacity takes 3-5 years to come online meaningfully. Bank of America estimates global cloud giants will spend $1.15 trillion next year alone.

Beneath it all lies a fundamental shift in AI inference economics. A single 600kW Rubin rack costs $1.44/hour in electricity alone (at $0.12/kWh), plus colocation and liquid cooling amortization. To break even, that rack needs to generate $50,000-75,000 in monthly inference revenue — a scale only achievable by consumer-facing products like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini with global reach. This creates a new form of competitive moat where only the largest AI labs can afford to run frontier inference hardware at sustainable margins.
