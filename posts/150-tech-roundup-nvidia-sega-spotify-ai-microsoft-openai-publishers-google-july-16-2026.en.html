1. Nvidia & Sega: 30 Years Later, a New Chapter with RTX Spark

Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang returned to Tokyo's Akihabara district on Wednesday for an emotional reunion with former Sega president Shoichiro Irimajiri — the executive who saved Nvidia from bankruptcy with a $5 million investment three decades ago. Together, they announced a new partnership: Sega's iconic game franchises are coming to Nvidia's RTX Spark platform.

"If not for what Sega did for Nvidia and what Mr. Irimajiri did for Nvidia, Nvidia would not be here today," Huang said at the event, held at what was once the Sega Akihabara Arcade (now GiGO Akihabara 3). "To imagine that in 1995, Nvidia was nearly out of business, that we had chosen exactly the wrong technology, and that we will be here today, the largest company in the world, is unimaginable."

"If not for what Sega did for Nvidia and what Mr. Irimajiri did for Nvidia, Nvidia would not be here today." — Jensen Huang, Nvidia CEO

The first title announced is Virtua Fighter Crossroads, scheduled for a 2027 release with a writing team that includes Metal Gear Solid voice actor David Hayter and staff from the Persona series. Sega confirmed that additional "legendary franchises" will follow, with speculation pointing to Yakuza, Persona, Total War: Warhammer 40,000, and Alien: Isolation 2.

The RTX Spark Platform

RTX Spark, unveiled at Computex 2026, is Nvidia's first Arm-based superchip for AI PCs. It combines a 20-core Grace CPU with a Blackwell RTX GPU via NVLink-C2C, delivering up to 1 petaflop of compute performance. The platform can run 120-billion-parameter LLMs locally, handle 12K video editing, render 90GB 3D scenes, and deliver triple-A gaming at 1440p/100fps with full ray tracing and DLSS support. RTX Spark PCs are expected to launch in Fall 2026.

Sega sold its Nvidia stake for approximately $300 million after the company went public — a stake that would be worth over $1 trillion today if held.

2. Spotify Launches "Talk to Spotify" AI Chatbot for Premium Users

Spotify is testing a new conversational AI feature called "Talk to Spotify" for Premium subscribers, transforming the world's largest audio streaming platform into an AI chatbot.

According to The Verge, the feature goes well beyond simple music recommendations and trivia. It taps into users' personal listening data — playlists, favorite artists, repeat listens, and listening history — enabling queries like "When did I first hear this song?" or "What genres have I been into lately?" — effectively giving users access to their personal Wrapped data year-round.

Conversational Music Discovery

Unlike Spotify's earlier "Prompted Playlist" feature, the new AI assistant supports natural back-and-forth conversation. Users can ask to "play some songs I haven't heard before," then refine with "make it more upbeat" or "only rock." The assistant understands context and adjusts playback in real-time. It can also answer general knowledge questions about songs, authors of audiobooks, or podcast guest appearances across shows.

Amazon Music introduced a similar Alexa Plus integration last year. Spotify's advantage lies in its massive user data — with over 500 million monthly active users and the world's largest music consumption behavior dataset, its AI can deliver personalization that competitors struggle to match.

3. Microsoft Trains Sales Teams to Undercut OpenAI and Anthropic

Bloomberg reported on Wednesday that Microsoft executives explicitly instructed the company's sales force to negatively compare AI products from OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic during an internal strategy session on Tuesday.

Executive Vice President Jay Parikh told hundreds of salespeople: "Everyone else is selling parts — we're selling the full end-to-end system. That's the story that we all need to get out there and tell in FY27." Another EVP, Jacob Andreou, delivered a presentation comparing Copilot directly to Anthropic's Claude, claiming that Claude was "slower and less accurate, and lacked the proper security integrations" within Microsoft's Office apps.

From Close Partners to Rivals

The shift marks a fundamental change in the Microsoft-OpenAI relationship. Microsoft invested over $13 billion in OpenAI and was its exclusive cloud provider, enjoying exclusive API access. But the companies amended their partnership in April 2026, dropping the exclusivity clause and allowing OpenAI to sell to Microsoft's competitors.

Earlier this month, reports emerged that Microsoft has been replacing OpenAI and Anthropic models in flagship apps like Word and Excel with its own in-house models as a cost-cutting measure. The new sales strategy appears aimed at reassuring investors who have questioned Microsoft's massive AI infrastructure spending.

4. Three Major Publishers Sue Google Over Gemini Training Data

Hachette, Cengage, and Elsevier — three of the world's largest educational publishers — joined forces with author Scott Turow to file a lawsuit against Google, accusing the tech giant of using millions of copyrighted books to train its Gemini AI models without authorization or compensation.

According to The Guardian, the lawsuit alleges that Google repurposed books provided for limited services like Google Books, Google Play Books, and Google Scholar — where they were used only for searchable snippets or ebook sales — and copied them for commercial AI training. The filing claims Google internally flagged the legal risks, acknowledging it could face "$10B-$100Bs in potential fines," but proceeded anyway.

"Desperate to maintain its online dominance, Google abandoned its early motto of 'Don't be evil' and engaged in one of the most prolific infringements of copyrighted materials in history." — Publisher lawsuit filing

The lawsuit cites specific examples: Gemini can generate "a 100-page novel set in a quiet town filled with secrets" in 20 minutes for $0.39, arguing that "no publisher or author can compete with that." The complaint names specific works allegedly used without permission, including novels by NK Jemisin and Lemony Snicket.

The Expanding AI Copyright Battlefield

This lawsuit is the latest front in an escalating legal war over AI training data. The New York Times has sued OpenAI and Microsoft, and Getty Images has sued Stability AI. As generative AI capabilities continue to advance, the question of whether using copyrighted material for training constitutes fair use or infringement is shaping up to be the defining legal question for the AI industry.

The publishers emphasize they are not seeking to block AI development — rather, they want a fair compensation framework where AI companies pay licensing fees for copyrighted training data, just as any other industry would.
