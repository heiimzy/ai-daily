IAEA Confirms Investigation into Darkhovin Nuclear Plant Strike

The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) issued a statement on July 19 confirming it is investigating reports of an overnight US airstrike on the construction site of the planned Darkhovin nuclear power plant in Iran's southwestern Khuzestan province. The agency said the facility "is in the very early stages of construction and contained no nuclear material when last visited by the IAEA," and the reported attack "is not believed to pose any radiological risk."

However, IAEA Director General Rafael Grossi used the occasion to reiterate a call for "military restraint in the vicinity of all nuclear-related sites." This marks the first time since the US-Israel coalition launched military operations against Iran in late February that a nuclear-related construction site has been directly caught up in targeting. Iran's Atomic Energy Organization (AEOI) subsequently condemned the US "terrorist act," calling the bombing of the Darkhovin site "an aggressive and barbaric act in violation of international law."

"The facility is in the very early stages of construction and contained no nuclear material when last visited by the IAEA. The reported attack is not believed to pose any radiological risk… but we reiterate our call for military restraint in the vicinity of all nuclear-related sites." — IAEA Director General Rafael Grossi

The Darkhovin plant, located on the Karun River, was originally planned to house two 1,000-megawatt nuclear reactors as part of Iran's strategy to reduce dependence on oil and gas and expand its nuclear power share. While the site contained no nuclear fuel, the precedent of military strikes on nuclear facilities — even those under construction — constitutes a serious strategic escalation. Analysts note that regardless of whether nuclear material was present, targeting a nuclear plant construction site signals dangerously unlimited escalation in targeting scope.

Iran Warns UAE: Evacuate Dubai and Abu Dhabi Airports Immediately

The Middle East saw a dangerous new escalation vector emerge on Sunday (July 19). Iran's semiofficial Fars News Agency — closely affiliated with the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) — quoted a security official as issuing a stark warning: if the United States continues attacking Iranian civilian infrastructure, the UAE must "immediately evacuate Dubai International Airport, Abu Dhabi International Airport, and the ports of Fujairah and Jebel Ali to protect civilian lives during Iran's retaliatory strikes."

This warning marks the first explicit extension of Iran's potential strike range to core UAE civilian infrastructure. Dubai International Airport is one of the world's busiest international passenger hubs. Jebel Ali Port is the Middle East's largest container port, and the Port of Fujairah is a critical global oil storage and bunkering hub. Should these facilities become targets, the impact on global aviation, supply chains, and energy trade would be catastrophic.

As of press time, neither US nor UAE authorities had officially responded to the warning, and all UAE airports and ports were operating normally. However, prediction markets reacted swiftly — Polymarket data shows the probability of Iran taking military action against a Gulf state by July 22 has been priced at 53.5%, a significant increase.

The UAE Foreign Ministry had previously issued a statement calling "for exercising the utmost restraint in order to avoid dangerous repercussions" and emphasizing that "the targeting of civilian infrastructure and civilian facilities in the region … constitute a flagrant and grave violation of international law." The UAE has been repeatedly targeted during the conflict — on February 28, Iran struck Al Dhafra Air Base near Abu Dhabi with ballistic missiles and drones, and in March, Dubai International Airport was indefinitely closed after drone interception debris littered runways.

Iran Warns of 'Full-Scale Offensive': No Political Border Safe

Major General Mohsen Rezaei, a military adviser to Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei and former IRGC commander, issued an even more severe deterrent signal on state television. Rezaei warned that if US attacks continue for another two or three days, "Iran will no longer limit itself to retaliatory, like-for-like responses… and will enter the phase of full-scale offensive operations — no political border will be safe."

Rezaei argued that Washington's attritional strategy of "war and negotiation" had reached a dead end and that the intensity of Iranian strikes would increase in the coming days. "Until now, we have not focused on expanding the war, nor on launching an invasion. The goal was deterrence, to end the conflict. We seized Hormuz to end the conflict. But this phase is ending." He emphasized that Iran will not abandon seeking revenge for the killing of its supreme leader — "the country's future security and stability depends on carrying it out."

Separately, Major General Ali Abdullahi, commander of Iran's Khatam al-Anbiya Central Headquarters, warned that Iran's armed forces would deliver a "decisive and devastating response" to any US "aggression or barbarism," accusing Washington of trying to create division inside Iran and pledging to "impose heavier costs on the US than in previous rounds."

The IRGC also announced it had intercepted and destroyed a US MQ-9 Reaper drone over the city of Ahvaz in Khuzestan province. Iran claims to have shot down 30 MQ-9 drones since the conflict began, including four in the past eight days.

US Completes Eighth Consecutive Night of Strikes — Reduced Intensity

US Central Command (CENTCOM) announced it had completed its eighth wave of strikes against Iran at 11:30 p.m. ET on Saturday (11:30 a.m. Beijing time Sunday), a campaign lasting approximately 5.5 hours. The strikes targeted "Iranian military coastal surveillance and air defense facilities, maritime capabilities, and missile and drone storage sites," and for the first time explicitly included IRGC units responsible for the July 17 attack on US forces in Jordan.

Compared to the previous three nights, this round was notably less intense and did not include civilian infrastructure targets. Analysts suggest the Trump administration adjusted its targeting strategy under pressure from Gulf allies — after US strikes on Iranian bridges and desalination plants triggered Iranian proportional retaliation against similar Kuwaiti facilities, prompting strong backlash from Gulf states.

Iranian Missiles Target Jordan's Aqaba; US Intercepts, Fragments Land Near Israel

Iran launched multiple ballistic missiles toward Jordan's southern city of Aqaba on Sunday. US air defense systems intercepted three of the missiles, while a fourth landed in an open area. The Israeli military activated Iron Dome interceptors over Eilat to shoot down fragments that could threaten Israeli territory. Air raid sirens sounded in Jordan's capital Amman. This marks the fourth Iranian attack on US-related facilities or targets in Jordan within five days.

Trump on Iran Suspending Ceasefire: 'I Couldn't Care Less'

President Donald Trump delivered a characteristically blunt response when informed that Iran had announced it was no longer abiding by the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding signed last month. "I couldn't care less," Trump told NewsNation. He expressed condolences for the two service members killed in Jordan, calling their deaths "a very sad thing," and reiterated his pledge that the US is "never allowing Iran to have a nuclear weapon."

Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth posted on X: "Godspeed, heroes. Their sacrifice only stiffens our resolve." The US State Department issued a worldwide travel alert, citing "elevated tensions in the Middle East with the potential for unforeseen escalation," warning that flight cancellations and periodic airspace closures could disrupt travel.

Iranian Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi had earlier stated on state television: "The United States has violated and suspended all of its commitments under the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding. We have also suspended our commitments; we are not implementing them." Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei, in a written statement, said US actions have shown that Trump's signature is "utterly worthless and devoid of credibility."

Situation Overview: Eight Days from Ceasefire to Full Confrontation

The escalation trajectory over the past week makes clear that the fragile June ceasefire between the US and Iran has completely collapsed:

  • July 11: Iran intercepts commercial vessels in the Strait of Hormuz; US launches seventh night of strikes, expanding target scope to bridges and power facilities
  • July 17: Iranian ballistic missiles and drones attack Jordan's Muwaffaq Salti Air Base; 2 US service members killed, 1 missing
  • July 18: US announces eighth consecutive night of strikes; Iran retaliates against Kuwaiti desalination plants and oil facilities; Kuwait residents begin stockpiling bottled water and canned food
  • July 19: IAEA confirms Darkhovin nuclear plant strike investigation; Iran warns UAE to evacuate Dubai and Abu Dhabi airports; Iranian leadership threatens full-scale offensive

In oil markets, Brent crude remains near $85/barrel, up approximately 16% for the week. Traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has fallen to a three-week low — the strategic waterway that carries approximately one-fifth of global oil consumption is now effectively under blockade. The US has reimposed a naval blockade on Iranian ports, while Iran warns that any vessel "influenced" by the US will "certainly face accidents."

UN Secretary-General António Guterres expressed concern through his spokesperson about the escalation, particularly "the targeting of civilian infrastructure in Iran and the region." The European Union and Gulf states issued a joint statement calling on Iran to immediately and unconditionally halt all attacks and interference with maritime navigation. Qatar's Emir and Iraq's Prime Minister met in Doha, stressing the importance of dialogue and diplomacy. But with both sides accelerating their military tempo, the diplomatic window is rapidly closing.
