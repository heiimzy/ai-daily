⚡ July 17 International Politics: US Expands Airstrikes to Bridges in Iran, Ukraine Defense Minister Firing Sparks Nationwide Protests

1. US Launches Sixth Night of Strikes on Iran, Bridges Hit for First Time

The United States military launched a sixth consecutive night of airstrikes against Iran on July 16, expanding its target set to include bridges for the first time. The Associated Press reported that US airstrikes hit bridges in Iran's southern Hormozgan province early Friday, killing at least seven people. This marks the first implementation of President Donald Trump's threat to strike Iranian infrastructure.

US Central Command (CENTCOM) said the latest wave of strikes began at 6 p.m. ET Thursday (2200 GMT), targeting Iranian command centers, air defense sites, missile and drone capabilities, and coastal surveillance facilities "to further degrade Iran's ability to threaten innocent mariners" in the Strait of Hormuz.

Strikes Expand Near Tehran

Unlike previous strikes concentrated in southern coastal areas, this round reached areas around Iran's capital, Tehran, for the first time. Iranian state media reported that missiles struck Semnan province — home to Iran's ballistic missile production and space program — as well as Hamedan, Hormozgan, Khuzestan, Lorestan, Markazi, and Sistan-Baluchestan provinces. Air defense systems were activated in Tehran and the nearby city of Pakdasht.

Iran's Fars news agency reported that US strikes on the Kehvarstan Bridge and a residential neighborhood in Bandar Abbas killed two people and wounded eight. A railway junction station in Bandar Abbas was also hit, injuring two others. Missiles struck air and naval bases in Bushehr province as well.

Hellfire Missile Disables Oil Tanker

A US aircraft fired Hellfire missiles into the smokestack of the Curacao-flagged VLCC Belma, an unladen supertanker heading toward Iran's main oil export terminal at Kharg Island. CENTCOM said the vessel had "ignored multiple warnings" and was no longer transiting to Iran. This marks the first forcible interdiction since the US reinstated its naval blockade of Iranian ports.

Maritime intelligence firm Kpler reported that only 13 commercial vessels transited the Strait of Hormuz on July 15, down sharply from a peak of 63 on June 25 during the brief truce period. The vast majority chose the northern route near Iran's coast; only one ship used the US-escorted southern route.

2. Iran Retaliates: Strikes US Bases, Threatens Red Sea Closure

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched a broad retaliatory response. The IRGC's aerospace force stated it fired ballistic missiles and drones at US military facilities in Jordan, Kuwait, and Bahrain.

Iranian military spokesman Brigadier General Mohammad Akraminia said Iran had struck the US base at Azraq in Jordan — a facility recently upgraded into a regional headquarters that Iran claims was used to launch attacks on its territory. Kuwait reported intercepting multiple "hostile drones" from Iran, while air raid sirens sounded in Bahrain.

"Hormuz Is Iran's Inviolable Red Line"

"The Americans thought that by attacking some of our bases on the southern coasts, they could take control of this strategic strait," General Akraminia said. "Iran can exert control over the Strait of Hormuz from every single point of its territory." He warned that if Trump follows through on his threat to hit Iranian power plants and bridges, Iran's armed forces would strike "all remaining infrastructure" across the region with a response "more severe, wider in scope, and more destructive."

Iran Tells Houthis to Close Bab el-Mandeb

Reuters reported on July 16, citing three sources, that Iran has instructed Yemen's Houthi movement to prepare to close the Bab el-Mandeb Strait at the mouth of the Red Sea if the US strikes Iranian power infrastructure. A source close to the Houthis said the group had deployed missiles and drones in the highlands overlooking Hodeidah and the Gulf of Aden, awaiting the order to begin.

If implemented, both of the Middle East's main oil export routes — the Strait of Hormuz and the Bab el-Mandeb — would be disrupted simultaneously. Saudi Arabia currently routes approximately 70% of its energy exports through the Red Sea port of Yanbu, making any Red Sea disruption a major global energy security concern.

Pakistan's Mediation Efforts Falter

Pakistan's Foreign Office spokesman Tahir Andrabi urged both sides to "immediately end hostilities" and return to technical-level negotiations under the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) brokered last month. He acknowledged the MoU's implementation was "facing challenges." Iran's chief negotiator Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf warned that a deal "only has meaning when its clauses are valid and being implemented."

3. Ukraine Defense Minister Fired, Sparking Mass Protests

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy's surprise decision to dismiss Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov on July 16 triggered the largest wartime protests since Russia's full-scale invasion began in 2022.

Fedorov: A Reformist Minister Sacrificed

Fedorov, 35, served as defense minister for just six months after previously serving as Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Digital Transformation. He was widely regarded as one of Ukraine's most effective wartime officials, credited with:

  • Revolutionizing drone warfare: Massively expanding domestic drone production, enabling long-range strikes deep inside Russian territory against energy targets and supply lines
  • Disabling Russian Starlink access: Using technical means to cut Russian forces off from SpaceX's satellite terminals
  • Defense procurement reform: Cleaning up corruption and increasing transparency, which angered established interests
  • Ballistic missile testing: Advancing Ukraine's domestic ballistic missile program

Public Power Struggle with Military Leadership

Fedorov's tensions with Commander-in-Chief Oleksandr Syrskyi had been simmering for months. Fedorov publicly accused Syrskyi of blocking ministry initiatives and failing to address battlefield problems. He revealed he had urged Zelenskyy to dismiss both Syrskyi and Chief of the General Staff Andrii Hnatov. Syrskyi, in turn, reportedly issued an ultimatum to Zelenskyy.

Fedorov declined Zelenskyy's offer to stay on as an adviser, stating bluntly: "In this configuration, I personally don't know how to win the war."

Protests Erupt Across 17 Cities

On July 16, over 1,000 protesters gathered at Ivan Franko Square in central Kyiv, chanting "Shame!" and carrying signs reading "The Russians are celebrating" and "Hands off Fedorov." The protests quickly spread to at least 17 cities across Ukraine, including Lviv, Odesa, Dnipro, and Kharkiv.

"I believe that the removal of M. Fedorov is a great evil for the country's defense capability." — Deputy Air Force Commander Pavlo Yelizarov, resigning in protest

Deputy Air Force Commander Pavlo Yelizarov, a key leader in drone warfare, resigned in protest, calling Fedorov's dismissal "a great evil." The pro-government media outlet United24 paused publication to participate in protests. Multiple lawmakers from Zelenskyy's own Servant of the People party voiced opposition, with one saying "100% we will not accept" the proposed replacement.

Zelenskyy Faces Political Crisis

Zelenskyy said at a press conference that he "hears what society is saying" but did not commit to reinstating Fedorov. Interior Minister Ihor Klymenko has been proposed as defense minister, though Zelenskyy said he was only "one of the candidates."

The reshuffle also includes a new prime minister: former Naftogaz CEO Serhii Koretskyi was approved by parliament to succeed Yulia Svyrydenko. Analysts suggest that Fedorov's growing popularity had made him a potential political rival to Zelenskyy, which may have been a deeper factor in his dismissal.

UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer visited Kyiv on the same day, reaffirming Western support. Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov dismissed the reshuffle, saying "whoever heads Ukraine's Defense Ministry does not matter" to Russia.
