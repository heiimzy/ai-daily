⚡ July 21 Tech Roundup: Z.ai Completes 1GW Data Center With Only Chinese AI Chips, Biren Unveils 1,024-GPU Optical Super Node, Chinese AI Chip Startups Pivot to Edge

📡 Z.ai Completes 1GW All-Domestic-Chip AI Data Center

July 21 — Z.ai (Zhipu AI), the developer of the GLM family of large language models, announced it has completed construction of a 1GW-class AI data center built entirely with Chinese-made AI chips, with part of the facility already operational, TechNode reported Monday afternoon.

The data center is designed to support training and inference for the company's frontier AI models. Beyond this flagship facility, Z.ai also operates multiple computing clusters each containing more than 10,000 chips, significantly expanding large-scale domestic computing capacity in its model development pipeline, according to Bloomberg.

This milestone carries outsized significance against the backdrop of escalating US export controls on advanced semiconductors to China. Chinese AI companies have long faced a compute bottleneck — cutting-edge GPUs are restricted, and domestic alternatives have struggled with ecosystem maturity. Z.ai's 1GW data center, running exclusively on domestic chips, demonstrates that Chinese-made AI accelerators can function at hyperscale deployment levels.

"Z.ai's all-domestic-chip data center marks a tangible step toward AI compute self-sufficiency," industry analysts noted. "In 2026, as US-led chip export controls tighten further, the question of whether Chinese chips can support frontier AI training has been a pressing unknown. Z.ai's practice provides an affirmative answer."

With Huawei's Ascend 950 SuperPoD and other domestic computing hardware entering mass production in the second half of 2026, more large-scale domestic chip deployments are expected across China's AI industry.

🔌 Biren Unveils 1,024-GPU Optical Super Node Architecture

At the World Artificial Intelligence Conference (WAIC) in Shanghai, Biren Technology — the Hong Kong-listed GPU company — unveiled its next-generation GPU interconnect architecture capable of linking up to 1,024 GPUs using advanced near-packaged optics (NPO) technology. The optical engines are integrated directly onto the company's upcoming BR2xx series boards.

The architecture's key innovation: physically separating compute and networking nodes, enabling deployment in standard server racks without custom cabinetry. This drastically shortens product development cycles and reduces infrastructure costs for AI operators.

Architecture Highlights

  • 1,024-GPU scale: Supports the largest-scale parallel training workloads for trillion-parameter models
  • Near-packaged optics: Optical engines integrated directly on board, significantly reducing interconnect latency and power consumption
  • Standard server deployment: No custom racks required — compatible with existing data center infrastructure
  • BR2xx GPU series: Biren's next-generation GPU with substantial performance improvements over previous generations

Biren Technology listed on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange in 2024 and is one of China's most prominent GPU players. The 1,024-GPU optical super node architecture positions it as a direct competitor to NVIDIA's NVLink/NVSwitch interconnect solution, offering a domestic alternative at a critical infrastructure layer.

📱 Chinese AI Chip Startups Pivot to Edge Devices

Chinese AI chip startups are collectively pivoting their focus from the cloud market to edge devices — including robots, smart hardware, and IoT — as competition in the cloud computing chip space intensifies, Caixin Global reported July 21.

The cloud AI chip market has become a battlefield dominated by giants: NVIDIA commands roughly 80% market share, while domestic players like Huawei Ascend, Cambricon, and Hygon compete fiercely for the remaining domestic replacement market. For startups, differentiation in this space has become nearly impossible.

Edge devices present a fundamentally different opportunity. As large language models are increasingly deployed on-device, a new blue ocean has opened for chip startups. Robots need real-time inference chips, smart cameras require on-device AI acceleration, and wearables demand ultra-low-power AI processors — all scenarios with radically different requirements for power, cost, and latency compared to cloud chips.

Three Driving Forces

  • Data privacy: Growing demand for local AI processing — medical imaging, financial risk control, industrial quality inspection — where sending data to the cloud is unacceptable
  • Low-latency requirements: Autonomous driving, robot control, real-time translation require inference delays measured in milliseconds, not the round-trip latency of cloud APIs
  • Long-term cost advantage: While edge chip deployment has higher upfront costs, eliminating recurring cloud API fees reduces total cost of ownership over time

"The pivot to edge devices is fundamentally a differentiation survival strategy," Caixin analysis notes. "With the cloud AI chip market already captured by established giants, edge computing represents not a niche market but a trillion-device grand narrative. From billions of smartphones and hundreds of millions of smart home devices to millions of robots, each device needs AI silicon — a total volume that dwarfs cloud server chips."

The shift also reflects a deeper trend across China's consumer electronics supply chain: bringing large language model inference directly onto local devices. China has the world's most complete consumer electronics supply chain, from phones and wearables to home appliances and vehicles. The addressable market for edge AI chips may be several times larger than the cloud market.
