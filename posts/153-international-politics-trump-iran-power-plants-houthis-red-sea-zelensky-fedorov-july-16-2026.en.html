⚡ July 16 International Politics Roundup: Trump Threatens Iran Power Plants Next Week, Iran Vows to 'Crush' Regional Infrastructure, Houthis to Close Red Sea Route, Zelensky Fires Defense Minister Fedorov

Trump: 'Next Week Comes the Power Plants, Next Week Comes the Bridges'

U.S. President Donald Trump issued his most severe threat yet in a Tuesday (July 14) Fox News interview, warning that American forces would begin targeting Iran's power plants and bridges "next week" if Tehran does not return to negotiations over its nuclear program and the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz.

"Next week it gets really bad for them because next week comes the power plants," Trump said in the interview. "Next week comes the bridges. We're going to knock out all their power plants. We're going to knock out all their bridges unless they get to the table and negotiate."

However, when asked Wednesday (July 15) whether he had set a deadline for Iran, Trump backtracked, saying he "doesn't like setting deadlines" and merely warned Tehran to "better behave." According to U.S. officials, Trump still prefers a diplomatic resolution internally but has demanded that the military prepare broader escalation options, including ground operations to seize islands and bombing underground nuclear facilities.

This week, the U.S. military has conducted strikes against Iran for five consecutive days, for the first time expanding the target zone to areas around Tehran and the northern Semnan province. U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) confirmed that the latest wave of strikes concluded at 9 p.m. ET Wednesday, targeting Iranian command centers, air defense systems, missile and drone capabilities, and coastal surveillance facilities using precision-guided munitions, including strikes on Bandar Abbas.

Iran Issues Strongest Warning: 'All Infrastructure in the Region Will Be Crushed'

In response to Trump's threats, Iran's top military command issued an unprecedentedly强硬 response. Brigadier General Ebrahim Zolfaghari, a spokesperson for Iran's Khatam al-Anbiya Central Headquarters, stated on Thursday (July 16):

"Everything that is still intact — that is, all the infrastructure in the region — will be crushed under the steel blows of the powerful armed forces of the Islamic Republic of Iran, so that no trace of them remains and it is as if they never existed in the first place."

Iranian army spokesman Mohammad Akraminia also warned that if the U.S. continues its strikes, Iran's attacks "will spread to new areas," adding that "a significant portion of the armed forces' capabilities have not yet been demonstrated." Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesperson similarly declared: "Our hands are not tied. Our fighters will respond with full force and power to US aggressions."

Iran emphasized that the Strait of Hormuz is an "invincible red line" and that under no circumstances will it allow the United States, as a foreign extra-regional power, to interfere in the waterway.

Iran Tells Houthis to Prepare to Close Red Sea's Bab el-Mandeb Strait

In a potentially game-changing development, Reuters reported Thursday that Iran has asked Yemen's Houthi movement to stand ready to close the Red Sea oil route (the Bab el-Mandeb Strait) if U.S. forces follow through on Trump's threats to attack Iranian power infrastructure, according to three unnamed sources.

The Bab el-Mandeb Strait — a narrow waterway connecting the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden — is one of the world's most critical energy arteries, alongside the Strait of Hormuz. A significant portion of Saudi oil exports and global shipping passes through this chokepoint. If both straits were to be closed simultaneously, the global oil supply would face an unprecedented crisis.

A senior Houthi official, politburo member Mohammad al-Farah, had previously warned in an interview on Iran's Press TV that Bab el-Mandeb would be closed if the situation continued to escalate. Houthi leader Abdul Malik al-Houthi has also stated that "our fingers are on the trigger at any moment should developments warrant it."

Analysts note that while the Houthis launched massive attacks on Red Sea shipping beginning in November 2023 — forcing Maersk, Hapag-Lloyd, and other major carriers to divert around Africa's Cape of Good Hope — the group has been relatively restrained since reaching a ceasefire with Saudi Arabia earlier this year. A renewed Houthi campaign to close Bab el-Mandeb would send global oil prices soaring and deal a severe blow to the already-fragile world economy.

The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since February 28, when the U.S. and Israel launched joint strikes on Iran — this has been Iran's core leverage throughout the conflict. The U.S. announced Tuesday it had resumed its naval blockade of Iranian ports. On Wednesday, U.S. forces fired Hellfire missiles into the smokestack of the Curacao-flagged oil tanker M/T Belma, which was attempting to sail toward Iran's Kharg Island, disabling the vessel.

Day One of U.S. Blockade: Two Compliant, One Disabled

CENTCOM said that during the first 24 hours of enforcing the blockade, it had redirected two compliant commercial vessels and disabled one non-compliant vessel. The command stated in a release:

"Forces observed Curacao-flagged M/T Belma transiting international waters toward Kharg Island. The commercial vessel ignored multiple warnings as it attempted to violate the US blockade. A US aircraft disabled the vessel after firing Hellfire missiles into the ship's smokestack. The ship is no longer transiting to Iran."

Iran's Health Ministry reported that U.S. airstrikes since the latest round of fighting began last week have killed at least 35 people and wounded more than 300 across Iran. The ministry did not break down the figures between civilians and military personnel.

Zelensky Dismisses Defense Minister Fedorov, Protests Erupt in Kyiv

In a major political shakeup in Ukraine, President Volodymyr Zelensky dismissed Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov on the evening of July 15, appointing Interior Minister Ihor Klymenko as his replacement. The Ukrainian parliament is expected to vote on Klymenko's nomination on July 16.

Why Was Fedorov Fired?

According to multiple lawmakers and analysts cited by the Kyiv Independent, the core reason for Fedorov's dismissal was his irreconcilable conflict with Ukraine's Commander-in-Chief Oleksandr Syrskyi. Zelensky told a closed-door parliamentary meeting that the rift between the Defense Ministry and military leadership had "crossed all lines," and hinted that a "reset" of military leadership could follow.

Fedorov confirmed his departure on social media, calling it "a great honor to serve the Ukrainian people as Defense Minister." A relatively young and popular figure, Fedorov previously served as Minister of Digital Transformation from 2019 to 2026 and played a key role in Ukraine's military digitalization efforts.

Mass Protests in Kyiv

Fedorov's dismissal triggered a wave of public anger. Widely regarded as one of the most reform-minded and popular officials in Zelensky's government, his removal was seen by many as a casualty of internal political infighting. Prominent activists and veterans called for peaceful protests in Kyiv against Zelensky's "constant reshuffles."

Analysts suggest that Fedorov's defense reforms — including the dismissal of obstructionist officials and the introduction of a digital conscription system — were necessary but had provoked fierce resistance from conservative elements within the military establishment. The conflict with Syrskyi centered on the direction of military reform and mobilization strategy.

Ukraine Launches 200 Drones at Moscow, Strikes Engels-2 Airbase

Meanwhile, Ukraine continued its long-range drone campaign deep into Russian territory. Moscow Mayor Sergey Sobyanin reported that more than 200 Ukrainian drones were launched toward the Moscow region overnight Wednesday into Thursday, with at least 10 destroyed as they approached the city. Sobyanin said more than 1,200 drones had been directed at Moscow in the past week alone.

More significantly, the Engels-2 airbase in Saratov Oblast — a strategic bomber hub housing Tu-95, Tu-22, and Tu-160 heavy bombers used for missile attacks on Ukrainian cities — was reportedly struck by Ukrainian drones. Local residents posted photos and videos showing fires at the base. The extent of damage was not immediately clear.

Russia's Defense Ministry claimed its air defense systems intercepted 375 Ukrainian drones across 18 regions, plus Crimea, the Black Sea, and the Sea of Azov. Russia's federal air transport agency Rosaviatsiya imposed temporary flight restrictions at all four Moscow international airports as well as seven other airports including Saratov, Nizhny Novgorod, and Samara.

President Zelensky stated that the large-scale drone attack was a direct response to Russia's continued bombardment of Ukrainian cities. "We are returning the war to Russian territory — this is completely fair," he wrote on Telegram. Ukraine's Air Force reported that Russia launched 18 missiles and 146 drones into the country overnight, of which 12 missiles and 129 drones were intercepted. Two people were killed in Kyiv by a Russian missile strike.

Other targeted regions included Kharkiv, Sumy, Zaporizhzhia, Mykolaiv, and Donetsk. UN data shows that June 2026 was the deadliest month for Ukrainian civilians since April 2022, underscoring the ongoing intensity of the war.
