⚡ July 21 Tech Evening: Google Debuts AI Security Model to Take on Anthropic Mythos, Stellantis Teams Up with Mobileye for L2 Autonomous Driving

Google Launches Gemini 3.5 Flash Cyber: A "Cost Revolution" in AI Security

On July 21, Google officially launched Gemini 3.5 Flash Cyber — an AI model purpose-built for vulnerability detection and patching. Built on top of Gemini 3.5 Flash, the model is positioned as a "cost-efficient and highly capable alternative" to Anthropic's flagship security model, Mythos 5.

In a blog post, Google explained that Flash Cyber integrates into CodeMender, Google's security-focused coding agent, allowing it to be called "multiple times at high speed and low cost" so that AI agents can scan more code paths and find more vulnerabilities. Google emphasized that the model achieved "competitive performance" compared to "significantly larger models" on the CyberGym AI cybersecurity benchmark.

Real-World Results: 55 Unique Issues Found in V8 Engine

In the most compelling real-world test, Gemini 3.5 Flash Cyber identified 55 "unique confirmed issues" in the V8 JavaScript Engine, compared to 47 found by Gemini 3.5 Flash and 36 by Opus 4.6. Notably, Flash Cyber discovered 10 issues that no other model found, and Google noted that the model continued to find new code paths and vulnerabilities even after being invoked multiple times.

The Pricing Battle: Google Plays the Value Card

Anthropic's Mythos 5, part of the Project Glasswing initiative, comes at a steep price — $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output tokens, double the cost of Claude Opus 4.8. Microsoft has already adopted Mythos for its security checks and just completed its biggest Patch Tuesday ever this month. Google's Flash Cyber is clearly aimed at turning this narrative around by delivering comparable performance at a fraction of the cost.

Google stated that Flash Cyber will first be available to governments and trusted partners, signaling a shift in AI security competition from "bigger is better" to "efficiency wins."

Stellantis Teams Up with Mobileye: L2 Hands-Free Driving by 2027

On the same day, Stellantis — the world's fourth-largest automaker behind Jeep, Ram, Chrysler, Dodge, Fiat, Peugeot, and Citroën — announced a partnership with Intel's Mobileye to deploy Level 2 hands-free driving features starting in 2027.

Technical Architecture: EyeQ Chip + Crowdsourced Mapping

Stellantis will use Mobileye's EyeQ system-on-a-chip and the Road Experience Management (REM) data crowdsourcing program. REM builds a global 3D map using real-time data from Mobileye-equipped vehicles, now covering more than 95% of public roads in the US and Europe, drawing data from over 8 million vehicles that collectively logged 34 billion miles last year. The technology will enable "hands-free, eyes-on driving and intelligent lane keeping on unmarked roads in designated areas and conditions."

The ADAS Market: A $90 Billion Opportunity

Automakers are racing to add more advanced driver-assist systems to their vehicles — not just for safety, but as a potential cash cow. Estimates project ADAS-related revenue will hit $90 billion over the next decade. Mobileye reported $558 million in Q1 2026 revenue, a 27% year-over-year increase, reinforcing this trajectory.

Stellantis's Autonomous Driving Roadmap

Stellantis already offers a "Hands-Free Active Driving Assist" feature in some Jeep and Ram models, but has been less aggressive than rivals Ford and GM in pushing the technology. Last year, the company introduced STLA AutoDrive, an "eyes-off" system for stop-and-go traffic, developed with UK-based self-driving company Wayve and expected by 2028. The Mobileye partnership fills the gap in its highway L2-level capabilities.

Mobileye noted that adding Stellantis means it now supplies "five of the world's ten largest automakers" contributing data to its REM network.

Industry Impact: Dual Transformation in AI Security and Automotive Tech

These two announcements, released on the same day, reflect two converging trends:

  • AI security price war begins: Google's low-cost security model directly challenges Anthropic Mythos's pricing structure. Microsoft's Patch Tuesday has already proven the value of AI-powered security, but high costs remain a barrier to broader adoption. Flash Cyber could accelerate the democratization of AI security tools.
  • ADAS competition enters the "ecosystem" phase: Stellantis's choice signals that automakers are increasingly opting for mature platforms like Mobileye rather than building in-house solutions, prioritizing speed-to-market. Mobileye's data network effect through REM is becoming its core competitive moat.

On the stock front, Google's share price rose nearly 3.7% in early trading, with markets reacting positively to both the Frozen v2 chip news and the Flash Cyber launch. Stellantis shares also edged up following the announcement, as investors welcomed the cost-and-time-to-market tradeoff.
