⚡ July 13 AI Chip Roundup: Bank of Korea Confirms Semiconductor Supercycle Has Not Peaked, Busan Forum Opens with 800 Experts, SK Hynix CEO Warns of Worst Shortage in 2027

1. Bank of Korea: AI Semiconductor Supercycle Has Not Peaked

The Bank of Korea (BOK) released a report on July 13 formally confirming that the global semiconductor market's AI-driven supercycle is still ongoing, with supply growth structurally constrained by the custom nature of high-bandwidth memory (HBM) production — the market is expected to maintain its expansion trend for a considerable period.

The report, submitted to National Assembly member Park Sung-hoon, states: "While semiconductor demand has surged significantly due to investments in AI infrastructure, the pace of supply expansion has been slow." The BOK emphasized that the current chip cycle differs fundamentally from previous ones — driven by competitive corporate investment anticipating fundamental industrial changes from AI adoption, rather than traditional consumer electronics cycles.

"The pace of supply expansion is more constrained than in the past, as the market is led by custom products such as HBM," the BOK said. "The global semiconductor market is expected to continue its expansionary trend for a considerable period."

The report comes amid growing concerns over debt-funded AI infrastructure spending, elevated market valuations, and the potential for memory chip oversupply — triggering selloffs in major technology stocks. Samsung Electronics fell more than 9 percent on July 2, while SK Hynix dropped nearly 15 percent. Even after Samsung reported Q2 operating profit of 89.4 trillion won ($59.9 billion), beating expectations, shares fell 7 percent last Tuesday.

"There is uncertainty surrounding the pace and scope of AI technology adoption, as well as its profitability," the BOK stated. "Major investment banks, including J.P. Morgan, Goldman Sachs, and Morgan Stanley, generally forecast that the global semiconductor market will remain robust at least through next year."

2. Busan Semiconductor Forum Opens: 800 Industry Leaders Gather

On the same day, Korea's Ministry of Science and ICT (MSIT) kicked off the third annual "2026 Semiconductor Program Results Exchange Meeting" at the Paradise Hotel in Busan, running July 13-14. Approximately 800 people are attending, including principal investigators of 125 government-funded projects and representatives from SK Hynix, NVIDIA Korea, and other major institutions.

On the opening day, NVIDIA Korea CEO Jeong So-young delivered a keynote titled "The New Industrial Revolution Led by AI Factories," predicting that data centers are evolving from cost centers into profit-generating factories that produce intelligence and tokens. She identified agentic AI and physical AI as the next battlegrounds, positioning South Korea as a key partner in the physical AI era due to its semiconductor and manufacturing competitiveness.

Specialist sessions cover semiconductor design and infrastructure, advanced packaging, nanomaterials, and compound semiconductors. To address surging demand for advanced packaging driven by HBM adoption, the event features tailored consulting sessions on packaging and next-generation devices.

An accompanying exhibition showcases 13 outstanding research outcomes, including Seoul National University's CMOS-NEM embedded security edge AI system and Dankook University's room-temperature 3D heterogeneous junction memory-in-sensor technology.

3. SK Hynix CEO Warns: 2027 Will Be Worst Shortage Year in History

SK Hynix CEO Kwak Noh-jung issued a stark warning last week during the company's Nasdaq listing ceremony in New York, telling Reuters that 2027 will be the worst year for memory supply in the industry's history, with customer demand continuing to exceed supply capacity well beyond 2030.

"We forecast that next year will be the worst year in the industry's history from the supply perspective," Kwak said. "Our customer demand continues to go up, while our capacity has limitations. We still forecast that customer demand will remain higher than our supply capacity even beyond 2030."

Kwak's warning aligns with NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang's recent comments that AI memory shortages would continue for several years and that SK Hynix would remain NVIDIA's largest memory partner. UBS similarly expects the global DRAM industry to remain undersupplied until at least Q2 2028.

Meanwhile, according to DigiTimes citing industry sources, HBM4 prices are expected to more than double next year, from approximately $2 per Gb in H2 2026 to $4-5 in 2027. HBM manufacturing consumes approximately three times the wafer capacity of conventional DRAM, with HBM4 production cycles up to twice as long as DDR5 and lower initial yields, creating significant upward price pressure.

SK Group Chairman Chey Tae-won stated in New York that AI has fundamentally reshaped the semiconductor supply-demand paradigm, breaking traditional boom-bust cycles. "Demand is growing at a pace far beyond how quickly we can build new manufacturing capacity. Constructing advanced semiconductor fabs requires enormous lead time. There are also significant infrastructure bottlenecks."

4. Nomura: AI Memory Demand Concerns Are Overdone

Nomura Securities released a report arguing that investor concerns over a potential slowdown in the AI memory market have been exaggerated, and recent long-term investment plans by Korean chipmakers and Meta's cloud strategy do not signal weakening demand.

The brokerage noted that the current market remains characterized by significant memory product shortages, with manufacturers continuing to prioritize higher-margin HBM, leaving conventional DRAM and NAND supplies relatively tight. Newly announced investments will take years to materially impact supply — the Yongin Semiconductor Cluster, launched nine years ago, is unlikely to begin meaningful production before late 2027.

Nomura also rejected suggestions that Meta's plans to commercialize excess computing capacity indicate softer AI demand, arguing that offering unused data-center resources is a logical way for big tech to improve returns on invested capital. Lower computing costs could ultimately encourage broader AI adoption rather than reduce hardware demand.

5. Samsung Expands Vera Rubin Supply Chain from HBM to eSSD

Samsung Electronics announced it has begun mass production of the PM1763, an enterprise SSD (eSSD) for NVIDIA's next-generation AI computing platform "Vera Rubin," adopting the PCIe 6.0 standard with double the data transfer bandwidth of its predecessor. The 16TB model delivers sequential read speeds of 28,400 MB/s and write speeds of 21,900 MB/s, capable of transferring a large language model in approximately 1.4 seconds.

This follows Samsung's February milestone as the world's first supplier of HBM4 for Vera Rubin, completing the company's "total memory" supply chain covering both DRAM and NAND for the AI server market. According to Omdia, the eSSD market is projected to expand from approximately $24.1 billion in 2025 to $154 billion in 2026, growing more than sixfold in a single year.
