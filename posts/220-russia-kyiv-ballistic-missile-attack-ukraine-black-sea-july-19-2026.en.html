⚡ Russia-Ukraine War July 19: Russia Launches Largest-Ever Ballistic Missile Attack on Kyiv, Ukraine Strikes Back at Black Sea Oil Tankers, Zelensky Faces Political Crisis

1. Russia Unleashes Record Barrage of Ballistic Missiles on Kyiv

In the early hours of Sunday, July 19, Russian forces launched the largest ballistic missile attack on Kyiv since the start of the full-scale invasion. Ukraine's Air Force reported that Russia fired 41 missiles — including 25 Iskander-M/S-400 ballistic missiles, 10 3M22 Zircon hypersonic anti-ship missiles, 3 Oniks anti-ship missiles, and 3 Kh-59/69 air-launched missiles — along with 125 attack drones. Acting Foreign Minister Andrii Sybiha described the attack as "around four dozen ballistic missiles," the largest number in a single assault since the war began in February 2022.

The assault began at 1:30 a.m. local time and lasted for hours, with explosions heard across six districts of the capital. Ukraine's State Emergency Service confirmed one fatality and 15 injuries across five districts: Shevchenkivskyi, Solomyanskyi, Dniprovskyi, Desnianskyi, and Sviatoshynskyi. Fires broke out at a shopping mall, a supermarket, a dormitory, office buildings, and residential homes. In the Sviatoshynskyi district, firefighters rescued four residents from a burning private home. The Lukianivska metro station was damaged by the blast wave and temporarily closed, with trains bypassing the station without stopping.

Kyiv Mayor Vitali Klitschko reported that multiple non-residential buildings and warehouses were struck, and parked cars and office buildings caught fire in several districts. In the surrounding Bucha district, strikes on logistics facilities injured two people.

2. Zelensky: Russia Used Over 1,640 Guided Bombs in One Week

President Volodymyr Zelensky revealed on Telegram that Russia had used approximately 1,450 strike drones, more than 1,640 guided aerial bombs, and 99 missiles of various types against Ukraine over the past week alone. "Protection against ballistic missiles is our constant and top priority right now," he said. "Interceptors are needed every day, and I am grateful to everyone who takes our agreements seriously and ensures the delivery of anti-ballistic capabilities."

Zelensky reiterated that the United States has committed to granting Ukraine a license to produce Patriot interceptor missiles domestically. President Donald Trump announced at the NATO summit in Ankara: "We'll give them the right to make Patriots. We'll show them how to do it. I think they can produce them pretty quickly once we explain it." However, defense analysts caution that setting up a licensed production line would take at least 18 months and cost over $1 billion. Japan is currently the only country authorized by the U.S. to produce the PAC-3 interceptor.

3. Ukraine Launches Dual Counterattack: Black Sea and Russian Heartland

Ukraine's military executed a massive overnight counterattack on July 18-19, striking 59 targets. Ukraine's Unmanned Systems Forces announced they had hit two Russian oil tankers and a cargo ship in the Black Sea, as well as a floating crane in the Sea of Azov. The General Staff stated these vessels were used to transport oil, petroleum products, and fuel for the Russian army, while the floating crane supported Moscow's maritime logistics. According to Ukrainian estimates, Operation MoLoCHKa has struck 176 vessels from Russia's shadow fleet between July 1 and 19 alone.

On Russian soil, Ukrainian drones struck an industrial park in the Stavropol region, causing a major fire. This followed a strike the previous day that destroyed two Wildberries warehouses — Russia's largest online retailer — in Tambov and Elektrostal, killing 8 people and wounding 62. Zelensky said these facilities supplied components for Russian drones and navigation equipment used in attacks on Ukrainian infrastructure.

Ukraine's General Staff also reported striking a Soviet-era Buk air defense system in occupied Zaporizhzhia and a road bridge in occupied Donetsk. Russia claimed to have intercepted 140 Ukrainian drones overnight.

4. Political Crisis: Zelensky Dismisses Defense Minister, Third Day of Mass Protests

Ukraine's domestic political landscape has been thrown into turmoil. Zelensky's decision on July 15 to dismiss Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov — just six months into his tenure — has triggered the largest street protests of his presidency. Fedorov, 35, is widely credited with transforming Ukraine's drone warfare capabilities, bringing Russia's frontline advances to a near standstill, and causing fuel shortages inside Russia through long-range strikes.

Protesters gathered in central Kyiv for the third consecutive day on July 18, chanting "Shame!" and "Syrskyi, go away!" — demanding Fedorov's reinstatement and the firing of Commander-in-Chief Oleksandr Syrskyi. Demonstrations spread to Lviv, Odesa, and Dnipro. Deputy Air Force Commander Pavlo Yelizarov resigned in protest, calling Fedorov's removal "a great evil" for Ukraine's defense.

Zelensky responded by saying he "understands, hears, and responds" to the voices of protesters, but simultaneously appointed acting SBU Chief Yevhen Khmara as acting defense minister and energy executive Sergiy Koretsky as prime minister. Analysts warn the political crisis could erode Western confidence in Ukraine's leadership at a critical juncture in the war, potentially giving Russia an opening on the battlefield.
