⚡ July 23 Financial Midday Report: Brent Crude Breaks $95 to 7-Week High, Trump Threatens Tehran City Targets; Alphabet/Tesla Plunge After-Hours as Free Cash Flow Turns Negative; Asia Markets Rout KOSPI -3.72%; ECB Decision Tonight; World Bank Warns Global Growth Could Fall to 1.3%

Global financial markets remain in turmoil on Thursday (July 23) as the US-Iran conflict enters its 12th consecutive night. Brent crude surged past $95 to a seven-week high as Trump threatened to strike infrastructure targets inside Tehran. Tech giants Alphabet and Tesla both tumbled in after-hours trading after reporting free cash flow turning negative for the first time in years, raising deep concerns about the efficiency of AI capital spending. Asia-Pacific equity markets plunged across the board, with South Korea's KOSPI crashing 3.72%. The European Central Bank will announce its rate decision tonight, with markets watching closely for signals on September policy.

1. Oil Markets: Brent Breaks $95.83 to 7-Week High, Trump Threatens Central Tehran

As of 13:00 Beijing time, Brent crude was trading at $95.83/barrel, up 0.27% on the day, extending Wednesday's 4.38% surge to the highest since early June. WTI crude rose in tandem to $88.17/barrel, testing the $90 level intraday.

Trump Escalation: Precision Targeting of Tehran

President Trump dramatically escalated his rhetoric in a Truth Social post and public remarks, explicitly threatening to bomb infrastructure "in or near" Tehran if Iran continues to attack vessels in the Strait of Hormuz. "If they strike one more ship, we will destroy a bridge or power plant in Tehran," Trump stated. This represents a significant ratcheting of geographic specificity in US threats, which oil markets interpreted as a signal the conflict is entering a new, more dangerous phase.

Red Sea Crisis: Three Saudi Tankers Reroute to Avoid Houthi Blockade

At least three crude-laden tankers originally destined for Saudi ports reversed course in the Red Sea, diverting toward Suez or planning extended voyages around Africa's Cape of Good Hope. This follows Houthi threats to impose a comprehensive naval blockade on Saudi Arabia, threatening the Bab el-Mandeb strait through which approximately 7% of global oil supply transits. Combined with the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz, approximately 25% of global seaborne oil and gas trade now faces dual chokepoint risk.

Cushing Inventory Nears Operational Minimum

EIA data showed Cushing, Oklahoma crude inventories fell another 674,000 barrels last week, approaching operational minimum levels. Goldman Sachs warned that if the Persian Gulf chokepoint crisis persists alongside disappearing Cushing buffer, Brent crude could break $120/barrel. US crude production has also declined from record highs, while total commercial crude inventories have fallen below pre-conflict (late February) levels.

Goldman Sachs: Brent faces material upside risk above $120/bbl if the Strait of Hormuz disruption persists alongside collapsing Cushing inventory buffers.

2. Tech Earnings Fallout: Alphabet/Tesla Free Cash Flow Turns Negative, Both Stocks Plunge After-Hours

Alphabet Q2: Revenue Beats But FCF Turns -$5.86B, Stock -4% After Hours

Alphabet reported Q2 revenue of $119.8 billion (+24% YoY), beating estimates of $116.9 billion. Google Cloud continued to shine with $24.8 billion in revenue (+82% YoY), and cloud backlog crossed $500 billion for the first time. However, the market fixated on capital expenditures: Q2 capex doubled to $44.9 billion, and full-year 2026 capex guidance was raised sharply to $195-205 billion (from $180-190 billion previously). Free cash flow turned negative for the first time since 2024 at -$5.86 billion. Alphabet shares plunged 4% in after-hours trading.

Tesla Q2: Record Revenue But FCF Turns -$1.09B, Stock -3% After Hours

Tesla posted record Q2 revenue of $28.24 billion but badly missed EPS estimates. Operating income of $398 million came far below the $1.39 billion consensus. Free cash flow turned negative at -$1.09 billion — the first negative quarter since early 2024 — as AI infrastructure and Dojo supercomputer spending weighed heavily. Analysts noted Tesla faces a "squeezed auto margins + burning AI cash" double bind. Shares fell 3% after hours.

SMCI Defies the Gloom: Surges 19.8% on Record $60B+ Order Backlog

In stark contrast, Super Micro Computer (SMCI) surged 19.8% — the S&P 500's biggest gainer — after disclosing Q4 order backlog exceeded $60 billion and announcing a joint AI data center project with SpaceX. Dell Technologies rallied 9.3% and Hewlett Packard Enterprise gained 3%, providing evidence that AI infrastructure demand is still accelerating at the hardware level even as mega-cap spending efficiency is questioned.

3. Asia-Pacific Markets in Freefall as Risk-Off Sentiment Sweeps Region

Asia-Pacific equity markets opened sharply lower on Thursday, extending overnight US losses and absorbing the oil price shock:

  • South Korea KOSPI: Crashed 3.72% — SK Hynix fell over 4%, leveraged ETF liquidation fears resurface. KOSPI now down over 20% from its peak, officially in a technical bear market
  • Japan Nikkei 225: Fell 1.67%, breaking below 53,000 as chip stocks led declines
  • India Sensex: Opened below 76,500, down 0.9% for a fourth consecutive session of losses
  • China A-shares: Shanghai Composite edged down 0.07% to 3,913, Shenzhen -0.62%, ChiNext -0.79%. Trading volume contracted sharply to 1.6 trillion yuan. Money rotated into defensive banking stocks — Agricultural Bank of China rose for a 14th straight day
  • Hong Kong Hang Seng: Fell 0.95%, dragged by Tencent and tech names

UBS Global Wealth Management strategists warned that Asia-Pacific markets face a "double hit" from oil price shock and AI valuation correction, with near-term volatility likely to intensify.

4. ECB Rate Decision Tonight: Expected Hold at 2.25%, September Hike Odds Rise

The European Central Bank will announce its July rate decision at 20:15 Beijing time (14:15 CEST), followed by President Lagarde's press conference at 20:45. Markets unanimously expect a hold at 2.25% for the deposit facility rate, 2.40% for the main refinancing rate, and 2.65% for the marginal lending rate.

The true focus, however, is Lagarde's tone on the September outlook. With US-Iran tensions driving energy prices sharply higher, eurozone inflation faces renewed upside risks:

  • Deutsche Bank: Expects the ECB to maintain neutral language, then hike 25bp in September
  • Commerzbank: Lagarde may strike a firm tone on inflation risks but is unlikely to pre-commit to September action
  • MUFG: Sees one more 25bp hike in September, taking the deposit rate to 2.50%
  • Market pricing: 98% probability of hold today; September hike odds have surged from 52% to 68% over the past week

ING noted that if Lagarde delivers a hawkish surprise, EUR/USD could retest 1.140; a dovish tone could send the pair back toward the June low of 1.1330.

5. Safe Haven Flows: Gold, Forex, and Bitcoin

Gold continued its rally as geopolitical risks drove safe-haven demand:

  • Spot Gold: $4,135/oz — up 1.44% on Wednesday, hitting a two-week high of $4,165.92 intraday
  • COMEX Silver: $60.03/oz, up 1.57%
  • US Dollar Index (DXY): 101.12, edging down 0.09%, with technical buying supporting gold
  • USD/JPY: 163.16 — yen lingers near 40-year lows, intervention pressure on Japanese authorities continues to mount
  • EUR/USD: 1.1412, up 0.13%, awaiting ECB guidance
  • Bitcoin: Holding steady near $66,000 — US spot Bitcoin ETFs have logged six consecutive days of net inflows totaling ~$930 million

6. World Bank Issues Dire Warning: Global Growth Could Crash to 1.3%

World Bank Chief Economist Indermit Gill issued a stark warning that the escalation of US-Iran hostilities could reignite global inflation, push interest rates higher, and slash global economic growth from last year's 2.9% to as low as 1.3% in a worst-case scenario. Global headline inflation could spike to 4.5%.

This is among the most severe warnings from an international institution about the economic impact of geopolitical conflict since the 1970s oil shocks. Gill specifically highlighted the dual chokepoint risk at the Strait of Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb as an "unprecedented supply shock" whose impact would far exceed the initial energy market turmoil following the Russia-Ukraine conflict.

Separately, the US House of Representatives passed the "Stop Insider Trading Act" (232-198), restricting members of Congress and their families from trading stocks. Trump also suggested in a Georgia speech that the federal government could face a "shutdown" in September. The House subsequently passed a short-term funding bill to maintain operations through December 4.
