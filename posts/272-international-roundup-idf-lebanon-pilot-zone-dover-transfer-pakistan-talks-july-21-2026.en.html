⚡ July 21 International Politics: IDF Begins South Lebanon Pilot Zone Withdrawal, Trump to Attend Dignified Transfer, Iran Envoys in Pakistan for Ceasefire Talks

1. IDF Launches South Lebanon Safe-Zone Withdrawal, Lebanese Army Deploys to First Village

A major diplomatic initiative moved from paper to practice on Tuesday as the Israel Defense Forces (IDF) began implementing the South Lebanon "pilot zone" withdrawal plan. The Lebanese army deployed troops to the village of Zawtar al-Gharbiyeh in the Nabatieh district on the morning of July 21 — the first location under the new security framework.

The U.S. State Department confirmed Monday that pilot zone operations had begun in the villages of Froun, Srifa, and Zawtar al-Gharbiyeh under the Trilateral Framework agreement signed by Lebanon, Israel, and the United States on June 26. The framework calls for a phased Israeli withdrawal from southern Lebanon, with the Lebanese Armed Forces (LAF) assuming security responsibilities and overseeing the disarmament of Hezbollah.

"This milestone is a direct outcome of last week's discussions between Israel and Lebanon in Rome," State Department spokesman Tommy Pigott said, adding that the U.S. "will continue working closely with both parties to implement the Framework to a successful conclusion." The announcement coincided with Lebanese President Joseph Aoun's visit to Washington for a meeting with President Trump, where the two leaders are expected to discuss Hezbollah disarmament and full Israeli withdrawal.

The IDF confirmed in a statement that the "Safe Zone pilot program began today," with an Israeli official saying the military would "adjust its force posture in one of the pilot areas to enable the Lebanese Armed Forces to carry out their mission." Under the agreement, the LAF will assume "exclusive security responsibility" in pilot zones, clear non-state armed group weapons and infrastructure, and undergo U.S.-supervised verification.

Notably, Froun and Srifa were already free of Israeli troops before the announcement — Israel had prepared to hand over these positions ahead of the Rome talks. Zawtar al-Gharbiyeh, located on the edge of Israel's self-declared "security zone," required actual troop withdrawal and was the site of Tuesday's deployment.

Analysts view the pilot program as a critical confidence-building measure. Since hostilities with Hezbollah resumed on March 2, Israeli forces have pushed approximately 10 km (6 miles) into southern Lebanon, establishing a "Yellow Line" buffer zone. Israel currently occupies about one-fifth of Lebanese territory. If successful, the pilot program could pave the way for broader withdrawal across southern Lebanon.

2. Trump to Attend Dignified Transfer of Fallen Soldiers at Dover AFB Tonight

White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt confirmed Monday that President Trump will travel to Dover Air Force Base in Delaware on Tuesday evening to attend the dignified transfer of the three U.S. service members killed in Iranian strikes over the weekend.

The fallen soldiers are: 1st Lt. Tyler James Feehan, 25, of Ewa Beach, Hawaii, and Pvt. Isabella Gonzales, 19, of Carrollton, Texas — both killed Friday in an Iranian missile attack on Muwaffaq Salti Air Base in Jordan; and Sgt. Michael Emmanuel Swinton, 30, of Fayetteville, North Carolina, who was killed during a controlled detonation of a downed Iranian drone at Erbil Air Base in Iraq. The Pentagon has identified all three.

Trump wrote on Truth Social: "Every time Iran kills an American Soldier they will pay for that killing many times over! This directive has been given to Secretary of Defense Hegseth, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff General Caine, and every military leader."

This will be the fourth dignified transfer Trump has attended during his second term. He previously described witnessing the transfer as "the toughest thing I have to do" as president. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth posted: "Their sacrifice only stiffens our resolve."

The U.S. military death toll in the war against Iran has now reached 17 since hostilities began on February 28.

3. Nearly 100 U.S. Troops Injured in Iranian Strikes This Month

Nearly 100 American service members have been injured in multiple Iranian strikes on bases across the Middle East this month — even before the deadly attack on the Jordanian air base over the weekend, U.S. officials told CBS News. Most injured personnel have returned to duty, two officials said.

According to U.S. Central Command data, 427 U.S. personnel have been wounded in the war so far. The Pentagon said the full breakdown has not been publicly disclosed for operational security reasons, noting many injuries were minor or non-combat related. The New York Times reported that Iran had repeatedly struck U.S. bases in Jordan before the deadly weekend attack, wounding dozens and damaging multiple helicopters — casualties the Pentagon had not previously disclosed.

Analysts warn that while both Washington and Tehran maintain diplomatic channels, every battlefield casualty risks overwhelming those efforts. The State Department has issued a "worldwide caution" alert urging increased vigilance for American citizens abroad.

4. Iran Interior Minister Visits Pakistan as Mediators Push 10-Day Ceasefire

On the diplomatic front, Iranian Interior Minister Eskandar Momeni arrived in Islamabad on Monday leading a high-level delegation for talks with Pakistan's army chief Field Marshal Asim Munir and expected to meet Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif. Pakistan has served as a key mediator throughout the conflict, having brokered last month's interim memorandum of understanding between the U.S. and Iran.

Iranian Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmail Baghaei confirmed that mediators have proposed a 10-day ceasefire to both the U.S. and Iran, aimed at reviving the June agreement. Axios reported that the Trump administration is studying the ceasefire option while simultaneously preparing for the possibility of full-scale war.

Sources say the ceasefire proposal includes provisions for easing restrictions on shipping through the Strait of Hormuz and suspending military operations by both sides. However, with three U.S. soldiers killed over the weekend, hawkish voices within the Trump administration are gaining ground. Multiple senior U.S. officials said Trump is expected to decide within days whether to escalate military operations to "full-scale combat."

Analysts describe a paradoxical situation: battlefield and diplomatic tracks are moving in opposite directions simultaneously. Neither side wants to completely close the negotiation door, but each new casualty erodes diplomatic space.

5. Kuwait Power and Water Desalination Plants Damaged by Iranian Strikes

Kuwaiti authorities reported Tuesday that Iranian attacks the previous evening damaged electrical power stations and water desalination plants, forcing some generation units offline. The Ministry of Electricity, Water and Renewable Energy described this as the fourth consecutive day of "fierce Iranian aggression attacks," with fires breaking out Monday evening at multiple facilities. Damage assessments and repairs are underway.

The announcement came after Iran's Revolutionary Guard Corps claimed to have destroyed a U.S. radar system in Bahrain's Muharraq and a U.S. Patriot air defense system in Riffa, Bahrain, using missiles and drones. Iran's army also claimed to have struck U.S. HIMARS missile systems at Camp Arifjan in Kuwait with ground-to-ground missiles.

U.S. Central Command completed its latest round of strikes against Iran at 9 p.m. ET Monday — the 10th consecutive night of attacks since July 11. The five-hour operation targeted Iranian military command centers, maritime capabilities, missile and drone launch sites, and air defense systems, according to CENTCOM.

Simultaneously, Yemen's Houthi militia has announced a maritime blockade of Saudi Arabia, threatening the Bab el-Mandeb strait — the chokepoint connecting the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden through which 11% of global maritime trade passes. With both the Strait of Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb under threat, two of the Middle East's main oil export routes are now at risk.

6. Trump Hits Canada with 50% Tariffs, UK Gets New Prime Minister

On the broader international stage, President Trump on Monday imposed 50% tariffs on most Canadian goods — including automobiles, aluminum, alcohol, dairy products, and hockey equipment — sharply escalating the trade dispute between the two nations. Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney vowed to "intensify" trade talks in response.

Meanwhile, the United Kingdom completed a leadership transition as Andy Burnham, former mayor of Manchester, succeeded Keir Starmer as the country's seventh prime minister in a decade. Burnham, considered more left-leaning than Starmer, took office after Starmer was forced to resign following his Labour Party's heavy losses in local elections.

In a related development, New York City Mayor Zohran Mamdani stated publicly that he is investigating the possibility of arresting Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu during his expected visit to New York for the U.N. General Assembly in September, calling Netanyahu "a war criminal who has been charged by the International Criminal Court."
