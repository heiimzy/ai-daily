⚡ July 24 Financial Roundup: Global PMI Data Delivers Mixed Signals, 30-Year Yield at 2007-Level Streak, Defense Stocks Rally, Gold Crashes Below $4,050

1. Global Flash PMI Day: Eurozone Edges Up, Germany Stalls, France Still in Contraction

Friday July 24 was a global Flash PMI super-day, with the Eurozone, Germany, France, the UK, and Japan all releasing their July preliminary purchasing managers' index data. The results paint a picture of deepening divergence and persistent fragility.

The HCOB Eurozone Composite PMI Output Index rose to 51.0 from June's 50.6, hitting an 11-month high and slightly beating the 50.8 consensus. The Manufacturing PMI climbed to 49.8 (from 49.5), its highest in 36 months but still below the 50.0 neutral threshold. The Services PMI edged up to 51.2 from 50.5. Despite the headline improvement, Hamburg Commercial Bank Chief Economist Cyrus de la Rubia cautioned that the PMI reading "is consistent with GDP growth of just 0.2% — this is a crawl, not a recovery."

In Germany, the Composite PMI slipped to 50.3 from 50.4, effectively stalled. Manufacturing PMI at 49.2 (36-month high but sub-50) and Services at 50.1 barely above contraction. Europe's largest economy continues to stagnate at the zero-growth line.

France remained the weakest link. Its Composite PMI stayed below 50 for an 11th consecutive month, though the pace of contraction moderated to the slowest in nearly a year. Domestic political gridlock and looming fiscal austerity continued to weigh on business confidence, with French firms' 12-month outlook deteriorating sharply in July.

The UK's Manufacturing PMI came in at 52.1 (prior 52.5), while Services PMI recovered to 49.4 from 48.8 but remained in contraction territory. UK June retail sales fell 0.3% month-on-month (vs. -0.1% expected, prior +1.2%), missing estimates as consumers tightened spending under the weight of 5.25% interest rates and elevated inflation. UK 10-year gilt yields pushed toward 4.9%.

Japan's Manufacturing PMI printed at 54.9, marking six straight months of expansion with output growth at its fastest since January 2022. However, USD/JPY shattered through 163.85 — a 40-year low — as the yen's collapse erased gains from export earnings. Japan posted a $2.5 billion trade deficit in June as energy import costs surged.

"The Eurozone is experiencing not a recovery but a feeble climb out of a very deep hole. Manufacturing is still contracting, and services are barely expanding. When the energy shock hits again, this fragile equilibrium could shatter at any moment." — Cyrus de la Rubia, Hamburg Commercial Bank

2. 30-Year Treasury Yield Posts Longest 5%+ Streak Since 2007

The US Treasury market is undergoing its most severe yield surge in nearly two decades. The 10-year yield hit 4.707% on Thursday — its highest since January 2025. The 30-year bond yield rose to 5.19%, and has now closed above 5% for 27 consecutive trading days — the longest such streak since 2007, before the Global Financial Crisis. The 2-year yield climbed to 4.37%, a 17-month high.

Three forces are driving the repricing:

Oil shock igniting inflation expectations. Brent crude spiked 7% to $100.69/barrel, WTI surged 6.2% to $92.19. Houthi militants struck two Saudi tankers in the Red Sea, and Trump threatened a "massive attack" on Iran. RBC Capital Markets warns that a full regional war could push Brent past $128, with some models targeting the 2008 record of $146.

Labor market overheating. US initial jobless claims plunged to 187,000 — the lowest since 1969, far below the 212,000 consensus. The ultra-tight labor market gives Fed hawks powerful ammunition to argue for rate hikes.

AI capex panic. Alphabet's quarterly capital spending doubled to nearly $45 billion, with full-year 2026 capex guidance raised to $205 billion. Tesla posted negative free cash flow for the first time in over two years. The market is deeply questioning whether mega-cap AI investments will deliver adequate returns.

CME FedWatch now prices an 80%+ probability of a September rate hike (up from 52% a week ago) and a 38% chance of a July hike (up from 12%). Nomura's Charlie McElligott warned the rates market is "trying to front-run the front-runners" in what he called a "mini market tantrum" — signaling that "on-hold is no longer sufficient."

3. Defense Stocks Surge: Lockheed Martin +10.5% All-Time High

Amid the tech wreck, defense stocks were Thursday's biggest winners. Lockheed Martin surged 10.5% to an all-time record high, simultaneously raising its full-year 2026 revenue and profit guidance. RTX (formerly Raytheon Technologies) jumped 7.3% on a strong earnings beat. The S&P 500 Industrials sector gained 1.77% — the only major sector in the green on Thursday.

The defense rally reflects the rapidly escalating US-Iran conflict. The US has now conducted 12 consecutive nights of airstrikes across Iran. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth disclosed to Congress this week that the war has cost $37.5 billion to date, with the administration requesting an additional $67 billion in supplemental funding. Analysts project the FY2027 defense budget could surpass $1.5 trillion if the conflict becomes protracted.

European defense stocks also rallied, with Rheinmetall gaining 4.2% and BAE Systems rising 3.8% as NATO members accelerate military spending commitments.

4. Cross-Asset Repricing: Gold Crashes Below $4,050, Silver Plunges 4%

Precious metals suffered a rare and violent selloff amid dollar strength and rising real yields. Spot gold crashed more than $80 to $4,049/oz (-1.95%), its lowest in nearly three weeks. Silver was hit even harder, tumbling 3.44% to $57.63/oz.

Notably, gold failed to act as a safe haven during the Middle East escalation — instead falling in lockstep with equities. This breaks the traditional narrative of gold as the ultimate避险 asset. Analysts pointed to the rising opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold when real rates are surging and the dollar is strengthening, forcing investors to liquidate gold for cash and USD.

The US Dollar Index rose 0.3% to 101.44. EUR/USD slipped to 1.1377, GBP/USD to 1.3315. USD/JPY surged to 163.85 — the yen's weakest level in 40 years. In Asian trading Friday morning, the dollar held near 101.40 while the yen continued to weaken.

Crypto markets also sold off. Bitcoin fell below $65,000 to ~$64,800, Ethereum dropped under $1,900. The transmission chain was textbook: Middle East conflict drives oil higher → oil reignites inflation expectations → inflation fears lift rate hike odds → higher rates compress all risk asset valuations.

5. ECB Holds but Lagarde Hints at September Tightening; UK Data Disappoints

The European Central Bank kept its three key interest rates unchanged on Thursday, with the deposit facility rate remaining at 2.25%. However, President Christine Lagarde struck a notably hawkish tone in her press conference, stating that "uncertainty remains very high" and that the "full inflationary impact of the energy shock has not yet materialized." Markets interpreted the statement as keeping September rate hike options open, pricing roughly 35% odds of a move.

In the UK, June retail sales fell 0.3% month-on-month (vs. -0.1% expected), following a 1.2% gain in May. Clothing and footwear saw the steepest declines, while department stores also suffered. The data underscores the toll that 5.25% interest rates are taking on consumer spending.

European equity markets closed deep in the red: Germany's DAX -1.56%, France's CAC 40 -1.6%, UK's FTSE 100 -0.73%, and the pan-European STOXX 600 -1.3%. STMicroelectronics plunged 17.5% after a disastrous earnings miss, dragging the European technology sector to the bottom of the board.

Looking ahead, all eyes turn to the Fed's July 29 FOMC meeting. With oil firmly above $100 and the labor market at 1969-level tightness, the Fed faces an excruciating choice — hike and risk crushing an already fragile equity market, or hold and risk unanchoring inflation expectations. The oil-rate-asset triple helix enters its most critical week yet.
