⚡ TSMC Reports Record $22B Quarterly Profit as AI Demand Surges, Yet Chip Stocks Plunge Globally

1. TSMC Delivers the Strongest Quarter in Its History

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) reported its second-quarter 2026 earnings on July 16, delivering the most profitable quarter in its 39-year history. Quarterly consolidated revenue reached $40.2 billion, up 36% year-over-year, landing exactly at the top end of the company's guidance range of $39.0-40.2 billion. Net profit hit NT$706.56 billion (approximately $22 billion), surging 77.4% year-over-year — marking the ninth consecutive quarter of double-digit profit growth and the fifth straight record.

Gross margin climbed to 67.7%, exceeding the company's own guidance ceiling of 65.5%-67.5% and reaching the highest level in over two decades. Operating margin hit 60.3%, well above the guided range of 56.5%-58.5%. Diluted EPS came in at NT$27.25 ($4.31 per ADR unit), surpassing analyst expectations of $3.80 by over 13%. The message from Taipei was unambiguous: AI chip demand is not just holding — it is accelerating.

2. Full-Year Outlook Raised Again, AI Driving a 'Super Cycle'

Alongside the blowout quarter, TSMC management delivered an unusually aggressive upward revision to full-year guidance. The 2026 full-year USD revenue growth outlook was lifted from "above 30%" (provided in Q1) to "slightly above 40%" — the second upward revision this year. Q3 revenue guidance of $44.6-45.8 billion implies approximately 37% year-over-year growth, suggesting the acceleration is still building.

"Our conviction in the multi-year AI megatrend remains very, very high," TSMC Chairman and CEO C.C. Wei told analysts on the earnings call. High-Performance Computing (HPC) — the segment anchored by AI accelerators — now accounts for 66% of total wafer revenue, up from 60% a year ago, while smartphone share has fallen to 22%. The company's 3-nanometer process contributed 30% of wafer revenue, while 2-nanometer chips delivered 3% in their first full commercial quarter.

3. $100 Billion More for Arizona: Largest Foreign Direct Investment in US History

TSMC announced an additional $100 billion investment in its Arizona operations, adding four or more new fabrication plants covering both front-end wafer manufacturing and back-end advanced packaging. This brings the company's total committed US investment to $265 billion — one of the largest foreign direct investments in American history. The company also raised its 2026 capital expenditure budget from $52-56 billion to $60-64 billion, with 70-80% flowing to advanced process technologies.

CEO Wei said spending "in the next three years will be even more significantly higher than the past three years," primarily directed at 2-nanometer capacity expansion and CoWoS (Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate) advanced packaging infrastructure — the technology that physically integrates AI accelerators with high-bandwidth memory.

4. Agentic AI Creates a Second Demand Channel for CPUs

Wei introduced a market-moving thesis on the earnings call: the rise of agentic AI is restructuring data center demand and creating an incremental silicon channel. Unlike the GPU-centric training era (2022-mid 2025), agentic AI workloads require sustained, always-on CPU processing for tool calling, memory management, API orchestration, and multi-step reasoning loops.

"The emergence of agentic AI is leading to a resurgence in the role of CPUs in AI data centers, which drives more silicon demand in addition to AI accelerators," Wei said. "No matter what CPU approach is taken — x86, Arm-based, or RISC-V architecture — they are almost all TSMC's customers." TrendForce analysis suggests agentic applications may require four times as many CPU cores per gigawatt of data center power as training-era infrastructure, potentially shifting CPU-to-GPU ratios from 1:8 toward 1:1 for some workloads.

5. The 'Sell the News' Paradox: Global Chip Stocks Routed

Despite TSMC's historic earnings report, global semiconductor stocks suffered a significant selloff on July 16-17. The Philadelphia SE Semiconductor Index crashed 4%. TSMC's ADR fell approximately 2.3% in after-hours trading. Nvidia dropped 2.4% — the single heaviest weight on the S&P 500 — while AMD declined 2.7%, Intel fell 1.9%, and Micron tumbled 5.6%.

The damage was most severe in South Korea, where Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix dragged the Kospi index down 6.4% in a single session — one of several 5%+ drops in recent weeks. Japan's Nikkei fell 2.8% and China's Shanghai Composite lost 1.8%.

"The revenue dynamics remain robust as the AI boom continues. The core debate has shifted from whether AI demand is real to whether current equity valuations already reflect the full upside of the cycle." — Adam Crisafulli, Vital Knowledge

6. Memory Crisis Deepens: 40+ Week Lead Times

Beyond the headline numbers, a broader semiconductor supply crisis is spreading into the enterprise server market. Inventec, one of Taiwan's largest server motherboard manufacturers, warned this week that memory and CPU supply gaps are widening and may dent Q3 server shipments. Large DRAM order lead times have extended beyond 40 weeks, CPU procurement backlogs are running at approximately 22 weeks, and distributor quote-validity windows have narrowed to 48-72 hours.

Memory stocks have been the hardest hit in the AI supply chain. Micron Technology has fallen approximately 30% from its late-June all-time high. SK Hynix is trading below its July 10 IPO price. SanDisk has dropped 35% from its peak. Despite record revenues and HBM supply being completely sold out, investors fear that memory — a historically cyclical commodity — is approaching a pricing peak as new capacity comes online.

SK Hynix CEO Kwak Noh-jung told Reuters at the company's Nasdaq debut that 2027 would be "the worst year in the industry's history from the supply perspective." Micron CEO Sanjay Mehrotra warned supply tightness will continue into 2027 and said the company is "more than sold out." Analysts estimate DRAM suppliers are meeting only 75-80% of demand in H2 2026, with fulfillment potentially falling to 60% in 2027.

7. Jensen Huang in Tokyo: A 30-Year Reunion and Japan's AI Ambitions

This week also saw Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang's Tokyo visit, marking a strategic pivot toward Japan as a cornerstone of Nvidia's physical AI blueprint. At the former site of the Sega Game Center in Akihabara, Huang shared the stage with former Sega President Shoichiro Irimajiri — 30 years after Sega's $5 million investment saved a then-bankrupt Nvidia. Today, Nvidia's market cap exceeds $5 trillion.

Huang announced a series of partnerships: 44 Japanese companies launching a sovereign AI foundation model project with Tokyo purchasing 27,500 Rubin GPUs; expanded collaborations with Toyota on autonomous driving; robotics partnerships with Fanuc, Yaskawa Electric, and Kawasaki Heavy Industries; and a strategic alliance with the Noetra consortium (Sony, Honda, and others) to build a national-level AI super-factory.

Dismissing AI bubble concerns, Huang stated: "We are still very far from an AI bubble, and demand is extremely strong. We need to build infrastructure for at least ten years."

8. Outlook: The Valuation Reckoning and What Comes Next

TSMC's landmark quarter has simultaneously proven the reality of AI-driven demand and exposed the market's anxiety over fully-priced valuations. With annual CapEx pushing past $60 billion, investors must now believe that future revenue growth can justify this unprecedented spending intensity. The bull case rests on agentic AI creating multi-year incremental demand; the bear case warns that diminishing returns on capital investment are already being priced in.

Three key events will shape the semiconductor landscape in the coming weeks: the DeepSeek V4 stable release and legacy API cutoff on July 24; Google's long-delayed Gemini 3.5 Pro launch (now rumored for late July/August); and the real-world deployment trajectory of agentic AI workloads. With CoWoS packaging lead times at 52-78 weeks, the physical constraints on AI chip supply remain the single most binding factor on the industry's growth trajectory.
