1. Tesla's Chip Ambition Realized: Musk Announces Terafab Project Launch in 7 Days

Elon Musk announced on X that Tesla's Terafab AI chip manufacturing project will launch in seven days, marking the company's most significant expansion beyond its core electric vehicle business. The announcement confirms that Musk's long-discussed ambition to build Tesla's own chip fabrication plant is finally entering its countdown phase.

The Ultimate Vertical Integration Ambition

While Musk did not disclose specific details about the facility, previous statements paint a clear picture: Terafab is envisioned as a Gigafactory-scale facility dedicated exclusively to AI chip manufacturing, aiming for complete vertical integration from chip design to fabrication. Musk has repeatedly stated that existing chip suppliers cannot meet Tesla's demand — he aims for an annual production capacity of 100 to 200 billion chips, which would make it one of the largest chip factories in the world.

Currently, most advanced chip manufacturing is concentrated among a handful of foundries — TSMC, Samsung, and Intel. Tesla currently procures chips from Nvidia while designing its own in-house chips, with manufacturing agreements already in place with Samsung and TSMC. Terafab represents a strategic leap from "design-only" to "design-and-manufacture," potentially breaking Tesla's dependence on external foundries.

From AI5 to AI9: Tesla's Chip Roadmap

Tesla is developing its fifth-generation AI chip, AI5, targeting mass production in 2027 with performance 50x that of the current AI4 — 9x the memory capacity and 10x the raw computing power. AI5 will power everything from Tesla's electric vehicles to Optimus humanoid robots, AI training, and data centers.

AI6 is already in early development for Optimus robots and data center computing, AI7 will target space-grade AI computation, and plans extend through AI8 and AI9 with a 9-month design cycle per generation. Industry analysts suggest Terafab might involve licensing agreements with Intel or TSMC, providing capital for production lines. However, some experts have cautioned that semiconductor manufacturing presents enormous barriers, and Musk's unconventional approach of challenging cleanroom standards may face significant technical hurdles.

2. Peking University's World-First Neuromorphic Dynamics Chip: 478x Faster Than GPU for Brain Reconstruction

A team led by Professor Yang Yuchao from Peking University's School of Integrated Circuits, in collaboration with Researcher Song Zhitang from the Chinese Academy of Sciences' Shanghai Institute of Microsystem and Information Technology, has successfully developed the world's first neuromorphic dynamics system chip based on phase-change memristors. This thumb-sized chip compresses single-step neural dynamics computation to just 2.12 milliseconds, breaking through a real-time computing bottleneck that has constrained the field for half a century. The results were published on July 3 in the journal Science.

A "Compute-In-Memory" Paradigm: Moving Beyond von Neumann

Traditional computing architectures suffer from a fundamental bottleneck: the separation of storage and computation. Solving neural dynamics equations requires massive intermediate variables to shuttle constantly between memory and processor — most time is wasted on data movement rather than actual computation. Yang's team solved this by mapping neural network weights onto the multi-level conductance states of phase-change memory, performing matrix multiplication and addition operations entirely within the same array.

The chip, fabricated on a 40nm process, demonstrated 478.18x speedup over advanced GPUs in high-fidelity brain cortex surface reconstruction tasks. Remarkably, the team exploited the "conductance drift" phenomenon of phase-change memory — traditionally considered a defect — as a computational feature, enabling physical laws themselves to "run" differential equations directly.

New Hardware Base for Brain-Computer Interfaces and Alzheimer's Screening

This breakthrough opens new possibilities for brain-computer interfaces and neurological disease diagnosis. Personalized, dynamic brain digital twins become feasible, with intraoperative neural navigation and early Alzheimer's screening gaining a real-time hardware foundation. Yang emphasized that this chip represents not just a technical improvement but a fundamental shift in computing paradigm — humanity is moving beyond the shadow of von Neumann architecture into an era where "storage is computation."

3. World's First Million-Level Optical Tweezer Array Chip: A "Base Revolution" for Neutral Atom Quantum Computing

Shanghai-based Xuanxiang Technology has successfully developed the world's first metasurface chip capable of generating million-level optical tweezer arrays for neutral atom quantum computing. In collaboration with quantum computing company Zhongqi Wuliang, the team achieved a complete closed loop from chip fabrication to real-platform verification. The chip generates millions of optical tweezer sites within a working area of just 4mm diameter — the largest reported metasurface optical tweezer array to date.

"Lane-Changing to Overtake": A Leap from Thousands to Millions

Neutral atom quantum computing works by trapping individual cooled atoms in vacuum using optical tweezer arrays, then exciting them with lasers to perform quantum gate operations through atomic interactions. The number of optical tweezers directly determines how many qubits a quantum computer can simultaneously manipulate. Historically, optical tweezer array size was limited by generation methods — conventional approaches rely on spatial light modulators (SLMs) or acousto-optic deflectors, with even the best SLMs capped at around 50,000 high-uniformity traps. In September 2025, a Caltech team using two SLMs managed to generate only about 12,000 optical tweezer sites.

Xuanxiang's solution uses metasurface chips, where pixel sizes can be as small as a few hundred nanometers — far smaller than the operating wavelength — integrating hundreds of millions of nano-scale optical units in a fingertip-sized area. The result: one laser beam in, a million-level optical tweezer array out. This is a classic "lane-changing to overtake" strategy — while the global quantum computing community was still struggling to generate more tweezer sites with conventional optics, the Chinese team used flat optics to jump from thousands to millions.

The team successfully captured 6,100 neutral atoms, setting a new scale record for neutral atom qubit arrays. A million-level optical tweezer array theoretically supports million-qubit scales — an order of magnitude that superconducting and ion trap approaches cannot match in the near term.

Industry Collaboration: Xuanxiang + Zhongqi Wuliang

Xuanxiang Technology handled chip R&D while Zhongqi Wuliang provided the neutral atom experimental platform and system-level verification. The two teams completed multiple rounds of collaborative validation covering optical coupling, array characterization, and platform adaptation. Another Shanghai-based neutral atom company, Taiyi Liangsheng, has also adopted the chip series for experiments. The quantum computing community widely views this "base-level" leap as opening an unprecedented door for scaling up neutral atom quantum computing.
