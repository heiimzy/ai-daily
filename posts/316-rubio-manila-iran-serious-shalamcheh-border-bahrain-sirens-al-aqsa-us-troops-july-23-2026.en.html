⚡ July 23 International Politics: Rubio Says Iran 'Not Serious' in Manila; US 12th Night Strike Kills 2 at Shalamcheh Border; Iran Destroys Patriot, THAAD; Bahrain Air Raid Sirens; 2,300 Settlers Storm Al-Aqsa

1. Rubio in Manila: Iran 'Not Serious About Making a Deal,' 'Head for an Eye'

US Secretary of State Marco Rubio held a press conference on the sidelines of the ASEAN Foreign Ministers' Meeting in Manila on Thursday, delivering a series of tough statements on the US-Iran conflict, the Strait of Hormuz situation, and the newly signed US-Saudi nuclear deal.

"I just don't think they're serious about making a deal," Rubio told reporters when asked about Iran's diplomatic signals. "The problem with them is you can't make a deal with people unless they're going to keep it," he said, accusing Tehran of routinely "breaking" or "changing" agreements after entering into them. "So now they're paying the price for it, and maybe they'll change."

When pressed on Iranian officials' "eye for an eye" doctrine, Rubio countered that Trump's approach was "a head for an eye." "They will pay a very heavy price for the things they are doing. They are already paying a heavy price," he said, claiming Iran's industrial infrastructure was being "decimated" with "billions" of dollars in damage.

On the US-Saudi civil nuclear agreement, Rubio confirmed the pact would be sent to Congress for review. "Even when the official announcement is made, it will have to go through a congressional notification process," he said, calling Saudi Arabia "a strategic ally." "When countries decide on a peaceful civil nuclear programme, we'd prefer them to do it with us rather than with some other country."

Turning to Yemen, Rubio expressed hope that the Houthis would de-escalate, claiming they had been "snookered by Iran" into the conflict.

2. US 12th Consecutive Night of Strikes: Shalamcheh Border Crossing Hit, 2 Killed

US Central Command (CENTCOM) completed its 12th consecutive night of strikes against Iran in the early hours of July 23, with the scope and intensity of the bombardment expanding. Iranian state media reported that US missiles struck the passenger terminal of the Shalamcheh border crossing in Khuzestan province, killing two people and wounding 11 others.

The Shalamcheh crossing is a critical land gateway connecting Iran and Iraq — serving not only as a major transit point for Shia pilgrims but also as a vital trade corridor. The strike on civilian infrastructure has drawn international attention. Iran's Red Crescent confirmed the death toll and said the wounded had been transported to nearby hospitals.

CENTCOM stated that the strikes targeted "Iranian military operations centers, maritime capabilities, aircraft hangars, drone storage facilities, and military logistics infrastructure" aimed at "further degrading Iran's ability to threaten commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz." Since resuming the naval blockade of Iranian ports, CENTCOM said it has redirected nine commercial vessels and disabled one.

US forces also struck a storage facility in Ahvaz and military targets in Kermanshah for the second consecutive night. Iranian media additionally reported a US missile strike on Larak Island, strategically located in the Strait of Hormuz.

President Trump had earlier issued an ultimatum on Truth Social: "From this point forward, any time the Islamic Republic of Iran shoots at a ship in the Strait of Hormuz, whether it be by Missile, Rocket, Drone, or any other device or weapon, the United States will bomb and destroy ONE BRIDGE OR POWER PLANT, including those located next to, or in, the Capital City of Tehran."

3. Iran Strikes Back: IRGC Claims Destruction of Patriot, THAAD Systems; Jordan Intercepts Missiles

In retaliation for continued US airstrikes, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) and Army launched the 23rd phase of "Operation Lightning" on July 23, targeting US military facilities in Kuwait and Jordan.

The IRGC claimed its forces destroyed a Patriot missile defense system, an MQ-9 Reaper drone hangar, and a military equipment warehouse at Kuwait's Ali al-Salem Air Base. It also asserted that helicopter maintenance facilities and communications infrastructure were damaged.

In Jordan, the IRGC claimed to have destroyed a THAAD (Terminal High Altitude Area Defense) radar system. "Your Mujahideen brothers in the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, in punishment of the child-killing American army and in response to the repeated aggressions against the territory of the Islamic Republic of Iran, destroyed a radar of the American THAAD missile defense system with lightning strikes on American bases," the IRGC said.

The Iranian Army separately announced it had struck three US bases in Kuwait using drones: ammunition and logistics depots at Camp Doha, fuel tanks at Ali Al Salem base, and an ammunition depot at Camp Arifjan.

Jordan's Armed Forces reported that its air defense systems intercepted 4 Iranian ballistic missiles and 6 drones over the past 24 hours, with 2 additional missiles falling in uninhabited areas without causing casualties or property damage.

4. Bahrain Sounds Nationwide Air Raid Sirens; Regional Alert Level Raised

At 09:16 GMT on July 23, Bahrain's Ministry of the Interior issued an emergency alert, sounding "hostile aircraft" air raid sirens nationwide and urging citizens and residents to remain calm and "head to the nearest safe location." This marks the first nationwide alert since the escalation of the US-Iran conflict.

Bahrain hosts the US Navy's Fifth Fleet headquarters, making it a strategically vital location. Earlier, Iranian forces had struck an Amazon data infrastructure facility in Bahrain, which Tehran claimed served as a key US military information exchange node.

Saudi Arabia's eastern city of Dammam also issued shelter warnings. Saudi authorities later declared the danger had passed without providing details. Iranian military sources warned that if the US strikes Iranian nuclear facilities or bridges and power plants, Iran would respond by "striking infrastructure and bridges across the region, including energy facilities in which the United States has interests," and prevent the export of "even a single drop of oil."

Brent crude continued its upward trajectory, approaching $98 per barrel — a seven-week high — as the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz combined with Houthi threats in the Red Sea created a "double blockade" on global oil supplies.

5. Over 2,300 Settlers Storm Al-Aqsa Mosque Led by Ben-Gvir

On the morning of July 23, during the Jewish holiday of Tisha B'Av, more than 2,300 Jewish settlers stormed the Al-Aqsa Mosque compound in East Jerusalem, led by Israeli National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir. The Islamic Waqf administration confirmed the figure, calling it one of the largest incursions in years.

Palestinian officials reported that Israeli forces imposed tight restrictions at the mosque's gates, preventing large numbers of Palestinian worshippers from entering, turning away students, and assaulting several worshippers. The Palestinian administration of the Jerusalem Governorate described Ben-Gvir's entry as a "dangerous escalation" reflecting "official Israeli backing for actions previously associated with hard-right groups."

Jordan's Foreign Ministry spokesman Fouad Majali warned that such actions violate the site's historical and legal status quo and urged the international community to pressure Israel to halt violations against Islamic and Christian holy sites in Jerusalem. Hamas issued a similar condemnation, warning that "our people and our resistance will not stand idly by in the face of the desecration of our holy sites."

The Al-Aqsa Mosque compound, known to Jews as the Temple Mount, is the third holiest site in Islam and the holiest in Judaism. Its status quo arrangement is overseen by Jordan as custodian. Non-Muslims may visit during specified hours but are not permitted to pray. Ben-Gvir and other far-right officials have repeatedly called for changing this arrangement.

According to the report, US transport aircraft have deployed special operations forces to the Middle East in recent days. Under "Operation Epic Fury," these forces have been conducting search and rescue missions but are also capable of "executing a range of other tasks." Multiple fighter squadrons have been positioned across the region — F-16s from Germany's Spangdahlem Air Base and F-35s from the UK's RAF Lakenheath have arrived. Bombers based in the US and the UK remain on heightened alert.

A US official said more than 150 medical personnel recently arrived at Landstuhl Regional Medical Center in Germany, the primary facility for treating US casualties from Middle East operations. The US currently maintains over 50,000 troops in the region, with the Navy operating 17 vessels including two carrier strike groups, 11 destroyers, and a Marine Expeditionary Unit.

Military analysts said the buildup does not necessarily signal an imminent large-scale ground operation but suggests the White House is preparing for various escalation scenarios. Former CENTCOM commander Joseph Votel noted the deployment aims to give the president and defense secretary "maximum flexibility" with more options on the table.

The US-Iran war, now entering its fifth month since late February, has shown no signs of de-escalation. Trump said after meeting with families of fallen service members that the US is "not finished" with Iran. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth testified before Congress that the war has cost $37.5 billion so far, with 18 US service members killed. Iran's Health Ministry reports 53 civilian deaths and nearly 600 wounded from US airstrikes.
