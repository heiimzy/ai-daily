⚡ July 21 International Politics Deep Dive: Mediators Propose 10-Day Iran-US Ceasefire, China-Russia Warships Fire Live Rounds Inside Japan's EEZ for First Time, US Sends Contradictory Signals on War

1. Mediators Float 10-Day Ceasefire as Trump Faces Pivotal Choice

International mediation efforts to end the US-Iran conflict achieved a significant breakthrough on July 21. Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmaeil Baghaei confirmed that Tehran has received a proposal from mediators for a 10-day ceasefire. Pakistan and Qatar jointly presented the plan, urging both sides to return to their positions before July 9 as a first step toward reviving the stalled US-Iran Memorandum of Understanding.

According to an exclusive Axios report, President Trump is facing the most consequential decision since the US-Iran war began on February 28. US and Israeli officials reportedly see only two viable paths forward: a 10-day ceasefire aimed at reopening the Strait of Hormuz, or a massive joint military campaign with Israel to force Tehran's capitulation. The coming days will determine which path prevails.

"Diplomacy and war are both tools for achieving goals — saying 'defense and diplomacy cannot go together' is incorrect," Baghaei stated at a press conference. He emphasized that Iran has never unilaterally violated the MoU and that it was US breaches that forced Tehran to take countermeasures.

Sources told CNN that the ceasefire proposal includes lifting restrictions on the Strait of Hormuz. However, the White House stopped short of endorsing the plan. "Right now, President Trump is focused on making Iran pay for their violations of the MOU and their continued acts of terrorism in the Strait of Hormuz," a US official said.

Secretary of State Marco Rubio, speaking before departing for the ASEAN summit in Manila, said: "The United States is always open to a diplomatic solution. We've tried multiple times with Iran and we'll continue to try. But right now, their behavior is what we're responding to — they're launching missiles and drones against ships."

2. Contradictory Signals: Trump Floats 'Winding Down' as Pentagon Sends 2,500 More Marines

In a remarkable display of contradictory policy signals, President Trump posted on Truth Social on Friday evening that the US was considering "winding down" military operations against Iran. Within 12 hours, the Pentagon confirmed 2,500 additional Marines were shipping out on three amphibious assault vessels to the Middle East.

The total US troop presence in the region has now reached approximately 52,500. Reports indicate the US military is also planning to deploy additional F-16 and F-35 fighter jets along with refueling aircraft from European bases. A US official revealed that Trump held a White House situation room briefing last week on escalation options, including seizing Iran's Kharg Island oil export terminal and striking the "Gad Mountain" nuclear facility.

Trump escalated his rhetoric further on Monday, posting on Truth Social: "Every time Iran kills an American Soldier they will pay for that killing many times over!" He stated the directive had been communicated to Defense Secretary Hegseth, Joint Chiefs Chairman Keane, and "every leader" in the military.

The US has now completed its 10th consecutive night of strikes against Iran. CENTCOM announced the latest wave of operations completed at 9 p.m. ET on Sunday, targeting Iranian military command centers, maritime capabilities, missile and drone launch sites, and air defense systems. Iran's Revolutionary Guard Corps retaliated by striking US assets at Jordan's Aqaba airport, Kuwait's Camp Arifjan and Ali Al Salem Air Base, and Bahrain's Al Sakhir Air Base.

Two Greek-owned oil tankers were struck in the Strait of Hormuz on Monday, with the UK Maritime Trade Operations reporting a third vessel hit off the coast of Limah, Oman. The IRGC warned: "This passage is unsafe for the transport of chemical fertiliser or even a single drop of oil and gas." Data shows only four vessels transited the strait on Sunday, compared with a pre-war daily average of approximately 140.

3. State Department Issues Global Security Alert

The US State Department issued a worldwide security alert on July 20, advising all American citizens to exercise increased caution. The department warned that "heightened tensions in the Middle East" and a "complex security environment" create risks of "unexpected escalation." US citizens in the Middle East were advised to maintain high vigilance and prepare for flight cancellations, airspace closures, and other travel disruptions.

The State Department further warned that US diplomatic facilities, including those outside the Middle East, have become targets, and that Iran and its supporters may target other US interests and locations associated with the US and US citizens worldwide.

The US military has now confirmed 17 service members killed and more than 420 wounded since the conflict began. The three most recent fatalities include 1st Lieutenant Tyler James Feehan, 25, of Hawaii; Private Isabella Gonzales, 19, of Texas; and a third service member killed in northern Iraq during the controlled detonation of unexploded ordnance from a downed Iranian drone.

4. China-Russia Warships Conduct First-Ever Confirmed Live-Fire Drills Inside Japan's EEZ

Japan's Defense Minister Shinjiro Koizumi revealed on July 20 during a speech at the Farnborough International Airshow in the UK that Chinese and Russian naval vessels conducted live-fire exercises inside Japan's Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ) over the weekend. This marks the first time Japan's Defense Ministry has confirmed and publicly disclosed live-fire drills by foreign military vessels inside its EEZ.

According to the Defense Ministry's announcement on July 21, a Chinese Type 052C (Luyang III-class) guided-missile destroyer conducted machine-gun firing drills approximately 180 kilometers southwest of Okinotorishima island — Japan's southernmost territory — within its EEZ on July 19. A total of four vessels were operating in the area: two Chinese destroyers, one replenishment ship, and one Russian Steregushchiy-class frigate. A Japan Maritime Self-Defense Force destroyer maintained close surveillance throughout the exercise.

"We recognize this as part of a move by the two militaries toward strengthening their cooperation," Chief Cabinet Secretary Minoru Kihara said at a press conference. "Conducting live-fire drills within the EEZ could have endangered the navigation of nearby ships." Japan lodged a formal protest with China through diplomatic channels.

Koizumi, in his Farnborough speech, expressed alarm at deepening China-Russia military cooperation and signaled Japan's readiness to serve as a "hub" for defense cooperation among allies and like-minded nations. He also announced plans for a new "Defense Production and Technology Base Strategy" outlining the medium-to-long-term direction of Japan's defense industry.

The ministry released images of the Chinese vessel conducting the drill. The joint China-Russia flotilla was first spotted transiting through waters between Okinawa's main island and Miyako Island on July 18, heading into the Pacific, before being detected near Okinotorishima early on July 19.

5. EU's 21st Russia Sanctions Package Collapses as Member States Reach Breaking Point

European Union member state ambassadors failed to reach agreement on the 21st package of sanctions against Russia after four days of negotiations in Brussels, with diplomats describing the level of opposition as unprecedented. Since EU sanctions require unanimous approval, multiple member states effectively vetoed the proposed measures.

The proposed package included measures targeting Russian exports and the financial system, along with a mechanism to cap Russian crude oil export prices. However, a string of countries including Greece, France, Italy, Germany, Austria, and Portugal demanded exemptions or outright blocked the measures, citing damage to national champions and core economic interests.

Jacob Kirkegaard, senior fellow at the Bruegel think tank, said the EU has "reached the limit of Russia sanctions. We've now come to the end. People realize that what member state political leaders now see as their core interests are truly at risk. Leaders believe they can no longer be shamed, bullied, or otherwise coerced into conceding on these issues."

The collapse comes at a critical juncture as Ukraine presses Russia with long-range strikes against oil refineries and military targets. Western officials had hoped for a unified front to compel Putin to negotiate, but deepening internal divisions now threaten the entire sanctions architecture.
