⚡ July 13 Financial Roundup: Oil Surges 3% on Hormuz Closure, SK Hynix Korea Stock Plunges 6%, Gold Drops Below $4,100, Warsh Reshapes Fed with King and Andreessen

1. Hormuz Closure Ignites Oil Rally: Brent Jumps to $78.69

Oil prices surged in early Asian trading on Monday after Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) declared the Strait of Hormuz closed indefinitely on Sunday, responding to a third wave of U.S. airstrikes that hit over 300 military targets this week. Brent crude opened approximately 4.5% higher at $78.69 per barrel, while WTI crude rose over 3% to $73.66.

The Strait of Hormuz handles roughly 20 million barrels of crude and petroleum products daily — about 20% of global consumption and one-third of all seaborne oil trade. The IRGC stated: "Following this incident... the Strait of Hormuz will be closed until further notice and until the end of American interventions in this region." Commercial shipping tracking data shows only 11 vessels — 8 tankers and 3 cargo ships — transited the waterway in the past 24 hours, far below normal levels.

The U.S. Central Command countered that Iran "does not control the strait" and that the southern route remains open for lawful transit. Oman has drafted a proposal to manage Hormuz traffic while Pakistan and Qatar work to revive U.S.-Iran negotiations. Analysts warn a sustained closure could push Brent above $100, reigniting inflation.

2. SK Hynix: Record Nasdaq Debut, But Korea Stock Crashes 6.6%

SK Hynix dominated financial headlines this week with its landmark Nasdaq listing on July 10. The South Korean memory chip maker raised a record $26.5 billion — the largest ever U.S. listing by a foreign company — pricing American Depositary Receipts at $149 per share. The ADRs closed at $168.49 on debut, up 13.08% from the offer price and approximately 15.8% above the domestic closing price.

However, SK Hynix's domestic shares in Korea told a very different story. By 9:40 a.m. local time on July 13, the stock had plunged 144,000 won (-6.61%) to 2,036,000 won. Profit-taking and heightened geopolitical risk drove the sell-off, compounded by an arbitrage strategy flagged by UBS, which noted in a July 7 client memo that "buying ADS on the first day and shorting the domestic common stock is a natural choice."

Despite the short-term volatility, analysts maintain that fundamentals for AI memory and High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) demand remain robust. Markets are watching whether long-term foreign capital inflows via ADRs will eventually drive a revaluation of the stock.

3. Safe-Haven Gold Fails, Tumbles to $4,070 as Rate Hike Odds Hit 69%

Geopolitical turmoil typically boosts gold, but this time is different. Spot gold traded around $4,070 per ounce in early Asian hours on Monday, extending last week's 1.34% decline from above $4,120.

TD Securities' Bart Melek explained that while U.S.-Iran tensions have escalated, the resulting oil price surge is reinforcing inflation fears and strengthening expectations of tighter Fed policy. The CME FedWatch tool now shows a 69% probability of a September rate hike — a significant headwind for the non-yielding precious metal.

Among other precious metals, silver fell 0.7% to $59.56, platinum rose 0.4% to $1,616.72, and palladium gained 2.2% to $1,274.50. Investors are now laser-focused on Tuesday's June CPI release and Fed Chair Warsh's first congressional testimony.

4. Warsh Appoints Mervyn King and Marc Andreessen to Reform the Fed

Fed Chair Kevin Warsh has fired his first major reform salvo, appointing former Bank of England Governor Lord Mervyn King and famed venture capitalist Marc Andreessen (a16z co-founder) to lead five new task forces overhauling the Fed's policy framework, communications strategy, and regulatory approach, according to the Financial Times.

The move signals a potential paradigm shift at the central bank. Warsh has already hinted at abandoning forward guidance in favor of a more flexible decision-making framework. Andreessen's appointment is particularly notable — as one of Silicon Valley's most influential venture capitalists, his involvement suggests the Fed is seeking breakthroughs in AI financial regulation and digital asset oversight.

The latest FOMC minutes revealed a deeply divided committee, with half favoring steady rates and the other half supporting a hike. The minutes' key innovation was a "scenario-based decision framework" replacing traditional risk-management language, adding uncertainty ahead of the July 28-29 meeting.

5. Japan's $1.8T GPIF to Dramatically Boost Domestic, Alternative Assets

The Japanese government is pushing the world's largest pension fund — the Government Pension Investment Fund (GPIF), with $1.8 trillion in assets — to "substantially" increase allocations to domestic assets. Finance Minister Katsunobu Kato stated Friday that the government will guide the fund to diversify and expand its investment scope.

As of March, alternative investments (private equity, real estate, etc.) comprised only 1.7% of GPIF's portfolio, well below the 5% ceiling. A government panel is expected to recommend raising this ratio to near 5%. Kato's comments triggered a jump in the yen and Japanese government bonds. Separately, Japan's wholesale inflation accelerated above 7%, driven by surging energy costs.

This week is packed with market-moving events: the U.S. June CPI release on Tuesday, Q2 bank earnings season kicking off, and Fed Chair Warsh's congressional debut. Goldman Sachs has flagged CPI as the single most consequential near-term variable for equities, noting that markets have priced in approximately 50 basis points of cumulative tightening — a number that could expand or contract sharply depending on Tuesday's print.
