AMD Pours Up to $5 Billion into Anthropic in AI Chip Blockbuster

On July 22, AMD and Anthropic announced a landmark strategic partnership that reshapes the AI chip landscape. Under the agreement, Anthropic will deploy up to 2 gigawatts of AMD Instinct MI450 Series GPUs, with AMD committing a strategic equity investment of up to $5 billion in Anthropic. This marks AMD's largest AI customer deal to date and positions Anthropic as a pivotal ally in AMD's challenge to Nvidia's AI chip dominance.

According to AMD's official release and reporting from the Wall Street Journal, deployment of the first gigawatt is set to begin in the first half of 2027. The chips will be delivered as AMD Helios rack-scale solutions featuring Instinct MI455X GPUs alongside AMD EPYC "Venice" CPUs, AMD Pensando networking, and the ROCm software stack. Anthropic's compute strategy splits between direct ownership and rented capacity — the company will acquire some AMD chips outright for its own facilities while securing the remainder through cloud providers or neoclouds.

"You can't just wake up one morning and say, 'Oh, I want a gigawatt of compute tomorrow,'" AMD CEO Lisa Su said. "You actually have to plan 12, 18, 24 months in advance for what you want." Separately, AMD is negotiating an arrangement to guarantee Anthropic's future data center lease obligations, signaling a partnership far deeper than typical customer relationships.

The deal includes extensive engineering collaboration: both companies will use Anthropic's Claude models to optimize workloads on AMD Instinct GPUs and accelerate ROCm software development. AMD will also adopt Claude across its engineering and product development teams. Anthropic, which already runs workloads on chips from Google, Amazon, and Nvidia, now adds AMD to its diversified compute portfolio — a major diversification strategy as AI compute demand continues to explode.

Meta's Content Seal Watermark Fails 55% of Detection Tests — Cropping Breaks the System

Meta's invisible watermarking system "Content Seal," launched alongside its Muse Image and Muse Video generation tools this week, has suffered an embarrassing start. An independent test by Reuters found that the system failed to detect AI-generated images 55% of the time when the images were cropped.

Content Seal is designed as a "hidden provenance signal" embedded into every image produced by Meta AI. Meta claimed the watermark "stays intact even when cropped, compressed, resized, or screenshotted." But Reuters' test told a different story: the outlet generated 40 images with Muse Image, cropped them to roughly one-third to one-half of their original size, and the detection tool only identified AI provenance in 45% of cases.

In response, Meta spokesperson Faith Eischen said Content Seal is still in preview and the signal "may be lost if an image is heavily cropped." Meta acknowledged that the detection tool currently only supports images from the latest Muse model, does not support video, and is incompatible with Google's SynthID or C2PA Content Credentials standards. The tool also has a daily usage limit — a constraint that critics say undermines Meta's stated goal of "improving AI transparency."

The Verge's editorial called Meta's approach misguided, noting the company could have adopted Google's SynthID standard (already used by OpenAI) rather than building a proprietary system that fails basic reliability tests. With the 2026 US midterm elections approaching and governments globally drafting punitive AI fraud legislation, the failure of Meta's anti-forgery watermark raises serious questions about the readiness of AI content provenance technology.

Swimlane Launches AI SOC for MSSPs: Agentic AI-Driven Security Automation

Cybersecurity automation company Swimlane today launched an AI Security Operations Center (SOC) specifically for Managed Security Service Providers (MSSPs). Built on Swimlane's Turbine automation engine, the platform uses AI agents to drive alert triage, threat intelligence analysis, and incident response — helping MSSPs scale their client base without proportional headcount increases.

Swimlane's approach differs sharply from some AI SOC vendors entering the MSSP market, who are turning former partners into competitors. Instead, Swimlane positions itself as an enabler: MSSPs build their AI SOC on Turbine while retaining full customer relationships, data ownership, and service ownership. Swimlane provides the underlying agentic AI and automation capabilities.

Key features include: cross-tenant threat intelligence sharing — analysis from one customer's alerts becomes instant context for all clients; agentic AI-driven triage and investigation — normalizing, enriching, and correlating every alert to produce explainable verdicts with MITRE ATT&CK mapping; and a central command center that aggregates prioritized cases from all connected client tenants in real time. The platform integrates with ServiceNow, Jira, and other ITSM tools, and includes pre-built dashboards and portfolio analytics.

Swimlane AI SOC for MSSPs is available starting today. The company says the standardized operating model accelerates customer onboarding, improves analyst efficiency, and enables MSSPs to scale their managed security services without scaling headcount at the same rate.
