⚡ July 15 International Politics Roundup: US Reimposes Iran Naval Blockade with 4th Night of Strikes, Iran Parliament Submits Hormuz Bill, Houthis Threaten Bab al-Mandeb, Senate Dems Block NDAA

US Formally Reimposes Naval Blockade on Iran, Launches Fourth Wave of Strikes

U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) officially reinstated a comprehensive naval blockade on Iranian ports at 20:00 GMT on Tuesday (July 14), marking the second blockade imposed since the U.S.-Iran interim deal was signed in June. The first blockade lasted from April 13 to June 18 — 66 days.

"U.S. forces resumed the naval blockade against vessels transiting to and from Iranian ports and coastal areas today at 4 p.m. ET," CENTCOM said in a statement. "There are currently more than 20 U.S. Navy warships and hundreds of military aircraft operating across the Middle East. American forces remain vigilant, lethal, and ready." The blockade covers the entire Iranian coastline including ports and oil terminals, applying to all vessels regardless of flag. Humanitarian shipments are permitted subject to inspection.

Just one hour before the blockade took effect, U.S. forces launched a fourth wave of strikes targeting Iran — following three consecutive nights of bombardment. CENTCOM said the strikes aimed to "degrade Iranian capabilities used to attack commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz." Iranian media reported explosions near Sirik, Bandar Abbas, and Qeshm Island. Previous strikes had already hit Bushehr (home to Iran's only civilian nuclear power plant), Abadan (hosting the Middle East's oldest refinery), and Mahshahr port.

Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi told state broadcaster IRIB that the U.S. had "destroyed" the June 17 Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) and that Iran no longer considers itself bound by any commitments under the agreement, including those regarding the Strait of Hormuz. "The U.S. is gravely mistaken if it believes it can push Tehran back to the negotiating table by imposing its own blockade," he said.

Iran Parliament Formally Submits Hormuz Strategic Action Bill

Iran's Parliament National Security Committee Chairman Ebrahim Azizi announced on Tuesday (July 14) that a bill titled "Strategic Action for the Security and Sustainable Development of the Strait of Hormuz and the Persian Gulf" had been formally submitted. It is among the first major pieces of legislation introduced since parliament reconvened after being suspended since the outbreak of war in February.

"Last night, coinciding with the downing of U.S. drones, the 'Strategic Action for the Security and Sustainable Progress of the Strait of Hormuz and the Persian Gulf' bill was formally introduced in Iran's Parliament," Azizi wrote on X. "We remain steadfast in defending our red lines, particularly regarding the management of the Strait of Hormuz."

Brigadier General Akraminia, spokesperson for the Khatam al-Anbiya military headquarters, warned that Iran would "under no circumstances" allow the U.S. to interfere in managing the strait and cautioned Gulf states that any cooperation with Washington would be regarded as "an act of war."

Analysts view the bill as an escalation from executive-level claims to legislative codification of Iran's position on strait management — transforming the ambiguous Paragraph 5 of the June MoU into domestic law that fundamentally denies the U.S. any role in strait security affairs.

Houthis Threaten to Close Bab al-Mandeb Strait, Risking Global Shipping Crisis

Senior Houthi official Mohammed al-Bukhaiti issued a stark warning to Al Jazeera on Tuesday (July 14), threatening to block the Bab al-Mandeb Strait — the narrow chokepoint connecting the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden — if Saudi Arabia continues its "aggression."

"The Bab al-Mandeb card is a strategic asset that Yemen has the luxury of utilizing," al-Bukhaiti said. "We will use this card against nations that are actively transgressing on us. We will use Bab al-Mandeb in a way that will bring no harm to nations that are not involved in hostilities toward Yemen."

The threat follows Monday's bombing of Sanaa International Airport runway by Yemen's internationally recognized government (backed by Saudi Arabia), which targeted an Iranian plane carrying a Houthi delegation returning from the funeral of Iran's late Supreme Leader. The Houthis retaliated by firing ballistic missiles at Saudi Arabia's Abha International Airport.

During the 2023-2025 period, Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping sank 4 vessels, killed at least 9 sailors, and forced major shipping lines to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope — dramatically raising freight costs. If Bab al-Mandeb closes simultaneously with the Strait of Hormuz (already effectively shut by Iran), the world's two most critical energy shipping chokepoints would be severed simultaneously — a scenario the International Energy Agency has estimated could drive oil prices up by more than 60%.

The UN Security Council held an emergency session on Monday, with Assistant Secretary-General Khaled Khiari warning that "Yemen and the wider region cannot afford another cycle of escalation."

Senate Democrats Block Defense Bill Over Iran War, Israel Integration

Senate Democrats on Tuesday (July 14) succeeded in blocking debate on the annual National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) in a rare setback for one of Congress's few must-pass bills. The vote split largely along party lines.

Democratic opposition centered on two issues: President Trump's unauthorized military campaign against Iran, and provisions that would further integrate U.S. and Israeli military establishments. The procedural blockade does not kill the NDAA but forces leadership to renegotiate terms.

Both chambers of Congress have passed War Powers Resolutions directing Trump to either end hostilities or seek authorization, but Trump — like several predecessors — has rejected Congress's interpretation of the law, asserting his constitutional authority as commander-in-chief.

According to an AFP tally, U.S. military fatalities in the Iran war have reached 14, with 414 wounded. The latest death was a Navy pilot whose helicopter crashed in the Arabian Sea on July 1.

Iran Strikes Kuwaiti Naval Vessel, Norwegian Tanker Hit Off Oman

Iran widened its retaliatory campaign on Tuesday. Kuwait's Defense Ministry confirmed that Iranian missile and drone strikes hit a Kuwaiti naval vessel, wounding four crew members — the first reported Kuwaiti casualties in the conflict.

A Norwegian-flagged tanker, the Stolt Magnesium, was struck by an unidentified explosive device off the coast of Oman, sparking an engine room fire. Dutch operator Stolt Tankers confirmed all crew were safe, but the attack's origin remains under investigation.

The International Maritime Organization (IMO) confirmed that the two UAE tankers struck in the Strait of Hormuz — Mombasa and Al Bahiyah — resulted in 2 seafarers killed and 14 wounded, revising earlier UAE figures of 1 dead and 8 injured. The IMO called for the "cycle of escalation" to end.

Bahrain's air defense systems sounded alarms multiple times throughout Tuesday, intercepting what officials described as Iranian missiles. Jordan confirmed it shot down 4 Iranian missiles. Qatar condemned Iran's "repeated attacks" on Jordan, Bahrain and Kuwait as an "egregious violation" of their sovereignty.

The European Union Aviation Safety Agency (EASA) warned airlines against operating in the airspace of Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, the UAE, and over the Gulf of Oman, citing "unpredictable military developments combined with the possible use of missiles, drones, combat aircraft and air-defense systems creating a high risk to civil flights."

Israel Approves $434M for 34 New Settlements; Lebanon-Israel Rome Talks Conclude Day One

Israel's security cabinet approved a $434 million (1.3 billion shekels) budget on Tuesday to establish 34 new settlements in the occupied West Bank. On the same day, settlers attacked the town of Beita south of Nablus, cutting down electricity poles and causing widespread blackouts, while concrete blocks were placed at the entrance to Idhna west of Hebron.

Meanwhile, the first day of U.S.-mediated Lebanon-Israel talks concluded Tuesday in Rome. A U.S. State Department official described the discussions as "productive and held in a positive atmosphere," adding that "both sides are eager to move forward." Israeli Foreign Minister Gideon Saar said Israel was "ready to move forward implementing two pilot zones" for withdrawal. However, the Lebanese presidency had instructed its delegation "to demand the immediate start of Israeli forces' withdrawal from the two pilot zones before any further discussion."

Hezbollah continues to reject the framework agreement. Analysts note that Tehran is using Lebanon as leverage in broader negotiations, seeking to link the Lebanon file with Hormuz and nuclear issues — a linkage Washington is determined to avoid.

In Gaza, Israeli strikes on Tuesday killed at least 7 people in the Sheikh Ijlin area of Gaza City. The total Palestinian death toll since the war began has surpassed 43,000.
