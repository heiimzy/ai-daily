⚡ July 23 International Politics Escalation: US 12th Night Strikes on Iran, Houthis Enter War Attacking Saudi Tankers, Trump Declares 'We Don't Need Straits,' Iran Vows 'Eye for an Eye,' Brent Crude Surges Past $95

1. US Launches 12th Consecutive Night of Strikes on Iran

U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) initiated the 12th consecutive night of strikes on Iranian military targets at 5:30 p.m. ET Wednesday (July 22), the military confirmed. In a statement, CENTCOM said the operation aims to "further degrade Iran's ability to threaten civilian mariners and commercial vessels transiting regional waters." The sustained bombing campaign follows the collapse of a temporary ceasefire agreement reached last month.

Speaking at a political rally in Georgia on Wednesday, President Donald Trump declared, "We don't need straits. We don't need the Hormuz Strait — but we do it because we have to do it, because we cannot let Iran have a nuclear weapon." Trump pointed to U.S. oil production and Venezuelan output, claiming, "If you combine us and Venezuela, we have about 62 percent of the world's oil market, so we don't need straits, we don't need anything."

Trump then escalated his threats on Truth Social: "From this point forward, any time the Islamic Republic of Iran shoots at a ship in the Strait of Hormuz, whether it be by Missile, Rocket, Drone, or any other device or weapon, the United States will bomb and destroy ONE BRIDGE OR POWER PLANT," including facilities "located next to, or in, the Capital City of Tehran."

Iran's semi-official Tasnim news agency reported that a U.S. missile struck Larak Island — a key Iranian oil export terminal in the Strait of Hormuz — on Wednesday afternoon. Damage assessment is ongoing. Iranian state media also reported U.S. missiles hitting areas near the cities of Ahvaz, Ramshir, and Andimeshk in western Iran.

2. Iran Declares 'Eye for an Eye' — Infrastructure War Looms

Iranian Foreign Minister Seyed Abbas Araghchi responded swiftly, announcing on X that Iran has adopted an "eye for an eye" defense doctrine. "Any aggression against Iran, including our infrastructure, will compel a powerful and decisive response," Araghchi warned.

The Khatam Al-Anbiya military command warned that any U.S. attack on Iran's nuclear facilities "will be considered as an expansion of war," threatening strikes on "all the interests of America, the allies and supporters." Foreign ministry spokesman Baqaei dismissed Washington's focus on the "Pickaxe Mountain" (Kolang Kouh) underground site — an area near Natanz — as "a fabricated pretext for aggression, destruction, and sabotage."

Iran's retaliatory strikes continued Wednesday, targeting U.S. military facilities across Kuwait, Bahrain, and Jordan. Iran's Revolutionary Guards claimed to have struck Prince Hassan Air Base in Jordan, air defense systems in Kuwait, and logistics infrastructure in Bahrain. Kuwaiti authorities confirmed Iranian attacks on desalination plants and power stations for the fourth consecutive day, with fires and damage reported at multiple facilities.

Bahrain sounded missile alert sirens, and an AFP correspondent in Manama reported hearing an explosion. Jordan's military said it intercepted four Iranian missiles and four drones over the port city of Aqaba, with two missiles falling in uninhabited areas.

"Our defence doctrine is clear: eye for an eye. Any aggression against Iran, including our infrastructure, will compel a powerful and decisive response." — Iranian Foreign Minister Seyed Abbas Araghchi, July 23, 2026

3. Houthis Open Second Front: Saudi Oil Tankers Struck in Red Sea

In a major escalation, Yemen's Iran-backed Houthi rebels announced Wednesday that they had struck two Saudi oil tankers — the Encelia and the Layla — with ballistic missiles, cruise missiles, and drones in the Red Sea. The Houthis accused the vessels of "violating the blockade" of Iran and threatened to blockade all Saudi ports.

This development creates a second major chokepoint crisis in the Middle East. After Iran effectively shut down the Strait of Hormuz, Saudi Arabia had been routing oil via overland pipelines to its Red Sea terminal at Yanbu. The Houthi threat now endangers that alternative route, potentially cutting off nearly all Saudi oil exports.

Maritime data from shipping tracker Kpler showed at least nine vessels had reversed course from entering the Bab el-Mandeb strait after the Houthi announcement, including three tankers that had loaded crude at Yanbu. Approximately 30 ships still crossed the strait on Tuesday.

President Trump warned he would "take care of" the Houthis if they followed through on their blockade threat, though no specific military response has been announced.

The Strategic Significance of Bab el-Mandeb

The Bab el-Mandeb Strait connects the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden and handles approximately 10% of global seaborne oil trade. A blockade would force vessels to circumnavigate the Cape of Good Hope, adding weeks to transit times and significantly increasing costs. The Houthis have demonstrated sophisticated anti-ship capabilities since late 2023, including the use of anti-ship missiles, drones, and naval mines.

4. Global Market Shock: Brent Crude Tops $95, War Cost Reaches $37.5B

Brent crude futures briefly surpassed $95 per barrel on Wednesday — a six-week high — before settling near $94. Since the ceasefire collapsed in early July, Brent has surged over 30%. U.S. gasoline prices have climbed to an average of $4 per gallon, intensifying political pressure on the Trump administration ahead of the midterm elections.

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth testified before the Senate Appropriations Committee on Tuesday that the Iran war has cost $37.5 billion to date, up from approximately $29 billion in mid-May. He is seeking tens of billions in additional funding, calling the request "urgent and necessary."

Responding to questions about the mounting costs, Trump said, "We're not finished at all… we're not leaving right now," suggesting the campaign will intensify rather than wind down.

5. Diplomacy Frozen: Pakistan Mediation Fails, Rubio Warns on Precedent

Iranian Interior Minister Eskandar Momeni visited Pakistan on Tuesday in a last-ditch effort to revive a 10-day ceasefire proposal, but returned empty-handed. Speaking from the Oval Office, Trump shut the door on negotiations, saying the U.S. had "no interest in meeting" with Iranian officials until they were "ready to meet in a meaningful way."

Secretary of State Marco Rubio, speaking at the ASEAN foreign ministers' meeting in Manila, said Washington remains "open to working it out in a negotiated way. But right now, they don't seem to be serious about that." Rubio specifically warned that allowing a nation-state "to control an international waterway, charge a toll, and blow up ships if you don't pay — that will repeat itself in other parts of the world," explicitly referencing the South China Sea.

U.N. Secretary-General António Guterres called for an immediate de-escalation, condemning attacks on civilian infrastructure as "unacceptable" and a violation of international law. A fourth American service member was confirmed killed in Iranian attacks, with President Trump receiving the remains at Dover Air Force Base in a dignified transfer ceremony Wednesday morning.
