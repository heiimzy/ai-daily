⚡ July 23 Financial Roundup: Brent Surges Past $95, Gold Defies Yields Above $4,100, ECB Decision Takes Center Stage

🔥 1. Oil Markets: Brent Breaks $95 to Six-Week High as Red Sea Becomes New Flashpoint

International oil prices surged on July 22, with Brent crude futures breaking through the $95/barrel mark to reach $95.47 — the highest level since June 11. U.S. West Texas Intermediate (WTI) followed suit, gaining over 4% to approach $88/barrel. Both benchmarks have risen for four consecutive sessions, with crude up more than 30% in July alone.

The immediate catalyst was a sharp escalation in U.S.-Iran military hostilities. U.S. forces carried out an 11th consecutive night of strikes on Iranian military targets, while Iran retaliated with drone attacks on American facilities in Bahrain, Kuwait, and Jordan. Kuwait's army confirmed its air defense systems were intercepting Iranian drones. U.S. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth disclosed that American military operations in Iran have now cost approximately $37.5 billion.

More alarmingly for global energy markets, Yemen's Iran-aligned Houthi militia declared a "maritime embargo" against Saudi Arabia, threatening to target vessels using Saudi ports. This triggered a dramatic response: multiple supertankers carrying Saudi crude to China and India reversed course in the Red Sea and diverted toward the Suez Canal, adding weeks to their voyages. According to Reuters, at least three tankers changed course on July 22 alone.

Goldman Sachs warned that Brent could surge to $120/barrel in the fourth quarter if supply disruptions through the Strait of Hormuz and the Red Sea persist. The bank's base case assumes eventual de-escalation, projecting Q4 Brent at $80 — but the risks are "tilted to the upside."

President Donald Trump posted on Truth Social that for every ship Iran hits, the U.S. will "bomb and destroy one bridge or power plant," and threatened to strike Iran's underground nuclear facility at Kuh-e Kolang Gaz with bunker-busting weapons. Iran's Khatam al-Anbiya Central Headquarters responded that any attack on nuclear sites would be viewed as a "full-scale expansion of the war."

Two Vital Chokepoints Under Threat

  • Strait of Hormuz: Traffic through the world's most important oil transit route has plummeted since the U.S.-Iran ceasefire collapsed earlier this month. Multiple tanker attacks have been reported.
  • Bab el-Mandeb / Red Sea: The Houthi blockade threat has forced Saudi crude exports to seek alternative routes. The Red Sea had become a critical alternative to Hormuz for Saudi exports — now it too faces disruption.

MUFG research analyst Soojin Kim noted that the widening geographic scope of supply disruptions means "the market will become increasingly vulnerable if geopolitical tensions persist." Brent's three-month timespread widened to $8.80/barrel, the steepest backwardation since May 22, signaling acute near-term supply tightness.

💰 2. Precious Metals: Gold Defies Higher Yields, Rises Above $4,100

Gold staged a powerful rally, breaking decisively above $4,100/oz. Spot gold touched $4,139.64 on July 22 — a two-week high — extending its winning streak to four sessions with a weekly gain of approximately 2.5%.

What makes this rally noteworthy is its persistence despite rising U.S. Treasury yields and surging oil prices. Traditionally, higher yields increase the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold, while an oil spike fuels inflation fears that could trigger tighter monetary policy. But this time, safe-haven flows and central bank buying overwhelmed these headwinds.

ING's commodities team noted: "Gold and silver extended gains, supported by bargain hunting after recent weakness and investors continuing to assess geopolitical risks in the Middle East. Gold climbed back above $4,000/oz, while silver outperformed, trading close to $60/oz."

Silver surged to $57.49/oz (+2.59%), approaching the psychologically important $60 level. Platinum and palladium also advanced, signaling broad-based buying across the precious metals complex rather than an isolated gold spike.

Goldman Sachs reaffirmed its year-end gold target of $4,900/oz, citing persistent central bank buying led by China as a structural floor. Markets are now looking ahead to the Fed's July 28-29 meeting, with rate futures pricing a steady Fed through the remainder of 2026.

🏛️ 3. ECB Preview: Rates Expected on Hold at 2.25%, September Hike in View

The European Central Bank announces its July rate decision on Thursday at 14:15 CET (20:15 Beijing time). Markets are unanimous in expecting the ECB to hold its deposit rate at 2.25% — a natural pause following June's surprise 25bp hike, the first since September 2023.

All attention will be on President Christine Lagarde's press conference at 14:45 CET (20:45 Beijing time). Without updated staff projections, her words carry exceptional weight.

Why Today's ECB Meeting Matters

  • Oil's lagged impact: June's hike was largely an "insurance move" against rising energy prices. With the Iran conflict escalating and oil surging again, inflation risks have reignited.
  • Sticky core inflation: The ECB's latest projections show core inflation at 2.5% in 2026 and 2.3% in 2027 — well above the 2% target. June's headline CPI cooled to 2.8%, but services and wage inflation remain elevated.
  • September hike nearly fully priced: Money markets see close to 100% probability of a 25bp hike in September. Lagarde's task is to either confirm or push back against that expectation.
  • EUR/USD sensitivity: The pair trades around 1.14. A hawkish hold could push EUR/USD toward 1.15; a dovish tone risks a retest of the June low near 1.133.

Deutsche Bank expects a September hike to 2.50% — the upper end of neutral — as the final move in this cycle. ING's baseline is a "hawkish-leaning hold," noting that a September hike signal may come through post-meeting media leaks rather than the official statement.

Markets are also watching whether the ECB will raise minimum reserve requirements — reportedly doubling the share of non-interest-bearing deposits banks must hold, draining €160-170 billion in excess liquidity.

📊 4. Global Equity Markets Mixed as Big Tech Earnings Take Focus

U.S. stocks closed mixed on July 22. The S&P 500 slipped 0.3% to 7,487, the Nasdaq declined 0.6% to 25,681, and the Dow edged 0.1% lower to 52,164. The previous session (July 21) had seen a strong tech bounce, with the Nasdaq rising 1.9% and the S&P gaining 0.9% on a semiconductor-led recovery.

All eyes are on Big Tech earnings: Alphabet (Google) and Tesla reported after the close on July 22, the most anticipated reports of the season. Investors are closely watching AI capital expenditure plans, cloud revenue growth, and EV demand outlook.

In Asia, Japan's Nikkei 225 edged 0.2% lower, while South Korea's KOSPI surged over 4.5% on a chip stock rebound after the previous day's 12% crash in Samsung and SK Hynix. China's Shanghai Composite briefly breached 3,600 before closing flat at 3,582, led by non-bank financials and brokerages. India's Sensex fell 0.93% to 76,749 on rising crude and will face weekly options expiry today with elevated volatility expected.

Looking ahead, markets will focus on: (1) the ECB rate decision and Lagarde's press conference; (2) a packed Big Tech earnings week (Alphabet, Tesla, Microsoft, Meta, Apple); (3) further U.S.-Iran escalation risks including potential strikes on nuclear sites; and (4) next week's Fed FOMC meeting on July 28-29.
