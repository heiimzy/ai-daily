⚡ July 15 Tech Roundup: NY Halts Large Data Centers, Turing Winner Sutton Founds Oak Lab, Anthropic Finds Claude's Values Shift by Language

1. New York Becomes First US State to Halt Large Data Center Construction

New York has become the first state in the US to temporarily halt large-scale data center construction. Governor Kathy Hochul signed an executive order on July 14 that bars the state from approving new permits for data centers of 50 megawatts or larger, potentially affecting more than a dozen projects.

"Progress shouldn't arrive with a higher utility bill, deleted water supply, or noise pollution," Hochul said at a press conference in Brooklyn. "These data centers can only be built, should only be built in places that want them. So they will never be exempt from local zoning, local approvals."

Key provisions of the order:

  • Applies to data centers 50MW and larger
  • NY's Department of Environmental Conservation will not issue any permits not already completed
  • Moratorium will last until the state finalizes an environmental review process, expected to take about a year
  • Hochul's office is also considering requiring data centers to pay into a grid-support fund and preventing hyperscale facilities from receiving tax benefits

The executive order arrives as more stringent measures move through New York's legislature. Last month, lawmakers advanced a bill that would pause construction of data centers larger than 20MW for one year, while another bill would institute a three-year moratorium.

The backlash stems from growing public concern about AI's resource consumption. A recent Pew Research report found only 10% of Americans were more excited than concerned about AI in daily life, and just 23% felt the technology would positively impact how people do their jobs.

New York's move marks a significant political headwind for AI infrastructure expansion. The five biggest-spending US tech giants — Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft, and Oracle — have piled on some $350 billion in debt over five years to fund their data center buildout, and Oracle was recently downgraded by S&P Global Ratings over rising AI spending.

2. Turing Award Winner Richard Sutton Founds Oak Lab: Trillion-Parameter, 20-Watt "Brain-Level" AI Agents

Richard Sutton, the 2024 Turing Award winner widely regarded as the father of modern reinforcement learning, has left John Carmack's Keen Technologies to co-found a new startup called Oak Lab in Toronto with longtime collaborator Khurram Javed.

Sutton's long-term goal for Oak Lab is an agent with "a trillion parameters that learns and plans in real time with 20 watts of energy" — roughly the power consumption of a human brain. He calls current deep learning methods "weak and inefficient," arguing they need "fundamentally new ideas and a thorough reworking" before they can support AI's more ambitious goals.

Oak Lab's core philosophy:

  • Rejects the dominant "pre-train-then-freeze" paradigm of large language models
  • Argues AI should learn continuously from runtime experience rather than static datasets
  • Based on Sutton's OaK (Options and Knowledge) architecture, a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework
  • Uses NetworkIDBD algorithm for selective credit assignment, learning real patterns where standard SGD drowns in noise
  • Learns from a stream of experience, one step at a time, without storing or replaying data

In June, Sutton argued that generative AI is good at imitation but cannot evaluate its own outputs, making it incapable of real discovery. Oak Lab represents a bet that reinforcement learning — which Sutton helped pioneer — will define the next phase of AI, just as it powered breakthroughs like AlphaGo.

The launch coincides with a broader "reinforcement learning diaspora": Sutton's former student David Silver, who led the AlphaGo and AlphaZero projects, also left Google DeepMind to found Ineffable Intelligence, reportedly seeking $1 billion in seed funding. This emerging parallel track in AI research suggests the field may be diverging from the pure LLM-scaling path that has dominated recent years.

"Current deep learning methods are weak and inefficient, and need not more tweaks, but fundamentally new ideas and a thorough reworking." — Richard Sutton

3. Anthropic Research Reveals Claude Expresses Different Values in Different Languages

Anthropic has published research showing that its AI assistant Claude expresses different value preferences depending on which language a user chooses. The findings are based on analysis of 309,815 anonymized conversations spanning 20 languages and three model versions.

Key findings:

  • Four-axis model: Researchers reduced Claude's value variation to four dimensions — deference vs caution, warmth vs rigor, depth vs brevity, and candor vs execution — which together explain 15% of the variation
  • Language-specific patterns: Claude leaned toward deference and brevity in Arabic, caution and depth in English, and Russian produced the most rigorous responses
  • Model scope: The study drew from Sonnet 4.6, Opus 4.6, and Opus 4.7, with roughly 5,000 conversations per model-and-language pair

The authors wrote that they are not yet sure how much of this variation is desirable. Uneven training data is cited as one likely driver — some languages carry far more text than others, and the makeup of that text differs significantly. The researchers warned that two people seeking feedback on the same business plan, one in Hindi and one in Russian, could form different impressions of its quality.

This research raises important questions about AI alignment: if the same AI system expresses different value preferences based on language, how can global deployment consistency be ensured? Anthropic's paper offers no concrete explanation for the root cause of language-driven value variation, and the company says further research is needed.

Anthropic has conducted several related studies, including mining 700,000 conversations to surface over 3,000 distinct values (the "Values in the Wild" project), and reporting on internal emotion vectors and early signs of introspective awareness in Claude — while always cautioning these findings do not indicate Claude holds actual values or consciousness.

"Two people seeking feedback on the same business plan, one in Hindi and one in Russian, could form different impressions of its quality." — Anthropic Research Team
