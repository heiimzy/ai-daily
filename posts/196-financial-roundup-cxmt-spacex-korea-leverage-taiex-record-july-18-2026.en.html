⚡ July 18 Financial Midday: CXMT IPO Frenzy Grips Wall Street, SpaceX Falls Below IPO Price as $1T Vanishes, Korea Margin Crisis Hits 1.2M Retail Traders

🔥 CXMT IPO: Wall Street Circles the Biggest Chinese Chip Listing in History

This week, CXMT (ChangXin Memory Technologies, SH688825) — the largest IPO ever on Shanghai's STAR Market — has captivated global capital markets. Priced at 8.66 yuan per share, the company is raising approximately 57.9 billion yuan ($8.1 billion), with the total potentially reaching 66.6 billion yuan if the over-allotment option is fully exercised.

The subscription data is staggering: 9.428 million valid subscription accounts with a final lottery rate of just 0.47% — both records for the STAR Market. Social media platforms are flooded with investors sharing their allocation results, calling it the "most coveted chip stock lottery ticket in history."

Even more remarkable is the parallel frenzy in overseas markets. Trade.xyz launched a CXMT pre-IPO perpetual contract "CXMT" on the Hyperliquid crypto platform, settled in USDC with up to 5x leverage. The contract surged from a $5 reference price to $8.64 at its peak — 6.76 times the 8.66 yuan IPO price — implying a market cap of approximately $560 billion, nearly 7x the IPO valuation of $81 billion.

Morgan Stanley's platform sales team wrote: "China semiconductor bulls expect CXMT's market cap to reach multiples of its IPO valuation." Some overseas analysts have even predicted a 1,000% first-day pop, pushing the valuation past $700 billion.

However, skeptics point to the 308x static P/E ratio — four times the industry average of 76x — sparking a heated valuation debate. Counterpoint Research Director MS Hwang offered a nuanced view: "If key strategic targets are met, the current valuation doesn't fully reflect long-term potential." Based on CXMT's projected 2026 net profit of 100 billion yuan, the forward P/E stands at just 5.8x — in line with Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix.

The fundamentals are undeniably explosive: CXMT reported Q1 2026 revenue of 50.8 billion yuan, up over 700% year-over-year, with net profit of 24.76 billion yuan — dwarfing the full-year 2025 net profit of 1.87 billion yuan. According to Omdia, its global DRAM market share surged from 3.97% in Q2 2025 to 7.67% in Q4 2025, securing fourth place globally and first in China.

Analysts believe CXMT's listing will reshape the global DRAM competitive landscape and break overseas pricing hegemony. Citrini Research projects CXMT's monthly DRAM wafer capacity will reach approximately 350,000 wafers by end-2026, approaching Micron's level. Together with YMTC (Yangtze Memory Technologies Corp.) which is also accelerating its IPO, China's "twin-dragon" memory ecosystem is taking shape, with mid-range DRAM self-sufficiency expected to rise from 8% to 25-30% by 2028.

🚀 SpaceX Crashes Below IPO Price: $1 Trillion Evaporated in a Month

SpaceX's post-IPO honeymoon is decisively over. On Friday (July 17), shares plunged another 5.43% to close at $123.99 — the first closing price below its $135 IPO price, marking six consecutive days of decline.

Since hitting a record intraday high of $2.64 trillion on June 16, SpaceX has shed over $1 trillion in market value — equivalent to vaporizing an entire Tesla. The immediate trigger was Thursday's aborted Starship Flight 13, during which multiple Raptor engines failed to ignite, automatically triggering a launch abort just seconds before liftoff.

Siebert Financial CIO Mark Malek noted that beyond the launch failure, looming insider lock-up expirations are adding to selling pressure. "Investors are revisiting their original assumptions, and sidelined capital is waiting for a more attractive entry point as valuation normalizes."

Despite more than 80% of analysts maintaining Buy ratings with a consensus target of $235.34 (implying ~90% upside), near-term sentiment has turned cautious. Industry insiders believe a reversal requires catalysts such as a successful Starship launch, Starlink subscriber acceleration, or a major government computing contract.

🇰🇷 Korea's Leveraged ETF Crisis: 1 in 30 Adults Face Margin Calls

South Korea is experiencing a full-blown deleveraging storm triggered by the stock market rout. Data shows forced liquidation volume in July has reached 344.2 billion won ($260 million), with a single-day record of 142.2 billion won on July 9 — a fivefold increase from the previous day. Retail investors' margin deposit balance has evaporated by nearly 30 trillion won from end-June to 107.1 trillion won, the lowest since June 2020.

According to the Financial Supervisory Service, over 1.2 million leveraged retail accounts had triggered margin calls as of July 13. Given South Korea's adult population, this means roughly 1 in 30 adults (3.4%) faces potential forced liquidation. Behind these numbers lies Korea's highly leveraged retail culture: early pension savings insurance cancellations surged 62.7% year-over-year in the first five months of 2026, while fund redemptions spiked 146.1%.

On July 16, the Financial Services Commission (FSC) announced emergency measures targeting single-stock leveraged ETFs: suspending new product listings, banning related advertising, and raising the minimum basic deposit from 10 million won to 30 million won. The "SK Hynix 2x Leveraged ETF" alone had $3.4 billion in AUM and has dropped over 60% from its peak. Regulators publicly expressed "regret" for approving the product.

"The policy logic is deleveraging, protecting retail investors, and stabilizing market structure," said Liu Ying, a researcher at the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences. "But raising the margin threshold could paradoxically intensify selling pressure in the short term. The key question is whether these measures can truly break the forced liquidation chain reaction already underway."

Meanwhile, foreign capital is accelerating its exit from Korean equities. From January to May 2026, net foreign selling reached 114.22 trillion won — more than 10 times the full-year 2025 total of 11.08 trillion won.

🇹🇼 Taiwan Stocks Suffer Worst Single-Day Crash: Taiex Plunges 6.47%

Taiwan's stock market suffered a historic collapse on Friday (July 17). The Taiex index opened at 45,234.08 and never recovered, closing at 42,671.27 — down 2,953.71 points or 6.47% — with turnover of NT$1.21 trillion. This marks not only the largest single-day point drop in Taiwan's history, but also exceeds the 2,065-point crash triggered by Trump's reciprocal tariffs in April 2025.

Semiconductor stocks were ground zero. TSMC plunged 7.29%, while MediaTek and UMC suffered comparable losses. The triple shock of Kimi K3-induced AI skepticism, TSMC's capex increase to $60-64 billion, and escalating US-Iran conflict pushed Taiwan stocks into a full-blown panic selloff.

More alarming is the looming default risk. Taiwan's stock exchange reported that year-to-date failed delivery settlements have approached NT$7.1 billion ($220 million). Under the T+2 settlement system, concerns are mounting that next week's default figures could set new records. The exchange has activated daily monitoring and early warning mechanisms.

Analysts note that unlike the 2008 financial crisis or the 2020 COVID crash, this selloff represents a "re-pricing of overvalued assets under multiple headwinds" — excessive leverage built up during the AI rally, now forced to unwind as semiconductor stocks correct.

📊 Global Markets Weekly Wrap: Semiconductor Bear Market Confirmed, Oil Surges Past $88

Three themes dominated global financial markets this week: AI chip deleveraging, surging Middle East geopolitical risk premium, and cascading Asia-Pacific selloffs.

In the US, the S&P 500 fell 1.01% on Friday to 7,457.69, ending the week down 1.6% and snapping a three-week winning streak. The Nasdaq dropped 1.4% to 25,520.24 for a 2.9% weekly loss. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index closed 20.2% below its June 22 record high, officially entering bear market territory with a 10% weekly plunge — the steepest since April 2025.

Energy was the week's only winning sector. Brent crude settled at $88.10/barrel on Friday, surging over 12% for the week, while WTI crude hit $81.77. The US-Iran conflict has entered its seventh night, with airstrikes expanding to northern Iran for the first time. Strait of Hormuz shipping is nearly paralyzed, with only 8 tankers transiting the waterway.

In Asia-Pacific, Japan's Nikkei 225 crashed 4% (2,694 points) to 64,141, with memory maker Kioxia plunging 16% into a technical bear market. China's A-shares saw broad declines with the Shanghai Composite losing the 3,800 level and over 5,000 stocks falling. Hong Kong's Hang Seng dropped 1.98% to below 25,000. Markets in Singapore, Australia, and India also traded lower.

Looking ahead to next week, market focus will shift to Big Tech Q2 earnings (Microsoft, Google, Meta) for AI capex guidance, any diplomatic developments in the US-Iran conflict, and CXMT's first trading day performance. Analysts broadly expect continued elevated volatility as AI deleveraging and geopolitical risks converge.
