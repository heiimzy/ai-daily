⚡ July 13 Tech Roundup: OpenAI GPT-5.6 Gets Government Greenlight for Global Launch, AI Giants Unleash Price War, Meta Iris Chip Set for September Production

OpenAI GPT-5.6 Passes US Security Review, Global Rollout Begins

After a two-week strict federal security review, OpenAI today announced the full global availability of its GPT-5.6 family of models. Following a limited preview period that began in late June—during which the model was only accessible to government-approved organizations—the Trump administration has now granted full clearance for public rollout.

The GPT-5.6 family introduces a new celestial naming scheme with three tiers:

  • GPT-5.6 Sol (Flagship): Designed for complex reasoning, code development, scientific research, and cybersecurity. Priced at $5/M input tokens and $30/M output tokens. Scored 53.6 on Agents' Last Exam, leading Claude Fable 5 by 13.1 points. Token efficiency improved 54% over GPT-5.5.
  • GPT-5.6 Terra (Balanced): Suitable for everyday workloads, matching GPT-5.5 performance at half the cost. Priced at $2.5/M input and $15/M output tokens.
  • GPT-5.6 Luna (Lightweight): Optimized for speed and cost efficiency at $1/M input and $6/M output tokens—the most affordable option in the lineup.

On the Artificial Analysis Coding Agent Index, Sol achieved a record 80 points—2.8 points above Fable 5—while using less than half the output tokens and costing roughly one-third less. Terra outperforms Anthropic's Fable 5 at approximately one-sixteenth the cost.

From Codex to ChatGPT Work: A Major Product Transformation

Alongside GPT-5.6, OpenAI merged the standalone Codex application into the new ChatGPT desktop client, unifying Chat, Work, and Codex under one interface. Codex has surpassed 5 million weekly active users, with over 1 million using it for non-development office tasks.

The new ChatGPT Work agent, powered by GPT-5.6, accepts high-level goals, autonomously decomposes tasks, and works across email, calendar, Slack, cloud storage, and CRM systems to deliver complete documents, reports, and web applications. Sales workflows that previously took weeks can now complete validation within 24 hours; monthly financial closes shrink from days to hours.

OpenAI also introduced Programmatic Tool Calling—rather than calling tools one at a time, the model writes JavaScript to orchestrate tool chains autonomously. Early customer data shows 38% to 63.5% reduction in total token consumption, up to 50% fewer model round-trips, and 28% faster task completion.

AI Giants Wage Price War: The Battle Over Token Pricing

Over the past week, the global AI industry has witnessed an unprecedented wave of price competition. OpenAI, SpaceXAI, and Meta have all released new-generation AI models whose primary selling point is not capability—but affordability.

The catalyst: growing enterprise anxiety over runaway AI spending. Uber exhausted its full-year 2026 AI budget by April, subsequently capping monthly per-employee token spending at $1,500. One company reportedly received a $500 million Claude bill after failing to set usage limits.

Ramp's token expenditure data shows median monthly corporate AI token spend at $2,246, but the average is a staggering $140,842—a handful of "super users" consuming the vast majority of AI budgets.

SpaceXAI Grok 4.5: Opus-Level Performance at a Quarter of the Tokens

SpaceXAI released Grok 4.5 on July 8, with Elon Musk touting it as "an Opus-level model, faster, more token-efficient, and cheaper." On SWE Bench Pro, Grok 4.5 averaged just 15,954 output tokens compared to Claude Opus 4.8's 67,020—less than one-quarter. API pricing is $2/M input and $6/M output tokens, over 60% cheaper than Claude Opus and GPT-5.5.

Meta Muse Spark 1.1: A Price Bombshell in the Coding Arena

Meta launched Muse Spark 1.1, with Mark Zuckerberg returning to X after three years to personally endorse it. Pricing is aggressively low—$1.25/M input and $4.25/M output tokens, roughly one-tenth of Anthropic's Fable 5. The model supports a 1M token context window and achieves state-of-the-art results in medical documentation, tax evaluation, and legal work.

"Other labs charge very high prices with large margins," Zuckerberg said bluntly. "We believe we can deliver frontier or very high-level intelligence at much more affordable prices." The strategy is enabled by Meta's highly profitable online advertising business.

The Chinese Model Shockwave

Behind the US AI price war lies a powerful competitive force: Chinese AI models. In June 2026, Coinbase set Chinese large language models as the default tool for its engineers. US startup Lindy switched entirely to DeepSeek after "API costs exceeded total payroll." On OpenRouter, US enterprises' token usage of Chinese AI models has stabilized above 30%, peaking at 46%—compared to just 4.5% in the first half of 2025.

Meta Iris AI Chip to Enter Production in September, Computing Capacity to Double to 14GW

According to an internal memo reviewed by Reuters, Meta Platforms plans to begin manufacturing its in-house AI data center chip, codenamed "Iris," starting in September as part of a strategy to boost total computing power to 14 gigawatts next year.

The Iris chip is the centerpiece of Meta's fourth-generation MTIA (Meta Training and Inference Accelerator) program. Designed in-house, it is being developed in partnership with Broadcom for design and manufactured by TSMC. The chip sailed through testing in just six weeks with no major issues, signaling positive momentum for a project that had struggled since its launch over five years ago.

Iris is designed to augment the large quantities of GPUs Meta purchases from Nvidia and AMD, helping the company lower computing costs and reduce dependence on external suppliers. "You can't become an AI titan if you are dependent on another company for chips," said Mike Gualtieri, VP and principal analyst at Forrester.

Meta plans to deploy 7GW of computing infrastructure in 2026, having already added 1GW in the first half, with another 2.5GW expected by year-end. The company aims to double capacity to 14GW by 2027. Meta expects to spend up to $145 billion on AI infrastructure this year.

To support this expansion, Meta has secured long-term supply agreements with Samsung Electronics for memory chips, Sandisk for flash storage, and Sumitomo Electric for fiber-optic equipment. Morgan Stanley analysts note that rapid memory and chip price increases have made "chipflation" a macroeconomic concern.
