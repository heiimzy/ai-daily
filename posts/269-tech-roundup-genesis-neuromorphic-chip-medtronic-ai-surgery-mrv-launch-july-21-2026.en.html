⚡ July 21 Tech Roundup: UTSA Unveils 'Non-Forgetting' Neuromorphic Chip Genesis, Medtronic Launches AI Surgery Platform, Northrop Grumman Space Robot Launches Today

1. UTSA's Genesis Chip: AI That Never Forgets

Researchers at the MATRIX AI Consortium at the University of Texas at San Antonio (UTSA) have unveiled Genesis, a spiking neuromorphic accelerator chip that solves one of AI's most stubborn problems: catastrophic forgetting — where AI systems overwrite previously learned knowledge when acquiring new information.

The chip mimics the human brain's metaplasticity mechanism: each processing element tracks not only its current usage but also its contribution history and firing frequency. High-importance connections resist being overwritten, while new learning is routed toward pathways that remain flexible. The result is a system that accumulates knowledge over its entire operational lifetime.

30-100x Energy Efficiency

Genesis leverages spiking neural networks (SNNs), where information travels in pulses similar to biological neurons, and the system rests when idle. A novel data movement strategy eliminates the von Neumann bottleneck by colocating computation and storage. Testing shows the chip consumes 30 to 100 times less energy than traditional hardware, operating at milliwatt levels.

Designed for edge computing — implantable medical devices, field-deployed drones, and wearable sensors — Genesis can operate for years in environments without data center or cloud connectivity. The chip is fabricated through a partnership with SUNY Albany using IBM's 65nm process technology.

"From medical devices to autonomous vehicles, the Genesis architecture is a major step toward a future where machines can build on what they know over many years like humans, all for a fraction of the energy costs." — Dr. Dhireesha Kudithipudi, Founding Director, MATRIX AI Consortium, UTSA

2. Medtronic's Touch Surgery Aide: NVIDIA-Powered AI Computing Platform for the OR

Medtronic today announced Touch Surgery Aide, an AI-native surgical computing platform built on NVIDIA's accelerated computing infrastructure (Holoscan, CUDA, and TensorRT). The platform will be unveiled at the Society of Robotic Surgery (SRS) 2026 Annual Meeting in Florida, July 23-26.

Touch Surgery Aide combines computer vision, multimodal AI, and accelerated inference to process surgical video and procedural context in real time, enabling multiple AI applications to run simultaneously during live procedures. The platform's first FDA-cleared application — Instrument Exit Point (IEP) — provides real-time visual notifications when surgical instruments move beyond the visible field of view during robotic procedures.

The Touch Surgery ecosystem is already deployed in over 1,500 operating rooms worldwide, covering the full surgical journey from pre-operative planning and training to intra-operative tele-mentoring and post-operative AI-powered analytics.

"Real-time AI marks a new frontier for healthcare — it is a fundamental evolution in how surgery is supported, moving from technology that assists the surgeon's hand to technology that helps teams work smarter, move faster, and deliver greater impact in the moment." — Jim Peichel, CTO, Medtronic

3. Northrop Grumman's MRV Space Robot Launches Today

Northrop Grumman subsidiary SpaceLogistics is launching its Mission Robotic Vehicle (MRV) today aboard a SpaceX Falcon 9 rocket. The four-hour launch window opens at 5:15 PM ET (21:15 UTC) from Space Launch Complex 40 at Cape Canaveral Space Force Station, Florida. MRV is America's first privately-owned multi-mission robotic spacecraft for satellite servicing.

Equipped with a robotic arm developed by DARPA and the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory, MRV can perform satellite life extension, inspection, repair, upgrades, orbital disposal, and assembly. The mission carries three Mission Extension Pods (MEPs) — described as "satellite jetpacks" — each capable of adding up to six years of operational life to aging satellites.

Notably, this marks the 32nd and final flight for this Falcon 9 first-stage booster, which previously launched CRS-24, Eutelsat HOTBIRD 13F, OneWeb 1, and 27 Starlink missions. Due to the heavy payload's geosynchronous transfer orbit requirements, the rocket flies in expendable configuration with no recovery attempt.

SpaceLogistics has successfully operated two Mission Extension Vehicles (MEVs) in orbit, providing over a decade of combined life extension services — making it the only commercial company worldwide to have docked with operational satellites for in-orbit servicing.
