⚡ July 17 Financial Evening: JPMorgan Warns Deleveraging Far From Over, Asia Tech 'Black Friday' Bloodbath, Gold Crashes Below $4,000

1. JPMorgan's Stark Warning: US Stock Deleveraging Far From Over, Nasdaq Could Slide to 25,000

JPMorgan's global market strategy team issued a stark warning in its latest report published Friday (July 17): the US equity deleveraging process that began in June is far from complete, with leveraged ETFs, options markets, and margin accounts still holding significant room to reduce risk exposure — posing a major headwind for global stock markets in the months ahead.

The team led by strategist Nikolaos Panigirtzoglou noted that the previous AI and semiconductor-driven rally attracted large numbers of investors using borrowed funds, options, and leveraged ETFs to chase returns. But as chip stock volatility intensified, some highly leveraged positions were forced to liquidate, and the market is transitioning from risk-on expansion to deleveraging.

According to the report, leveraged ETF assets tied to memory chip stocks have shrunk by approximately 34% from their June peak, while overall leveraged equity ETF assets have declined by about 13%. JPMorgan believes that if the market continues to experience high volatility and range-bound trading, it could take roughly three months for the asset size of related products to return to pre-April normal levels relative to underlying stocks.

The options market is also flashing risk-off signals. US retail call option buying volume approached 14 million contracts on June 5 — close to cyclical peaks in October 2025 and November 2021, after which technology stocks experienced corrections lasting several months. Declining call option demand could also reduce market makers' need to buy stocks for hedging, weakening a key capital driver for tech stock gains.

Meanwhile, margin account borrowing, while beginning to pull back, remains at historically elevated levels. If stock markets continue to fall or equity financing costs rise further, investors may be forced to sell holdings to meet margin calls, amplifying market volatility. Data shows that bank-provided repo financing for equity trading previously exceeded $220 billion, reflecting the market's heavy reliance on leveraged funds.

Technically, the Nasdaq — after hitting an all-time high of 27,190 in June — has been in a sustained correction, with each rebound making lower highs, indicating weakening bullish momentum. Currently under pressure below the 26,300 resistance level, the Nasdaq could test the 25,000 support level, with further downside toward 24,150 if that level breaks.

2. Asia 'Black Friday': Nikkei Crashes 3.45%, SoftBank Plunges 9%, Kioxia Collapses 16%, TSMC Sinks 7.3%

Asian markets suffered a broad-based selloff Friday, with AI and semiconductor stocks leading the decline, tracking overnight losses on Wall Street. South Korean markets were closed for Constitution Day, escaping the carnage.

Japan: The Nikkei 225 crashed 3.45%, breaking below the 65,000 level to close at 64,527 — a one-month low. SoftBank Group plunged 9%, completely erasing recent gains. Chip equipment maker Tokyo Electron lost over 8%, Advantest slid 7.2%, and memory chipmaker Kioxia cratered over 16% after a Texas federal jury ordered it to pay $229 million in damages for patent infringement.

Taiwan: TSMC fell 7.29% despite reporting record Q2 earnings the previous day — net profit surging 77% year-on-year to $22 billion — and raising full-year capital expenditure guidance to $60-64 billion. Investors focused instead on concerns that aggressive spending could compress cash flow and near-term margins.

Hong Kong: The Hang Seng Tech Index components weakened across the board. Tencent slipped 4.8% in its final hour of trading, Meituan fell 4.6%, Kuaishou lost over 7%, while Baidu and Alibaba eased 3.7% and 3.9%, respectively.

Andrew Jackson, strategist at Ortus Advisors, said the sell-off reflects an unwinding of crowded AI momentum trades rather than deterioration in the sector's long-term fundamentals.

3. Gold Crashes Below $4,000 for First Time in Two Weeks

Spot gold closed 2.06% lower on Thursday at $3,969.41 per ounce, breaking below the psychologically critical $4,000 level for the first time since early July. The yellow metal traded weakly around $4,000 during Friday's Asian session.

Analysts point to three main drivers behind the breakdown. First, softer-than-expected US CPI and PPI data reduced gold's appeal as an inflation hedge. Second, rebounding US Treasury yields — the 10-year note at 4.56% — raised the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding assets. Third, hawkish commentary from Federal Reserve officials weighed on prices: Vice Chair Jefferson stated that "one month of softer CPI and PPI does not make a trend," signaling rates may stay higher for longer.

TradingKey analysts noted that despite cooling inflation data, rising yields and hawkish Fed rhetoric prevented gold from holding the $4,000 level, with near-term sentiment turning bearish.

4. Not a Broad Market Crash: S&P Equal Weight Hits All-Time High as Sector Rotation Accelerates

Despite the tech and semiconductor rout, US markets tell a more nuanced story. The S&P 500 Equal Weight Index rose approximately 1% on Thursday to a record close, outperforming the market-cap-weighted S&P 500 (down 0.51%) by the widest margin since 2024. This divergence signals that stocks outside mega-cap tech are performing strongly.

Sector performance was sharply divided: Healthcare (XLV) surged 2.22%, lifted by UnitedHealth Group's strong Q2 earnings and raised full-year guidance; Energy (XLE) gained 0.92% on rising oil prices amid US-Iran tensions; Financials (XLF) edged up 0.34%, benefiting from rotation out of overvalued tech. In contrast, Technology (XLK) tumbled 2.24%, with semiconductors and AI hardware stocks leading the decline.

Seven of eight sector ETFs posted gains, indicating healthy market breadth. Analysts believe the rotation from crowded AI hardware trades into healthcare, financials, and other sectors with reasonable valuations could continue in the weeks ahead.

5. Other Market Highlights

Bitcoin Falls Below $64,000: The leading cryptocurrency slipped to around $63,600, extending Thursday's near-1.4% decline, as renewed US airstrikes on Iran and Trump's allegations of Chinese election interference added to geopolitical uncertainty, driving risk-off sentiment.

Japan Warns on Yen Intervention: Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama said Friday that authorities are "ready to act on currency moves whenever necessary," with USD/JPY trading around 162.43. A stronger yen added pressure on Japan's export-heavy stock market.

Oil Up 11%+ This Week: Brent crude traded at $84.79/barrel Friday, on track for its best weekly performance since late April with gains exceeding 11%. The US completed a sixth consecutive night of strikes on Iran, and Tehran warned it would "crush all regional infrastructure" if Trump hits its power facilities, keeping the Strait of Hormuz — through which 20% of global oil passes — effectively disrupted.

Oracle Credit Rating Nears Junk: S&P Global Ratings downgraded Oracle's credit rating to BBB-, one notch above junk, with Moody's maintaining a negative outlook. Oracle has invested over $250 billion in AI infrastructure, and investors are questioning the pace of monetization.
