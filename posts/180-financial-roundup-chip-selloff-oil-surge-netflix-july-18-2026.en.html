⚡ July 18 Financial Roundup: Global Chip Rout Deepens, Netflix Plunges on Earnings Miss, Oil Surges Past $86

1. Global Semiconductor Sell-Off Accelerates: SOX Nears Bear Market Territory

The global semiconductor sell-off intensified on Friday, July 17, with the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOX) falling another 4.3%, bringing its decline from the June peak to approximately 20% — approaching the technical definition of a bear market. The trigger was South Korea's Financial Services Commission (FSC) announcing stricter regulations on single-stock leveraged ETFs on July 16, including tripling the minimum margin requirement to 30 million won ($20,300), accepting only cash as collateral, capping single-stock leveraged trades at 20 shares per transaction, and banning new single-stock leveraged products.

Memory chip stocks bore the brunt of the sell-off. SK Hynix's US-listed shares plunged over 13%, SanDisk dropped 12%, Seagate Technology fell 10%, Western Digital slid 9%, and Micron Technology lost over 5%, falling below the $1 trillion market cap threshold. In the chip equipment space, Applied Materials (AMAT) dropped over 6%, Intel (INTC) and AMD each fell approximately 4.5-5%.

Notably, despite the carnage in semiconductor names, the S&P 500 Equal Weight Index actually rose 1% to an all-time high — suggesting this is a highly concentrated correction in AI leaders rather than a systemic collapse. Matt Maley of Miller Tabak noted: "Semiconductor stocks continue to decline despite reporting very positive results and guidance. This is a classic 'sell the news' reaction."

2. Netflix Q2 Revenue Misses Estimates, Shares Plunge 10%

Streaming giant Netflix reported Q2 2026 earnings after Thursday's close, posting revenue of $12.56 billion — slightly below the $12.6 billion consensus forecast — making it the first major tech earnings disappointment of the season. While EPS of $0.80 marginally beat expectations, the company's Q3 guidance for both revenue and profit fell short of FactSet consensus, sending shares down over 10% in pre-market trading on Friday. The stock is now down 21% year-to-date, firmly in bear market territory.

Netflix raised its full-year content budget to $20 billion, covering original programming, live events, and podcasts. The company also disclosed that generative AI has been applied to post-production work on over 300 titles to date. However, investors were unimpressed by what Barron's called the company's "naturally maturing growth profile," with concerns that expansion into advertising and gaming has failed to meaningfully reverse the growth deceleration. Netflix now faces competition not only from traditional media but also from social media platforms for user engagement.

As the bellwether of tech earnings season, Netflix's stumble casts a shadow over upcoming reports from Alphabet (scheduled for Tuesday), Tesla, and Intel. The Nasdaq 100 has now retreated 5% from its early June high.

3. US-Iran Strikes Enter 6th Night, Brent Crude Surges Past $86

The United States and Iran exchanged fresh military strikes for the sixth consecutive night. US Central Command (CENTCOM) announced late Thursday that it had completed another wave of strikes targeting coastal surveillance systems, air defense positions, logistics infrastructure, and maritime assets near Bandar Abbas, Qeshm Island, and Bushehr. Iran retaliated by launching missiles and drones at US military facilities in Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, and Syria, with a power and water desalination plant in Kuwait struck and set ablaze.

Oil prices surged on supply disruption fears. Brent crude futures rose approximately 3% to $86.73 per barrel, recording an 11% weekly gain — the largest since April. WTI crude gained 3.3% to $81.53. IEA Executive Director Fatih Birol warned that if the US and Iran do not restore oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz soon, global energy security will face serious challenges. Approximately 20 million barrels of petroleum traverse the strait daily, representing roughly one-fifth of global consumption.

Rising oil prices are triggering ripple effects: the average 30-year US mortgage rate has climbed to its highest level in nearly a year, and market expectations for Fed rate hikes are re-emerging. The White House maintains that President Trump remains open to negotiation, but with both sides escalating attacks on strategic military infrastructure, prospects for a near-term ceasefire remain dim.

4. Asia-Pacific Markets Rout: Nikkei -4%, Shanghai Below 3800, Taiwan Sees Largest Point Drop Ever

Asia-Pacific equity markets suffered a systemic sell-off on Friday, with nearly every major index posting steep losses:

  • Japan's Nikkei 225: Closed 4.0% lower at 64,141, retreating over 10% from its June high. Advantest and Tokyo Electron each fell over 7%, while Kioxia collapsed 16%, losing half its value since briefly becoming Japan's largest company by market cap.
  • China's A-Shares: The Shanghai Composite lost the 3,800 level, closing 3.05% lower at 3,764 — the lowest since September 2025. The Shenzhen Component fell 5.4%, and the ChiNext plunged 7.15%. Over 5,000 stocks declined, with 198 hitting the daily limit down. Turnover surged to 2.65 trillion yuan.
  • Taiwan's Taiex: Crashed 6.5% — the largest single-day point drop in history at 2,953 points — closing at 42,671. TSMC fell over 7%. Foreign investors sold a record NT$189 billion in a single day.
  • Hong Kong: The Hang Seng Index fell 2.1%, with the Hang Seng Tech Index losing 4.37%. Tencent dropped 4.8%, Meituan 4.6%, and Kuaishou over 7%.
  • Other Markets: South Korea's KOSPI was closed for a public holiday but had fallen 6.37% in the previous session. In Europe, the Euro Stoxx 50 fell 1.3%, while the FTSE 100 eked out a 0.3% gain on energy sector strength.

5. Fed Vice Chair Hints at Possible Rate Hike if Inflation Persists

Federal Reserve Vice Chair Philip Jefferson said the current policy stance remains "appropriate" to support both the labor market and inflation's return to the 2% target. However, he warned that if inflation fails to show sustained improvement soon, policymakers "may need to reassess," leaving the door open to rate hikes. Jefferson placed significantly greater emphasis on inflation risks than on labor market concerns, reflecting continued confidence in employment conditions.

Jefferson's remarks were more hawkish than markets had anticipated. Dallas Fed President Lorie Logan had earlier argued that "higher interest rates may already be warranted." Despite this week's softer-than-expected June CPI print — which has led markets to largely price out a July rate hike — several Fed officials remain cautious about drawing conclusions from a single month of favorable data. Jefferson also highlighted AI's dual impact on inflation: it could eventually reduce price pressures by boosting productivity, but in the near term, stronger investment and consumer spending may outpace productivity gains, fueling inflation.

6. Sector Rotation Accelerates: Healthcare Leads as Money Flows from Tech to Defensives

Beneath the surface of this week's tech rout, a significant sector rotation is underway. Eight of the S&P 500's 11 sectors closed higher, and the equal-weight index hit a new all-time high, indicating money is rotating out of overly concentrated AI names into broader market segments:

  • Healthcare (XLV): +2.22%, the top performer. UnitedHealth Group beat Q2 earnings estimates and raised its full-year guidance, catalyzing the sector.
  • Energy (XLE): +0.92%, benefiting from the oil price surge. Valero, ConocoPhillips, and ExxonMobil each gained approximately 2%.
  • Financials (XLF): +0.34%, supported by rising rate expectations.
  • Technology (XLK): -2.24%, the worst performer. Alphabet (GOOGL) fell 4.4%, erasing $200 billion in market cap after Bloomberg reported delays to its Gemini 3.5 Pro AI model.

The Cboe Volatility Index (VIX) surged 11% to 18.57, signaling elevated near-term uncertainty. However, of the 47 S&P 500 companies that have reported Q2 earnings so far, 95% have beaten EPS consensus — suggesting the fundamental backdrop remains intact. The coming week will be pivotal, with earnings from Alphabet, Tesla, Intel, IBM, and Texas Instruments on deck to determine whether the AI investment narrative can withstand the current valuation reset.
