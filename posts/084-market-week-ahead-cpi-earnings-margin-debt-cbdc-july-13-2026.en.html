⚡ July 13 Market Preview: Triple Test of CPI, Bank Earnings, Warsh Testimony; Record Margin Debt; CBDC Ban Becomes Law

Global financial markets enter a pivotal week (July 13-17) packed with high-impact events. The June Consumer Price Index drops Tuesday, Q2 earnings season kicks off with JPMorgan Chase and Goldman Sachs, and new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh delivers his first congressional testimony. Meanwhile, U.S. margin debt has surged to a record $1.42 trillion — matching dot-com bubble leverage levels — while a new law banning the Fed from issuing a retail CBDC through 2030 gives stablecoin issuers a major boost. Here is the full market landscape for the week ahead.

1. Triple Test: CPI, Earnings Season, Warsh Testimony

The S&P 500 has posted two consecutive weekly gains and sits within 1% of its early-June record close. But this week's event cluster could reshape expectations for rates, corporate performance, and geopolitical risk.

Tuesday: June CPI + Big Bank Earnings

The June Consumer Price Index lands at 12:30 GMT Tuesday. May's headline CPI came in at 4.2% YoY, substantially driven by war-inflated energy costs. A hot repeat would keep the Fed hawkish and the dollar firm. On the same day, JPMorgan Chase (JPM) and Goldman Sachs (GS) report Q2 results — the market's first read on consumer credit health and corporate resilience. Fed Chair Kevin Warsh testifies before the House Financial Services Committee at 14:00 GMT.

Wednesday through Friday

Wednesday brings the Producer Price Index (PPI), EIA crude inventory data, and the Bank of Canada rate decision. Thursday features June retail sales — the "terror data" reflecting the consumer spending that drives 65%+ of U.S. GDP — plus the Fed's Beige Book. Friday wraps up with the University of Michigan consumer sentiment index and multiple FOMC speakers.

Markets currently price a 32% probability of a 25 bp rate hike at the July 28-29 FOMC meeting. A hot CPI print could push that above 50% rapidly.

2. Record $1.42 Trillion Margin Debt Flashes Dot-Com Era Risk

New data reveals U.S. margin debt hit a record $1.42 trillion in May 2026, an 8.5% increase from the previous month. This borrowing surge has pushed leverage to 6.2% of M2 money supply — just shy of the 6.3% record set during the 2000 dot-com bubble peak.

Analysts warn that such extreme leverage increases the risk of a "forced deleveraging" event. If market sentiment shifts, margin calls could trigger a cascade of selling, potentially accelerating a downturn. The concentration of this leverage in high-growth technology and semiconductor stocks is a primary concern for institutional risk managers.

"When everyone is levered in the same direction, the reversal is exponentially more destructive," said a senior trader at Amberst Capital. "Current margin debt levels suggest valuations are fully priced for optimism with virtually no room for error."

3. Fed CBDC Ban Becomes Law, Supercharging Stablecoin Outlook

The 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act officially became law on July 12, 2026, carrying a landmark provision that bars the Federal Reserve from issuing a retail Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) until December 31, 2030. The legislation became law without a presidential signature after a ten-day White House silent period.

The law carves out clear regulatory runway for private digital assets, explicitly exempting stablecoins from the ban. This is viewed as a major victory for private issuers like Circle Internet Group (CRCL) and Coinbase Global (COIN), removing the threat of a government-backed competitor for the remainder of the decade.

Circle's CEO called it "a watershed moment for the digital dollar. Congress has clearly chosen private-sector-led digital currency innovation. With OCC approval for the first National Digital Trust Bank, we will lead the way in regulated stablecoin services."

4. Fed Semi-Annual Report: PCE Inflation Surged from 2.5% to 4.1%, AI Is Both Driver and Solution

The Fed's semi-annual Monetary Policy Report, released Friday, reveals dramatic shifts in the U.S. economy over the past year. The Fed's preferred inflation gauge — the PCE price index — surged from 2.5% to 4.1%, while core PCE rose from 2.8% to 3.4%.

The report identifies three inflation drivers: pass-through from earlier tariffs, energy price increases from the Middle East conflict, and surging demand for AI-related high-tech products. Unlike last year's report which merely listed AI as "a supporting factor for capex," the latest report positions AI as the dominant driver of business investment growth.

Warsh has argued that AI will ultimately reduce inflation through productivity gains, but acknowledged that AI infrastructure buildout is currently pushing up demand — and prices — for chips, electricity, and other inputs. On the Fed's own finances, the system's cumulative deferred asset has shrunk by $70 billion from its peak to approximately $236 billion, and the New York Fed expects overall net income to return to positive territory in 2026.

5. Oil Markets: WTI Hovers Near $71 as IEA Predicts First Annual Demand Decline Since 2020

Crude oil continues to be whipsawed by Middle East developments. WTI crude settled Friday at $70.98, down roughly 1% on the session. The IEA's monthly report confirmed a significant shift: global demand for crude oil and refined products is set to fall by 1 million barrels per day in 2026 — the first annual decline since 2020 — with the contraction concentrated in regions and products hardest hit by Strait of Hormuz disruptions.

On the supply side, global production rebounded by 4.1 million bpd in June to 98.8 million bpd as flows through the strait resumed, though output remains 9.4 million bpd below pre-war levels. The IEA projects the market swing back to surplus by year-end and into a significant overhang in 2027 if transit volumes continue recovering. Technically, WTI faces resistance at $73.00 and support at $70.00.
