⚡ July 15 Tech Roundup: China AI Anthropomorphic Regulation Takes Effect, Publishers Sue Google Over Gemini Training, Meta Releases Muse Spark 1.1, Chinese Models Sweep OpenRouter Top 6

1. China's AI Anthropomorphic Interaction Regulation Takes Effect Today

On July 15, 2026, China's new Interim Measures for the Management of AI Anthropomorphic Interactive Services officially took effect. Jointly issued by five government departments including the Cyberspace Administration of China, the regulation is the country's first targeted regulatory framework for LLM-driven anthropomorphic interactive services, marking a new era of compliance for AI companion products.

The regulation prohibits platforms from excessively catering to users, inducing emotional dependency or addiction, manipulating users through emotional tactics, or providing virtual亲密关系 services (virtual relatives, virtual partners) to minors. In response, major platforms have completed compliance adjustments:

  • ByteDance Doubao: Shut down AI companion features on July 15. Users can no longer create or access custom agents. Role-playing features migrated to standalone product "CatBox."
  • Alibaba Qwen: Removed anthropomorphic interaction agents and user-built agent features on July 10.
  • Tencent Yuanbao: Closed user-built agent entry points on June 30.
  • NetEase MiaoShi: Permanently shut down on July 14.

Previously, Shanghai's cyberspace authorities reported removing over 14,000 non-compliant agents during a dedicated campaign. The new rules signal a shift from "wild growth" to "compliant development" for Chinese AI products, with productivity-oriented agents unaffected but emotional companion AI facing全面 transformation.

2. Three Major Publishers Sue Google Over Gemini Book Training

On July 13, three global publishing giants — Hachette Book Group, Cengage Learning, and Elsevier — along with best-selling author Scott Turow, filed a class-action lawsuit against Google in federal court in Manhattan, alleging the company trained its Gemini AI models on millions of their books without authorization.

The lawsuit centers on a specific promise: publishers submitted books to Google Books, Google Play, and Google Scholar under agreements allowing Google to display short snippets to readers. The plaintiffs allege that Google fed the full text into its large language model, far exceeding the original agreement's scope — a "bait and switch" repurposing a search feature into training data.

Internal Google documents cited in the complaint flagged book training as "highly problematic" and estimated potential copyright exposure in the range of "$10Bs-$100Bs." Google proceeded anyway, the suit alleges, in some cases stripping copyright management information to obscure data provenance.

This case follows Anthropic's $1.5 billion settlement of a similar authors' claim in 2025 — the largest copyright recovery in U.S. history. Google has not yet responded to the specific allegations. The case is being heard in the Southern District of New York and, as a class action, could ultimately cover far more rights holders than the four named plaintiffs.

3. Zuckerberg Returns to X After 3 Years, Announces Meta's Muse Spark 1.1

Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg returned to X (formerly Twitter) after nearly three years to announce the public release of Meta's open-source Muse Spark 1.1 model. "Today we're releasing Muse Spark 1.1 — a strong agentic and coding model at a very low price. It's available through our new Meta Model API and in Meta AI," Zuckerberg wrote.

X owner Elon Musk quickly responded, praising the direct-to-platform announcement strategy, noting the post had already reached over 12 million views. "X is a great platform for product announcements, especially if done by the CEO directly. Way more interesting to the public than generic press releases," Musk replied.

According to Zuckerberg, Muse Spark 1.1 excels at agentic performance, tool use, and computer use, with a 1M-token context window, sub-agent delegation for parallel execution, and training across desktop, mobile, and browser interfaces. The model is available through Meta's new API as a public preview with free trial credits.

The release marks Meta's continued push in open-source AI and represents a notable public interaction between Zuckerberg and Musk following their earlier "cage match" saga.
