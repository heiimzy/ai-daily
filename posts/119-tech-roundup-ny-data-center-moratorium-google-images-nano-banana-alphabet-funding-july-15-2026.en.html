⚡ July 15 Tech Roundup: New York Enacts First US Hyperscale Data Center Moratorium, Google Images Turns 25 with Nano Banana AI, Alphabet Raises $80B for AI Infrastructure

1. New York Becomes First State to Ban Hyperscale Data Centers

On July 14, 2026, New York Governor Kathy Hochul signed Executive Order No. 62, imposing the first statewide moratorium on hyperscale data center construction in the United States. The order takes immediate effect, halting new environmental permits for large data center projects exceeding 50 megawatts (MW) of power consumption for up to one year.

The moratorium gives New York up to 12 months to develop a regulatory framework for data center development, including standards for environmental impact assessment covering water usage, air quality, and community impact. "As data center development threatens to hike up utility bills, deplete our natural resources, and create uncertainty for New Yorkers, it's my responsibility to take action and lead," Hochul stated.

Key Policy Details

  • Threshold: Targets projects with power demand ≥50MW — higher than the 20MW threshold in the legislature's version, which Hochul said was designed to exempt smaller facilities used by hospitals and research institutions.
  • Backlog: Developers have submitted grid connection requests for over 30 hyperscale projects, which together would consume more electricity than the entire country of Ireland.
  • Tax Break Repeal: Hochul plans to push the legislature to roll back sales tax exemptions for large data centers — a policy first enacted in 2000 that has become a major incentive for attracting data center investment.
  • Community Benefits: Empire State Development will create a "community investment framework" to help localities negotiate benefit agreements with developers, including infrastructure improvements, childcare investments, or direct financial support.

At least a dozen US states are considering similar moratoriums. Maine's legislature passed the nation's first data center pause bill, but Governor Janet Mills vetoed it in April. New York's executive order marks the first time the tension between AI infrastructure expansion and environmental protection has been resolved at the state level through executive action.

"By giving our state the time to plan, we can ensure that development and innovation do not come at the expense of all of us." — New York State Senator Kristen Gonzalez

2. Google Images Turns 25: Nano Banana AI Image Generation Comes to Search

Google Images celebrated its 25th anniversary on July 14 with two major updates: a redesigned desktop dynamic gallery homepage and integration of the Nano Banana image generation model into AI Overviews.

What's New

  • Dynamic Gallery: A completely redesigned desktop homepage featuring a real-time updating, personalized image gallery that intelligently tailors content to user interests. Users can save images to collections that appear as quick-access tabs above the main gallery.
  • Nano Banana Text-to-Image: Google's lightweight Nano Banana image generation model is now integrated directly into AI Overviews. Users can type a text description into the main search box and generate high-quality custom images. The feature rolls out over the coming weeks in supported regions.

Google Images began in July 2001 when Google engineers noticed users were desperately searching for images of Jennifer Lopez's green Versace dress from the 2000 Grammys — and couldn't find them. Twenty-five years later, the platform processes billions of searches monthly, and Nano Banana marks its evolution from an "image finding" tool to an "image creating" tool.

3. Alphabet Plans $80B Equity Raise for AI Infrastructure

Bloomberg reported on July 14 that Alphabet, Google's parent company, is planning a massive $80 billion equity raise to fund AI infrastructure, computing capacity, and related capital expenditures — the largest single-company AI infrastructure financing plan to date.

Financing Structure

  • Public Offering: $30 billion in underwritten public offering
  • At-the-Market (ATM): $40 billion in Class A or Class C stock at market prices
  • Private Placement: $10 billion direct investment from Berkshire Hathaway

Alphabet previously raised its full-year 2026 capital expenditure guidance to $180-200 billion. CEO Sundar Pichai stated in the Q1 earnings call that "2027 CapEx will significantly increase." Part of the funds will also be used for employee equity award tax obligations.

Notably, Google is now productizing its custom TPU silicon — Pichai confirmed the company is selling 8th-gen TPUs to "select data center customers." Google Cloud posted $20 billion in Q1 FY2026 revenue, up 63% YoY, with backlog nearly doubling sequentially to $462 billion. At the chip level, Google is increasingly competing with NVIDIA's Blackwell ecosystem through its vertically integrated TPU strategy.
