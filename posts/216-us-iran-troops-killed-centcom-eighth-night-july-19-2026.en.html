⚡ July 19 International Politics: Two US Troops Killed in Jordan, CENTCOM Launches 8th Night of Retaliatory Strikes, Iran Suspends Islamabad MoU

Two US Soldiers Killed in Jordan: First Combat Deaths of the Conflict

The US Central Command (CENTCOM) confirmed on July 19 that two American service members were killed and one remains missing after an Iranian ballistic missile and drone strike on the Azraq base in northeastern Jordan. Four additional personnel were wounded and evacuated to Jordanian hospitals before being discharged. This marks the first confirmed US combat deaths from direct Iranian fire since the conflict began on February 28, fundamentally altering the political dynamics of the confrontation.

According to the CENTCOM statement, the attack occurred on July 17 while US and partner forces were "defending against Iranian ballistic missile and drone attacks." Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) claimed responsibility for the Azraq strike as part of a broader coordinated salvo that simultaneously targeted the US fuel pier at Kuwait's al-Ahmadi port and Sheikh Isa Air Base in Bahrain. The IRGC separately claimed to have destroyed two US fighter aircraft — a claim the Pentagon has not confirmed.

The total US military death toll since the conflict began now stands at 16, with more than 430 wounded. US Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth responded on X: "Godspeed, heroes. Their sacrifice only stiffens our resolve."

Military analysts describe the Azraq attack as a "qualitative shift" — Iran had repeatedly struck US military infrastructure in Bahrain and Kuwait since the Islamabad MoU collapsed, but Thursday marked the first time IRGC munitions killed serving American personnel. The deaths change Washington's political calculus in ways that drone attacks on fuel piers and air base perimeters did not.

Eighth Consecutive Night of Strikes: US Moves to "Swiftly Punish" IRGC

At 6:00 PM ET on July 19, President Donald Trump ordered a new wave of airstrikes against Iran — the eighth consecutive night of US attacks. CENTCOM stated the operation was designed to "further degrade Iran's ability to threaten commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz and swiftly punish Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps forces who launched attacks against American service members in Jordan."

Targets included Iranian military coastal surveillance facilities, air defense systems, maritime capabilities, and missile and drone storage sites. Iranian state news agency IRNA reported that Qeshm Island in the Strait of Hormuz was struck by at least six missiles, and further attacks targeted the Sirik and Hajiabad areas. According to Iranian media, US strikes in Hormozgan Province killed three people and wounded eight, damaging two bridges and a road tunnel.

In immediate retaliation, Iran's military announced drone strikes on two US bases in Kuwait — Camp Udairi's ammunition depot and Ali Al Salem Air Base's Patriot radar and air surveillance systems. Iran's navy also fired a shore-to-sea cruise missile toward what it described as a "hostile US vessel" in the northern Indian Ocean, claiming the launch caused "fear and panic" and forced the vessel to move out of range.

CENTCOM stated that more than 50,000 US military personnel remain deployed across the Middle East and are "highly vigilant, focused, lethal, and ready."

Iran Formally Suspends Islamabad MoU: Khamenei Calls Trump's Signature "Worthless"

On July 19, Iranian Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi formally announced Tehran's suspension of all commitments under the June Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) with the United States. "The US has violated and suspended all its commitments within the framework of the Islamabad MoU. We have also suspended our commitments; we are not implementing them, and we are busy defending the country," Gharibabadi said in a statement carried by the IRGC-affiliated Fars news agency.

In a simultaneous development, Iran's Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei issued a written statement broadcast on state television — his most confrontational public address since taking office. The statement declared that repeated US breaches of the interim deal had shown that "the signature of the US President is utterly worthless and devoid of credibility." Khamenei warned that "the dear nation of Iran and the axis of resistance have unforgettable lessons in store" for the United States if attacks continue. The phrase "unforgettable lessons" carries particular weight in Iranian political vocabulary, having historically preceded escalation decisions rather than diplomatic openings.

The Islamabad MoU — brokered by Qatar and Pakistan and signed in mid-June — had been the most substantial diplomatic framework for ending the conflict since hostilities began in February. However, President Trump declared the agreement "over" at the NATO Ankara summit on July 8, reimposing a naval blockade on Iranian ports and revoking oil sanctions waivers. The Azraq attack and the subsequent collapse of the MoU have left no active diplomatic framework for de-escalation.

Former IRGC commander-in-chief Mohsen Rezaei stated on July 19: "Both diplomacy and negotiations, as well as war, are over. If America continues the war in the next 2-3 days, we will enter the stage of the enemy's 'full-scale invasion and annihilation.'"

Kuwait City Emergency Power Rationing: Conflict Reaches Civilian Life in Gulf Capitals

Iran's sustained attacks on Kuwait's power generation and water desalination facilities brought the direct impact of the US-Iran conflict into the daily lives of residents in a Gulf capital for the first time. On July 19, Kuwait's national electricity authority issued an emergency load-shedding schedule for residential districts across Kuwait City and surrounding towns, forcing rolling blackouts.

Kuwait Petroleum Corporation confirmed that one of its facilities had been hit in "repeated Iranian attacks," causing significant damage and injuries. Kuwait International Airport temporarily suspended flight operations due to security concerns. According to Reuters, residents began stockpiling drinking water and canned goods. Hassan Rayan, a 61-year-old Kuwait resident, said: "The demand for water and canned goods has increased since this morning amid fears that services or supply chains will be affected."

Meanwhile, Iran struck targets in Saudi Arabia for the first time in three months. Saudi civil defense issued emergency shelter alerts in Al-Kharj (home to a military base hosting US troops) and Yanbu (a key Red Sea oil export terminal). The Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) Secretary-General Jasem Mohamed Albudaiwi condemned the Iranian attacks, stating: "Attacks on civilian infrastructure are war crimes. Iran's actions constitute a highly dangerous escalation, a grave violation of international law and the United Nations Charter."

UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres expressed concern over "attacks on civilian infrastructure in Iran and across the region."

Oil Surges 4%, US Issues Worldwide Travel Caution

International oil prices surged more than 4% on July 19, with Brent crude returning above $88 per barrel — the highest level in over a month — following the news of US troop deaths and continued escalation. Transits through the Strait of Hormuz fell to a three-week low as the waterway that previously carried one-fifth of the world's crude oil remains effectively closed due to the conflict. Iran insists the strait must be under its sole control, while the Trump administration has reimposed a naval blockade on Iranian ports.

The US State Department issued a "Worldwide Travel Caution" on the same day, advising American citizens to "exercise increased caution," particularly those in the Middle East. The warning stated that the regional security environment "remains complex" with "potential for further escalation" and periodic airspace closures that could disrupt travel.

President Trump, in an interview with NewsNation, described the two fallen soldiers as having died "in service of our country" but said he "couldn't care less" about Iran's suspension of the MoU, reiterating that Washington's central objective remains "never allowing Iran to have a nuclear weapon." However, with oil prices climbing, US soldiers returning home dead, and midterm congressional elections approaching in November, political pressure on Trump is intensifying rapidly.

Israel Poised to Join? Washington Refuses Israeli Request to Join Strikes

Israel is closely monitoring the escalation. According to Kan public broadcaster, the US has deployed dozens of refueling tankers to Israeli airports and bolstered its F-16 and F-35 fleets in the Middle East in anticipation of potential wider escalation. Hebrew media outlets Channel 12 and Ynet reported that Israel requested to join the strikes on Iran but Washington refused.

Israel assesses that Iran is currently not interested in attacking the Jewish state due to the anticipated severity of the response, but could still order its proxies in Lebanon, Syria, and Yemen to attack. The Israeli military is making extensive preparations for a potential regional flare-up. Meanwhile, Israel continues attacks on southern Lebanon, where the Health Ministry reported 4,328 killed and 12,227 wounded since March 2.

In Yemen, the Houthi movement has threatened to close the Bab el-Mandeb strait if US strikes reach further into Iranian power grid infrastructure. Piracy is also resurgent in the Gulf of Aden: the UK Maritime Trade Operations confirmed a tanker hijacking 65 nautical miles south of Yemen's Al Mukalla on July 19, with the vessel diverted to Somali waters.
