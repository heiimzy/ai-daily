🚀 China's Gravity-1 Rocket Successfully Launches 9 Satellites in First Open-Sea Commercial Mission

At 10:54 AM Beijing time on July 22, OrienSpace's Gravity-1 (Yinli-1) Yao-4 carrier rocket lifted off from waters east of Shanghai, successfully delivering nine satellites—Dongpo 13-14, Dongpo 17-20, Xiguang Erhao 01, Tianyi 49, and Zijing-3—into their designated orbits. The mission was declared a complete success.

This launch marks a significant milestone: it is the Gravity-1's first open-sea launch mission and the first time a Chinese private commercial rocket has been launched from the East China Sea in the Yangtze River Delta region. Previous two flights were conducted nearshore from Haiyang, Shandong. This time, the launch vessel sailed approximately 170 kilometers east of Shanghai into the East China Sea, facing significantly more complex sea conditions.

The Gravity-1 is a solid-boosted medium-lift carrier rocket independently developed by OrienSpace, with a low-Earth orbit payload capacity of 6.5 tons and a 500 km Sun-synchronous orbit capacity of 4.2 tons. It can support "one-arrow-30-satellite" launches for hundred-kilogram-class satellites. The rocket made its maiden flight in January 2024, setting multiple records including the world's largest solid launch vehicle by thrust and China's most powerful commercial rocket. With its third successful flight, Gravity-1 has officially entered the stage of stable, commercial-scale operations.

The satellites aboard this mission serve various purposes: the Dongpo series provides all-weather, all-day Earth observation capabilities; Xiguang Erhao 01 and Tianyi 49 are positioned at the intersection of "AI + commercial space + quantitative remote sensing," serving industries including mining, agriculture, environmental monitoring, and urban planning.

🛡️ AI Security Startup Glow Emerges from Stealth at $1.2B Valuation

On July 22, Glow, a cybersecurity startup founded by former Meta and Snowflake executives, officially emerged from stealth mode, announcing a $180 million all-equity Series A funding round at a $1.2 billion valuation. The company joins a select group of cybersecurity startups to achieve unicorn status before disclosing any revenue metrics.

The round was led by Sequoia Capital, Cyberstarts, Greenoaks, and Redpoint Ventures, with participation from Index Ventures, Swish Ventures, Lux Capital, Operator Collective, and Holly Ventures. Headquartered in Palo Alto, California, Glow was founded in 2025.

Glow is building an end-to-end security platform that helps enterprises monitor and control the software, AI agents, and developer tools running on employee devices. The platform uses specialized AI agents to continuously map enterprise environments, assess risk in real time, and enforce security policies. Co-founder and CEO Roi Tiger explained: "If you think of the past decade, everything was moving to the cloud and SaaS. Suddenly, AI lands on the endpoint in a way we've never seen."

The platform has already demonstrated real-world value: it has prevented malicious npm packages from being installed in customer environments, identified AI agents attempting to pull in such software, and detected employee devices where endpoint detection and response tools were missing or operating with reduced functionality. Unlike existing products that focus primarily on detecting threats after they emerge, Glow is designed to prevent risky software, AI agents, and developer tools from entering enterprise environments in the first place.

Glow enters a crowded endpoint security market dominated by CrowdStrike, Microsoft, SentinelOne, and Palo Alto Networks, but its prevention-first approach positions it to capture market share during a critical window as AI-driven threats accelerate.

💾 Nvidia's Vera CPU Enters Mass Production, Delivers 10x Energy Efficiency Improvement

Nvidia this week disclosed full specifications and benchmark results for its data center CPU, Vera, confirming that first shipments were completed in June to customers including OpenAI, Anthropic, and SpaceX. Ian Buck, Nvidia's Vice President, stated: "We are absolutely in full production. All of our major customers are deploying these systems."

Performance figures are striking: Vera delivers 1.9x performance improvement over x86 chips on agentic AI tasks with 6x lower latency, and exceeds AMD's flagship EPYC Turin CPU by nearly 100% in some industry-standard benchmarks. More importantly, early production testing by cloud partner CoreWeave showed that the Vera Rubin NVL72 platform achieves 10x tokens-per-watt throughput running DeepSeek R1 compared to the previous-generation Blackwell-based GB200 NVL72 system.

Vera features Nvidia's custom Olympus cores—88 cores with 176 hardware threads, 1.2 Tb/s LPDDR5X memory bandwidth, and 164 MB of unified L3 cache. NVLink-C2C interconnect provides 1.8 TB/s of coherent CPU-GPU bandwidth. Nvidia emphasizes that Vera is not designed to replace all x86 servers but to serve as the "max single-threaded CPU at scale," optimized specifically for AI factory workloads where it orchestrates agents, feeds GPUs, and manages memory movement.

Industry analysts view Vera's launch as a pivotal moment in Nvidia's transformation from GPU leader to full-stack AI infrastructure provider. OpenAI plans large-scale Vera Rubin deployment starting this quarter. Wolfe Research estimates Vera's average selling price at approximately $5,000 per chip, with potential shipments of 1.3 million units this year, opening a new $200 billion CPU market for Nvidia.
