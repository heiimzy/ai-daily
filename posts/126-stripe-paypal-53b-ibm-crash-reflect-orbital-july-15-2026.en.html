💳 Stripe and Advent Launch $53 Billion Bid for PayPal

Payments company Stripe and private equity giant Advent International have jointly submitted an offer to acquire PayPal Holdings Inc for $60.50 per share, valuing the payments pioneer at more than $53 billion, Reuters reported exclusively, citing sources familiar with the matter.

Deal Structure: $50B in Committed Bank Financing

The offer, submitted earlier this month, is backed by approximately $50 billion in committed financing from banks, representing a roughly 28% premium over PayPal's closing price on Tuesday. Under the proposal, Stripe and Advent would jointly own PayPal with equal stakes rather than breaking up the company. The approach follows initial discussions between the parties in early April.

PayPal has not yet responded to the offer. Stripe and Advent are seeking to advance discussions in the coming weeks, though sources caution there is no certainty the approach will result in a transaction. Advent declined to comment, while PayPal and Stripe did not immediately respond to requests for comment.

Industry Impact: Reshaping Global Payments

The potential deal would fundamentally reshape the global payments landscape. Stripe, one of the world's most valuable private tech companies (recently valued at ~$70 billion), would create a super-payments platform spanning online payments, merchant services, and consumer wallets. Analysts note that if completed, the deal would directly challenge Block (formerly Square) and traditional banks' dominance in the payments space. Bloomberg previously reported in February that Stripe was considering an offer for PayPal.

📉 IBM Suffers Worst Single-Day Crash in Nearly 40 Years

IBM shares plunged 25% on Tuesday to close at $217.05, marking the company's worst single-day drop in nearly four decades. The collapse came after the technology giant unexpectedly released preliminary second-quarter earnings that fell well short of Wall Street expectations.

Q2 Preliminary Results: Miss on Revenue and Profit

IBM reported adjusted earnings of $2.93 per share on $17.2 billion in revenue, missing analyst estimates of $3.01 per share and $17.86 billion in revenue. CEO Arvind Krishna attributed the shortfall primarily to weakness in the company's Z mainframe business — its enterprise computing systems with advanced AI capabilities.

"Given this was the strongest start to a mainframe program in our history, we expected Infrastructure revenue to decline low-single digits for the year, beginning this quarter," Krishna wrote in a letter to investors. "What played out was worse than our expectations, driven by a shortfall in our Z performance and the associated software stack."

"Clients are cutting spending on IBM products and reprioritizing their capex spend on servers, storage, and memory purchases ahead of expected price increases." — IBM CEO Arvind Krishna

Ripple Effects: Tech Sector Under Pressure

IBM's earnings warning sent shockwaves through the technology sector, dragging down software names including ServiceNow, Salesforce, and Microsoft. The rout raises fresh questions about whether companies are seeing sufficient near-term returns from their artificial intelligence spending. While AI infrastructure investment remains robust, the conversion to software and services revenue appears to be taking longer than anticipated, casting a shadow over the broader tech outlook.

🛰️ FCC Approves Reflect Orbital's Space Mirror, Sparking Astronomers' Outrage

The Federal Communications Commission has authorized California-based startup Reflect Orbital to launch and operate Eärendil-1, an experimental satellite equipped with an 18-meter thin-film reflector designed to beam sunlight to dark parts of Earth at night. The July 9 approval has drawn sharp criticism from astronomers and environmental advocates.

Technical Specifications: An 18-Meter Mirror in Low Earth Orbit

The 142-kilogram spacecraft is scheduled to launch later this year into an orbit 600 to 650 kilometers in altitude. It will deploy a steerable 18-meter reflective surface capable of directing sunlight to specific areas on Earth for several minutes at a time, illuminating areas approximately 5-6 kilometers in diameter. Reflect Orbital says the technology could enable nighttime solar farm operations and provide emergency lighting for disaster-stricken areas.

The company has far more ambitious long-term plans: operating a constellation of 50,000 or more such mirror satellites by 2035. CEO Ben Nowack described the FCC license as "the first step toward rigorously testing our technology's efficacy and the safeguards we have developed."

Scientific Opposition: An 'Existential Threat' to Astronomy

The FCC received nearly 2,000 public comments opposing the project. The American Astronomical Society, the Royal Astronomical Society, and DarkSky International were among the bodies warning about the satellite's impact on astronomy, ecosystems, and public safety. Betty Kioko, institutional affairs officer for the European Southern Observatory, called it "an existential threat" to optical astronomy.

DarkSky International stated it is evaluating legal options to prevent the satellite's launch, warning that "moving beams of light from space-based mirrors could create sudden flashes, glare, or sweeping illumination patterns if systems malfunction," posing risks to night vision, aircraft operations, and drivers. The organization also warned of potential disruption to nocturnal ecosystems, human sleep patterns, and metabolic processes.

The FCC largely concluded that environmental and astronomical impacts fell outside its jurisdiction, stating that "it is in the public interest to make spectrum available to encourage companies to test new and innovative space activities." Reflect Orbital has committed to collaborating with NASA and the National Science Foundation to address astronomical concerns.
