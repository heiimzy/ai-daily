⚡ July 22 International Politics: US Expands 11th Night Strikes Across Iran — Tehran Air Defenses Open Fire, Bidboland Gas Refinery Hit, IRGC Destroys US Radars; Trump Approves $30B Saudi Nuclear Deal

1. 11th Night Strikes: Campaign Expands Across Iran for the First Time

The US Central Command (CENTCOM) confirmed the completion of the 11th consecutive night of strikes against Iran early Wednesday (Beijing time). For the first time, the strikes expanded to cover virtually all of Iran — from Tabriz in the northwest to Bushehr in the south, from Kermanshah in the west to Chabahar in the southeast.

Iranian state television (IRIB) reported that air defense systems were activated across east, west, and northeast Tehran simultaneously — marking the first time the capital's entire air defense network has been triggered since the conflict escalated. Semi-official Fars News Agency reported heavy anti-aircraft fire in Tehran's northeastern Sohanak district, near military and command facilities in the hills above the city.

Military analysts say US drones may have been flown over the capital to force Iranian air defenses to switch on, revealing their positions for future targeting — a standard air campaign tactic known as "defense suppression by exposure."

CENTCOM stated on X that its forces struck Iranian military operations centers, maritime capabilities, aircraft hangars, drone storage facilities, and military logistics infrastructure, designed to "further degrade Iran's ability to threaten commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz." The command noted Iran has attacked more than 30 commercial vessels transiting the international waterway over the past three months.

CENTCOM announced the "successful completion" of the 11th night of operations at 8:15 p.m. ET on Tuesday.

2. Bidboland Gas Refinery — Iran's Largest Gas Processing Facility Hit

The most notable target in this round of strikes was the Bidboland Gas Refinery in Mahshahr, Khuzestan Province — Iran's largest gas processing facility and the largest gas hub in West Asia. Local Iranian media reported the refinery was hit by US airstrikes; the extent of damage is still being assessed.

Additional confirmed targets include:

  • Bushehr Airport Military Section — Bushehr province is also home to Iran's only operational nuclear power plant
  • Military site near Tabriz — East Azerbaijan Province Crisis Management confirmed a military installation was struck
  • Behbahan and Omidiyeh — Khuzestan officials confirmed missile strikes hit these southwestern cities
  • Kangavar — western Kermanshah province hit by missile strike
  • Chabahar — four explosions reported in southeastern Sistan and Baluchestan, with US fighter jets flying over the port city

3. IRGC Destroys US Radar Systems in Kuwait and Bahrain

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) published multiple battle reports confirming the 24th wave of Operation Nasr 2:

  • Destroyed an early-warning radar and a tactical radar complex near Ali Al Salem Air Base in Kuwait
  • Destroyed another radar system on Kuwait's Bubiyan Island
  • All targets confirmed "rendered inoperable"

The IRGC also claimed multiple missile and drone strikes on US facilities in Bahrain and Jordan. Iran's Khatam al-Anbiya Central Headquarters released a statement early Wednesday warning that any US attack on Iran's nuclear facilities or other sensitive sites would be considered "an expansion of war in the region," threatening strikes on "all the interests of America, the allies and supporters."

The statement comes in direct response to President Trump's threat to strike the Pickaxe Mountain underground nuclear tunnel complex near Natanz.

4. War Cost Surges to $37.5 Billion; Hegseth Requests $87 Billion More

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth told the Senate Appropriations Committee on Tuesday that the US-Iran war has cost $37.5 billion so far in fiscal 2026 — up nearly $8 billion from the $29 billion estimate in May. Hegseth said the figure includes military pay, operations, and other anticipated costs through September 30.

Hegseth warned the Pentagon urgently needs an additional $87 billion in emergency funding ($67 billion for Middle East operations alone), or US forces will face critical resource gaps. The hearing was repeatedly interrupted by anti-war protesters shouting that Hegseth's hands are "covered in blood" and demanding an end to the "illegal war" against Iran.

The Pentagon has confirmed 18 US service members killed since the conflict began on February 28, with over 100 wounded — though an anonymous US official told the AP the actual number of wounded now exceeds 500.

"These devastating blows will continue until the president deems otherwise." — Senior US official speaking to Axios

5. Trump Approves Landmark Saudi Civilian Nuclear Deal

Amid the ongoing conflict, President Trump has formally approved a landmark civilian nuclear agreement with Saudi Arabia, according to The Wall Street Journal. The 30-year deal, estimated to be worth tens of billions of dollars, is designed to give American companies a central role in developing Saudi Arabia's nuclear infrastructure.

Key provisions include:

  • American primacy — US firms take the lead in nuclear infrastructure development, excluding foreign competitors
  • Uranium enrichment plant — American companies would build an enrichment facility if a joint US-Saudi review deems it justified
  • US oversight — The agreement provides Washington with monitoring rights to prevent weapons proliferation

The deal is expected to be sent to Congress for review in the coming days but faces potential opposition from lawmakers concerned about expanding nuclear capabilities in the Middle East during a period of heightened regional tensions.

6. Houthi Red Sea Blockade: Saudi Oil Tankers Divert to Suez

The impact of the Houthi-declared naval blockade on Saudi Arabia is already visible in real shipping data. According to British maritime risk management firm Vanguard, two tankers that had just loaded Saudi crude at the Red Sea port of Yanbu — destined for China and India — made U-turns on Tuesday, heading instead toward the Suez Canal.

The Saudi-led coalition has announced it will deploy forces to ensure safe passage through the Bab el-Mandeb Strait. However, shipping companies remain on high alert given the Houthis' track record of successful Red Sea attacks. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed, Saudi Arabia had been relying on a pipeline to the Red Sea to export oil — but the Red Sea route is now also under threat.

Analysts warn that a full blockade of the Bab el-Mandeb — threatening 7% of global oil supply — combined with the near-complete closure of the Strait of Hormuz (approximately 20% of global oil and gas transit) would represent the most severe dual strait blockade crisis since 1973.
