⚡ July 19 Tech Roundup: Alibaba Cloud Bailian Launches HappyOyster World Model, Anthropic-Meta $10B Compute Talks, OpenAI Ships Codex Micro

Alibaba Cloud Bailian Launches HappyOyster 1.0 Open World Model

On the morning of July 19, Alibaba Cloud Bailian officially launched HappyOyster 1.0, an open world model product capable of real-time construction and interaction. This marks the first time the world model — developed by Alibaba's ATH Innovation Business Division — has been made available as an enterprise-grade API on Alibaba Cloud's flagship AI development platform, transitioning from consumer experience to commercial deployment.

HappyOyster 1.0's core proposition is that users can generate a complete, interactive, and explorable AI digital world with just a single sentence or image. The product offers two core modes:

  • Adventure Mode: Users freely explore AI-generated digital spaces and interact with environments and characters in real time, similar to an open-world game.
  • Directing Mode: Users can control narrative direction, character behavior, and scene changes like a film director, enabling highly interactive storytelling.

The Bailian version ships with Android, iOS, and Web SDKs and is currently in grey-scale testing. This means developers and enterprise customers can integrate HappyOyster 1.0's world model capabilities directly into their own applications — spanning game development, virtual social experiences, interactive education, digital tourism, and live-streaming interactions.

A representative from Alibaba's ATH Innovation Business Division noted that HappyOyster 1.0 can deeply model physical world state transition rules, proactively deduce causal chains from actions to feedback, and maintain long-term consistency for characters and environments. This physics-aware modeling capability gives it a significant advantage in interactive games, virtual companionship, and interactive short-form drama.

Beyond SDK access, Alibaba Cloud Bailian will gradually open HappyOyster 1.0's API, further unleashing the productivity of world models and enabling industries to cross the threshold from "watching AI generate content" to "stepping into worlds created by AI."

Anthropic in Talks with Meta for $10 Billion AI Computing Lease

AI startup Anthropic is negotiating with Meta over a potential $10 billion data center computing resource lease, according to reports from The Chosun Ilbo citing Reuters sources. If finalized, the deal would mark Meta's first sale of its internal AI infrastructure to an external customer, signaling the company's official entry into the cloud computing business.

Sources say Anthropic proposed a two-year lease plan to Meta last month, and Meta is currently reviewing the proposal. The contract could be worth up to $10 billion, with monthly rental payments and terms allowing either party to terminate early under specific conditions. Talks remain in early stages, and the possibility of no final agreement remains.

This negotiation comes as Anthropic aggressively expands its computing capacity for its Claude AI service. In May, Anthropic signed a deal with SpaceX to use the "Colossus 1" AI data center in Memphis, Tennessee — housing over 220,000 NVIDIA GPUs — at $1.25 billion per month. This month, it also signed a 20-year, $19 billion data center lease with TeraWulf for a 401 MW facility in Kentucky, with partial operations expected in the second half of 2027.

For Meta, this transaction represents a significant step in its revenue diversification strategy. Currently heavily reliant on advertising, Meta has been exploring cloud computing for some time. CEO Mark Zuckerberg stated at the May shareholder meeting that external companies contact Meta almost weekly to purchase its AI models or surplus computing power. Meta's capital expenditure reached approximately $72.2 billion last year, with this year's projection raised to $125-145 billion.

Notably, Meta plans to reassign Dave Brown, a senior executive who spent about 20 years at Amazon Web Services, to oversee data center construction — reporting directly to infrastructure head Sanjay Jaanardan. This personnel move further signals Meta's serious commitment to building external cloud services.

Analysts suggest that if Meta successfully enters the cloud market, it would directly compete with "neocloud" companies like CoreWeave and Nebius, as well as traditional cloud giants AWS, Azure, and GCP. In an era of AI computing shortage, "compute-as-a-service" is becoming the new battleground for tech giants.

OpenAI Unveils Codex Micro, Its First Consumer Hardware for AI Coding

OpenAI has officially launched the Codex Micro — its first consumer-facing hardware product. Priced at $230, this mechanical keypad is designed specifically for developers using the Codex AI coding platform. Developed in collaboration with premium keyboard maker Work Louder, the device is positioned as a limited-run product, with pre-orders open now and first units expected to ship later this month.

Key hardware specifications include:

  • 13 mechanical switches: 6 dedicated Agent keys plus customizable Command keys
  • Rotary encoder dial: For real-time adjustment of Codex AI's reasoning level
  • Analog joystick: Launches a radial menu for common workflows like code review, debugging, and refactoring
  • Capacitive touch sensor: For auxiliary interactions
  • RGB lighting: Per-key and perimeter lighting indicates agent status (white=idle, green=unread chat, blue=thinking, peach=awaiting input, red=error)

The device connects via Bluetooth or USB-C and is compatible with both Windows and macOS. It features a CNC-machined aluminum body with polycarbonate parts and includes 32 interchangeable keycaps — including playful "yolo" and "yeet" caps.

Codex Micro's standout feature is its deep software integration. Developers can monitor and control up to six Codex AI agents simultaneously through physical keys without switching between IDE and desktop applications. The rotary dial allows fine-grained adjustment of AI reasoning depth — low for quick tasks, high for complex coding challenges. According to OpenAI, Codex's weekly active users have surged from 3 million in early April to 8 million by mid-July, with 9 million projected soon.

However, the $230 price tag has drawn some criticism. The device is essentially a customized version of Work Louder's existing Creator Micro 2 ($174), with the $56 premium primarily covering OpenAI-specific keycaps and software integration. For heavy Codex users, the physical shortcuts and reasoning dial can significantly boost coding efficiency; for casual users, general-purpose macro keypads may offer better value.

Industry observers note that Codex Micro represents a new trend of AI companies expanding into hardware — locking users into their ecosystem through physical devices. While its limited-run status suggests it's more experiment than product line, it marks an important step in AI's evolution from pure software to integrated "software + hardware" experiences.
