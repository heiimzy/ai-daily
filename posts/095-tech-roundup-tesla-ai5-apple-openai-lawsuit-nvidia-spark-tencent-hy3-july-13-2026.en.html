⚡ July 13 Tech Roundup: Samsung Taylor Fab Mass-Produces Tesla AI5 Chip, Apple Sues OpenAI for Trade Secret Theft, Nvidia RTX Spark Debuts at BW2026, Tencent Open-Sources Hy3

1. Samsung Taylor Fab Completes Tesla AI5 Chip Tape-Out, 2nm Production Begins

Samsung Electronics' Taylor fab in Texas has completed the tape-out of Tesla's AI5 chip for self-driving systems and is set to begin mass production, The Korea Times reported on July 13. This marks a major milestone in the Samsung-Tesla partnership reaching full-scale manufacturing.

James Kim, a principal engineer at Samsung Foundry, posted on LinkedIn that "the Tesla-Samsung AI5 chip has reached tape-out" and is "scheduled to be manufactured at the Taylor fab using our latest 2nm process and will soon be integrated into Tesla's newest products." He subsequently deleted the post after news reports picked it up.

Tape-out represents the completion of a chip's design phase, meaning the blueprint is finalized and handed off for fabrication. After passing pre-validation, the taped-out design is used to create photomasks and undergo wafer fabrication for engineering samples, which then go through qualification with the customer before entering mass production.

In April, Elon Musk posted on X that Tesla's AI5 team had taped out the chip's design to both Samsung and TSMC. Musk noted that slightly different versions would be produced at the two foundries because "they translate designs to physical form differently."

The fact that Samsung will use its 2nm process for AI5 — rather than the 3nm many had expected — suggests the company's 2nm yields have exceeded 60 percent. This development comes amid reports that Anthropic is also exploring AI chip manufacturing through Samsung Foundry, adding to optimism about the Korean foundry's business recovery. Industry sources estimate Samsung Foundry's mid- to long-term order backlog could approach 50 trillion won ($38 billion).

2. Apple Sues OpenAI for Trade Secret Theft, Alleges 400+ Employees Poached

Apple filed a federal lawsuit on July 11 in the Northern District of California accusing OpenAI and its hardware chief Tang Tan of orchestrating a coordinated campaign to steal trade secrets for OpenAI's consumer hardware expansion.

The iPhone maker alleges that OpenAI encouraged Apple employees to share information, components, drawings, and materials related to upcoming products. According to the complaint, more than 400 former Apple employees now work at OpenAI. "At every level, from members of its technical staff to its chief hardware officer, and in coordination with business partners, OpenAI has been stealing Apple's trade secrets and confidential information," the suit states.

Named defendants include OpenAI Foundation, OpenAI Group PBC, and io Products — the hardware startup OpenAI acquired for $6.5 billion last year. Tang Tan, Apple's former VP of product design for iPhone and Apple Watch who left in 2024 to co-found io Products with Jony Ive, is also named. The suit additionally calls out former iPhone hardware engineer Chang Liu, who allegedly "surreptitiously accessed and downloaded dozens of Apple's confidential hardware-related files" before joining OpenAI in January.

Apple demands that OpenAI cease all infringing practices, destroy proprietary materials, and redesign upcoming products to exclude any Apple technology. Apple says it attempted to resolve the dispute out of court months ago but received no response.

"Apple is developing smart glasses, pendant devices, camera-equipped AirPods, and more — all part of its bid to stay competitive in the AI era."

This lawsuit highlights the strategic importance of next-generation AI devices. While Apple and OpenAI announced a partnership at WWDC 2024 to integrate ChatGPT into Siri and iOS, their relationship has soured as OpenAI aggressively expands into hardware. The case could potentially slow OpenAI's hardware timeline through injunctions or discovery proceedings.

3. Nvidia RTX Spark Superchip Debuts at Bilibili World 2026: Laptops Run 120B Models Locally

At Bilibili World 2026 in Shanghai, Nvidia publicly demonstrated laptops powered by the RTX Spark superchip for the first time. Designed for personal AI agents, the chip represents a major leap in AI PC hardware capabilities.

The RTX Spark connects a Blackwell RTX GPU with Nvidia's custom 20-core Grace CPU via NVLink-C2C technology, delivering 1 Petaflop of compute power and 128GB of unified memory. This configuration enables laptops to run 120-billion-parameter large language models locally with a 1-million-token context window — eliminating the need to chunk long documents.

Live demonstrations showed the system rendering 90GB+ 3D scenes in Unreal Engine 5 and maintaining stable 100+ FPS on AAA games at 1440p resolution. Nvidia also showcased the DGX Spark desktop supercomputer for developers, supporting fine-tuning of up to 200B parameter models.

Industry analysts view the RTX Spark as a pivotal transition from cloud-dependent AI toward local deployment, bringing data-center-grade AI capabilities to personal computing for the first time.

4. Tencent Open-Sources Hy3: 295B MoE Model Under Apache 2.0

Tencent has officially released Hy3, an open-source large language model that represents a significant strategic move by a Chinese tech giant in the open-source AI ecosystem. Hy3 employs a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture with 295 billion total parameters, activating only 21B per forward pass through Top-8 routing, plus a 3.8B MTP layer.

The model supports up to 256K context length and outperforms models five times its parameter count on multiple benchmarks, achieving impressive performance while maintaining high inference efficiency at a fraction of the computational cost.

Tencent has released Hy3 under the Apache 2.0 license on Hugging Face, ModelScope, and GitHub, with FP8 quantized versions also available. This open approach means developers worldwide can freely use, modify, and commercialize the model.

On the application front, Tencent has integrated Hy3 into its enterprise work assistant, Yuanbao, WeChat, and QQ products. The model's efficient MoE architecture delivers inference costs at roughly one-fifth those of equivalent-performance dense models — a critical advantage for real-world deployments at scale.

Industry experts view Hy3 as a milestone in the maturation of China's LLM ecosystem. It demonstrates Chinese AI teams' ability to achieve state-of-the-art performance with significantly fewer activated parameters, while adopting the most open licensing approach to accelerate global AI innovation.
