⚡ July 18 Tech Roundup: Apple Widens Legal War on OpenAI, Anthropic-Meta $10B Compute Talks

Friday, July 17 brought a wave of major tech developments. Apple escalated its trade secret lawsuit against OpenAI, sending legal preservation letters to roughly 40 former employees now working at the AI company; Anthropic entered preliminary talks with Meta about a potential $10 billion compute lease; Google rebranded its popular NotebookLM as Gemini Notebook and expanded code execution to Pro users; and Intel's 18A process node achieved 85% yield, marking the first volume shipment of logic chips using High NA EUV lithography. Here's the full rundown.

🍎 Apple Sends Legal Preservation Letters to ~40 Ex-Employees at OpenAI

Apple has sent legal preservation letters to approximately 40 former employees now working at OpenAI, according to the Financial Times. The letters require recipients to preserve all documents, communications, and evidence potentially relevant to Apple's trade secret lawsuit against the AI company, signaling a significant escalation in the legal battle.

Apple filed the lawsuit on July 10 in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California, accusing OpenAI of systematically recruiting Apple employees to steal hardware-related trade secrets. The complaint names OpenAI's Chief Hardware Officer Tang Tan — a 24-year Apple veteran who served as VP of Product Design for iPhone and Apple Watch — and Chang Liu, a senior systems electrical engineer. Apple alleges Tan used job interviews to extract details about unreleased Apple products and coached departing employees on how to bypass company security procedures.

The filing claims over 400 former Apple employees now work at OpenAI. Apple stated in its court filing that the evidence uncovered so far may represent only the "tip of the iceberg." The company wrote: "Discovery will expose that the misappropriation has been occurring on a scale many times greater than the several instances described below." OpenAI has denied all allegations, stating it is "not aware of any evidence that this complaint has merit."

The timing is particularly sensitive for OpenAI as it prepares for what is expected to be one of the largest tech IPOs in history. According to Kalshi prediction markets, the probability of OpenAI completing its IPO in 2026 dropped from approximately 22% to roughly 18.5% following Apple's filing.

🤝 Anthropic in Talks With Meta for $10B Compute Lease

AI startup Anthropic is in preliminary discussions with Meta to lease computing power from the social media giant's data centers, according to The New York Times. Sources familiar with the matter said the companies are negotiating a potential deal worth up to $10 billion over two years. Meta shares jumped about 2% intraday on Friday following the report.

Anthropic proposed the arrangement in June, and Meta is currently evaluating the offer. Under the reported terms, Anthropic would make monthly payments over the two-year period, with both companies retaining the option to exit early. The deal would be similar to Anthropic's existing arrangement with SpaceX, under which it pays approximately $1.25 billion per month for capacity at SpaceX's Colossus 1 data center.

For Meta, the potential deal marks a strategic shift toward monetizing its massive AI infrastructure. The company expects 2026 capital expenditures of up to $145 billion, predominantly for AI infrastructure. CEO Mark Zuckerberg has previously indicated the company is considering entering the cloud computing business by selling excess compute capacity. This week, Meta hired former Amazon Web Services executive Dave Brown as its head of infrastructure, further signaling its infrastructure-as-a-service ambitions.

For Anthropic, securing adequate compute remains a critical bottleneck forcing the company to limit access to its most advanced models, including Claude Fable 5. Beyond Meta and SpaceX, Anthropic has signed a 10-year, ~$100 billion partnership with AWS and a $19 billion, 20-year data center lease with TeraWulf in Kentucky.

📝 Google Rebrands NotebookLM as Gemini Notebook, Expands Code Execution

Google is renaming its popular AI research assistant NotebookLM to Gemini Notebook, folding the product into its unified Gemini brand. The tool now serves more than 30 million users and 600,000 organizations since its debut as Project Tailwind at I/O 2023.

Alongside the rebrand, Google is expanding the tool's cloud code execution capability from Ultra-tier subscribers to Pro users. The feature, rolled out last month, gives each notebook a secure cloud computer that can write and run Python scripts against uploaded source documents, enabling complex data analysis, chart generation, and output formats including spreadsheets and slide decks.

Google is also pushing notebooks into more products across its ecosystem. Users can already create and access notebooks directly within the Gemini app with full cross-app syncing. The company plans to bring notebooks into AI Mode in Search as well, though it has not shared a timeline.

The rebrand continues Google's pattern of absorbing successful experimental products into the Gemini brand. NotebookLM's core proposition — grounding AI responses exclusively in user-provided sources rather than the open web — remains unchanged. Josh Woodward, VP of Google Labs, the Gemini app, and AI Studio, oversees the product.

🔬 Intel 18A Reaches 85% Yield Milestone, Ships First High NA EUV Logic Chips

Intel's advanced 18A process node has achieved approximately 85% yield, up sharply from roughly 65% in the previous quarter, according to KeyBanc Capital Markets. The figure crosses a threshold widely viewed as the point at which a leading-edge node can compete commercially in the foundry market. ASML confirmed that Intel Foundry has become the first company to ship a high-volume logic product using High NA EUV lithography, with Panther Lake processors already reaching customers.

The yield improvement changes the node's economics. Semiconductor yields determine how many functioning chips come off a wafer, and at Intel's target geometries, even modest improvements significantly impact cost and supply stability. KeyBanc's analysis portrays 18A as having made a decisive step toward merchant foundry viability, while still slightly trailing TSMC's N2 node at approximately 90% yield.

In advanced packaging, Intel's EMIB-T has reached approximately 98% yield, placing it on par with TSMC's CoWoS. If verified, Intel would offer chip designers and hyperscalers a U.S.-based alternative for integrating accelerators with high-bandwidth memory, potentially easing a critical AI infrastructure bottleneck.

KeyBanc also reported a wide set of potential design wins including Apple, AMD, Nvidia, Marvell, Microsoft, Micron, and OpenAI, though none have publicly confirmed contracts. Intel's decision to bring more than 80% of its next-generation Nova Lake desktop chip orders back in-house on 18A signals growing internal confidence. Intel shares rose approximately 4.5% on the day.
