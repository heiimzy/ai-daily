⚡ July 13 Financial Roundup: KOSPI Crashes 9% to New Low, Europe Slides, Fed Chair Warsh's Debut Testimony Anchors a Pivotal Week

Global financial markets experienced a "Black Monday" on July 13 as escalating Middle East hostilities triggered a broad selloff across asset classes. South Korea's KOSPI led the rout with a 9% crash, SK Hynix lost 15% in a single session, European markets extended losses with the Euro Stoxx 50 closing 0.43% lower, Brent crude held firm above $79, and gold slumped below $4,060. This week also brings a triple-header of market-moving events: Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's first congressional testimony, June CPI data, and simultaneous Q2 earnings from five major US banks.

1. Korea's KOSPI Crashes 9%: SK Hynix's Whiplash Week

Asian markets suffered their worst day in months, with South Korea's KOSPI plunging 9% to close at 6,806.93 — a fresh year-to-date low and the largest single-day drop since the pandemic era. The index briefly extended losses to 9.4%, triggering the SIDECAR circuit breaker.

The primary culprit was semiconductor stocks. SK Hynix cratered more than 15% in Seoul, a spectacular reversal from Friday's 13% surge on its New York debut — the largest overseas listing in Korean corporate history, raising approximately $26.5 billion. Market sentiment flipped from euphoria to panic over a single weekend. Samsung Electronics also tumbled 10.7%, amplifying the selloff. Since mid-June highs, SK Hynix has now lost nearly 40% of its value.

Japan's Nikkei 225 fell 1.9% to 67,242.73, with chip equipment makers Advantest and Tokyo Electron hit hard. China's Shanghai Composite dropped 2.1% to 3,913.79. Markets across Singapore, Wellington, Mumbai, and Jakarta also declined. Hong Kong's Hang Seng Index bucked the trend, edging up 0.2% to 24,213 as the region's sole bright spot.

2. European Markets Slide as Oil Spikes

European stocks opened sharply lower and never recovered. The pan-European Euro Stoxx 50 closed down 0.43% at 6,243 points, with the technology sector falling 1.1%. Germany's DAX futures indicated a 0.6% decline, France's CAC 40 traded lower, while the UK's FTSE 100 managed to hold roughly flat.

The core anxiety for European markets was energy. Brent crude surged 4.1% to $79.11 a barrel, while WTI crude climbed 4.1% to $74.37 — a sharp rebound from the mid-June trough near $70. Over the weekend, US and Iranian forces exchanged heavy missile and drone strikes, and Iran's Revolutionary Guards announced the "indefinite closure" of the Strait of Hormuz. Just six vessels were tracked crossing the strait on Monday, compared to the pre-war average of 130 per day.

"Clearly, the risk is that this escalates to levels seen early in the war, where neighboring countries and their energy infrastructure are also targeted," warned ING commodities strategists Warren Patterson and Ewa Manthey. Analysts cautioned that a sustained Hormuz closure could disrupt roughly 20 million barrels per day of crude flows, potentially driving oil prices to $110-150 in an extreme scenario.

3. Gold Defies Safe-Haven Logic, Drops to $4,060

In a rare departure from traditional safe-haven dynamics, gold prices fell despite the surge in geopolitical risk. COMEX gold dropped approximately 1.5% to trade near $4,060 an ounce, touching an intraday low of $4,061. Silver fared worse, shedding about 2% to $58.63 an ounce.

The paradox is explained by the sharp rise in bond yields and the US dollar. The 2-year Treasury yield surged to 4.2393%, its highest level since early 2025, as the market repriced rate hike expectations. The US Dollar Index held firm at 101.13. In a rising-rate environment, non-yielding gold loses its appeal—even intensifying geopolitical tensions cannot fully offset the headwind from climbing real yields.

"Gold is caught between two opposing forces: geopolitical risks supporting safe-haven buying, and macro factors—particularly higher yields and a stronger dollar—that continue to pressure prices," noted ET Now markets desk.

4. The Super Central Bank Week: Warsh + CPI + Big 5 Earnings

This week represents a concentrated stress test for global markets, with three mega-events converging:

A. Fed Chair Warsh's Congressional Debut (Tue-Wed)

Newly appointed Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh will testify before the House Financial Services Committee on Tuesday and the Senate Banking Committee on Wednesday—his first public Congressional appearance since taking office. The Fed's latest Monetary Policy Report reinforced its commitment to "deliver price stability," revising 2026 inflation forecasts up to 3.6% (core 3.3%) while trimming growth projections from 2.4% to 2.2%. Markets will scrutinize his tone for any signal on the rate path.

B. June CPI Data (Tuesday)

The June Consumer Price Index, releasing Tuesday at 8:30 AM ET, is expected to show headline CPI rising just 0.1% month-over-month (down from 0.5% in May), with the annual rate slowing to 3.8% from 4.2%. Core CPI is forecast at +0.2% m/m. However, the recent oil price surge risks reversing some of the gasoline-driven disinflation.

C. Big 5 Bank Earnings (Tuesday)

JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, Goldman Sachs, and Citigroup all report Q2 results on the same day—an unusually dense earnings calendar. Goldman Sachs projects 2026 S&P 500 EPS of $340 (+24% YoY), but warns that a Fed rate hike cycle could compress S&P 500 P/E multiples by approximately 6% due to rising cost of capital. AI infrastructure names now account for 42% of S&P 500 market cap and are highly sensitive to rate changes.

5. Rate Hike Expectations Surge: 39bps of Tightening Priced

Fed funds futures underwent significant repricing on Monday. Markets now price in roughly 39 basis points of total tightening by year-end, with the CME FedWatch Tool showing a 52.1% probability of at least two rate hikes by December, up from 47.6% on Friday.

The dollar benefited from both safe-haven inflows and rising rate expectations, with the DXY index holding at 101.13. USD/JPY traded at 162.03, EUR/USD slipped to 1.1394. Cryptocurrencies also sold off: Bitcoin fell 0.6% to $63,770, and Ethereum dropped 1.1% to $1,801.

"This reignites concerns that if energy prices rise from here, we could start to see rate hikes pulled forward," said Tony Sycamore, market analyst at IG in Sydney.

Looking ahead, the fate of global markets hinges on Tuesday and Wednesday's data and testimony trifecta. A softer CPI print could temporarily ease rate hike fears, while a hot number combined with hawkish Warsh commentary could trigger another wave of volatility. The trajectory of Middle East geopolitics remains the largest wild card.
