1. Samsung Galaxy Unpacked 2026 London: Three Foldables + Two Smartwatches Debut

Samsung Electronics hosted its summer Galaxy Unpacked event in London on July 22, themed "A New Shape Unfolds," unveiling an unprecedented three foldable smartphones and two smartwatches in what is the company's largest single hardware launch event to date.

Galaxy Z Fold 8: A New Wide Form Factor

The highlight of the event is the completely redesigned Galaxy Z Fold 8. Abandoning the tall, narrow design Samsung has used for seven generations, the new Fold adopts a passport-style wide form factor with a 5.5-inch cover display and a 7.6-inch inner display in a nearly square 4:3 aspect ratio. The inner screen features 2448×1848 resolution at 120Hz dynamic refresh rate, and incorporates Samsung's newly announced Flex Titanium hinge technology that dramatically reduces the crease, reportedly approaching the industry benchmark set by the Oppo Find N6.

Under the hood, the Z Fold 8 packs a Qualcomm Snapdragon 8 Elite Gen 5 For Galaxy processor, 12GB or 16GB of RAM, and up to 1TB of storage. It features a dual 50MP rear camera setup (main + ultrawide), a 4,800mAh battery, and supports 45W wired charging. Starting price is expected between $1,899 and $1,999, positioning it as the more accessible large-foldable option.

Galaxy Z Fold 8 Ultra: The True Flagship Successor

The Galaxy Z Fold 8 Ultra serves as the genuine successor to the Z Fold 7. It retains the traditional tall foldable design with an 8-inch inner display and 6.5-inch cover screen, with resolution boosted to approximately 500 PPI — matching the Galaxy S26 Ultra. It sports a triple rear camera setup (including a 50MP ultrawide upgrade), a substantially larger 5,000mAh battery (14% increase over the Fold 7's 4,400mAh), and 45W fast charging. Expected starting price is approximately $2,099.

Notably, Samsung has restructured its foldable lineup naming: the wide variant takes the standard "Galaxy Z Fold 8" name, while the traditional flagship carries the "Ultra" suffix — a strategy designed to compete with Apple's anticipated foldable iPhone while covering a broader consumer base through differentiated positioning.

Galaxy Z Flip 8, Watch 9 & Watch Ultra 2

The Galaxy Z Flip 8 clamshell foldable is expected to feature an Exynos 2600 processor with a thinner, lighter chassis, starting at approximately $1,099. On the wearable front, Samsung launched the Galaxy Watch 9 series and Galaxy Watch Ultra 2, both upgraded to the Snapdragon Wear Elite chip platform. The Ultra 2 starts at $650, the Watch 9 at $350. Samsung also teased its upcoming Galaxy Glasses smart glasses project, expected later this year.

Industry Context: IDC data shows global foldable phone shipments grew 42% year-over-year in Q2 2026, with Samsung maintaining 55% market share. The expanded Z Fold 8 lineup represents Samsung's strategic response to Apple's rumored foldable iPhone launch expected in September.

2. Apple-OpenAI Trade Secret Lawsuit Escalates: Preservation Letters Sent to 40+ Former Employees

The legal battle between Apple and OpenAI is rapidly intensifying. Following its July 10 filing of a 41-page complaint in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California, Apple sent legal preservation letters on July 17 to approximately 40 former Apple employees now working at OpenAI, instructing them to retain all documents and communications relevant to the litigation.

The move dramatically expands the scope of the investigation beyond the two initially named defendants — former Apple senior systems electrical engineer Chang Liu and former VP of Product Design for iPhone and Apple Watch Tang Yew Tan, now OpenAI's Chief Hardware Officer. Apple's complaint characterizes its findings as merely "the tip of the iceberg."

Key Allegations

The most concrete allegation involves Chang Liu, who joined OpenAI in January 2026 after eight years at Apple. According to the complaint, Liu failed to return his company-issued MacBook and exploited a previously unknown authentication vulnerability to access Apple's internal file servers for weeks after his departure, downloading confidential engineering files including a multi-layer logic board manufacturing presentation totaling over 1,000 pages. Liu allegedly messaged a colleague still at Apple: "LOL, I found out I can access the [network storage], so funny."

Allegations against Tang Tan are more systematic. Apple claims Tan used his 24 years of insider knowledge at Apple to question candidates using internal Apple project codenames about unreleased products, and directed candidates still employed at Apple to bring "actual parts" — batteries, logic boards, system-in-package components — to OpenAI interviews for "show and tell" sessions. Apple further alleges Tan retained an internal Apple document marked "Need to Know" detailing employee departure security procedures and shared it with incoming OpenAI hires to help them evade Apple's exit protocols.

Additionally, Apple alleges OpenAI used insider terminology to approach Apple's manufacturing partners, and caused one supplier to perform Apple's proprietary metal-finishing technique under the false pretense that Apple had authorized the work.

OpenAI's Response and IPO Implications

OpenAI responded stating: "We have no interest in other companies' trade secrets. We remain focused on building innovative technology that empowers people everywhere." CEO Sam Altman added on social media: "I am not afraid of Apple, but I have tremendous respect for them."

Legal experts note the case poses significant risk to OpenAI's anticipated IPO. Avery Williams, co-chair of the trade secret practice at McKool Smith, told The Verge: "It's never fantastic to get sued by Apple when you're trying to IPO. Apple is a tenacious litigant. They do not tend to back down." OpenAI confidentially filed an S-1 with the SEC in June and is targeting a valuation above $1 trillion, with a potential October 2026 IPO.

Deep Dive: This case transcends a simple trade secret dispute, reflecting the escalating hardware competition between AI giants and consumer electronics incumbents. OpenAI acquired Jony Ive's hardware startup io Products for approximately $6.5 billion last year and is developing smart speakers, smart glasses, and potentially AI smartphones. As AI moves from software into hardware, patent walls and trade secret moats are becoming the new battlegrounds for Big Tech.

3. Robinhood Officially Launches AI Agent Trading: Let AI Trade Your Stocks Autonomously

Robinhood has officially launched Agentic Trading, allowing users to connect external AI agents — including Claude, ChatGPT, Codex, Cursor, and Grok — to dedicated brokerage accounts and authorize them to place stock trades autonomously without per-transaction manual approval.

First launched in beta on May 27, 2026, the feature graduated to full production after nearly two months of testing. Robinhood CEO Vlad Tenev says AI agent trading capabilities will "soon match the abilities of human traders," aiming to give retail investors the computational speed and sophistication that institutional and high-frequency trading firms have enjoyed for decades.

How It Works: MCP-Based Architecture

Built on Anthropic's open-standard Model Context Protocol (MCP), Robinhood's Agentic Trading enables AI agents to:

  • Portfolio analysis: Analyze holdings, assess concentration risk, identify rebalancing opportunities
  • Strategy construction: Build thematic portfolios around AI, semiconductors, and other sectors
  • Order execution: Place trades directly through Robinhood's Trading MCP server
  • Continuous monitoring: Track positions in real-time and auto-adjust based on preset strategies

Robinhood has implemented several safety mechanisms: the Agentic account is segregated from the main portfolio, users receive real-time notifications for every trade, and a disconnect button is always available. However, the company explicitly states it is not responsible for losses resulting from AI-generated decisions.

Industry Race: The AI Brokerage Arms Race

Robinhood is not alone. Coinbase launched Coinbase for Agents on June 11 and Coinbase Advisor on June 16 (registered with the SEC as an investment adviser). eToro announced Agent Portfolios in March and expanded AI-powered sub-accounts in July. Interactive Brokers integrated ChatGPT and Grok for research and order workflows in June.

Charles Schwab and Fidelity remain more cautious, offering AI-generated market summaries and research tools without autonomous trading capabilities.

Industry Perspective: As Startup Fortune notes, "Automation did change Wall Street, but it didn't remove risk. It made risk faster and cheaper — and easier to scale. Put that in a retail brokerage app and the obvious question is no longer whether an AI agent can trade. It can. The question is whether most customers understand what they have authorized before the first bad order lands."

Today's Tech Landscape: Three Converging Trends

July 22's three major tech stories, seemingly unrelated, collectively point to the defining trends of 2026's technology landscape:

First, hardware innovation enters a "form factor revolution." Samsung's foldable lineup has expanded from a single product into three distinct form factors — standard, Ultra, and clamshell — marking the transition of foldables from exploratory innovation to scaled market segmentation. With Apple's foldable iPhone on the horizon, the entire smartphone form factor landscape is being rewritten.

Second, AI moves from software into hardware, making intellectual property the new battlefield. The Apple-OpenAI lawsuit represents a consumer electronics giant's counterattack against an AI company's "cross-border invasion." As AI companies transition from running software on existing hardware to building their own devices, traditional patent, trade secret, and supply chain systems face unprecedented challenges.

Third, AI agents evolve from "assistants" to "principals." Robinhood's Agentic Trading marks a paradigm shift: AI agents are moving from providing recommendations to exercising autonomous decision-making authority. When AI can independently make and execute financial decisions, the frameworks of trust, liability, and regulation between humans and AI will undergo a fundamental restructuring.
