⚡ July 19 Financial Weekend Wrap: SOX Confirms Bear Market, Gold Defends $4,000, Bitcoin's Weekend 'Shadow Market' Role, SpaceX Falls Below IPO

📉 Philadelphia Semiconductor Index Enters Bear Market as Global Chip Stocks Lose $3.3 Trillion

The defining event of this week's financial markets was the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOX) confirming a technical bear market, plunging more than 20% from its June record high. Since peaking in late June, the index has erased months of gains, marking the most severe challenge to the two-year chip supercycle.

The catalyst was Moonshot AI's release of Kimi K3—a 2.8 trillion parameter open-weight model that rivals leading US closed-source models at a fraction of the cost. This breakthrough triggered a wholesale Wall Street reassessment of AI hardware valuations. When the technological moat appears to shrink, the premium placed on infrastructure suppliers begins to compress rapidly.

TSMC's record Q2 results—$40.2 billion in revenue and a 77% profit surge to $22 billion—failed to calm markets, instead triggering a "sell the news" reaction. South Korea's KOSPI saw heavy foreign outflows, with SK Hynix recording its worst single-day drop in history. Japan's Nikkei 225 fell more than 10% from its June peak, entering correction territory, while Taiwan's weighted index plunged over 6% in its steepest decline since the April 2025 tariff shock.

Goldman Sachs strategists noted that the selloff represents more than simple profit-taking. It reflects a systemic rotation from concentrated AI momentum trades into value and defensive assets, with individual stock correlations falling to generational lows while the equal-weight S&P 500 hits new highs—signaling the market is broadening beneath the surface.

🥇 Gold's $4,000 Fight: Wall Street Turns Most Bearish in Weeks

Spot gold spent the week battling in a narrow $4,000-$4,100/oz range, closing Friday at $4,019.30—barely holding the key psychological and technical support level. Opening the week at $4,108, gold recorded its sharpest weekly decline in six weeks.

Frustrating gold bulls, the traditional "geopolitical避险 logic" failed completely this week. The US-Iran conflict escalating into its eighth night, near-paralysis of Strait of Hormuz shipping, and Brent crude surging nearly 16% weekly to $88—all catalysts that should have boosted gold—instead suppressed it through the "oil spike → inflation → rate hike expectations" transmission chain. Fed Chair Kevin Warsh, in his first congressional testimony, declared "zero tolerance" for persistent inflation and listed AI as one of three key inflation risks, reinforcing expectations that rates will remain elevated.

The Kitco News weekly gold survey shows Wall Street at its most bearish in weeks: only 1 of 14 experts (7%) predicts higher gold prices next week, while 11 (79%) expect further declines. Citigroup strategists highlight a "triple pressure" on gold: elevated real rates, resurgent dollar safe-haven demand, and oil-driven inflation expectations keeping the Fed hawkish.

₿ Bitcoin's Weekend 'Shadow Market' Role Takes Center Stage

Over the weekend, Bitcoin assumed an unprecedented role: the world's only continuously trading macro risk asset during the traditional market closure. As of Sunday afternoon, BTC was oscillating in the $64,000-$65,000 range, up about 0.3% in 24 hours. Polymarket data shows the probability of BTC staying above $52,000 by July 20 at 99.95%, but the odds of breaking $66,000 have collapsed to just 11.5%.

Analysts note that Bitcoin's weekend price action is becoming the most important "price discovery" window before traditional markets open on Monday. With oil futures, Treasuries, and US equities closed over the weekend while Strait of Hormuz conflict risks continue to simmer, Bitcoin—as the only liquid 24/7 global risk asset—is forced to absorb geopolitical shocks in isolation. On Friday, BTC briefly touched $62,900, down 38% from its October 2025 all-time high.

Woofun AI data reveals significantly reduced weekend order book depth, with the number of active market makers sharply declining, widening spreads. Any new tanker attack or military strike news could impact Bitcoin within hours, with traders unable to hedge through other markets. The transmission chain—rising energy costs pushing up inflation expectations, which in turn strengthen Fed rate hike expectations—ultimately pressures Bitcoin as the terminal risk asset.

"Bitcoin isn't directly linked to oil, but sits at the end of a risk asset chain starting with energy prices and transmitted through monetary policy," market observers note. If BTC falls sharply on Sunday while Brent crude rises, it validates Bitcoin's role as an "early warning system." If BTC rebounds while oil stays flat, weekend volatility was merely a liquidity anomaly.

🚀 SpaceX Crashes Below IPO Price: Record-Breaking Debut Unravels in One Month

SpaceX shares closed Friday at $123.99, falling below their $135 IPO price and reducing the company's market capitalization to approximately $1.63 trillion. Since peaking at around $211 on its third trading day (June 16), Elon Musk's rocket and AI giant has lost over $1 trillion in market value.

The direct trigger was Thursday's Starship Flight 13 abort at T-0 due to engine ignition failure, delaying the planned deployment of the first batch of Starlink V3 satellites. Raymond James maintained its Wall Street-high $800 target, arguing such anomalies are inherent to Starship's aggressive development cycle. But Clear Street warned that any substantial schedule slippage directly impacts Starlink's scale expansion and the "orbital computing" business plan.

The deeper issue is valuation. SpaceX reported under $19 billion in revenue against a $4.9 billion net loss in 2025, yet went public at a $2 trillion valuation—a price-to-sales ratio exceeding 105x. Morgan Stanley previously issued a $300 target, calling SpaceX "AI's final frontier," but RBC noted that "investors may be forced to accept a non-linear de-risking pace." With insider lockup periods expiring over the coming months, additional selling pressure looms.

SpaceX's decline casts a shadow over the AI IPO boom. As the signature deal of the largest US IPO in history, continued weakness could severely dampen prospects for future high-valuation tech listings.
