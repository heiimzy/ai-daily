⚡ July 22 International Politics Roundup: Rubio at ASEAN Says Iran Signaling for Talks But 'Not Serious', Jordan Intercepts 6 Iranian Missiles, South China Sea Clash Intensifies, Zelensky Fires Top General

Multiple major international political developments unfolded on July 22, 2026: U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio arrived in Manila for ASEAN foreign ministers' meetings, delivering a mixed message on Iran—signaling openness to diplomacy while calling Tehran the region's "main troublemaker"; Jordan intercepted 6 Iranian missiles with explosions heard in the port city of Aqaba; Iran and Pakistan reached a counterterrorism and border management agreement; a fresh clash erupted between Philippine and Chinese personnel in the disputed South China Sea; Ukrainian President Zelensky fired his commander-in-chief after days of mass protests; and a Russian warship conducted a live-fire exercise just 40 nautical miles off the UK coast. Here are the full details.

1. Rubio at ASEAN: Iran Signaling for Talks but 'Not Serious'

U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio arrived in Manila on July 22 for the ASEAN Foreign Ministers' Meeting, where Iran and Middle East tensions dominated the agenda alongside regional maritime disputes.

Speaking to reporters at 09:45 GMT, Rubio said the United States "remains open to diplomacy" with Iran but stressed that Tehran cannot be allowed to control the Strait of Hormuz. "Iran is targeting global shipping and an international waterway and demanding to control which ships can go and which ones cannot. That just is unacceptable. We're not going to accept that," Rubio stated.

The top U.S. diplomat revealed that Washington is receiving signals through multiple channels about Tehran's willingness to negotiate, citing a "growing split within the regime." However, he cautioned that "if they're serious, we're serious. If they're not, then we will do what's necessary to protect our interests and those of our allies."

Rubio labeled Iran the "main troublemaker" of the Middle East, pointing to its support for the Houthis, Hezbollah, Iraqi militias, and Hamas. He confirmed the U.S. is in contact with Saudi Arabia over Houthi threats to shipping in the Red Sea's Bab al-Mandeb Strait.

ASEAN foreign ministers issued a joint statement expressing "deep concern" that the renewed U.S.-Iran hostilities "severely undermine the ongoing efforts of key mediators and diminish the prospects for a peaceful resolution." Southeast Asia, being a major oil-importing region, remains highly exposed to energy price volatility stemming from the conflict.

2. Jordan Intercepts 6 Iranian Missiles; Explosions Heard in Aqaba

Jordan's military announced on Wednesday that it intercepted 4 of 6 Iranian missiles fired toward the kingdom, with the remaining 2 falling in remote uninhabited areas causing no casualties or damage. Air defense sirens sounded in the southern port city of Aqaba, where residents reported hearing explosions.

According to an official military source cited by the state-run Petra News Agency, air defense systems detected six missiles launched from Iranian territory and engaged them immediately upon entering Jordanian airspace. The Aqaba airport and port continued operating normally following the incident.

The attack is part of Iran's ongoing retaliatory campaign against U.S. allies in the region that host American military assets. Separately, Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) announced new drone strikes targeting the U.S. military's Camp Doha base in Kuwait, claiming hits on ammunition depots and logistics equipment. Bahrain and Kuwait have both been repeatedly targeted by Iranian ballistic missiles and drones in recent days.

3. Iran and Pakistan Reach Counterterrorism and Border Deal

Iranian Interior Minister Eskandar Momeni and Pakistani Interior Minister Mohsin Naqvi signed a cooperation agreement in Islamabad on July 22, pledging enhanced collaboration in counterterrorism, anti-narcotics operations, human smuggling prevention, and border management, Al Jazeera reported at 09:20 GMT.

Momeni's visit to Pakistan is primarily aimed at discussing a de-escalation framework developed by Pakistan and Qatar to revive the collapsed ceasefire between the U.S. and Iran. He held meetings with Pakistan's Army Chief Field Marshal Asim Munir and Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif.

Sharif expressed "deep concern" over the recent escalation of U.S.-Iran hostilities, urging all sides to exercise restraint. Pakistan played a key role in brokering the June memorandum of understanding between Washington and Tehran, which collapsed last week when both sides resumed military operations over the Strait of Hormuz.

4. South China Sea: New Philippines-China Clash Casts Shadow Over ASEAN Talks

A fresh confrontation erupted on July 20 between Philippine Navy personnel and China Coast Guard (CCG) at Second Thomas Shoal (Ayungin in the Philippines, Ren'ai Jiao in China) in the disputed South China Sea, leaving a Filipino sailor injured. The incident has dominated discussions at the ongoing ASEAN foreign ministers' meeting in Manila.

The Armed Forces of the Philippines (AFP) reported that a CCG rigid-hull inflatable boat carrying eight personnel approached the grounded BRP Sierra Madre warship—which serves as Manila's military outpost on the shoal—and began taking photos and video. When two Philippine Navy rubber boats attempted to drive them away, CCG personnel "reacted violently and aggressively," striking a Filipino marine on the head with a wooden baton and causing a 1.5-inch laceration. The Navy's rubber boat was also damaged.

China's Coast Guard disputed the Philippine account, claiming that Philippine personnel first rammed a Chinese patrol vessel and attacked CCG officers with paddles and long poles. China's Foreign Ministry accused Manila of spreading misinformation and summoned the Philippine ambassador.

The U.S. State Department condemned China's "dangerous and aggressive actions" and reaffirmed that the 2016 Permanent Court of Arbitration ruling—which invalidated China's expansive claims—is legally binding. Rubio, in an opinion piece published in Philippine newspapers ahead of the meetings, reiterated U.S. security commitments to the Philippines.

Notably, both Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi and U.S. Secretary of State Rubio are attending the ASEAN meetings, with markets watching closely for any potential bilateral meeting on the sidelines. The long-stalled Code of Conduct negotiations in the South China Sea are a core agenda item.

5. Ukraine Military Reshuffle: Zelensky Fires Commander-in-Chief After Mass Protests

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky fired Armed Forces Commander-in-Chief General Oleksandr Syrskyi late on July 21 and appointed 43-year-old Major General Mykhailo Drapatyi as his replacement, bowing to pressure from nationwide protests that have gripped the country for days.

According to BBC News, the dismissal comes just days after the firing of popular Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov, which had triggered mass demonstrations across Ukraine. Protesters demanded Syrskyi's removal, viewing him as emblematic of the old Soviet-style military command culture resistant to reform.

Drapatyi, who previously led Ukraine's Land Forces from 2024 to 2025, represents a new generation of Ukrainian commanders. Known for his discipline and battlefield effectiveness, he forged his reputation in the early days of Ukraine's fight against Russia's full-scale invasion.

"The operations of the Ukrainian Defense Forces are ongoing and must continue steadily. Ukraine's plan for long-range sanctions and our program of middle-range strikes will be carried out with absolute precision," Zelensky said while introducing Drapatyi.

Fedorov himself congratulated Drapatyi, calling the appointment "a new hope for free people fighting for freedom and justice." In Russia, war bloggers celebrated the command changes as a sign of political infighting that could weaken Ukraine's military capabilities.

Meanwhile, Russian Deputy Prime Minister Alexander Novak said the domestic fuel market has "partially stabilized" after Ukrainian drone strikes on refineries caused supply disruptions, long queues at gas stations, and rationing in several regions.

6. Russian Warship Conducts Live-Fire Exercise off UK Coast; New Defense Secretary Calls It 'Irresponsible'

Britain's new Defence Secretary Wes Streeting described a Russian naval live-fire exercise conducted 40 nautical miles south of Plymouth as "irresponsible" on July 22, as tensions between London and Moscow continue to simmer.

A Russian naval vessel conducted a "brief live-fire weapons exercise" in international waters off the southwest coast of England, a government minister confirmed. The exercise occurred just days after new UK Prime Minister Andy Burnham took office.

"We should see yesterday's events as performative and irresponsible," Streeting told reporters. "It's not the first time that Russia has behaved like this, and frankly, it's the tip of the iceberg of the daily threats that this country faces and our allies face."

The incident comes as Burnham's government approved U.S. use of UK bases—including RAF Fairford and Diego Garcia—for "defensive strikes" against Iran, a decision that has drawn criticism from opposition parties concerned about legal implications.

In a related development, EU diplomats said envoys are set to meet today to negotiate a 21st package of sanctions against Russia, listing approximately 215 individuals and entities including 94 financial institutions. A European intelligence report warns that targeting more than half of Russia's internationally connected banks could trigger an "explosive" banking crisis.
