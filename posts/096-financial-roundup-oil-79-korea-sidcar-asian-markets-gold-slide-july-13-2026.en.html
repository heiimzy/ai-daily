⚡ July 13 Financial Roundup: Brent Surges to $79, Korea Triggers SIDECAR, Gold Breaks Below $4,100

🔥 At a Glance

Asian markets opened sharply lower on Monday as weekend escalations between the US and Iran sent shockwaves through global financial markets. Brent crude surged 4.1% to $79.11, WTI jumped 4.2% to $74.40, nearing the psychologically critical $80 mark. South Korea's KOSPI plunged 5.4%, triggering the SIDECAR circuit breaker for programmatic sell orders. Gold lost its safe-haven luster, falling 1%+ below $4,100 to $4,076 as oil-driven inflation fears strengthened rate hike expectations. The dollar rose to 101.13 while the 2-year Treasury yield hit its highest since early 2025. Markets now brace for a pivotal week featuring June CPI data and Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's first Congressional testimony.

🛢️ Oil Surges 4% Toward $80 as Hormuz Standoff Deepens

US Central Command launched fresh strikes on Iranian military targets at 5:00 PM ET Sunday, the third wave of large-scale operations this week, bringing the total to over 300 targets struck. Iran's Revolutionary Guard Corps responded by declaring the Strait of Hormuz closed indefinitely and launching missile and drone attacks on Qatar, Kuwait, Oman, Bahrain, and Jordan.

The Strait of Hormuz, through which roughly one-fifth of the world's seaborne oil passes, has become the epicenter of global supply risk. Brent crude for September delivery surged 4.1% to $79.11 per barrel as of 05:00 GMT Monday, the highest since June 22, while WTI rose 4.2% to $74.40. According to maritime intelligence platform Windward, just six vessels traversed the strait between 18:00 GMT Thursday and 06:00 GMT Friday, down sharply from the pre-war daily average of 130.

"The per-barrel price could remain in the upper $70s during August and September amid the heightened geopolitical uncertainty," said Mukesh Sahdev, founder and chief oil analyst at XAnalysts in Sydney. "There could be occasional spikes and dips outside that range."

Monday's rally partially reverses the declines seen after OPEC+ agreed earlier this month to raise production beginning in August. Oil prices are now about 9% higher than before the US and Israel launched their initial strikes on Iran in late February.

🇰🇷 South Korea Stock Market Crashes 5.4%, SIDECAR Triggered

South Korea bore the brunt of Monday's Asian selloff. The KOSPI index opened 0.9% lower and accelerated losses to a 5.4% intraday decline, with SK Hynix plummeting 8.2% at one point. Korea Exchange triggered the SIDECAR mechanism, temporarily halting programmatic sell orders on the KOSPI - the first such activation this year.

SK Hynix's sharp reversal reflects dual pressures: profit-taking after its ADR surged 13% in its Nasdaq debut on Friday, and Korea Investment & Securities' forecast that Q2 operating profit could miss market estimates by 8% due to its heavy HBM revenue exposure limiting ASP growth. The Korean market had already shed nearly 8% last week as leveraged semiconductor bets came under pressure.

Japan's Nikkei fell 1.6%, while MSCI's broadest index of Asia-Pacific shares outside Japan dropped 0.9%. S&P 500 futures eased 0.4%, Nasdaq futures lost 0.9%, and EUROSTOXX 50 and DAX futures both fell 0.6%.

🥇 Gold Defies Geopolitics, Slips Below $4,100

In a counterintuitive market reaction, gold fell despite escalating geopolitical tensions. Spot gold dropped 1.1% to $4,076 per ounce in early Asian trade, while COMEX gold fell 0.74% to $4,083. Silver came under sharper pressure, with COMEX silver sliding 1.86% to $59.045.

The core mechanism driving gold's decline: soaring oil prices fuel inflation expectations → markets price in tighter Fed policy → the dollar and Treasury yields strengthen → the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold rises. The Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index rose 0.2% to 101.13. The 2-year Treasury yield climbed 3 basis points to 4.2393%, its highest since February 2025, while the 10-year yield reached 4.59%.

Since the Iran war erupted in late February, gold has lost more than one-fifth of its value, ending a three-year bull run. A massive profit-taking wave had already pushed prices below $4,000 in late June for the first time since November 2025.

TD Securities' global head of commodity strategy Bart Melek noted: "The renewed US-Iran tensions have made investors generally unwilling to hold gold and silver. Higher energy prices are amplifying inflation concerns, reinforcing expectations of central bank rate hikes."

🏛️ The Week Ahead: CPI + Warsh Testimony = Double Catalyst

Financial markets face a triple test this week, with the two biggest catalysts arriving back-to-back on Tuesday:

1. June CPI (Tuesday 8:30 AM ET)

The Bureau of Labor Statistics releases June CPI figures. Goldman Sachs estimates core CPI at approximately 0.17% month-over-month (below consensus) and headline CPI at roughly -0.11%, with declining energy prices doing most of the heavy lifting. However, weekend oil price surges mean any smaller-than-expected energy decline could produce an upside surprise. Options markets are pricing an implied S&P 500 move of approximately 0.8% on CPI day itself, the highest CPI-day implied volatility in 12 months. The Fed's semi-annual report showed PCE inflation surging from 2.5% to 4.1%.

2. Fed Chair Warsh's Humphrey-Hawkins Testimony (Tue-Wed)

Kevin Warsh will deliver his semi-annual Monetary Policy Report testimony before the House Financial Services Committee on Tuesday at 10:00 AM ET, followed by the Senate Banking Committee on Wednesday. This marks his first appearance before Congress as Fed Chair since his April confirmation. At his June FOMC meeting debut, Warsh held rates steady but delivered a hawkish signal: nine committee members penciled in higher rates ahead, with six forecasting two quarter-point increases by year-end. Interest rate swaps now price nearly 40 basis points of cumulative tightening by December, up sharply from ~15 basis points in early June. The CME FedWatch tool puts September rate hike odds at approximately 69%.

Warsh has been reshaping Fed communications since taking office. At the ECB Forum in Sintra, he made clear that the era of forward guidance is ending, to be replaced by a meeting-by-meeting, data-dependent approach. Markets will be laser-focused on his characterization of inflation, balance sheet reduction plans, and his stance on Fed independence.

3. Q2 Earnings Season Begins

Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan report Tuesday, kicking off Q2 earnings season. Citi analysts maintain their overweight stance on global IT and the US, stating: "Tech continues to screen highly in our models, supported by standout earnings growth/momentum and attractive valuations." Bloomberg data shows S&P 500 2026 EPS forecast at $340, with AI-related investment contributing roughly half of earnings growth.

📊 Other Market Developments

USD/JPY: The dollar rose 0.2% against the yen to 162.03. Japanese Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama floated an idea on Friday to encourage the $1.8 trillion GPIF and other retirement vehicles to bring some of their money home, briefly strengthening the yen before it retreated.

GBP: Sterling eased 0.2% to $1.3379. UK politics enters a pivotal week as Andy Burnham is expected to be formally anointed as Labour leader on Friday and named as Prime Minister on July 20.

Crypto: Bitcoin traded near $64,175 after recovering from earlier losses. JPMorgan turned cautious on digital assets, advising MicroStrategy to rebuild dollar reserves to restore investor confidence.

Japan GPIF: Japan's $1.8 trillion Government Pension Investment Fund is being nudged toward boosting domestic allocations to combat yen weakness and global market volatility. Analysts note allocations tend to move slowly and the FY26 investment plan is already in place.
