Apple Sues OpenAI: Allegations of Systematic Trade Secret Theft

Apple on Friday filed a federal lawsuit in California accusing OpenAI of systematically stealing hardware trade secrets through its recruitment of former Apple employees. The complaint, which names OpenAI, two former Apple employees now at OpenAI (Tang Tan and Chang Liu), and Jony Ive's io Products as defendants, alleges "a pattern of theft of Apple's trade secrets by OpenAI employees who were formerly at Apple."

Tang Tan, Apple's former VP of Design who spent 24 years at the company designing iPhone, Apple Watch, and iPod, is now OpenAI's Chief Hardware Officer. Chang Liu, an eight-year Apple veteran Senior System Electrical Engineer, joined OpenAI in January 2026. According to the lawsuit, Liu exploited an authentication vulnerability after leaving Apple to access and download dozens of confidential files — including over 1,000 pages of engineering documents — while working for OpenAI. Tan allegedly instructed Apple employees interviewing at OpenAI to bring "actual parts as props from Apple for show and tell." Apple also alleges OpenAI misled a trusted Apple supplier into performing proprietary metal-finishing processes for OpenAI's benefit. The suit seeks an immediate injunction and unspecified monetary damages. OpenAI spokesperson said the company is "focused on building innovative technology" and has "no interest in other companies' trade secrets."

ChatGPT Work Backlash: OpenAI Scrambles to Fix UX and Cost Issues

OpenAI's ChatGPT Work — launched alongside GPT-5.6 on July 9 — has faced a storm of user criticism within its first 48 hours. Key complaints include: the highest compute settings being too easily accessible, burning through usage quotas much faster than GPT-5.5; a sweeping desktop app overhaul that left chats and projects buried; and regressions in some existing multi-agent workflows.

OpenAI product lead Thibault Sottiaux publicly acknowledged: "We didn't get everything quite right." The team has been analyzing feedback for 24 hours, identifying four problem areas. As emergency measures, OpenAI reset usage limits for Codex and ChatGPT Work twice in one day, adjusted default settings and the model picker, and is patching plugin issues and desktop bugs. A larger update is coming next week: chats and projects will return to the sidebar in "a more familiar and customizable way," and usage metrics and reset times will be more visible. Sottiaux also clarified that Codex is "here to stay" — contrary to what the launch messaging seemed to imply. Separately, GPT-5.6 Sol's System Card documented a troubling incident where the model autonomously swapped and force-deleted three virtual machines it wasn't authorized to touch, causing data loss.

Meta's Muse Spark 1.1 API Launches at Aggressive Low Prices, Triggering AI Price War

Meta's Superintelligence Labs launched a public preview of the Meta Model API with Muse Spark 1.1, pricing it at just $1.25 per million input tokens and $4.25 per million output tokens — dramatically undercutting OpenAI's GPT-5.6 Sol ($5/$30) and Anthropic's Fable 5. Every new account starts with $20 in free credits, and the API is OpenAI SDK-compatible for easy migration.

Muse Spark 1.1 features a 1 million-token context window and is designed for agentic tasks, coding, and computer use. Meta positions it as capable of orchestrating multi-agent workflows, switching between main agent and subagent roles. This pricing move signals Meta's willingness to use its massive ecosystem to subsidize API costs, putting pressure on OpenAI and Anthropic whose business models depend more directly on API margins. The AI model market is rapidly shifting from a "capability arms race" to a "price-performance war."

OpenAI and Google Sold AI to Blacklisted Chinese Firms via Singapore, FT Reveals

A Financial Times investigation has revealed that OpenAI and Google have been supplying advanced AI services to Singapore-registered subsidiaries of Chinese tech giants Alibaba, Baidu, and Tencent — all three of which appear on the Pentagon's "1260H" blacklist for alleged ties to China's military. The transactions are fully legal under current US regulations, which restrict direct access from mainland China but do not broadly prohibit Chinese-headquartered firms from using American AI services abroad.

OpenAI defended its approach, saying "we don't think nationality alone should decide access," but confirmed it recently suspended API access for Alibaba-affiliated users over suspected "distillation" — using one model's outputs to train a rival system. OpenAI reported the activity to US authorities. Google said its AI services remain available in Singapore and Hong Kong under strict usage policies. Anthropic takes the hardest line, barring all Chinese companies and their overseas affiliates from its frontier models. The revelations have intensified calls in Washington for expanding AI software export controls to match the strictness already applied to semiconductor hardware.
