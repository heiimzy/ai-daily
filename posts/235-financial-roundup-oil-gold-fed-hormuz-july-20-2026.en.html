⚡ July 20 Financial Roundup: Oil Surges Past $85 to 1-Month High, Gold Breaks Below $4,000, Fed Rate Hike Odds Rise to 58%

Oil Markets Surge: WTI Breaks $85, Brent Tops $91 as Hormuz War Premium Returns

Crude oil futures skyrocketed in Asian trading Monday morning (July 20), with WTI briefly topping $85 per barrel and Brent North Sea crude breaking through the psychologically significant $90 mark — both hitting roughly one-month highs. The surge comes as escalating tit-for-tat military exchanges between the United States and Iran rapidly intensify concerns over safe navigation through the Strait of Hormuz, the world's most critical energy chokepoint.

WTI crude settled at $82.49 on Friday, July 17, but was bid up to $85.05 in early electronic trading Monday. Brent jumped to $90.85 per barrel, up $2.75 from the previous weekend's close. The rally represents a complete reversal of the price relief brought by the previously agreed US-Iran memorandum on cessation of hostilities, effectively returning prices to levels seen before that agreement took effect.

Over the weekend, the US launched its eighth consecutive night of airstrikes against Iranian military infrastructure, targeting coastal surveillance facilities, air defense systems, missile and drone storage sites. Iran retaliated with ballistic missiles and drones against two US bases in Kuwait and continued attacks on Jordan. On the prediction market Polymarket, the probability of Strait of Hormuz traffic returning to normal by July 31 has collapsed to just 1.3%, reflecting traders' near-certainty that the crisis will continue.

Major Wall Street analysts warn that if elevated geopolitical risks persist, WTI could test $90 and Brent could challenge $95, with the $100 threshold coming back into view. Brent crude logged an over 16% weekly gain last week — the largest single-week advance in months.

"The prospect of tighter monetary policy into a potential energy shock is rarely supportive for risk assets." — TickMill market strategy partner Patrick Munnelly

Gold Breaks Below $4,000: Safe-Haven Logic Fails as Oil Shock Becomes the Dominant Driver

Counterintuitively, escalating Middle East conflict has weighed on gold rather than boosted it. Spot gold weakened in early Asian trading Monday, briefly breaching the critical $4,000 support level to hit a low of $3,990.67 per ounce by 07:15 Beijing time, down approximately 0.6%. By 07:22 Beijing time, spot gold was trading at $3,991.32.

The bearish logic for gold is clear: US-Iran conflict drives energy prices higher → global inflation expectations rise → the Fed is forced to maintain high rates or even hike → dollar strengthens → gold gets squeezed. While spot gold recovered 1% on Friday to close at $4,016.36, it still recorded a weekly loss of approximately 2.5% — the largest weekly decline since early June. Since the US-Iran war erupted in late February, gold prices have fallen roughly 25%.

EverBank global markets president Chris Gaffney identified dollar strength and rising global inflation concerns as the primary drivers of gold's sell-off, as they push global interest rates higher. The US Dollar Index edged higher to 100.86 on Monday morning, further pressuring bullion by making it more expensive for overseas buyers.

In the latest Kitco News gold survey, Wall Street sentiment has turned decisively bearish. Of 14 Wall Street analysts, only 1 expects gold prices to rise this week — a staggering 79% predict decline, with 14% neutral. Main Street retail investors were slightly more optimistic at 40% bullish, but overall caution dominated.

  • Bearish view: Commerzbank's Thu Lan Nguyen argues that energy price risks from the escalating Middle East conflict will keep rate hike expectations elevated, limiting gold's upside. investingLive's Adam Barton warns the tech stock sell-off could trigger widespread "sell everything" liquidation that gold cannot escape.
  • Bullish counterpoint: Sprott Inc.'s Paul Wong contends gold is deeply oversold, with multiple indicators at two-to-three standard deviation lows. The $4,000 level is being repeatedly tested below the 200-day moving average, while Chinese ETF buying partially offsets Western outflows. Seasonally, gold tends to form bottoms in summer — especially early August.

Fed Policy Fog: September Rate Hike Probability Hits 58%, Market Prices 30bps More Tightening by Year-End

The oil price surge is not just directly impacting inflation expectations — it is reshaping market assumptions about the Federal Reserve's policy trajectory. CME FedWatch data shows traders now price a roughly 58% probability of a rate hike in September. While the July hike probability has fallen to about 15%, markets still expect a cumulative 30 basis points of tightening by year-end.

The US economy's resilience has further supported rate hike expectations. June retail sales rose modestly, consumer confidence hit a five-month high, and the labor market remained stable. While these data have somewhat alleviated recession fears, they give the Fed more room to address inflation. Fed officials have delivered mixed signals recently — some expressing inflation concerns while others acknowledging labor market strength.

US Treasuries reflected the same complex sentiment. Long-term yields edged lower last week, with the 10-year yield falling to 4.541% and the 30-year yield to 5.064%, both recording weekly declines. TD Securities' Gennadiy Goldberg noted that single-month data can't fully alleviate policymakers' concerns, especially against a backdrop of renewed geopolitical risk.

Israel Signals Readiness to Join Fight, Regional Escalation Risks Mount

A series of developments over the weekend suggest the Middle East crisis is far from peaking. Israel has signaled it is ready to resume military operations against Iran at any time. The US Energy Secretary confirmed that US military operations against Iran will continue, and the State Department issued a global security advisory. Jordan and Qatar are pushing for diplomatic resolution, calling for an immediate ceasefire and a return to negotiations — but neither side shows signs of backing down.

Kuwait suffered a second consecutive day of Iranian missile strikes, with electricity generation and water desalination facilities hit. The Kuwaiti Defense Ministry confirmed multiple hostile ballistic missiles and drones were detected in its airspace since dawn Monday. The conflict is rapidly evolving from a bilateral US-Iran confrontation into a broader regional crisis affecting the entire Gulf.

Outlook: Oil's Next Move and Gold's Summer Bottom

Looking ahead, crude oil markets will focus on two key variables: whether the US and Iran can reconstruct a ceasefire framework, and whether freedom of navigation through the Strait of Hormuz can be restored. If elevated geopolitical risks persist, WTI could test $90 and Brent could challenge $95, with the $100 threshold coming back into view. For Asian energy-importing nations, weak local currencies combined with surging oil prices create a "double whammy" directly impacting corporate earnings and household budgets.

For gold, the $4,000 level has become both a psychological and technical battleground. Near-term volatility is likely to continue, but from a medium-to-long-term perspective, gold's intrinsic value as an inflation and uncertainty hedge remains intact. Historical patterns suggest that in oversold conditions, any positive catalyst — whether a policy pivot or geopolitical development — can ignite a sharp rebound. Gold's summer low may be quietly taking shape.
