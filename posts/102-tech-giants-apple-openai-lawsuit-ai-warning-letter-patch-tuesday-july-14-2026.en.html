⚡ July 14 Tech Roundup: Apple Sues OpenAI in 41-Page Trade Secret Bombshell, 200+ Experts Warn of AI Job Loss, Microsoft Patches 127 Flaws Including Wormable 0-Day

🍎 Apple v. OpenAI: The 41-Page Complaint That Shook Silicon Valley

Apple filed a federal lawsuit against OpenAI on July 10 in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California, submitting a 41-page complaint accusing the AI company of systematic trade secret theft. The lawsuit names OpenAI's Chief Hardware Officer Tang Tan, former Apple engineer Chang Liu, and io Products — the hardware company founded by Jony Ive that OpenAI acquired for $6.5 billion.

The complaint reads like a Silicon Valley spy thriller. Liu, an 8-year Apple veteran who joined OpenAI in January, allegedly failed to return his company-issued laptop and discovered an authentication zero-day vulnerability that still allowed him to access Apple's internal network after leaving. Over several weeks, he downloaded dozens of confidential files, including a technical document compilation exceeding 1,000 pages covering multi-layer motherboard manufacturing processes and testing procedures.

According to the filing, over 400 former Apple employees now work at OpenAI. Liu allegedly helped another Apple employee heading to OpenAI copy files and avoid security checks, moving their communications to LINE to evade monitoring.

Apple alleges OpenAI asked candidates to bring physical Apple devices to interviews for on-the-spot "reverse engineering" demonstrations, and used Apple's supply chain relationships to have partners apply Apple's proprietary metal finishing processes for OpenAI's benefit. Apple is seeking an extraordinary remedy: a court order preventing OpenAI from using any of the stolen secrets and requiring a redesign of upcoming products.

CNBC's Jim Cramer called the lawsuit "heavyweight — not the kind of thing Apple typically does." The case is widely seen as a "legal狙击" targeting OpenAI's trillion-dollar IPO narrative. If the court finds OpenAI's hardware was built on stolen trade secrets, the company's entire hardware roadmap may need to be restructured.

Meanwhile, Apple has decisively pivoted to Google's AI ecosystem. The Siri AI unveiled at WWDC 2026 has abandoned ChatGPT in favor of Google's 1.2 trillion-parameter Gemini MoE model, paying Google an estimated $1 billion annually in licensing fees.

📋 Eric Schmidt, Reid Hoffman, and 200+ Experts Sign AI Warning Letter

The Stanford Digital Economy Lab's "We Must Act Now" open letter was published with signatures from over 200 of the world's most influential economists, technology executives, and researchers. The letter warns that AI could become "radically more powerful" over the next decade, leading to "large-scale job displacement."

Signatories include former Google CEO Eric Schmidt, LinkedIn co-founder Reid Hoffman, Nobel laureates Joseph Stiglitz, Daron Acemoglu, and Simon Johnson, AI godfathers Yoshua Bengio and Yann LeCun, Google AI lead Jeff Dean, Anthropic co-founder Jack Clark, and OpenAI finance chief Sarah Friar — a rare document bringing together both AI builders and economists studying the technology's consequences.

The concise 88-word letter states: "AI may become radically more powerful over the next 10 years. This could drive an unprecedented transformation of our economy, larger than the Industrial Revolution, but unfolding over a vastly shorter time frame. It could bring risks, including large-scale job displacement, as well as opportunities such as major gains in living standards. Economists, policymakers and technology leaders must act now to understand the economics of transformative AI and to build the incentives, guardrails, and institutions needed to steer AI in a direction that complements humans and benefits society."

Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei had previously predicted AI could eliminate up to half of all entry-level white-collar jobs within five years. This letter signals the AI industry is taking the employment threat seriously.

🛡️ Microsoft July Patch Tuesday: 127 CVEs Including CVSS 9.8 Wormable Flaw

Microsoft released its July 14 security update, patching 127 CVEs. The most critical is CVE-2025-47981, a heap-based buffer overflow in the Windows SPNEGO Extended Negotiation (NEGOEX) security mechanism. With a CVSS score of 9.8, the vulnerability is network-addressable, requires no credentials or user interaction, and is fully wormable. It affects every supported Windows version from 10 1607 and Windows Server 2008R2 onward.

Microsoft assesses exploitation as "more likely within 30 days." Security firm ZeroPath has published a technical breakdown. There is no workaround — all users are urged to apply the July cumulative update immediately.

The update also permanently removes the Kerberos RC4 rollback control (RC4DefaultDisablementPhase). Since January 2026, Microsoft has been progressively disabling RC4 support; after this update, the safety net is gone. Any service accounts still using RC4 for Kerberos authentication may fail after the update, requiring advance audit and password changes.

Adding to the urgency, a security researcher known as Nightmare-Eclipse — who has been in an escalating public feud with Microsoft since April 2026 — had previously threatened to release additional zero-days on July 14. While the threat has been partially withdrawn, security teams remain on high alert.

☁️ Google and SpaceX Sign Nearly $30 Billion Cloud Compute Deal

According to regulatory filings, Google has reached a major compute partnership with SpaceX. Google will pay $920 million per month to lease approximately 110,000 Nvidia GPUs, with the agreement running from October 2025 through June 2029, totaling nearly $30 billion.

Google Cloud will use SpaceX data center resources — including CPU, memory, and GPU capacity — to meet the growing computational demands of its Gemini Enterprise AI platform. If SpaceX fails to deliver the contracted GPU count by September 2026, Google has the right to terminate the contract or reduce fees proportionally.

The deal comes as SpaceX's AI division continues to struggle financially, posting a $2.5 billion operating loss this quarter on just $818 million in revenue. SpaceX is working to commercialize its data center infrastructure originally built to support Grok. Earlier, Anthropic also announced it would use the full capacity of SpaceX's Colossus 1 data center in Memphis.

🏢 Anthropic Files Confidential IPO Paperwork, Targets Trillion-Dollar Valuation

Anthropic announced it has confidentially submitted a Form S-1 registration statement to the SEC, initiating the IPO process. The company recently completed a $65 billion Series H funding round at a $965 billion post-money valuation, surpassing OpenAI's latest $730 billion valuation.

Anthropic has experienced explosive growth over the past year, with annualized revenue surging to $47 billion. Powered by Claude Code and the Claude model family, it has overtaken OpenAI in enterprise market share (41% vs 39.5%).

The IPO would make Anthropic the third major AI company — alongside OpenAI and SpaceX — to enter public markets, forming one of the largest technology IPO waves in history. Analysts note that the Apple v. OpenAI lawsuit could become a critical variable in how OpenAI's own IPO is priced.
