🇪🇺 EU Issues Landmark DMA Rulings: Google Must Open Android to AI Competitors

The European Commission issued two landmark decisions under the Digital Markets Act (DMA) on July 16, ordering Google to open its Android operating system to rival AI assistants and share search data with competitors. The rulings represent the EU's most aggressive regulatory action in the AI space since the DMA took effect.

The first decision requires Google to grant third-party AI assistants system-level Android access equivalent to what Google Gemini enjoys, by July 2027. This means Android users across Europe will be able to choose their preferred default AI assistant — for instance, using ChatGPT or Claude instead of the "Hey Google" voice activation feature. The second decision mandates that Google share anonymized search data with rival search engines starting January 2027, aiming to "rebalance the playing field."

"Thanks to these measures we hope to see emerging alternatives to Google Search and Google's AI services, such as Gemini, and that users in the EU can enjoy greater choice of services," said EU tech chief Henna Virkkunen. This is one of the most targeted enforcement actions under the DMA since its inception in 2023.

Google's head of global affairs, Kent Walker, strongly objected, warning that the measures "would introduce unprecedented risks to user privacy, device security, and national security." He argued that sharing search data would expose "Europeans' private searches to unfamiliar companies, without adequate anonymisation of the data and without user knowledge or consent." A senior European official countered that the EU "took integrity, security and privacy into utmost account," with Brussels confirming anonymization protocols for shared search data.

Separately, the Financial Times reported that the EU could hit Google with a fine next week in a separate DMA investigation, potentially reaching 10% of the company's global annual turnover. Google already faced €8.2 billion in cumulative EU fines between 2017 and 2019 under different competition rules. The company could also face daily penalties of up to 5% of average daily turnover for non-compliance.

The rulings follow the EU's first interim antitrust measure in 17 years — the June 9 order forcing Meta to restore ChatGPT access on WhatsApp. Together, these actions signal that the EU is systematically using the DMA to dismantle Big Tech's walled gardens around AI services.

📱 OnePlus Officially Exits US and European Markets: End of a Decade-Long Journey

Chinese smartphone brand OnePlus officially confirmed on July 16 that it is exiting North America and Europe, ending months of speculation. The company, which launched in 2014 with the disruptive "Never Settle" ethos, is ending its global expansion after 12 years.

In community posts directed at North American and European users, OnePlus stated: "As part of the proactive global strategy adjustment, OnePlus has decided to conclude new product rollouts in Europe and North America." This means the OnePlus 15, launched in late 2025, will be the last OnePlus phone available in these regions. The company also announced that its North American community website (communityus.oneplus.com) will shut down entirely on August 16, 2026.

Existing device owners will continue to receive software updates, security patches, and after-sales support as promised. However, a major change looms: OnePlus's iconic OxygenOS will be replaced by parent company Oppo's ColorOS 17. Eligible OnePlus devices will have the option to "voluntarily update" to ColorOS 17 after its release, with a rollback path to OxygenOS allegedly preserved. Analysts note that this effectively marks the end of OxygenOS as a distinct product.

OnePlus's struggles stem from multiple factors. Supply chain pressures have driven up memory and storage costs — RAM alone now accounts for over 25% of a flagship phone's bill of materials, driven by AI demand for chips. In the US market, lack of carrier partnerships and retail visibility has long constrained OnePlus's growth. In Europe, market fragmentation and fierce competition made it difficult to gain traction.

Notably, Bloomberg previously reported that Oppo's restructuring may not stop here — OnePlus's India operations could also wind down in 2027, leaving China as the brand's sole market. Meanwhile, Realme, another Oppo sub-brand, will exit the Chinese market to focus on overseas markets.

"OnePlus's exit from US and European markets further reduces consumer choice," Ars Technica noted. "With Chinese brands retreating, Samsung and Apple will continue to dominate, leaving other brands as little more than a rounding error — giving the giants plenty of room to raise prices."

🤖 Moonshot AI Quietly Launches Kimi K3: 2.8T Parameters, 1M Context Window

No press conference, no press release, no website update — Moonshot AI quietly launched its most powerful flagship model to date, Kimi K3, on the evening of July 16, dropping a bombshell on the AI community.

According to official API documentation, Kimi K3 is a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model with a staggering 2.8 trillion total parameters — nearly triple the size of its predecessor K2 (1T parameters). The model natively supports a 1,048,576-token (approximately 1 million token) context window, built on Moonshot's proprietary Kimi Delta Attention (hybrid linear attention) architecture with Attention Residuals. It accepts text, image, and video inputs.

On pricing, Kimi K3 charges $0.30 per million input tokens (cache hit), $3.00 (cache miss), and $15.00 per million output tokens. While significantly more expensive than the K2.6 series, it remains competitive with frontier models — slightly undercutting OpenAI GPT-4o ($5/M output on standard tier) and matching Claude Opus 4.8's $15/M output pricing. Moonshot has also eliminated context-length tiering, charging a flat rate regardless of prompt length.

Notable details: reasoning is always enabled but currently only supports "max" mode; default maximum output is 131,072 tokens, configurable up to 1 million; vision capabilities accept base64-encoded input but not public URLs. The model ID is kimi-k3, accessible via an OpenAI-compatible API.

The launch was remarkably low-key — Moonshot's homepage still features K2.6 as its headline model. There is no K3 blog post, no Hugging Face model card, no GitHub repository, and no published benchmark data. The only capability claim in the API docs is "industry-leading intelligence." The Financial Times previously reported that K3 targets parity with Claude Opus 4.8. Early user reports suggest impressive performance on coding and complex reasoning tasks.

K3's launch underscores Moonshot's rapidly rising valuation — the company was valued at $20 billion just two months ago, and the Financial Times reports it is now raising at a $31.5 billion valuation. Whether K3 will follow Moonshot's pattern of releasing open weights remains unconfirmed, though every prior flagship (K2, K2.6, K2.7 Code) was open-source.

⌨️ OpenAI Ships First Hardware Product: Codex Micro Programming Macropad

OpenAI released its first branded hardware product on July 16 — the Codex Micro, a limited-edition programmable macropad co-developed with Montreal-based keyboard maker Work Louder, priced at $230. The move marks OpenAI's first step from pure software toward a hardware-integrated ecosystem.

The Codex Micro's core innovation is its six "Agent Keys" on the top row, which display real-time status of Codex AI coding agents through RGB LED colors — white for idle, blue for thinking, green for completed, amber for awaiting human input, and red for error. Developers can monitor up to six parallel Codex agent threads at a glance without switching windows.

Unlike generic macro keypads like the Stream Deck, the Codex Micro's Agent Keys are not simple shortcut mappings. They communicate through OpenAI Codex's bidirectional JSON-RPC App Server architecture, using the ChatGPT desktop app as a bridge to continuously read Codex thread states and translate them into LED signals. A single tap selects the associated agent; a double-tap brings it to the foreground.

The device has clear limitations: Agent Key RGB feedback only works when the ChatGPT desktop app is running — developers using Codex exclusively through CLI, VS Code extensions, browser, or JetBrains plugins will see no LED status at all. Additionally, the six-key ceiling covers at most six concurrent threads; agents beyond that won't appear on the hardware dashboard.

The launch comes amid rapid growth for the Codex platform — from under 1 million weekly active users when the desktop app launched in February 2026 to over 5 million today. Gartner named OpenAI a Leader in its Enterprise AI Coding Agents Magic Quadrant alongside Anthropic's Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, and Cursor.

Orders opened July 16, with the first wave shipping July 24. For developers managing multiple parallel AI coding agents, this is a genuinely productivity-enhancing tool. For occasional Codex users, the $230 premium over a standard macropad is harder to justify.
