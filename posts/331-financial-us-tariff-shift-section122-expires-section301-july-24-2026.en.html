⚡ July 24 Financial Roundup: Section 122 Tariff Expires at Midnight Reshaping Global Trade, Section 301 Forced-Labor Tariffs on 60 Countries Loom, Japan PMI Expands but Yen Tumbles to 40-Year Low

1. US Tariff Regime Change: Section 122 Expires, Section 301 Takes Over

At 12:01 AM ET on July 24, 2026, the Trump administration's Section 122 global import surcharge — a 10% across-the-board tariff imposed as an emergency bridge after the Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariff authority in February — expires automatically by operation of law. The statute caps Section 122 tariffs at 150 days, the president cannot unilaterally extend them, and Congress has shown no appetite for legislation to continue them. The result: the average US effective tariff rate falls from approximately 13% to approximately 7.2% overnight, according to Capital Economics estimates.

But any celebration from importers may be short-lived. US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer has signaled that replacement tariffs under Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974 are imminent. Following investigations launched in March into forced-labor enforcement across 60 economies, the USTR has proposed a two-tier tariff structure: 10% on 15 economies that have taken partial compliance steps (including the EU, UK, Canada, Mexico, Taiwan, Indonesia), and 12.5% on the remaining 45 economies (including China, Japan, South Korea, India, Vietnam). Unlike Section 122, Section 301 carries no statutory rate cap and no expiration date — making it a potentially permanent trade barrier.

Greer told CNBC's Squawk Box on Tuesday: "We expect to see some action soon." The USTR missed its self-imposed July 20 internal deadline for finalizing the Section 301 determination, creating a window of uncertainty. If Section 301 is not finalized before Section 122 expires, there could be a brief gap where goods enter at MFN rates — a temporary reprieve that trade lawyers warn businesses not to assume will be meaningful. Once the Section 301 forced-labor duties take effect, the 45 economies in the higher tier will face 12.5% duties with no sunset clause.

"This is not a tariff reduction — it is a tariff reallocation. The administration is replacing a blunt, legally fragile, universally applied instrument with a durable, targeted one." — Model Diplomat trade analysis

Adding to the complexity, the US imposed 25% Section 301 tariffs on Brazilian imports on July 22, and a separate Section 301 investigation into excess manufacturing capacity covering 16 economies (including China and the EU) is expected to yield additional tariffs later in 2026, likely after the November midterm elections.

2. Japan PMI Expands for 6th Month, But Yen Crashes to 40-Year Low

Friday's Asian session brought encouraging manufacturing data from Japan. The July flash Manufacturing PMI came in at 54.9, up from June's 54.5 and above the 54.3 consensus estimate — marking the sixth consecutive month of expansion. Output growth was the second-fastest since January 2022, driven partly by pre-emptive stockpiling amid ongoing Middle East supply disruptions. The Services PMI also rebounded to 51.8 from 50.0, lifting the Composite PMI Output Index to 52.5 — the strongest reading since the outbreak of the Middle East conflict.

However, the positive economic data did nothing to stem the yen's relentless decline. USD/JPY breached the 163 barrier, hitting levels not seen since 1986 — a four-decade low. Japanese Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama issued fresh intervention warnings, stating authorities are ready to take "appropriate and bold action," but markets have grown desensitized to verbal cues. Japan's June trade deficit of $2.5 billion — more than double expectations — underscores the country's vulnerability to surging energy import costs. The Bank of Japan meets next week and is widely expected to keep rates on hold, offering no near-term relief for the beleaguered currency.

The PMI report also highlighted persistent cost pressures: input cost inflation remained near 44-month highs driven by energy, fuel, and raw material costs, while business sentiment stayed below its historical trend amid Middle East uncertainty.

3. Treasury Yields Surge, September Rate Hike Odds Top 80%

The US bond market faced another wave of selling on Thursday. The 10-year Treasury yield surged to 4.707% — its highest since January 2025 — while the 30-year long bond broke above 5.18%, marking 27 days above the 5% threshold this year, the longest such streak since 2007. The 2-year yield hit 4.37%, a 17-month high.

Three forces are driving the yield spike in parallel: Brent crude's surge past $100/barrel reigniting inflation expectations; weekly jobless claims falling to 187,000 (the lowest since 1969), signaling an overheated labor market; and Fed rate hike probabilities repricing sharply. Fed funds futures now price an 82% chance of a September rate hike (up from 52% a week ago), with July odds climbing from 12% to approximately 38%.

In oil markets, Brent crude settled at $100.69/barrel on Thursday, up 7.0%, while WTI rose 6.2% to $92.19. Houthi rebels struck two Saudi oil tankers (the Encelia and Layla) in the Red Sea, with one vessel losing steering capability. Trump threatened "major military punishment" against the Houthis and said he is "seriously considering" resuming large-scale combat operations against Iran.

"If oil prices stay in the $90-$120 range through the end of summer, it will have a significant impact on consumer spending," warned Rob Haworth, Senior Investment Strategy Director at US Bank Wealth Management.

4. Defense Stocks Surge as Lockheed Martin Hits All-Time High

While tech stocks suffered heavy losses, the defense sector emerged as Thursday's biggest winner on Wall Street. Lockheed Martin surged 10.5% to an all-time high after raising its 2026 full-year revenue and profit guidance, benefiting from surging Pentagon spending amid the ongoing US-Iran conflict. RTX (formerly Raytheon Technologies) gained 7.3% on strong earnings. The S&P 500 industrials sector rose 1.77%, the best performer among all 11 sectors.

The US has conducted 12 consecutive nights of airstrikes against Iran, with ammunition consumption and equipment replenishment needs driving massive defense procurement. Analysts note that if the US-Iran conflict becomes protracted, the defense budget could break further historical records.

5. ECB Holds Steady but Sounds Hawkish, September Hike in Play

The European Central Bank held its main refinancing rate at 2.25% on Thursday, as widely expected. However, ECB President Christine Lagarde struck a distinctly hawkish tone in her press conference, stating that "uncertainty remains high and the full inflationary impact of the energy shock has yet to play out." She emphasized that the Governing Council is "closely monitoring the intensity and duration of the shock, as well as its indirect and second-round effects."

Markets interpreted the statement as a clear signal that September is live for a rate hike, with probability now exceeding 80%. Germany's 10-year Bund yield broke above 3.2% for the first time since 2011, returning to Eurozone debt crisis-era levels. The pan-European STOXX 600 fell 1.3%, with chipmaker STMicroelectronics plunging 17.5% after an earnings miss.

In currency markets, the euro slipped back below $1.14 following the ECB decision. The dollar index rose about 0.3%, supported by higher Treasury yields and safe-haven demand amid Middle East turmoil.

Overall, global financial markets are navigating a confluence of historic inflection points: a once-in-a-century restructuring of US tariff policy, an energy crisis driven by Middle East conflict, central banks grappling with stagflationary pressures, and a valuation reckoning for the AI investment bubble. The information density and uncertainty levels facing investors in the final week of July are at historic extremes.
