⚡ July 18 International Politics Roundup: US Widens Iran Strikes to Bridges, Iran Retaliates Across Gulf, Ukraine Protests Erupt Over Defense Minister's Firing

1. US Launches Sixth Consecutive Night of Strikes on Iran, Targets Bridges and Infrastructure

The United States carried out its sixth consecutive night of major airstrikes against Iran on Thursday, significantly expanding the scope of its military campaign by targeting civilian infrastructure for the first time. The operation struck at least six bridges in Hormozgan province, a railway station in Bandar Abbas, and Iranshahr airport, according to Iranian state media IRNA.

The Gariveh Bridge and Kahurestan Bridge—key transport links connecting Bandar Abbas to Shiraz—were among those hit. Strikes also extended near Tehran and in Semnan province, home to Iran's ballistic missile production and space program. Iran's Health Ministry reported 38 deaths and over 400 injuries since June 22, with at least 7 killed and 20 wounded in the latest wave alone. Iran's Energy Ministry acknowledged attacks on power infrastructure for the first time since the conflict began on February 28, urging citizens in southern provinces to reduce electricity consumption.

US Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth shared satellite imagery showing the apparent collapse of a surveillance tower at Iran's Chabahar Port—a key trade gateway for Afghanistan—after repeated strikes. CENTCOM stated that US forces, including fighter jets, drones, and warships, struck "dozens of Iranian military targets" including coastal surveillance systems, air defense sites, logistics infrastructure, and maritime capabilities.

2. Iran's Massive Retaliation: Missiles and Drones Strike US Assets Across Six Countries

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched what appears to be its largest wave of retaliatory strikes since the ceasefire collapsed, targeting US military assets across the region:

  • Bahrain: IRGC claimed Arash drone strikes on US helicopters and P-8 reconnaissance aircraft at Sakhir Air Base. Air raid sirens were activated and the Defense Force intercepted multiple incoming projectiles.
  • Kuwait: IRGC claimed to have hit a missile defense radar, weapons depots, and two HIMARS launchers at Camp Arifjan. The Kuwaiti government confirmed a power and desalination plant was damaged by Iranian fire, with "a large number of electricity generation units" destroyed. The fire has since been extinguished and emergency contingency plans activated.
  • Qatar: The key US-Iran mediator came under two waves of Iranian missile barrages within one hour. Qatar's Defense Ministry said air defenses intercepted all incoming projectiles, but the Interior Ministry reported a child was wounded by falling shrapnel.
  • Jordan: The Jordanian military confirmed its air defense systems intercepted three Iranian missiles, causing no casualties.
  • Syria: In a major escalation, IRGC announced it struck the US special operations command center at al-Tanf in southeastern Syria with drones and missiles—marking the first Iranian attack on Syrian territory since the regional war erupted. CENTCOM denied any US casualties.
  • Oman: IRGC claimed to have destroyed a US air control radar in Oman's Ghanim region and a maritime control radar in the Strait of Hormuz. A tanker was struck by an unknown projectile off Khasab, causing minor structural damage.

3. Strait of Hormuz and Red Sea: Dual Waterway Blockade Threat Looms

The situation at the Strait of Hormuz continues to deteriorate. Iranian military spokesperson Col. Ebrahim Zolfaghari declared that the strait "will never return to what it was before the 40-day war." The US has reimposed its "Steel Wall" naval blockade on Iranian ports, with CENTCOM announcing that three commercial vessels attempting to breach the blockade were redirected, one disabled for non-compliance, and another boarded for inspection.

More alarmingly, three sources told Reuters that Iran has instructed Yemen's Houthi rebels to prepare to close the Bab al-Mandab strait at the mouth of the Red Sea. A simultaneous closure of both waterways would represent an unprecedented threat to global energy supplies. IEA chief Fatih Birol warned that oil and gas security could come under "serious pressure" if the situation does not improve within weeks. Brent crude rose approximately 1% to $85.83 per barrel on Friday. Maritime intelligence firm Lloyd's List reported that only 21 vessels transited the strait on July 14, compared to a pre-war average of 150-200 daily.

4. Ukraine in Turmoil: Zelensky Fires Defense Minister, Nationwide Protests Erupt

Ukraine was plunged into political crisis on Friday as President Volodymyr Zelensky dismissed Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov after just six months in the role. Fedorov, 35, was the architect of Ukraine's highly successful drone warfare program and a symbol of the country's technological innovation in combat. His firing triggered massive street protests in Kyiv, Kharkiv, Lviv, and other major cities, with thousands demanding his reinstatement.

The dismissal followed a bitter feud between Fedorov and Armed Forces Commander-in-Chief Gen. Oleksandr Syrskyi, which Zelensky acknowledged as a key factor. In his first public statement after leaving office, Fedorov confirmed that Syrskyi had presented him with an ultimatum and subsequently blocked his reform agenda.

Zelensky appointed Maj. Gen. Yevhen Khmara, acting head of the SBU security service and architect of Operation Spiderweb—one of Ukraine's most spectacular long-range strikes—as acting defense minister. However, Ukrainian law requires the defense minister to be a civilian, and parliament is on summer recess until mid-August, leaving his formal confirmation in limbo.

On the battlefield, Ukraine's Unmanned Systems Forces commander announced that 12 Russian shadow fleet vessels were struck in the Black Sea overnight, including nine bulk carriers, one oil tanker, one gas carrier, and one tugboat. Since Operation Molochka began on July 6, 159 Russian vessels have been hit. Outgoing UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer, making his farewell visit to Kyiv, pledged €300 million ($254 million) to help Ukraine acquire a squadron of 16 Swedish Saab Gripen fighter jets by 2029.

5. UK: Andy Burnham Officially Declared Labour Leader, Set to Become PM Monday

Andy Burnham was officially declared leader of Britain's governing Labour Party at a special conference in central London on Friday, clearing his final hurdle to become prime minister on Monday, July 20. The former Greater Manchester mayor, who returned to Parliament only last month, was the sole candidate after securing the backing of 379 out of 403 Labour MPs in the House of Commons.

In his victory speech, Burnham promised to "bring hope back" and deliver "the biggest change in British politics in 40 years," criticizing the centralization and privatization trends since the Thatcher era. He pledged a government "unashamedly Labour in our priorities" focused on devolution, economic renewal, and reindustrialization. Outgoing PM Sir Keir Starmer said he resigned to prevent the government from "imploding into some massive fight" and expressed confidence that Labour can win the next election under Burnham.

6. 7.3 Magnitude Earthquake Strikes Mexico-Guatemala Border, Tsunami Warning Issued

A powerful 7.3-magnitude (later revised to 7.4) shallow earthquake struck off the coast of Chiapas, Mexico, near the Guatemalan border on Friday morning. The US Geological Survey reported the quake at a depth of approximately 10 kilometers. The Pacific Tsunami Warning Center issued a tsunami threat for coastlines within 300 kilometers of the epicenter, covering parts of Mexico, Guatemala, El Salvador, and Honduras. Mexican civil protection authorities reported no immediate serious damage but continued to monitor for secondary hazards.
