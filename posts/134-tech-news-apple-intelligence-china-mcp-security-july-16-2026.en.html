⚡ Apple Intelligence Gets China Approval with Alibaba Qwen, First Malicious MCP Server Exposed, Run 744B-Parameter Models Locally

1. Apple Intelligence Finally Clears China Regulatory Hurdle, Partners with Alibaba's Tongyi Qianwen

On July 15, China's Cyberspace Administration officially published a new batch of mobile-side generative AI service registrations, with Apple's "Apple Intelligence" appearing on the list alongside 6 other AI products including Huawei Xiaoyi, OPPO AndesGPT, vivo BlueHeart, Xiaomi HyperMind, Samsung Galaxy AI, and Doubao Mobile. This marks the critical regulatory milestone for Apple Intelligence's launch in the world's largest smartphone market — Chinese iPhone users who have waited nearly two years for the AI features have finally cleared the biggest obstacle.

Alibaba Confirms Qwen Integration

An Alibaba spokesperson confirmed to media: "For Chinese users, Tongyi Qianwen will be integrated into the Apple Intelligence experience across iOS, iPadOS, macOS, and visionOS." This means Chinese Apple users will be able to access Qwen's text and image understanding and content generation capabilities directly on their Apple devices without switching between apps.

Significantly, Alibaba is not Apple's only Chinese AI partner. Sources familiar with the matter reveal that Baidu will also participate in building the China-specific version of Apple Intelligence's AI search functionality and assist in upgrading the Chinese Siri voice assistant. This signals Apple's multi-model partnership strategy in China — unlike its partnership with OpenAI in the US, AI capabilities in the Chinese market will be delivered by local providers.

PrismML Shrinks 27B-Parameter Model to 4GB for On-Device Inference

On the same day, Khosla Ventures-backed Caltech spinout PrismML publicly demonstrated its compression technology: shrinking the ~54GB open-source Qwen model to under 4GB, enabling the full 27-billion-parameter model to run locally on iPhone 15 and newer models. This breakthrough solves a core bottleneck for on-device AI deployment and provides technical assurance for Apple Intelligence's actual user experience in the Chinese market.

Apple's Long Road to AI in China

Apple Intelligence was originally announced at WWDC 2024, but due to regulatory hurdles and localization challenges, it was never able to launch in China. Over the nearly two years since, Apple went through a series of setbacks including replacing its base model, negotiating with multiple Chinese AI companies, and adjusting its technical approach. While the regulatory filing removes a key barrier, Bloomberg's Mark Gurman reported that a beta version of the China Apple Intelligence was briefly pushed to some users for about an hour before being rapidly withdrawn, suggesting final compliance and technical adjustments are still needed before a full launch.

Following the announcement, Alibaba's US-listed shares rose 5.59% to $118.60, while Apple gained 4.27%, reflecting market optimism about the partnership.

2. First Malicious MCP Server 'postmark-mcp' Exposed in the Wild

Security research firm Safeguard disclosed a disturbing supply chain attack: a third-party MCP (Model Context Protocol) server impersonating the Postmark email service called "postmark-mcp" was discovered secretly harvesting emails after being downloaded 1,643 times from npm.

How the Attack Worked

The malware dates back to September 17, 2025, when version 1.0.16 quietly added a one-line change: silently BCC'ing every email the server processed to an attacker-controlled inbox at phan@giftshop[.]club. The package had nothing to do with Postmark, the legitimate transactional email company — it was a third-party MCP server borrowing the name.

MCP (Model Context Protocol), introduced by Anthropic in November 2024, allows AI agents to call tools through small server processes — sending emails, querying databases, reading files, calling APIs. Unlike traditional software dependencies, MCP servers are handed live credentials (API keys, SMTP permissions) and execute arbitrary logic on every invocation, making them far riskier than ordinary npm packages.

Impact Assessment

Researchers estimate roughly 300 organizations were running the compromised server in production, each routing 10 to 50 emails per day through it. At peak, this meant 3,000 to 15,000 emails per day were quietly copied to an attacker-controlled inbox. The attacker didn't need to steal credentials — they just needed the legitimate, already-authorized traffic to route through one extra BCC line.

Industry Warning

This incident is widely cited as the first documented malicious MCP server found in the wild. As MCP adoption expanded rapidly through 2025 with the rise of agentic coding tools, teams were wiring these servers into production email, ticketing, and database systems faster than most security programs had processes to review them. Safeguard notes that the trust model for MCP servers needs a complete redesign — having 15 clean historical versions on npm should not be sufficient grounds for trust.

3. Open-Source Breakthrough: Running 744B-Parameter Models on Consumer Hardware

Open-source inference engines have reached a milestone. Two new engines — Colibrì and DwarfStar — let developers run genuinely frontier-scale AI models on ordinary consumer hardware, fundamentally changing the binary question of "can I run this model?"

Colibrì: 744B-Parameter Model Needs Just 25GB RAM

Colibrì is an open-source inference engine by developer JustVugg, released under Apache 2.0 license and sitting at around 8,000 GitHub stars. It runs GLM-5.2, a 744-billion-parameter model, on a normal consumer machine with about 25GB of RAM.

The core insight leverages Mixture-of-Experts architecture: only 5-7% of a model's parameters fire per token. Colibrì keeps the ~10GB "dense core" resident in RAM permanently, while the other ~370GB of expert layers live on the SSD and get streamed in on demand. Your storage drive effectively becomes a very large, reasonably fast extension of your RAM.

DwarfStar: DeepSeek V4 at 35-40 Tokens/Second Locally

DwarfStar, built by renowned developer antirez (creator of Redis), is designed for running DeepSeek V4 locally and takes SSD streaming a step further: it treats the SSD-stored KV cache as a "first-class disk citizen." On a MacBook Pro M5 Max, DwarfStar generates DeepSeek V4 Flash output at around 35-40 tokens/second — genuinely usable for real work, not just a demo.

Antirez's most elegant framing of the shift: SSD streaming turns available RAM "from a hard cutoff (can I run this model or not?) into a continuous spectrum of speed levels." What was once a pass/fail binary choice is now a dial.

The Real Value: Data Sovereignty

The true significance of these engines isn't speed — it's data sovereignty. Sensitive data can now be processed by frontier-class models without ever leaving your device. This is revolutionary for scenarios handling financial data, medical records, or corporate secrets. Of course, local doesn't automatically mean safe — it still runs with your permissions, and you still need to verify its output. It's a privacy fix, not a trust fix.
