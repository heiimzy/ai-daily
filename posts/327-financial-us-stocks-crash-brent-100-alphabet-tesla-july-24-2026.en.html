⚡ July 24 Financial Roundup: Brent Breaks $100, US Stocks Routed, Alphabet & Tesla AI Spending Triggers Market Panic

1. Brent Crude Surges Past $100, Soaring 7% in a Single Day

International oil prices surged violently in overnight trading on July 23, with Brent crude futures settling at $100.69 per barrel, a massive 7.04% single-day gain — the highest level since May 2025. WTI crude also broke through $92/barrel, extending its rally for a fifth consecutive session.

The immediate trigger was a new wave of Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping lanes. The Iran-backed militant group claimed responsibility for striking two Saudi oil tankers in the Red Sea and announced a naval blockade of Saudi ports, marking a significant expansion of the Middle East conflict from the Strait of Hormuz to the Red Sea.

President Donald Trump issued a stern warning on his Truth Social platform: "From now on, whenever Iran fires at ships in the Strait of Hormuz — whether by missile, rocket, drone, or any other device or weapon — the United States will bomb and destroy a bridge or power plant, including such a facility in or near the capital Tehran." He later added that he was "seriously considering" launching a massive military operation against Iran.

Brent crude has now surged over 36% from its early-July low and is up more than 45% year-to-date. Kazakhstan's decision to suspend crude exports through the Caspian Pipeline Consortium (CPC) terminal following drone attacks further tightened the supply outlook.

2. US Stocks Routed: Nasdaq Crashes 600 Points, Dow Down 530

All three major US indexes closed sharply lower on Thursday, July 23:

  • Dow Jones Industrial Average: Down 530.82 points (-1.02%) to 51,687.76
  • Nasdaq Composite: Down 599.11 points (-2.33%) to 25,091.79 — the worst single-day drop in nearly two months
  • S&P 500: Down 102.11 points (-1.36%) to 7,396.85

The VIX volatility index surged to 17.75, its highest level in a month. Pre-market futures were even more brutal, with Dow futures plunging over 600 points and Nasdaq 100 futures falling 1.5% at session lows.

Deutsche Bank analyst Jim Reid noted: "Inflation remains the market's top concern today, with Brent crude continuing to rise as the Middle East situation escalates. There's no sign of de-escalation in US-Iran strikes, and the Houthi Red Sea attacks are amplifying fears that the conflict is widening."

3. Alphabet CapEx Shock: $205 Billion Sinks Stock 6.9%

Google parent Alphabet (GOOGL) reported Q2 earnings after the bell on Wednesday, with revenue of $119.8 billion (+24% YoY) and Google Cloud revenue surging 82% — headline numbers that beat expectations. However, markets zeroed in on a single metric: capital expenditure.

Alphabet raised its full-year 2026 CapEx guidance to as high as $205 billion, far exceeding Wall Street estimates. The massive AI infrastructure spending pushed the company's free cash flow negative for the first time in two decades. This spooked investors who have grown increasingly concerned about whether AI spending is translating into proportional returns. Shares fell 6.9% in after-hours trading.

4. Tesla Earnings Miss: Shares Plunge 14.3%

Tesla (TSLA) delivered a Q2 earnings report that badly disappointed the market. Key figures:

  • Revenue: $28.236 billion (+26% YoY), roughly in line with estimates
  • Operating profit: just $400 million, down 57% YoY
  • Operating margin: just 1.4%, down sharply from 4.1% a year ago
  • Free cash flow: -$1.1 billion
  • Automotive gross margin: 16.8% (vs. 17.2% a year ago)

CEO Elon Musk characterized 2026 as a "massive CapEx year," with continued heavy investment in the Optimus robot program, robotaxi expansion, and data center buildout. The cash burn far exceeded expectations, and with investors already jittery about Big Tech AI spending, Tesla shares cratered over 14.3% — their worst single-day drop since June 2025.

Notably, the semiconductor sector held up relatively well. SK Hynix and Micron both rose about 3%, and the Philadelphia Semiconductor index slipped only about 0.44%. AMD announced a strategic partnership with Anthropic, gaining 1.45% on the day.

5. Fed Rate Hike Probability Surges, Bond Market Reacts

The inflation shock from surging oil prices is rapidly repricing rate expectations. Money market data shows:

  • The probability of a July Fed rate hike jumped from roughly 10% a week ago to 35%
  • Markets have fully priced in a rate hike by September at the latest
  • The US 10-year Treasury yield rose to 4.67%, its highest level since January 2025

Wells Fargo Investment Institute's Sameer Samana commented: "Escalating Middle East tensions are pushing crude prices higher, fueling concerns that inflation could re-accelerate and delay rate cuts or potentially force the Fed to hike. We believe oil prices will eventually normalize, but conditions may worsen before they improve."

Goldman Sachs has previously warned that if the Middle East conflict continues to escalate, Brent crude could break $120 in Q4, further intensifying global inflationary pressures and extending central bank tightening cycles worldwide.

6. Asian Markets Open Under Pressure

With Wall Street ending sharply lower and oil prices continuing to surge, Asian markets face a challenging open. Japan's Nikkei 225 futures declined overnight, and South Korea's KOSPI futures moved lower. Investor attention is focused on:

  1. Whether Middle East tensions escalate further — both the Strait of Hormuz and the Red Sea are under simultaneous pressure
  2. Japan's June CPI data, due for release, which will influence the Bank of Japan's next policy move
  3. Whether Asia-Pacific markets follow Wall Street into a systemic selloff

In China, surging international oil prices could boost the energy sector, but tech and manufacturing face rising input cost pressures. Alibaba and other Chinese ADRs bucked the trend overnight, rising over 3% in pre-market trading as some value-focused capital continued to seek allocation.
