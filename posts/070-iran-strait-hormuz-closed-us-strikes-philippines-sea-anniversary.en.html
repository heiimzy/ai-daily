⚡ July 12 Geopolitics Update: Iran Closes Strait of Hormuz Paralyzing Global Shipping, US Launches 3rd Wave of Airstrikes, IRGC Fires Ballistic Missiles at Qatar Base

July 12, 2026, 06:01 UTC — The Middle East crisis has dramatically escalated in the past hours. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) announced the complete closure of the Strait of Hormuz, prompting U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) to launch a third round of airstrikes against Iranian targets. Meanwhile, the Philippines marks the 10th anniversary of its landmark 2016 arbitration victory against China in the South China Sea, with 14 nations jointly reaffirming the ruling.

1. Iran Closes the Strait of Hormuz: A Global Shipping Artery Severed

The IRGC Navy declared early Sunday morning that the Strait of Hormuz would be "closed until further notice," claiming that several vessels had attempted to transit through "unauthorized routes" despite repeated warnings to alter course. One vessel had even switched off its Automatic Identification System (AIS), the IRGC said.

"The Strait of Hormuz will be closed until further notice and until the end of US interventions in this area. No vessel will be allowed to pass." — IRGC Navy Statement

This marks the second time Iran has closed the strategic waterway since the outbreak of the US-Israel War on February 28, 2026. The Strait — through which approximately one-fifth of the world's traded oil and natural gas passes — is one of the most critical chokepoints in global energy trade. Analysts warn that the closure could trigger a new global energy crisis and send oil prices soaring.

The IRGC confirmed it fired warning shots at the Cyprus-flagged container ship M/V GFS Galaxy, which sustained severe engine room damage and caught fire. One civilian crew member remains missing. The UK Maritime Trade Operations (UKMTO) confirmed the vessel was disabled and the crew had abandoned ship into lifeboats.

2. US Third-Round Strikes: CENTCOM Says 140 Targets Hit

U.S. Central Command confirmed it began the third round of strikes this week at 7:15 PM ET on July 11. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth delivered a stark message on social media:

"Iran made a poor choice. Now they pay." — U.S. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth

According to CENTCOM, the strikes targeted a wide array of Iranian military assets, including:

  • Air surveillance radars — early warning systems along the Strait's coastline
  • Maritime surveillance systems — coastal monitoring networks tracking vessel traffic
  • Missile and drone storage facilities — depots housing anti-ship ballistic missiles and attack drones
  • Missile launch sites and UAV launch points — mobile launchers along Iran's southern coast
  • Surface-to-air missile launchers — air defense positions

Iranian state broadcaster IRIB reported three explosions in the port city of Bandar Abbas, two in Sirik, and additional blasts on Qeshm Island in the Strait of Hormuz. Iran's Health Ministry reported at least 17 killed and 115 wounded across two days of strikes.

The U.S. also struck coastal bases and communication towers along Iran's southern coast, according to IRGC statements. The Aq Tekeh Khan railway bridge — a critical link connecting northern Iran with Turkmenistan and Central Asian rail networks — was also reportedly damaged.

3. IRGC Retaliates: Ballistic Missiles Strike Al Udeid Air Base

The IRGC launched a sweeping retaliation, claiming to have struck the U.S. Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar with ballistic missiles, destroying its fighter jet maintenance center and command-and-control facility.

Iran also targeted Jordan's Prince Hassan Air Base, destroying drone hangars and a command center. In a further escalation, the IRGC claimed to have struck and disabled a second vessel in the Strait of Hormuz.

The UAE Ministry of Defense confirmed its air defenses intercepted Iranian drone and missile threats, saying explosions heard across the country resulted from defense operations. Qatar also confirmed intercepting a missile attack, while Kuwait's Armed Forces reported intercepting "hostile aerial targets" in its airspace.

4. Diplomacy in Tandem: Oman Proposes Toll-Free Transit Plan

Despite the military escalation, diplomatic channels remain active. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi traveled to Muscat to meet Omani Foreign Minister Badr Albusaidi, discussing safe navigation arrangements through the Strait of Hormuz.

According to CNN, Omani mediators presented a key proposal:

  • Southern route (near Oman's coast) — toll-free passage, consistent with pre-war arrangements
  • Northern route (near Iran's coast) — requiring permits from Tehran but also toll-free
  • A joint management framework involving both Tehran and Muscat

Iran has not yet approved the proposal. Axios reported that U.S.-Iran technical teams are expected to meet in Pakistan on Sunday, July 12. However, Iran's Fars news agency quoted a source saying "no negotiations will take place until the United States retracts from its positions."

Meanwhile, Iran's new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei — in his first statement since his father's funeral — vowed revenge: "Avenging the blood of our martyrs is the demand of the nation and must certainly be carried out." President Trump responded on Truth Social, warning that "1,000 missiles are locked and loaded and aimed at Iran" and that any assassination attempt would result in Iran being "completely decimated."

5. Philippines Marks 10th Anniversary of 2016 South China Sea Ruling

July 12 marks the 10th anniversary of the Philippines' landmark arbitration victory at the Permanent Court of Arbitration in The Hague, which declared China's nine-dash line claims had no basis in international law. The Philippines commemorates the date as "West Philippine Sea Victory Day."

President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. delivered a speech at a diplomatic reception, emphasizing that the ruling is more than an "abstract triumph of jurisprudence" — it is about defending the lives and livelihoods of Filipino fishermen who have cast their nets in these waters for generations.

"When we defend the rule of law, we are not merely defending lines on a map. We are defending the lives, the livelihoods and the future of our peoples." — President Ferdinand Marcos Jr.

Defense Secretary Gilbert Teodoro Jr. called the ruling "not just an award for the Philippines, but an award for the world," asserting that it establishes the peremptory nature of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS).

According to the Manila Bulletin, 14 nations jointly reaffirmed the 2016 arbitral ruling and rejected China's expansive South China Sea claims. However, China continues to reject the ruling and has spent the past decade intensifying island-building activities and consolidating de facto control over features like Scarborough Shoal.

Philippine military chief General Romeo Brawner Jr. called for stronger collaboration with "like-minded forces" led by the U.S. for deterrence, and pledged to continue the military's "assertive transparency" strategy of documenting Chinese aggressive actions.

Ten years on, local fishermen still dare not venture to Scarborough Shoal. Rony Drio, a 59-year-old fisherman from Masinloc, told Reuters: "We won in 2016, but it doesn't feel like a victory to me." Fellow fisherman Henrilito Empoc, 47, said he has not been to the shoal since 2022 — Chinese vessels have used water cannons against their boats and cut their anchor lines.

6. Market Impact and Outlook

The Strait of Hormuz closure has immediate global economic implications. International energy analysts warn that a sustained blockade could send oil prices back above $120 per barrel — the wartime highs seen in early 2026. The IMF, which had already downgraded global growth forecasts due to the US-Iran conflict, may be forced to revise further.

The key variables going forward are: whether Iran can sustain the Strait closure, whether the U.S. expands its targeting to include nuclear facilities and refineries, and whether Omani and Pakistani mediation can open a diplomatic off-ramp. Analysts assess both sides are probing each other's red lines — Iran using the Strait as its ultimate bargaining chip, while Washington seeks to degrade Tehran's military capabilities to force concessions.
