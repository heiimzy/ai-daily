Circle Stock Crashes 22% as Open USD Stablecoin Consortium Threatens USDC's Dominance

The stablecoin market is facing a historic shakeup. Open USD, a new stablecoin backed by a consortium of over 140 organizations including Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, Coinbase, BlackRock, Google, Shopify, Solana, Base, and Ripple, has officially launched — directly challenging Circle's USDC dominance. Circle (CRCL) shares plunged 22% within 48 hours of the announcement, and Mizuho downgraded the stock from Buy to Underperform, slashing its price target from $85 to $50.

Open USD's core competitive advantage lies in its consortium governance model. Unlike traditional stablecoin issuers that keep all reserve income, Open USD shares reserve income with its partners and offers free minting and redemptions. This model directly targets Circle's profit engine — with USDC circulating supply at approximately $78 billion, Circle's main revenue source is the interest earned on the dollar reserves backing the stablecoin. If major payment networks route transaction volume to Open USD, Circle's pricing power could face material erosion.

"Open USD has to break USDC's network effect — collecting high-profile backers alone is not enough." — Jeremy Allaire, Circle CEO

Circle's CEO sought to calm markets by highlighting USDC's three moats: deep liquidity, exchange integrations, and compliance infrastructure, noting that consortium-backed stablecoins have never successfully scaled before. However, Mizuho analysts warned that the combination of free minting, reserve-income sharing, and distribution partner alliances could fundamentally alter the stablecoin industry's economics. Total global stablecoin transaction volume reached $55 trillion in 2025, with payment activity up 76% year-over-year — the allocation of this incremental market will determine the industry's future structure.

Notably, Coinbase's participation in both Circle's revenue-sharing agreement and the Open USD consortium suggests even USDC's closest partners are hedging their bets.

Bitcoin ETFs See $367.9M in Net Inflows Over Three Days: BlackRock's IBIT Leads Institutional Return

The Bitcoin spot ETF market is experiencing a long-awaited capital revival. According to Woofun AI data, institutional investors recorded net inflows for three consecutive trading days, totaling $367.9 million — signaling a capital rotation back after weeks of aggressive outflows.

The flows showed notable diversification across products. BlackRock's IBIT led the charge, absorbing $80.8 million on day one and another $33.4 million on day two, pushing its cumulative net inflows past $60.3 billion. Fidelity's FBTC reversed a previous outflow streak with $16.9 million in inflows on day one, followed by $30.7 million on day two. Bitwise's BITB contributed an additional $15 million. This multi-product diversification reduces single-fund dependency risk.

A key variable is the material easing of GBTC selling pressure. Previously, large-scale redemptions from Grayscale's Bitcoin Trust had significantly offset industry-wide inflows, but these outflows have now paused, freeing up room for net positive flows through other products. Cumulative net inflows across all Bitcoin ETFs have now surpassed $51.2 billion since launch.

As of press time, Bitcoin is trading around $64,500, up 0.34% over 24 hours, maintaining its narrow range between $64,000 and $65,000. On-chain data shows short-term holder cost basis concentrated in the $62,000-$65,000 range, forming a near-term support cluster. $66,000 represents the next key resistance level, while $69,000 marks the broader short-term holder breakeven point.

Polymarket prediction data shows a 99.95% probability Bitcoin closes above $52,000 on July 20, and 98.95% above $60,000, but the probability drops sharply to just 11.5% above $66,000 — indicating the market expects continued tight-range consolidation in the near term.

Gold's Painful Week: 79% of Wall Street Analysts Bearish, HSBC Slashes Price Forecast

The gold market just recorded its sharpest weekly decline in six weeks. Kitco's latest weekly gold survey shows Wall Street analysts at their most pessimistic in weeks — of 14 respondents, only 1 (7%) predicted higher prices, 11 (79%) predicted lower, and 2 (14%) expected sideways trading. Among 169 retail investors surveyed, 40% expected prices to rise, 36% expected declines, and 24% were neutral.

Spot gold closed Friday at $4,019.30 per ounce, up 1.06% on the day but still recording the deepest weekly loss in six weeks. During the week, gold briefly broke below the $4,000 psychological threshold to hit $3,959.37 — a new阶段性 low. Gold previously hit an all-time high of $5,608 in January and has now retreated approximately 28% from that peak.

HSBC published a closely watched report this week, slashing its 2026 and 2027 average gold price forecasts from $4,864 and $5,000 to $4,560 and $4,925 respectively. HSBC Chief Precious Metals Analyst James Steel noted that $4,560 implies further downside from current levels near $4,000. The intensifying US-Iran conflict pushes oil prices higher, which in turn strengthens inflation expectations and bond yields — a persistent headwind for non-yielding gold.

Market analysts point to two competing forces driving gold prices:

  • Safe-haven support — Escalating Middle East conflict and Strait of Hormuz risks provide underlying避险 demand for gold
  • Rate hike headwind — Rising oil prices fuel inflation expectations, markets price continued Fed tightening, the 10-year Treasury yield has risen to 4.62%, and USD strength pressures gold

Adrian Day, president of Adrian Day Asset Management, believes gold is unlikely to break out of its current range until the Fed confirms it will no longer raise rates. However, Paul Wong, CEO of Sprott Inc., notes that gold is technically oversold — trading 2-3 standard deviations below its normal trendline — and historically, early August tends to be a seasonal bottoming window for gold.

"Gold's fundamental support has not completely collapsed — global geopolitical risks are at multi-decade highs and central bank buying remains strong. The current sell-off is more about liquidity pressure and crowded trade unwinding than fundamental deterioration." — CICC Research

Oil markets remain volatile this week. Brent crude holds near $85, supported by continued Strait of Hormuz shipping disruptions. The oil → inflation → rate hike transmission chain remains the core macro logic suppressing gold. Markets are closely watching Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's first congressional testimony next week and the July 28 FOMC rate decision.
