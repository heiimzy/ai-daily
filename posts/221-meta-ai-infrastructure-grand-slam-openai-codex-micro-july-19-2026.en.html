I. Meta's Textbook Counterattack: From "Compute Glut" Doubts to a Triple Salvo

Just over a week ago, news that Meta was considering leasing out computing power triggered a global market panic, sending semiconductor stocks sharply lower. But within days, Meta fired off multiple重磅 signals in a textbook narrative reversal.

CEO Mark Zuckerberg demonstrated through action that the social media giant not only has the resolve but also the resources to seize the high ground in the AI arms race. Meta's stock surged nearly 15% this week, making it one of the standout performers in the tech sector.

Compute Infrastructure: 7GW Target, Doubling to 14GW Next Year

Meta announced plans to deploy 7GW of AI compute infrastructure this year, having already added 1GW in the first half. Zuckerberg stated bluntly: "I don't know anyone in the industry who thinks they have excess compute." Research firm SemiAnalysis projects that by year-end, Meta will have more AI compute than OpenAI and Anthropic combined.

To secure its supply chain, Meta has signed memory chip supply agreements with Samsung, flash storage deals with SanDisk, and fiber optic equipment contracts with Sumitomo Electric. The company's 2026 capital expenditure is expected to reach a record $125-145 billion, nearly double the $72 billion spent in 2025, with the bulk directed toward AI hardware and data center construction.

Iris Chip: Production Ready by September

Meta's self-developed AI chip, codenamed Iris, completed testing in just six weeks with no major issues found. Production is slated to begin as early as September. Iris marks Meta's gradual move away from sole reliance on Nvidia and AMD, building a complete闭环 from chip to model to application. The chip is designed to complement Nvidia and AMD supply for Meta's increasingly massive AI workloads.

Muse Spark 1.1: First Paid Model Launch

Meta released its most advanced AI model, Muse Spark 1.1, charging developers and enterprises for the first time. The model focuses on "agentic" capabilities — autonomously completing full workflows including code writing, bug fixing, cross-software operations, and long-document processing. Its API pricing is set at approximately 25% of OpenAI and Anthropic's top-tier models, targeting the enterprise market with a high cost-performance ratio.

II. Meta-Anthropic $10B Compute Partnership: A New Model for AI Infrastructure Monetization

Confirmed by multiple outlets including The New York Times, Meta is in deep negotiations with AI startup Anthropic for a potential $10 billion compute lease deal spanning two years. The partnership was first proposed by Anthropic in June and is currently under Meta's evaluation.

This potential deal marks the formal launch of Meta's "Meta Compute" cloud business expansion plan. Zuckerberg had previously stated the company is considering turning surplus computing resources into new revenue streams to demonstrate that its massive AI capital expenditure can generate returns beyond advertising. Meta cut 8,000 jobs in May while redirecting billions toward AI infrastructure, and recruited former AWS senior executive Dave Brown to lead the effort.

For IPO-bound Anthropic (targeting an October listing), securing stable compute supply is critical to commercialization. In May, Anthropic signed a $45 billion, three-year compute deal with SpaceX. Industry experts believe Anthropic is building a distributed infrastructure network to reduce dependence on any single partner.

Market analysis highlights the structural transformation this represents: Meta's Llama and Anthropic's Claude are direct competitors, yet extreme compute scarcity is blurring competitive boundaries at the infrastructure level. This "build the base and lease surplus to competitors" model echoes Amazon's early AWS strategy.

III. OpenAI Enters Hardware: $230 Codex Micro Mechanical Keypad

OpenAI has officially entered the hardware market — not with the rumored screenless AI companion, but with a mechanical keypad specifically designed for AI coding agents. The $230 Codex Micro, built in collaboration with luxury keyboard maker Work Louder, is the company's first consumer hardware product.

Specifications and Design Highlights

The Codex Micro features 13 mechanical switches (6 of which are Agent Keys that monitor multiple Codex agent statuses), a rotary dial for adjusting reasoning levels, a joystick for radial menu navigation, and a capacitive touch sensor. It supports Bluetooth and USB-C connectivity and is compatible with Windows and macOS. The body is CNC-machined aluminum with polycarbonate parts, and includes 32 interchangeable keycaps.

The standout feature is its RGB lighting system: each key and perimeter light displays real-time agent status — white for idle, green for unread chat, blue for thinking, peach for questions, and red for errors. Developers can monitor multiple AI agents' working status through the lighting alone, without switching desktop windows.

Market Signals and Strategic Implications

OpenAI has described the Codex Micro as a "limited-run project." Pre-orders are now open, with first shipments expected later this month. The launch coincides with explosive Codex user growth: from 3 million weekly active users in early April to 8 million by mid-July, with OpenAI projecting 9 million imminently. The Codex Micro provides developers a physical interface for managing increasingly complex AI coding workflows, signaling the evolution of AI tools from pure software toward integrated hardware-software solutions.

This $230 device is separate from OpenAI's other hardware project — a screenless AI companion being developed with former Apple design chief Jony Ive, which remains in development and is expected to feature multiple cameras and sensors for spatial awareness.

IV. Conclusion: The AI Arms Race Enters the Infrastructure Monetization Era

From Meta's compute infrastructure blitz to OpenAI's hardware debut, this week's tech news reveals a clear trend: AI competition is shifting from "whose model is better" to "whose GPU costs less" and "whose infrastructure generates more revenue." Meta's three-pronged strategy — leasing compute to competitors, self-developing chips, and launching paid models — is transforming it from a social advertising company into an AI infrastructure provider. Meanwhile, OpenAI's entry into hardware via the Codex Micro explores diversified revenue paths beyond software subscriptions. The AI arms race is far from over, but the battlefield has become significantly more diverse and complex.
