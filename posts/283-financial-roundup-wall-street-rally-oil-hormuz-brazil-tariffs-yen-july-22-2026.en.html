⚡ July 22 Financial Roundup: Wall Street Semiconductor Rebound, Brent Above $91, US 25% Brazil Tariffs Take Effect, Yen at 39.5-Year Low

1. Wall Street Rallies on Semiconductor Rebound

U.S. stocks closed sharply higher on Tuesday (July 21), driven by a powerful rebound in semiconductor shares as bargain hunters flooded back into the sector following a steep sell-off. The Dow Jones Industrial Average gained 385.38 points (+0.74%) to close at 52,224.64. The S&P 500 rose 65.92 points (+0.89%) to 7,509.20, and the Nasdaq Composite surged 329.13 points (+1.29%) to 25,837.21.

The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOX) soared 5.21%, its biggest single-day gain since June 22. Memory chip stocks led the charge: SanDisk jumped over 14%, SK hynix ADR surged 13.75%, Western Digital and Micron each rallied over 12%, Seagate rose over 11%, and Kioxia ADR climbed over 17%.

The SOX had entered a technical bear market on Friday after falling more than 20% from its all-time high on June 22, but has since rebounded for two consecutive sessions, trimming the decline to approximately 15%. Analysts attributed the buying to hedge funds rebuilding positions ahead of this week's Big Tech earnings after having significantly cut semiconductor holdings during the sell-off.

"Investors are really buying back into semiconductors ahead of earnings because they fear missing out (FOMO) — that these companies could report outsized earnings beats and raise their outlooks," said Lindsey Bell, chief investment strategist at 248 Ventures. However, she cautioned that stocks rallying sharply ahead of earnings "makes it more difficult for them to run in response to earnings."

On the earnings front, industrial conglomerate 3M surged 7.32% after posting earnings that beat expectations and raising its full-year profit outlook. General Motors climbed 4.91% on stronger-than-expected results driven by solid North American sales. Market attention now shifts to Big Tech results due Wednesday, including Alphabet, Tesla, and AMD's Advancing AI event.

2. Brent Crude Breaks $91, Oil at Five-Week High

Oil prices continued their upward trajectory on Tuesday, with Brent crude for September delivery settling at $91.01 per barrel, up 2.01%, and WTI crude for August delivery rising 2.02% to $84.91 per barrel — both reaching their highest levels in approximately five weeks. Murban crude outperformed with a 4.90% gain to $85.77 per barrel.

The rally is driven by escalating geopolitical tensions in the Middle East. The US-Iran military conflict entered its 11th consecutive night, with both sides striking military targets. Yemen's Iran-aligned Houthi movement has declared a maritime blockade against Saudi Arabia, threatening commercial shipping through the Red Sea and forcing multiple tankers to alter course. The Strait of Hormuz — which handles roughly one-fifth of global oil trade — continues to see reduced vessel traffic, with only four ships transiting on Sunday, according to LSEG data, far below normal levels.

Goldman Sachs warned in its latest report that if Strait of Hormuz disruptions persist, Brent crude could break above $120 per barrel in Q4 2026. However, the bank's baseline forecast remains at $80 for Q4, predicated on a de-escalation between the US and Iran. Barclays analysts noted that global oil inventories are at their "tightest in five years," leaving minimal buffer against further supply shocks. Goldman Sachs recommended going long the December 2026 to March 2027 European diesel timespread to hedge persistent supply risks.

3. US 25% Tariffs on Brazil Take Effect Today

The United States' 25% tariff on imports from Brazil officially took effect on Wednesday (July 22), following a year-long Section 301 investigation by the USTR. The measures target Brazilian practices related to digital trade, ethanol market access, intellectual property enforcement, and content moderation policies that Washington deemed unfair to U.S. commerce.

According to Global Trade Alert estimates, Brazil's trade-weighted U.S. tariff rate surges from 11.7% to 18.2% on July 22, then settles at 14.4% after the temporary Section 122 surcharge expires on July 25. The final exemption list was expanded from the June proposal, adding 12 product groups covering 86 tariff lines and $2.4 billion in Brazilian trade, including pig iron, unflavored instant coffee, organic honey, and aluminum hydroxide.

Brazilian President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva condemned the tariffs as "unjustified" and announced countermeasures under Brazil's Reciprocity Law, alongside a formal complaint at the World Trade Organization. Trade Minister Marcio Elias Rosa stated that approximately 18% of Brazil's exports to the U.S. (about $7 billion) will be affected, with the timber, machinery, furniture, and footwear sectors hit hardest.

USTR Jamieson Greer signaled that this Section 301 strategy could serve as a template for action against India, China, the European Union, Japan, and South Korea, raising concerns about a broader global trade war. A separate USTR forced-labor investigation due to conclude on July 24 could add an additional 12.5% tariff on certain Brazilian products.

4. Yen Plunges Past 163, Hitting 39.5-Year Low

The Japanese yen weakened past the psychologically significant 163 level against the U.S. dollar in New York trading, reaching 163.23 — its lowest level since December 1986, which marks 39.5 years. The dollar index rose 0.226 to 101.188.

Three factors are driving the yen's relentless depreciation: First, "crisis dollar buying" has accelerated as Middle East geopolitical risks drive capital into the dollar as a safe haven. Second, surging oil prices are expected to widen Japan's trade deficit, given the country's heavy reliance on energy imports, adding structural downward pressure on the yen. Third, rising oil prices have fueled speculation that the Federal Reserve may need to hike rates again this year, widening the US-Japan interest rate differential.

Finance Minister Katayama has warned that the government stands ready to take "decisive action," but verbal intervention has had limited effect so far. The Bank of Japan raised its policy rate to 1% in June, but with the Fed funds rate at 3.50%-3.75%, the interest rate gap remains wide, sustaining carry trade-driven yen selling. Some analysts project the yen could weaken further to 165 or even 170.

5. China A-Shares Hit New 2026 Highs, Turnover Reaches 1.89 Trillion Yuan

China's A-share markets rose on Wednesday (July 22) with all three major indices hitting new highs for 2026. The Shanghai Composite gained 0.62% to close at 3,581.86, the Shenzhen Component rose 0.84% to 11,099.83, and the ChiNext Index added 0.61% to 2,310.86. Combined turnover on the Shanghai and Shenzhen exchanges reached 1.893 trillion yuan ($261 billion), surging 193.1 billion yuan from the previous session.

The Yarlung Tsangpo River hydropower station concept continued its rally for a second consecutive day, with over 20 stocks hitting the daily limit. New energy and solid-state battery themes also gained. Banking stocks, however, declined across the board as profit-taking set in after recent gains. Despite the indices' rise, declining stocks outnumbered advancers by approximately 2,700 to 2,300.

Analysts noted that the Shanghai Composite's firm footing above 3,500 points has reinforced bullish sentiment. China Galaxy Securities stated that the inflow of long-term institutional capital, particularly from insurance funds, provides solid support for A-share market liquidity.

6. UK CPI Beats Expectations, Indonesia Cuts Rates

The UK's Office for National Statistics reported Wednesday that June CPI rose 2.8% year-over-year, above the 2.7% consensus estimate. Core CPI came in at 2.6%, also above the 2.5% forecast. Producer Price Index (PPI) input costs surged 8.7% annually, signaling persistent upstream inflation pressure. The data dampened expectations for near-term Bank of England rate cuts.

Separately, Bank Indonesia surprised markets by cutting its benchmark interest rate by 25 basis points to 5.75%, with the deposit facility and lending facility rates adjusted to 4.75% and 6.50% respectively. BI cited the need to support economic growth while keeping inflation under control.
