⚡ July 17 Financial Midday: Trump China Allegations Rock Markets, Hang Seng Loses 25000, Nikkei Plunges 3.9%

1. Trump Declassifies China Election Intel, Markets on Edge

President Donald Trump delivered a primetime address from the White House East Room on Thursday evening, declassifying intelligence documents he claims show China "carried out the largest compromise of election data in history" by illicitly acquiring 220 million U.S. voter records during the 2020 election cycle. Trump ordered the Director of National Intelligence and FBI to launch a full investigation and called on Congress to pass the SAVE America Act for nationwide voter ID requirements.

China's embassy in Washington firmly denied the allegations, stating that "China has never and will never interfere in U.S. presidential elections." Notably, several of the documents Trump himself declassified appeared to undercut his claims — one stated that vote tabulation systems "would be difficult to manipulate on a wide enough scale to alter the election outcome." Democratic Senator Mark Warner, vice chair of the Senate Intelligence Committee, dismissed the allegations as "totally bogus."

Markets reacted negatively. S&P 500 futures dropped 0.96% following the speech. Australia's ASX 200 fell 0.84%, and the Australian-listed IVV ETF tracking the S&P 500 opened sharply lower. Analysts warned the allegations risk complicating the planned Trump-Xi summit in September, which markets had been counting on to consolidate the U.S.-China trade truce.

2. Hong Kong Stocks Sink at Midday: Hang Seng Below 25000, Tech Index Down 4%

Hong Kong stocks reversed Thursday's gains on Friday, with all three major indices falling sharply. At midday close, the Hang Seng Index fell 1.98% to 24,514.29, losing the key 25,000 level. The Hang Seng Tech Index plunged 4.04%, while the State-owned Enterprises Index dropped 2.42%.

Tech names led the declines: Meituan fell over 6%, Kuaishou and Bilibili each dropped over 5%. PCB concept stocks tumbled with Kingboard Laminates down over 9%. Chip stocks were among the worst performers, with GigaDevice falling over 8%. On Thursday, the Hang Seng had rallied 1.33% to close above 25,000, but the overnight U.S. tech selloff and fresh U.S.-China tensions reversed sentiment entirely.

3. Japan's Nikkei Crashes 3.9% as Chip Rout Deepens

Japan's stock market suffered severe losses on Friday, with the Nikkei 225 plunging 3.9% to 66,835 points — the second straight day of heavy selling. Chip stocks bore the brunt: Kioxia Holdings collapsed 16%, losing more than half its value since hitting a record high in June and entering technical bear territory. SoftBank Group tumbled 9.2%, Tokyo Electron lost 9%, and Advantest slid 9.4%.

South Korean markets were closed for a public holiday, following Thursday's 6.37% KOSPI crash. Taiwan's Taiex fell 4%, with TSMC down 3.64% despite reporting a record 77% Q2 net profit surge and announcing an additional $100 billion Arizona investment. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index has now corrected over 22% from its June peak, officially entering a bear market.

The MSCI Asia Pacific Index fell 0.8%, with the semiconductor sub-index on track for its worst weekly drop since early March.

4. China A-Shares Open Lower, Chip Stocks Under Pressure

China's A-share markets opened lower across the board on Friday. At midday, the Shanghai Composite was down 0.44% near 3,882, the Shenzhen Component fell 0.97%, and the ChiNext dropped 1.36%. Sectors were broadly in the red, with memory chip concepts, MLCC, gold, and semiconductor stocks leading the declines.

In memory chips, SanDisk continued its slide after U.S. memory giants Western Digital and Seagate each fell over 9% overnight. CXMT (ChangXin Memory Technologies) announced its final online subscription lottery rate at 0.47141739%, with payment due on July 20. Some market participants expressed concern over capital diversion from the STAR Board's largest-ever IPO (raising up to 66.6 billion yuan), though seasoned analysts noted ample market liquidity should limit the impact.

5. Middle East Tensions Escalate, Oil Rebounds Above $85

Oil prices rose on Friday as geopolitical tensions in the Middle East continued to escalate. WTI crude for August delivery gained 1.32% to $80.09 per barrel, while Brent crude for September advanced 1.33% to $85.35 per barrel. Brent is up approximately 12% this week, the largest weekly gain since April.

Iran's top military command posted a statement on Telegram warning that if Trump follows through on threats to strike Iran's critical infrastructure, "everything that is still intact — that is, all the infrastructure in the region — will be crushed." Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps has also threatened to expand the conflict by targeting additional energy supply routes, while Yemen's Houthis have been instructed to prepare to close the Bab el-Mandeb Strait.

Rystad Energy senior vice president Jorge León noted Friday that while a limited agreement remains the base case scenario, confidence has eroded significantly, with the probability of "no substantive agreement" rising to 55%. Shipping traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has dwindled to a trickle, posing a growing threat to global energy supplies.

6. Netflix Tumbles 9% After Hours on Growth Concerns

Netflix reported Q2 results after Thursday's close: revenue of $12.56 billion (+13.4% YoY), slightly below the $12.59 billion consensus, and EPS of $0.80, a penny above the $0.79 estimate. However, Q3 revenue guidance of $12.86 billion (implying 11.7% growth, down from 13.4%) disappointed investors. The company also announced it would reduce its "What We Watched" viewership report from twice-yearly to annual starting in 2027, shifting focus to financial metrics.

Netflix shares plunged over 9% in after-hours trading. The stock has now fallen approximately 40% from its mid-2025 all-time high, as the market re-prices the streaming giant from a high-growth story to a more mature business facing slowing subscriber growth and intensifying competition.
