⚡ July 22 Tech Mega-Roundup: Samsung Galaxy Unpacked Goes Live, Google Play Opens to Rivals, Anthropic Files for IPO

1. Samsung Galaxy Unpacked 2026 Kicks Off in London: Three Foldables Lead the Charge

Samsung Electronics is holding its annual Galaxy Unpacked 2026 event in London today (July 22), its biggest hardware launch of the year. The company is expected to unveil three foldable phones — the Galaxy Z Fold 8, Galaxy Z Fold 8 Ultra, and Galaxy Z Flip 8 — alongside the stable release of One UI 9 based on Android 17, two new smartwatches (Galaxy Watch 9 and Watch Ultra 2), and possibly its first smart glasses, the Galaxy Glasses.

Galaxy Z Fold 8: Shorter, Wider, Passport-Style Design

The Galaxy Z Fold 8 abandons the tall, narrow form factor for a shorter, wider "passport-style" design. It features a 5.5-inch cover display (16:9) that opens to a 7.6-inch inner display (4:3 aspect ratio), making video viewing and reading more comfortable. Powered by the Snapdragon 8 Elite Gen 5 for Galaxy, it offers up to 16GB of RAM and 1TB of storage, a 4,800mAh battery with 45W charging, and weighs just 201g at 4.5mm thickness when unfolded. Trade-offs include the removal of the telephoto lens and S Pen support. US pricing starts at $1,899 for the 256GB model.

Galaxy Z Fold 8 Ultra: True Flagship Successor

The Z Fold 8 Ultra is the real successor to last year's Fold 7. It upgrades the inner display to 2504×2256 resolution, the ultra-wide camera from 12MP to 50MP, and the battery from 4,400mAh to 5,000mAh. It retains the 200MP main sensor and 10MP 3x telephoto. At just 4.1mm unfolded and 218g, it starts at $2,099 — a $100 increase over the Fold 7.

Z Flip 8 and Flex Titanium: The Crease Nearly Disappears

The Galaxy Z Flip 8 gets Samsung's first 2nm chip — the Exynos 2600 — offering roughly 28% better performance than its predecessor. It retains the 6.9-inch 120Hz main display and 4.1-inch cover screen.

Samsung also introduced Flex Titanium, a new foldable display structure combining an ultra-thin titanium-alloy film (20× stiffer than the old polymer layer, thinner than a human hair) with a micro-perforated titanium plate. The result is a nearly invisible crease — described by tipsters as "the biggest foldable display breakthrough since Ultra Thin Glass."

One UI 9 and Galaxy Glasses

One UI 9 (based on Android 17) will ship pre-installed on the Z Fold 8 series, with Gemini Intelligence — Google's agentic AI layer that chains multiple actions into single commands. Samsung may also preview the Galaxy Glasses, an audio-first, display-free design running Android XR with Gemini AI, a camera and speakers, priced at $379-$499 to compete with Meta's Ray-Ban smart glasses.

The Galaxy Watch 9 and Watch Ultra 2 will be the first Samsung wearables powered by Qualcomm's Snapdragon Wear Elite (SW6100) 3nm chip, boasting over 50% CPU and GPU improvement. The Watch Ultra 2 features a 590mAh battery, titanium case, 100m water resistance, and a 5,000-nit display.

2. Google Play Opens to Third-Party App Stores: Android's Biggest Distribution Shift

Starting July 22, 2026, Google Play has launched its Play Catalog Access Program, allowing third-party Android app stores in the US to be listed directly within Google Play. This landmark change stems from Epic Games' antitrust victory against Google.

The Six-Year Legal Battle

Epic sued Google and Apple in 2020 over monopolistic app store practices. In 2023, a jury ruled Google's Play Store an illegal monopoly. Judge James Donato issued a permanent injunction in October 2024 requiring Google to host rival stores and share its app catalog. Google and Epic reached a settlement in late 2025 to soften the requirements, but the court signaled disapproval. On July 14, 2026, both sides withdrew the settlement, reinstating the original injunction. Google confirmed July 22 as the launch date.

Terms, Fees, and Conditions

Third-party stores must pay a $5,000 annual fee for security and compliance review, keep malware below 1% of install attempts, operate only in the US, accept all eligible developers, and provide app update and uninstall mechanisms. Google still processes all downloads through its infrastructure and collects its standard service fees. Apps can be shared by default unless developers opt out via Play Console.

What This Means for Users and Developers

Amazon Appstore, Samsung Galaxy Store, and Epic's store are expected to be early enrollees. For the first time, Android users in the US can discover and install rival app stores as easily as any other app on Google Play. While Google retains control over the download pipeline and fee collection, rival stores gain front-door visibility in the channel that handles 95% of US Android app downloads — a seismic shift in the Android ecosystem's competitive landscape.

3. Anthropic Confidentially Files for IPO, Targeting $1 Trillion Valuation

In the early hours of July 22, Anthropic announced it has confidentially submitted a draft Form S-1 registration statement to the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), taking a major step toward going public. The move puts Anthropic ahead of OpenAI in the race to IPO.

Valuation and Financials

Just last week, Anthropic closed a $65 billion Series H round at a $965 billion post-money valuation — surpassing OpenAI's most recent $730 billion valuation. From $61.5 billion in March 2025 to nearly $1 trillion today, the company's valuation has surged roughly 15× in 14 months. Its annualized revenue run rate has reached $47 billion, driven mainly by Claude Code developer tools and the Claude model family.

The Three-Way AI IPO Race

Anthropic's S-1 filing intensifies the AI IPO competition: SpaceX (which absorbed xAI) is reportedly planning a public offering this month at a $1.75 trillion valuation, while OpenAI is expected to file within weeks. Together, these could become the largest IPOs in history, potentially making Elon Musk — who holds roughly 50% of SpaceX — the world's first trillionaire.

Challenges Ahead

Anthropic faces intense competition from OpenAI (now pivoting to its Codex coding tool) and Google (which just released three Gemini models and started Gemini 4 pretraining). The company spends roughly $1.25 billion per month on GPU compute from SpaceX, and faces a $1.5 billion copyright settlement over training data. Its IPO will force full financial transparency on a company that has commanded some of the richest private valuations in tech history — putting the profitability of frontier AI models under unprecedented market scrutiny.
