⚡ July 22 Tech Roundup: Jack Dorsey Launches Buzz AI-Native Platform Challenging Slack; Sila Raises $300M for Next-Gen Battery Factory; IEA Warns Data Center Power Demand to Quadruple by 2035

I. Jack Dorsey Launches Buzz: AI-Native Team Collaboration Platform Takes on Slack

On July 22, 2026, Twitter (now X) co-founder and former CEO Jack Dorsey officially unveiled Buzz, a new AI-native team collaboration platform that directly challenges Slack and Microsoft Teams. Buzz's core philosophy is a "group chat platform built for the AI Agent era" — it not only enables efficient communication among human team members but embeds AI agents as first-class citizens into every conversation and workflow.

What Makes Buzz Different

Unlike existing collaboration tools that bolt AI on as an add-on feature, Buzz is architected from the ground up with AI agents as native participants:

  • Agent-native integration: Every channel and workspace can directly invoke multiple AI agents that autonomously execute tasks, retrieve information, generate content, and collaborate in real-time within conversations
  • Multi-agent orchestration: Supports multiple different AI agents working in parallel within the same workspace, with agents able to communicate and hand off tasks between each other
  • Intelligent workflow automation: Create automated workflows through natural language, with AI agents automatically understanding intent and orchestrating execution steps
  • Cross-platform compatibility: Buzz offers open APIs and integration frameworks supporting major AI models (Claude, GPT, Gemini, etc.) and third-party SaaS tools

Dorsey stated in the launch announcement: "For the past decade, we've been using tools designed for humans to get work done. Buzz is designed for humans and AI to work together. When AI agents can actively participate in discussions and execute tasks like team members, the productivity ceiling gets redefined."

Buzz launches as the enterprise collaboration market undergoes AI agent-driven structural transformation. Slack has added AI features (Slack AI), and Microsoft Teams has deeply integrated Copilot — but Buzz is the first collaboration platform to design AI agents as first-class citizens rather than add-on features. Analysts suggest that if Buzz can successfully establish the "AI-native collaboration" user mindshare, it could carve out a new category in the increasingly crowded enterprise collaboration market.

"Buzz isn't layering AI features on top of Slack — it's rebuilding collaboration on top of AI. This is innovation from the foundation up, not adding a new roof to the old house." — Tech industry analyst

Market Response

Buzz is currently in limited beta, open to teams and enterprises for registration. Pricing details haven't been fully disclosed, but Dorsey indicated it will use a hybrid per-seat plus per-agent-call model. Salesforce (Slack's parent company) and Microsoft have not yet formally responded, but industry observers expect a new wave of AI collaboration tool competition has just begun.

II. Next-Gen Battery Breakthrough: Sila Raises $300M to Expand Washington State Factory

On the same day, next-generation battery materials company Sila Nanotechnologies announced a $300 million funding round to expand its battery materials manufacturing facility in Moses Lake, Washington. The round included both new and existing investors, including Sutter Hill Ventures and 8VC, marking Sila's transition from technology validation to scaled production.

Sila's Technology

Sila's core technology replaces traditional graphite anodes in lithium-ion batteries with silicon-based composite materials. Compared to traditional graphite anodes, Sila's silicon-based anodes can store significantly more lithium ions, increasing battery energy density by 20-40% while maintaining cycle life and safety. This technological pathway is considered one of the most promising evolutionary directions for next-generation lithium-ion batteries.

Unlike traditional silicon anode materials that experience up to 300% volume expansion during charging, Sila's proprietary nanocomposite structure keeps expansion within acceptable ranges, solving the core technical bottleneck for silicon anode commercialization. The company says its materials have passed rigorous validation from multiple top-tier automotive manufacturers and consumer electronics brands, and have begun small-volume shipments.

Capacity Expansion Plans

The funding will primarily be used for:

  • Ramping production capacity at the Moses Lake facility to hundreds of tons of silicon anode materials annually
  • Building supporting quality inspection and R&D laboratories
  • Hiring engineering and production teams to support scaled operations

Sila CEO Gene Berdichevsky (Tesla employee #7) commented: "The bottleneck for EV adoption isn't building cars — it's building batteries. Without better battery materials, there won't be better electric vehicles. Sila's goal is to become the world's leading next-generation battery materials supplier, making higher-energy-density, lower-cost batteries a reality."

Sila's Washington factory benefits from proximity to low-cost hydropower (the region has some of the cheapest industrial electricity in the US), a critical advantage for energy-intensive battery materials production. The company expects to achieve large-scale commercial deliveries by 2027, with initial customers spanning both EV and consumer electronics sectors.

III. Data Center Energy Crisis: IEA Warns Global Electricity Consumption to Quadruple by 2035

A major new report from the International Energy Agency (IEA) issued a stark warning today: driven by explosive growth in AI compute demand, global data center electricity consumption is projected to surge from approximately 415 TWh in 2025 to over 1,600 TWh by 2035 — equivalent to the combined annual electricity consumption of France, Germany, and the United Kingdom. This trajectory far exceeds any previous forecast model, signaling the AI industry's transition from a "compute race" to a "power race."

Growth Drivers

The report identifies three main factors behind the surge:

  • Exponential growth in AI inference workloads: As AI applications expand from search and chat to multimodal generation, real-time agents, autonomous driving, and industrial automation, per-inference compute requirements jump from millions to billions of tokens. The IEA estimates that AI inference will account for over 70% of total data center compute loads by 2030
  • Soaring per-rack power density: AI training cluster power density has surged from traditional 5-10 kW/rack to 40-120 kW/rack, with NVIDIA's latest DGX series peaking above 140 kW per rack, posing unprecedented challenges to data center cooling and power infrastructure
  • Accelerated global data center expansion: Major cloud providers (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) and AI-specific compute providers (CoreWeave, Lambda, Nscale) are accelerating global deployments, with total data center power under construction expected to exceed 60 GW between 2025-2027

Energy Structure Challenges

Quadrupling data center electricity consumption within a decade would require adding generation capacity equivalent to hundreds of large nuclear plants or thousands of large solar farms. The IEA warns that if new capacity relies on fossil fuels, the data center carbon footprint will increase dramatically, conflicting with global climate goals.

Major tech companies have pledged 100% renewable energy usage, but implementation faces serious challenges — the intermittency of wind and solar creates significant uncertainty for 24/7 data center operations. The report recommends that tech companies increase investment in small modular nuclear reactors (SMRs), geothermal power, and long-duration energy storage to ensure sustainable AI growth.

"We are witnessing one of the most rapid structural changes in energy demand in human history. AI isn't just changing computing — it's reshaping the global power system. Without action, data centers could become the single biggest obstacle to the global energy transition." — IEA Energy Analyst

Industry Response

The industry is pursuing multiple approaches: chipmakers like AMD and NVIDIA continue to improve performance-per-watt through architectural innovation; liquid cooling has transitioned from optional to mandatory, with direct-to-chip and immersion cooling rapidly proliferating. Microsoft, Google, and Amazon have begun incorporating nuclear energy into their data center plans — Microsoft signed a long-term power purchase agreement for the Three Mile Island nuclear plant, and Google has invested in multiple SMR startups.

The IEA notes that the key question for the next decade isn't "whether AI will continue to develop" — it's "where AI's energy will come from." If energy supply can't keep pace with compute demand growth, the AI industry could face a substantive growth ceiling.
