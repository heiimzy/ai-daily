⚡ July 23 International Politics: US House Passes $95B Iran War Budget 216-214; Houthis Strike Saudi Tanker Encelia in Red Sea; IRGC Mines Hormuz, Strikes Kuwait Base

1. US House Narrowly Passes $95B Iran War Budget in Party-Line Vote

The US House of Representatives on Wednesday (July 22) adopted a Republican-only $95 billion budget resolution by a razor-thin 216-214 vote, funding the Iran war and other White House priorities. All Democrats opposed, joined by two Republicans (Thomas Massie and Warren Davidson) and independent Kevin Kiley.

The measure leverages the budget reconciliation process (Reconciliation 3.0) to bypass the Senate's 60-vote filibuster threshold, requiring only a simple majority. The breakdown:

  • $60 billion — Pentagon supplemental military funding (direct Iran war costs)
  • $13 billion — Other national security needs (intelligence, diplomacy)
  • $12 billion — Farmer aid for tariff-affected agriculture
  • $10 billion — Enforcing Trump's SAVE America Act voting restrictions (proof of citizenship to vote)

Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth testified this week that the war has already cost $37.5 billion and the Pentagon is running short of cash, despite a $150 billion one-time allocation in last year's omnibus. Speaker Mike Johnson pushed the package through over internal GOP divisions, with conservatives alarmed at the lack of offsetting spending cuts. The resolution now heads to the Senate, where Majority Leader John Thune has been noncommittal and its prospects appear uncertain.

"Republicans plan to spend tens of billions of dollars on Trump's failed war in Iran," said Rep. Pete Aguilar (D-CA), chairman of the House Democratic caucus. "Think about what we could spend with these resources instead."

A recent AP-NORC poll shows a majority of Americans disapprove of Trump's Iran strategy. The partisan battle over war authorization and funding continues to intensify ahead of November's midterm elections.

2. Houthis Strike Saudi Oil Tankers in Red Sea — Encelia on Fire, Second Maritime Choke Point Threatened

Yemen's Iran-backed Houthi militia announced Thursday that it had carried out military operations against two Saudi oil tankers — the first confirmed attacks since declaring a maritime blockade on Saudi Arabia on July 20. Houthi military spokesman Yahya Saree identified the vessels as "ENCELIA" and "LAYLA," stating they were targeted with ballistic missiles, cruise missiles, and drones for violating the Red Sea shipping ban.

Saudi Arabia's state news agency SPA, citing the Transport General Authority, confirmed the Encelia was struck by an unknown projectile causing a fire on the bow, with all crew members safe. The UK Maritime Trade Operations (UKMTO) reported the vessel was hit approximately 70 nautical miles southwest of Al Shuqaiq, Saudi Arabia, and the crew was fighting the blaze. British maritime risk firm Vanguard confirmed the Saudi-flagged tanker was struck on its starboard side.

The Houthis claimed they also forced approximately 10 ships to retreat. Ship-tracking data shows at least five tankers have changed course in the Red Sea, with two rerouting toward the Suez Canal — a sign that Saudi Arabia's alternative oil export route is now under direct threat.

The situation has created a "dual strait crisis" unprecedented in recent decades:

  • Strait of Hormuz — ~20% of global oil and gas trade, effectively closed under Iranian control
  • Bab el-Mandeb / Red Sea — ~12% of global trade, Saudi's primary alternative export channel, now under Houthi blockade

Analysts warn that with two critical energy arteries simultaneously threatened, shipping insurance rates are soaring and global supply chains face severe disruption.

3. IRGC Claims Hormuz Mined: Tanker Catches Fire, Strait "Completely Closed"

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) issued Statement No. 44 on Thursday claiming that three oil tankers, "provoked and enticed by the aggressive U.S. military," attempted to navigate a mined route in the southern Strait of Hormuz. The IRGC said one tanker suffered "a severe explosion and fire," while the other two immediately turned back.

"The powerful IRGC Navy emphasizes that the Strait of Hormuz is under our control and remains fully closed as long as U.S. aggression in the region continues. No oil tanker will be permitted to enter or exit, and any vessel deceived by the U.S. that attempts to cross without prior coordination with the Islamic Republic of Iran will suffer the same fate."

The IRGC also announced it had launched "punitive operations" against US bases in Kuwait, striking the Ali al-Salem Air Base — reportedly destroying a US military equipment warehouse and Patriot missile system. Kuwaiti military sources confirmed they had intercepted some Iranian drones.

US Central Command (CENTCOM) swiftly rebutted Iran's assertions, stating: "Iran does not control the Strait of Hormuz. The international waterway remains open for transit regardless of IRGC threats and attacks. Since early May, American forces have helped more than 900 ships transit the strait." Independent tracking data, however, shows only 11 vessels transited in the past 24 hours — a 90%+ decline from pre-war levels of 100-150 ships daily.

4. Brent Crude Surges Past $95 on Dual Blockade Fears

International oil prices extended gains Thursday, with Brent crude rising more than 2% to break above $95/barrel — a seven-week high. WTI crude settled Wednesday at $86.83/barrel (+2.95%), while Brent closed at $94.07. The average US gasoline price has climbed to $4.06/gallon, up sharply from under $3 before the war began.

The World Bank has warned that if the US-Iran conflict persists, global economic growth could fall to 1.3%. Surging energy costs are reigniting inflationary pressures: the 10-year US Treasury yield rose to 4.65%, and mortgage rates hit their highest level in nearly a year. The IMF has flagged the risk of a "wartime inflation spiral" for import-dependent economies.

5. Trump Attends Dover Dignified Transfer as US Death Toll Reaches 18

President Trump traveled to Dover Air Force Base, Delaware, on Wednesday evening for the dignified transfer of four US service members killed in Iranian attacks. The ceremony brought the total US military death toll since the war began on February 28 to 18. Defense Secretary Hegseth and Chairman of the Joint Chiefs Gen. Dan Caine accompanied the president.

"All I'm going to say is, 'we love you. We love your child,'" Trump told reporters before departing Joint Base Andrews. He later reiterated on Truth Social that the war is "far from over." Iran's Health Ministry reports 53 civilians killed and 590 wounded by US strikes this month alone. Iran also held burial ceremonies for 32 victims of a February school bombing — an attack that killed 168 students and teachers and remains under investigation by both sides.

With midterm elections approaching, public disapproval of the Iran war is rising, and the political battle over war powers and funding is intensifying across Washington.
