⚡ July 22 Financial Midday Roundup: Brent Breaks $92 to 6-Week High, Trump Unveils 200% Generic Drug Tariff, KOSPI Surges 5.5% to Reclaim 7,000

1. Oil Surges Past $92: Houthi Red Sea Blockade Forces Saudi Tanker Reversals

Oil prices continued their relentless climb in Asian trading Wednesday, with Brent crude breaking above $92 per barrel to hit $92.23 — the highest level in six weeks. WTI crude followed, rising to around $85.47/bbl. Brent has now remained in technically overbought territory for seven consecutive sessions, the longest stretch since June 2025.

The immediate catalyst was the Houthi-declared naval blockade on Saudi Arabia threatening Red Sea shipping lanes. According to Reuters citing LSEG shipping data, two tankers loaded with Saudi crude bound for China and India reversed course in the Red Sea and headed back toward the Suez Canal. Analysts at consulting firm Gelber & Associates noted that while the rally isn't necessarily about lost barrels today, the market is assigning a higher probability that logistics remain unstable through the week.

The Houthi group warned that vessels engaged in loading or unloading cargo at Saudi ports could be targeted "in any location." This threatens Saudi Arabia's Red Sea export hub at Yanbu, through which the kingdom shipped a record 5.9 million barrels per day during the week ending July 17 — a critical alternative route as Kuwaiti tankers also came under attack in the Strait of Hormuz.

"The rally is not necessarily about lost barrels today, but rather the market assigning a higher probability that logistics remain unstable through the week, especially if Saudi exports to Asia or Red Sea transit face additional disruption." — Gelber & Associates

Beyond the Red Sea, the Caspian Pipeline Consortium (CPC) stopped receiving oil from Kazakhstan after suspending loadings at its Black Sea terminal due to attacks on oil tankers, threatening further supply disruptions. Two Indian state-owned refiners have also canceled crude loading plans from Iraq's Basra port over Strait of Hormuz safety concerns.

2. Trump Announces Phased Generic Drug Tariff: 0% → 100% → 200%

President Donald Trump announced a phased tariff regime on imported generic medicines via Truth Social on Tuesday. Starting August 1, 2026, generic drugs will enter the U.S. duty-free for two years, after which the tariff rises to 100% for one year, then to 200% permanently. The stated goal is to "reshore generic pharmaceutical production into America."

India's Nifty Pharma index fell approximately 1.6% in early trading, making it the worst-performing sector. Cipla and Lupin declined about 2.5% each, Sun Pharma fell 2%, and Dr. Reddy's slipped over 1%. India supplies roughly 40-50% of generic medicines consumed in the U.S., with annual exports valued at $9-10 billion.

Industry analysts expressed skepticism about the two-year timeline. Building a compliant manufacturing facility takes at least 2-3 years, and India retains a 40-60% manufacturing cost advantage that tariffs alone may not fully erase. "To create an ecosystem for generics within two years seems a distant possibility," one industry source commented.

3. KOSPI Soars 5.56% Reclaiming 7,000: Buy Sidecar Triggered, Samsung & SK Hynix Lead Rally

South Korea's KOSPI index surged 5.56% to 7,123.43 points in Wednesday trading, reclaiming the 7,000 mark for the first time in four sessions. The KOSPI 200 jumped 5.91% to 1,136.78, while the tech-heavy KOSDAQ rose 3.29% to 778.13. The rally triggered a buy-sidecar at 9:06 AM KST, temporarily halting program buy orders for five minutes.

Foreign investors led the charge with net purchases of 601.8 billion won. Market heavyweights posted strong gains: Samsung Electronics rallied 5.79%, SK Hynix surged 9.15%, Hyundai Motor climbed 8.27%, and NAVER gained 3.21%. The rally was fueled by exceptional export data — South Korea's exports surged 52.3% in the first 20 days of July, with semiconductor exports skyrocketing 180% year-on-year.

4. Yen Crashes Past 163 to 40-Year Low: Finance Minister Warns of Intervention

The Japanese yen extended its historic decline Wednesday, with USD/JPY breaking through the 163 barrier to hit 163.24 — the weakest level since December 1986 (39.5 years). The yen has been the worst performer among G10 currencies, depreciating 0.6% against the dollar in just the past two weeks of the U.S.-Iran conflict.

Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama stated Wednesday that the government will "respond appropriately and decisively whenever necessary," hinting at possible intervention. However, markets showed little reaction. The Nikkei reported that Japan's shift in policy language from "fiscal consolidation" to "fiscal sustainability" in the Honebuto Policy has been interpreted by markets as tacit acceptance of yen weakness.

Multiple factors are driving the yen's slide: crisis-driven dollar buying amid Middle East tensions, surging oil prices inflating Japan's energy import costs, the persistent U.S.-Japan rate gap (Fed at 3.50-3.75% vs BOJ at 1%), and waning confidence in intervention effectiveness. Analysts now eye 165 as the next critical threshold, where Tokyo may be forced to act before the BOJ's September meeting.

5. Fed Rate Hike Odds Jump: July Probability Rises to 26.2%

Surging oil prices have reignited inflation fears, driving up expectations for Federal Reserve action. U.S. short-term interest rate futures now price a 26.2% probability of a July rate hike, up sharply from 16% just last week. The 10-year U.S. Treasury yield rose 4 basis points to 4.63%, while the dollar index held near 101.2.

With Q2 earnings season delivering an 88% beat rate so far, market focus is shifting to heavyweight reports from Alphabet and Tesla due tonight. AI capital expenditure efficiency will be the key theme as investors assess whether massive infrastructure spending is translating to returns.

6. Asian Markets Mixed: Shanghai +0.5%, Hang Seng -0.83% on Tencent 5% Drop

Chinese A-shares showed divergence at Wednesday's midday close. The Shanghai Composite rose 0.50% to 3,883.58, while the Shenzhen Component gained 0.61% to 14,351.91. However, the ChiNext fell 0.13% to 3,681.20. Precious metals led gains with Shanjin International hitting the daily limit, while semiconductor equipment stocks strengthened. Total half-day turnover reached 1.78 trillion yuan.

Hong Kong's Hang Seng Index fell 0.83% to 24,923 at midday, with the Hang Seng Tech Index dropping 2.11% to 4,713. Tencent tumbled 5%, becoming the biggest drag on the market. Geopolitical uncertainty, oil-driven inflation concerns, and tech valuation correction risks weighed on sentiment.

7. Wall Street's Strong Rebound: Chip Stocks Lead, SanDisk Surges 14%

U.S. stocks staged a powerful rebound overnight. The Dow Jones rose 385.38 points (+0.74%) to 52,224.64, the S&P 500 gained 0.89% to 7,509.20, and the Nasdaq surged 1.29% to 25,837.21. Chip stocks led the charge: the VanEck Semiconductor ETF (SMH) soared 4.52%, SanDisk skyrocketed 14.27%, Micron Technology surged 12.17%, and AMD jumped 8.11%.

COMEX gold futures advanced 1.69% to $4,083.70/oz, nearing the $4,100 threshold, while silver surged 4.23% to $59.065/oz. Energy and precious metals sectors were buoyed by geopolitical risk premiums. Q2 earnings delivered an 88% beat rate so far, with 3M rising 7.32% and General Motors gaining 4.91% on strong results.
