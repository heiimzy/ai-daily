⚡ US-Iran War Night 8: Fighter Jets Strike 650 km Inland in Yazd, Tehran Threatens "Full-Scale Offensive" With "No Political Border Safe"

✈️ Night 8 Escalation: Fighter Jets Reach Central Iran for the First Time

The United States Central Command (CENTCOM) announced a new wave of airstrikes against Iran at 6:00 PM ET on Saturday, July 18, marking the eighth consecutive night of the renewed air campaign. While the first seven nights relied predominantly on standoff weapons — cruise missiles, drones, and sea drones — Night 8 brought a qualitative shift: US fighter jets struck targets near Yazd in central Iran, approximately 650 kilometers inland from the Strait of Hormuz coast.

According to Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty (RFE/RL), "US strikes on Iran widened early on July 18 from the Strait of Hormuz hotspot to multiple sites throughout central and southern Iran, including through the use of jet fighters." The distinction is critical — the first seven nights had relied on standoff munitions launched from beyond the engagement envelope of Iran's air defenses. Fighter jet operations over central Iran require sustained aerial refueling corridors, suppression of enemy air defenses, and a willingness to risk airframes and pilots that standoff weapons do not.

Iran's semi-official Mehr News Agency reported that the Yazd strikes fell "outside the city limits" with no casualties. However, Iran's Hormozgan province Communications Department reported that US airstrikes had knocked out 116 communication towers across the province. Al Jazeera reported five explosions in Yazd on July 17, marking the most striking geographic departure from the campaign's established pattern.

⚠️ Iran Issues "Full-Scale Offensive" Ultimatum: 2-3 Days to Cease Fire

Major General Mohsen Rezaei, a senior military adviser to Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei and former commander-in-chief of the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), issued the conflict's starkest warning to date: if US strikes continue for another "two or three days," Iran will transition from calibrated retaliation to what he called "full-scale offensive operations" with "no political border safe."

"Iran will no longer limit itself to retaliatory, like-for-like responses… and no political border will be safe." — Major General Mohsen Rezaei, senior military adviser to Iran's Supreme Leader, in an interview with state broadcaster IRIB, July 17

Rezaei explicitly declared that the policy of "both war and negotiation" had ended. "If the Americans continue the war within the next two to three days, the Islamic Republic of Iran will enter an aggressive and destructive phase and will no longer suffice to reciprocity; in this case, American bases and forces will not be safe on any political border," he stated. The 72-hour ultimatum window is set to expire approximately July 19-20. IRGC Aerospace Commander Major Mousavi separately vowed that Iranian missile and drone operations would persist until the US ceases attacks on Iran's southern coastal installations.

📜 Iran Formally Suspends Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding

Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi announced on July 18 that Tehran has suspended all commitments under the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) signed with the United States in June. "The US has violated and suspended all its commitments within the framework of the Islamabad MoU," Gharibabadi told local media. As a result, Tehran has "likewise suspended all our own commitments" and implementation of the agreement and is "busy defending the country."

Later on Saturday, Iran's Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei issued a written statement declaring that repeated US breaches of the interim deal had shown that President Donald Trump's signature was "utterly worthless and devoid of credibility." "Now that the American enemy is seeking to escalate the conflict thereby incurring even heavier costs and further humiliation, it should know that the noble nation of Iran and the Resistance Front have unforgettable lessons in store for it," the statement said.

The ceasefire agreement signed in mid-June lasted less than a month before unraveling. President Trump declared the MoU "over" at the NATO Ankara summit on July 8, after which the US reimposed a naval blockade on Iranian ports and revoked sanctions waivers on Iranian oil exports.

🇸🇦 Conflict Widens: Iran Strikes Saudi Arabia for First Time in 3 Months

In a significant escalation, Iran struck targets in Saudi Arabia on July 18 — the first such attack in over three months. Saudi Arabia's civil defense system issued shelter-in-place alerts for residents in Al-Kharj (east of Riyadh, home to Prince Sultan Air Base hosting US troops) and Yanbu (the Red Sea port that serves as Saudi Arabia's primary oil export terminal). Two sources briefed on the matter confirmed to Reuters that an Iranian missile attack triggered these alerts.

Yanbu is particularly strategic — with the Strait of Hormuz effectively paralyzed, the Red Sea route has become Saudi Arabia's critical alternative for crude oil exports. Striking Yanbu sends a powerful signal that Iran intends to sever all of Saudi Arabia's energy export options.

Furthermore, Iranian leadership signaled to Houthi proxy forces in Yemen to prepare to block the Bab el-Mandeb Strait if US strikes on Iran continue. The Bab el-Mandeb connects the Red Sea to the Gulf of Aden and handles approximately 11% of global maritime trade. A simultaneous blockade of both the Strait of Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb would be catastrophic for global energy supplies and shipping.

🇰🇼 Kuwait Devastated for Second Day: Water, Power and Oil Infrastructure Hit

Kuwait has emerged as one of the hardest-hit Gulf states in Iran's retaliation campaign. For the second consecutive day, Iranian strikes targeted Kuwaiti power and water desalination plants and oil facilities. Kuwait's Ministry of Electricity, Water and Renewable Energy confirmed that a power generation and water desalination plant was hit, causing a fire and suspending some power generation equipment. The IRGC also claimed strikes on the US military support center at Camp Arifjan and destroyed a radar facility at Ali Al Salem Air Base.

Kuwait Petroleum Corporation confirmed "significant material losses and injuries" at one of its oil facilities from "repeated Iranian attacks." Kuwait International Airport suspended flight operations due to security concerns. Resident Hassan Rayan, 61, told media: "The demand for water and canned goods has increased since this morning amid fears that services or supply chains will be affected."

Iran additionally struck Bahrain's Sheikh Isa Air Base (where US combat aircraft are stationed) and an intelligence data center, as well as the US base at Al-Azraq in Jordan. Iranian state TV claimed the attack destroyed at least two US fighter aircraft and three other aircraft. The Jordanian military confirmed it had intercepted 10 Iranian ballistic missiles. Bahraini authorities issued two air raid alerts and urged residents to take shelter. Qatar also reported intercepting incoming missiles.

⚖️ Gulf Cooperation Council: Attacks on Civilian Infrastructure Amount to "War Crimes"

GCC Secretary General Jasem Mohamed Albudaiwi issued a strongly worded statement condemning Iranian attacks on Kuwait, Bahrain and Jordan. "Iran's actions constitute a highly dangerous escalation, a grave violation of international law and the United Nations Charter, as well as war crimes requiring international accountability and prosecution, given the deliberate targeting of infrastructure and civilian facilities in flagrant violation of all international norms and conventions," he said.

UN Secretary-General António Guterres expressed deep concern over the escalating conflict, specifically mentioning "attacks on civilian infrastructure in Iran and the region." The UAE Ministry of Foreign Affairs called for "exercising the utmost restraint in order to avoid dangerous repercussions, and the region being pulled into new levels of violence and instability," urging a return to negotiations.

The European Union and Gulf states jointly called on Iran to "immediately and unconditionally halt all attacks and interference with maritime navigation" and to keep the Strait of Hormuz open without conditions.

🛢️ Hormuz Transits Collapse to 8 Ships/Day, Brent Crude Back Above $88

Shipping through the Strait of Hormuz has effectively ground to a halt. According to CNBC citing shipping data, daily transits fell to just 8 vessels on July 17 — a more than 90% decline from the pre-crisis baseline of 88 vessels per day. War-risk insurance premiums have reached 2% of hull value, eight times pre-crisis levels, effectively pricing most commercial operators out of the strait. Oil shipments are being forced onto longer routes around the Cape of Good Hope or through Saudi Arabia's Red Sea pipeline infrastructure.

The IRGC detained four additional tankers on July 18, further constricting flow. Brent crude prices surged back above $88 per barrel. Standard Chartered analysts warned that if the conflict continues to escalate and persist for months, global oil prices could face uncontrolled risks and potentially approach $100 per barrel again.

The US is enforcing a comprehensive naval blockade of Iranian ports through its naval forces. CENTCOM confirmed that more than 50,000 American service members are operating across the Middle East, remaining "vigilant, lethal, and ready." The Pentagon has not ruled out ground assault options — approximately 5,000 Marines and 2,000 paratroopers from the 82nd Airborne Division are positioned in the region awaiting orders.

As of press time, neither side has signaled any intention to cease hostilities. General Rezaei's 72-hour ultimatum window is set to expire this weekend — July 19-20 will determine whether the Middle East slides from a calibrated "tit-for-tat" conflict into an uncontrollable full-scale regional war.
