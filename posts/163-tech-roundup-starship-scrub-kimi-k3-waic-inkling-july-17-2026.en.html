🚀 SpaceX Starship Flight 13: Automatic Abort at T-0 as Engine Ignition Fails

SpaceX's highly anticipated Starship Flight 13 ended in a last-second abort on Thursday evening, with the vehicle's automated flight computer triggering a hold at T-0 as Raptor engines began their ignition sequence. The launch from Starbase, Texas was targeting a 90-minute window opening at 6:45 p.m. EDT (2245 UTC).

"We did trigger a hold on the booster that aborted our liftoff as we were starting to light those Raptor engines," SpaceX livestream host Dan Huot confirmed as coverage ended. The team immediately began offloading liquid methane and liquid oxygen propellants while evaluating next steps. No new launch date has been announced.

Flight 13 marked the second flight of the upgraded Starship Version 3 (V3) and SpaceX's first Starship launch since its record-breaking ~$86 billion IPO in June. The mission was significant for several reasons: it planned to deploy 20 production Starlink V3 satellites for the first time, which would have deployed solar arrays and attempted to establish laser links with the existing Starlink constellation before burning up during reentry approximately 20 minutes after deployment.

Other critical objectives included relighting a Raptor engine in space for the first time and conducting a controlled booster splashdown in the Gulf of Mexico — key milestones toward full reusability. SpaceX President and COO Gwynne Shotwell had told CNBC that success on Flight 13 could pave the way for an orbital attempt as early as Flight 14.

Both Booster 20 and Ship 40 were flying for the first time, and SpaceX had noted hardware modifications to improve engine re-light reliability after issues encountered during Flight 12 in May. Weather conditions were assessed at 90% favorable, and propellant loading had been completed successfully before the abort.

🤖 Kimi K3 Launches: 2.8 Trillion Parameters Makes It the World's Largest Open-Source Model

Moonshot AI quietly launched its flagship Kimi K3 model on the evening of July 16, just ahead of the WAIC 2026 conference in Shanghai. With 2.8 trillion parameters, Kimi K3 becomes the world's largest open-source model by parameter count. The model first appeared on Arena.ai's leaderboard under the anonymous codename "Kivine," scoring close to Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 and quickly drawing industry-wide attention.

According to Xinhua News Agency, Kimi K3 natively supports vision understanding and features a 1-million-token context window. It is optimized for software engineering, knowledge work, deep research, and multimodal understanding tasks. Moonshot AI developed the model using proprietary architecture innovations and a comprehensive scientific training methodology.

Kimi K3's competitive edge lies in three targeted areas: programming — optimized for agentic coding scenarios; gaming/3D — targeting emerging applications from Unity scripting to 3D model generation; and knowledge tasks — building on the Kimi series' long-standing strength in document summarization. Combined with K2.6's ability to dispatch up to 300 sub-agents for parallel task processing, K3's 1M-token context window provides critical support for long-horizon complex agent tasks.

Benchmark evaluations show Kimi K3 approaching the capability level of frontier closed-source models. Industry analysts view K3's launch as marking a strategic shift for Chinese AI labs — from simply catching up to identifying specific domains for competitive advantage.

🌐 WAIC 2026 Opens in Shanghai Today: Over 300 AI Products Make Global Debut

The 2026 World Artificial Intelligence Conference and High-Level Meeting on Global AI Governance (WAIC 2026) officially opens in Shanghai today. The conference has set new records with an exhibition area exceeding 100,000 square meters for the first time, over 1,100 enterprises participating, more than 3,000 exhibits on display, and over 300 products making their global debut.

The conference focuses heavily on "token cost" reduction — a key pain point limiting AI adoption. Major announcements include:

  • Kunlun Tech debuting Matrix-3.5 world model, Mureka v9.5 music AI, and Riemann-1.0 robotics model;
  • Geling Shentong showcasing VE2S visual intelligence workshop with on-site autonomous evolution capabilities;
  • Moore Threads demonstrating the world's first 5D world model "EvoPhys-World," which has topped Stanford's WorldScore leaderboard for 37 consecutive days;
  • Zhonghao Xinying unveiling its new TPU chip "Xuyu" aimed at reducing per-token costs through specialized hardware scheduling;
  • China Telecom debuting a "token security router" for enterprise-level AI cost governance.

Huatai Securities analyst Wang Xing noted that the expanding scale of WAIC reflects the continuous extension of the AI industry chain, with China's AI ecosystem maturing to address long-standing challenges in application deployment and token cost reduction.

💰 Thinking Machines Inkling API Prices Rise 50% Effective Today

Thinking Machines Lab, founded by former OpenAI CTO Mira Murati, has implemented a broad API price increase on its Tinker platform effective July 17. Prefill (input) and sample (output) rates are rising approximately 50%, while training rates increase by about 10% across all models on the platform, including the flagship Inkling model released just two days ago on July 15.

Inkling is a 975-billion-parameter mixture-of-experts (MoE) model with 41 billion active parameters — the largest American open-weights model to date. Released under the Apache 2.0 license, it supports text, image, and audio modalities with up to 1 million tokens of context. The model was trained from scratch on 45 trillion tokens using Nvidia GB300 NVL72 systems. Inkling achieved 97.1% on the AIME 2026 math benchmark, surpassing DeepSeek V4 Pro.

After the price adjustment, Inkling at the 64K context tier costs: $2.80/million input tokens (up from $1.87), $7.02/million output tokens (up from $4.68), and $6.17/million training tokens (up from $5.61). The 50% limited-time launch discount has also been discontinued. Thinking Machines attributed the increase to "rising compute costs."

Industry observers see this as reflecting the broader trend of rising AI infrastructure costs. As trillion-dollar AI computing investment cycles accelerate, pricing pressures are spreading from traditional API providers to emerging model platforms.
