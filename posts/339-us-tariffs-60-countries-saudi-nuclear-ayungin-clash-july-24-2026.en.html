⚡ July 24 International Politics Update: Trump Imposes New 10-12.5% Tariffs on 60 Nations, US-Saudi Nuclear Deal Hits Snag Over Israel Recognition, China-Philippines Clash Draws US Condemnation

1. Trump's Landmark Tariff Action: Section 301 Covers 60 Economies, 99.4% of US Imports

The Trump administration's new tariff regime took effect at 12:01 AM EDT on Friday, July 24 (12:01 PM CST), simultaneously replacing the expiring temporary 10% global tariff under Section 122 with newly enacted duties under Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974. The sweeping action targets 60 trading partners representing 99.4% of all US imports.

The tariffs are split into two tiers: a 10% rate for economies that have implemented or committed to implementing forced labor import prohibitions — including the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada, India, Indonesia, Mexico, and Pakistan — and a higher 12.5% rate for 38 economies deemed not to have adequately banned forced labor goods. Those facing the 12.5% rate include China (including Hong Kong), Japan, South Korea, Australia, Singapore, Switzerland, Brazil, Saudi Arabia, Israel, and Russia. China faces the highest applicable rate of 12.5%.

US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer stated: "The United States imposes and rigorously enforces a ban on imports of forced labor goods. Our trading partners should do the same." Key exemptions include oil, natural gas, fertilizers, certain food products, and goods qualifying for duty-free treatment under the US-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA). Goods already in transit are exempt until July 28.

This marks the most sweeping tariff action since the April 2025 "Liberation Day" reciprocal tariffs. Following the Supreme Court's February ruling that Trump's IEEPA-based tariffs were unconstitutional, the administration has been working to rebuild its tariff wall through alternative legal authorities. Unlike IEEPA tariffs, Section 301 has survived prior court challenges and is considered on more solid legal ground. The Peterson Institute for International Economics characterized the move as "not a labor-standards exercise but a mechanism for exporting America's import ban on Chinese goods, as well as an attempt to recreate the tariff regime struck down by the Supreme Court."

USTR is also conducting separate Section 301 investigations into "structural excess capacity" in 16 economies representing 70% of US imports, which could lead to additional tariffs. Trump administration officials have signaled they intend to rebuild tariffs on Chinese goods back to the 20% level agreed upon in the November 2025 trade truce — China's current rate had fallen to 10% (excluding the 25% first-term industrial tariffs).

2. Global Reaction: China Warns of Retaliation, Japan 'Regrets', Brazil Plans Countermeasures

The new tariffs drew swift condemnation from affected nations, though none have yet announced formal retaliatory measures.

China, facing the highest 12.5% rate, reacted strongly. Foreign Ministry spokesman Lin Jian stated at the July 24 press briefing that China "consistently opposes all forms of unilateral tariff measures" and warned that "tariff wars and trade wars benefit no one." The Chinese Embassy in Washington lodged formal representations, accusing the US of "once again abusing the Section 301 process and placing domestic law above international rules." The Ministry of Commerce noted that the WTO has already ruled US Section 301 tariffs against China as violating WTO rules, calling the new action "a serious mistake."

Despite the rhetoric, US-China trade talks continue. Both sides are exploring reciprocal tariff reductions of $30 billion each under a newly proposed Board of Trade framework. China's Foreign Ministry also confirmed on July 24 that both nations are working to implement important consensus reached by their leaders on artificial intelligence cooperation. Trump and Xi are expected to meet in September.

Japan expressed "regret," with government spokesman Minoru Kihara arguing that the higher 12.5% rate is "based solely on the grounds that there is no ban on the import of products produced through forced labor." Australian Trade Minister Don Farrell called the tariffs "completely unjustified" and "inconsistent with our free trade agreement." Brazil called the decision "arbitrary and unjustified" and plans to retaliate under its reciprocity law. Singapore's Foreign Minister Vivian Balakrishnan raised the issue directly with US Secretary of State Marco Rubio on the sidelines of the ASEAN Foreign Ministers' Meeting in Manila.

Analysts note that most affected countries are choosing negotiation over immediate retaliation, reflecting the complexity of confronting the new US tariff architecture. The Hinrich Foundation's Deborah Elms observed that impacted nations "will find it difficult to prove they have taken sufficient measures to prevent forced labor imports."

"It's a little bit ridiculous to think that over 60 major trading partners, including countries in the EU, are really relying on that much forced labor. It's kind of hard to believe." — John Diamond, Baker Institute

3. US-Saudi Nuclear Deal Thrown into Uncertainty: Trump Adds Condition of Israel Recognition

A landmark US-Saudi civil nuclear cooperation agreement, two decades in the making, was thrown into controversy within hours of its signing. The US Department of Energy formally signed the "peaceful nuclear cooperation agreement" with Saudi Arabia on Wednesday, July 23, in Washington. The deal would allow US companies to participate in Saudi nuclear energy projects, including conditional uranium enrichment — a provision that had already raised nonproliferation concerns.

But in a Truth Social post on Thursday morning, President Trump dropped a bombshell: the nuclear deal "will be approved, but only on the condition that Saudi Arabia joins the highly respected and very successful Abraham Accords." This would require Riyadh to normalize diplomatic relations with Israel — a step the kingdom has long resisted without the establishment of a Palestinian state.

The demand blindsided Saudi officials and even caught many within the US administration off guard. According to CNN, Trump was reportedly furious that the Department of Energy announced the deal without proper White House coordination. White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt defended the president's position, stating Trump "has always been the final decision-maker" and had previously communicated this condition to Saudi counterparts.

Saudi Arabia has consistently refused to join the Abraham Accords, which Trump championed during his first term and which led to normalization between Israel and the UAE, Bahrain, Sudan, and Morocco. Riyadh has insisted on the establishment of a viable Palestinian state as a prerequisite. The Wall Street Journal reported on Tuesday that the deal had received Trump's initial approval, but the new condition represents a significant hardening of Washington's position.

Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu hailed the development, calling Saudi recognition "a historic leap forward" for Middle East peace. But analysts are deeply skeptical. Crispin Hawler, Senior Research Fellow at the Royal United Services Institute, said: "If the US tries to force Saudi Arabia to normalize with Israel, it will not end well. A 180-degree reversal of Saudi Arabia's position over the past three decades? That is highly doubtful."

The nuclear deal's enrichment provisions had already drawn criticism from nonproliferation advocates. The additional condition of Israel normalization makes the agreement's fate even more uncertain, with experts questioning whether Riyadh will accept terms that fundamentally alter its long-standing diplomatic position.

4. China-Philippines Ayungin Shoal Clash Escalates: US Condemns 'Escalatory' Action, Marcos Seeks 'Reset'

Tensions in the South China Sea erupted into violence on July 20 when Chinese Coast Guard personnel attempted to approach the grounded BRP Sierra Madre at Ayungin Shoal (Second Thomas Shoal), where a small contingent of Filipino sailors and marines are stationed. Two Philippine Navy personnel — Seaman First Class Underwater Operator Carmelo Aballe and Private Eric Lacida — were struck in the head with batons by Chinese personnel during the confrontation.

US Secretary of State Marco Rubio, attending the ASEAN foreign ministers' meetings in Manila, condemned the Chinese action in the strongest terms. "The whole world saw it, and it's disturbing," Rubio told journalists on July 23. He described the incident as "sort of an escalatory thing that we think is very negative and benefits no one." Reaffirming the US-Philippines Mutual Defense Treaty, he stated: "We have a role, and we're not going to abandon our allies. We've made that abundantly clear."

Rubio said he had briefed President Trump on the incident and that Trump had assured Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. he would raise Manila's concerns directly with Chinese President Xi Jinping at their anticipated September meeting. Marcos and Trump held a phone call on July 23 covering the "current global, economic and security situation," according to the Philippine Presidential Communications Office.

President Marcos has signaled a dual-track approach — firmly condemning the violence while seeking diplomatic engagement with Beijing. He met with Chinese Ambassador to the Philippines Jing Quan at Malacañang on July 21 for one hour and twenty minutes. Officials described the meeting as "constructive," with Marcos expressing his desire for a "reset" of Philippines-China relations and demanding that the Ayungin incident not be repeated.

On July 23, the Philippine Department of Foreign Affairs confirmed the existence of a "provisional understanding" with China governing routine rotation and resupply (RORE) missions to Ayungin Shoal. However, Foreign Secretary Ma. Theresa Lazaro stressed that the understanding "is not a grant of or a request for permission to any foreign government" and "does not recognize any foreign jurisdiction over Philippine waters." The DFA said the provisional understanding has facilitated 13 successful RORE missions since July 2024. Defense Secretary Gilbert Teodoro Jr. publicly stated he was unaware of any such agreement and asserted that "China has no business being near Ayungin Shoal in the first place."

President Marcos personally visited the two wounded sailors at Camp Ricarte Station Hospital in Palawan, awarding them the Order of Lapu-Lapu and providing P100,000 each in financial assistance. He also conducted an aerial inspection of the Malampaya Phase 4 energy project, highlighting the government's push for energy security amidst the regional tensions.

On the sidelines of the ASEAN meetings, China and ASEAN foreign ministers also adopted a joint statement on the Middle East situation, calling for an "immediate and comprehensive cessation of hostilities" and enhanced regional energy cooperation.
