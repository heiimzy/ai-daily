⚡ July 15 International Politics: Trump Situation Room Plans Major Iran Escalation, Threatens to Strike Power Plants and Bridges, US Reimposes Naval Blockade, Oil Surges to $86

Trump Holds Situation Room Meeting to Plan Massive Iran Offensive

President Donald Trump convened a Situation Room meeting Tuesday to discuss a massive offensive in Iran that will be wider in scope than the current strikes around the Strait of Hormuz, three sources with knowledge told Axios in an exclusive report.

The meeting included Trump's top national security team: Vice President JD Vance, Secretary of State Marco Rubio, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs Gen. Dan Caine, CIA Director John Ratcliffe, White House envoy Steve Witkoff and other senior officials. The sources said the meeting focused on new plans for devastating strikes on strategic targets in Iran, in addition to operations around the Strait of Hormuz.

The White House declined to comment on the meeting's contents, but the report indicates Trump appears willing to escalate the war significantly to cause enough damage that the Iranian regime will open the Strait of Hormuz and accept his nuclear demands.

"Next Week Comes the Power Plants": Trump Issues Ultimatum

In a Fox News interview before the Situation Room meeting, Trump issued his clearest escalation warning yet, declaring that strikes on Iran would continue "until I say it's enough" and threatening to expand targets to power plants and bridges next week unless Tehran returns to negotiations.

"We're going to hit them very hard tonight. We're going to hit them very hard tomorrow night. We're going to hit them very hard the night after," Trump said. "And then next week, it gets really bad for them because next week comes the power plants. Next week comes the bridges. We're gonna knock out all their power plants. We're gonna knock out all their bridges unless they get to the table and negotiate."

Trump revealed that U.S. representatives had spoken with Iranian officials about an hour before the interview, delivering a blunt message: "You better make a deal, [or] you're not gonna have anything left." He said he believes Iran has "no choice" but to make a deal, criticizing Iran for tearing up agreements after signing them.

"I said, 'You better make a deal, [or] you're not gonna have anything left.'" — Donald Trump on Fox News

When asked whether Washington was ruling out deploying ground forces, Trump did not give a definitive no: "I would say no, if I thought it was appropriate. Sometimes you need a ground campaign, but we have other people that will do the ground campaign for us."

US Reimposes Naval Blockade, Fourth Day of Consecutive Strikes

At 4:00 PM ET Tuesday (20:00 GMT), the U.S. Navy formally reimposed a comprehensive naval blockade on Iranian ports, prohibiting any vessels from sailing to or from Iranian ports and coastal areas. This marks the second time the U.S. has imposed such a blockade since mid-April, when it was first implemented and then lifted in mid-June after the interim ceasefire deal.

The U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) simultaneously announced a new round of strikes against Iran, the fourth consecutive day of military action. CENTCOM said the seven-hour operation deployed fighter aircraft, drones, and naval vessels firing precision munitions against Iranian missile and drone sites, naval capabilities, and coastal defense systems, aiming to "further degrade Iran's ability to threaten commercial shipping and civilian crews."

CENTCOM commander Adm. Brad Cooper stated that over the past week Iran "has intentionally targeted civilians across the region by attacking seven commercial ships resulting in nearly a dozen civilian crew members killed, missing, or injured." Despite the attacks, the U.S. military managed to coordinate the transit of 300 ships through the strait over the past week, U.S. officials said.

Meanwhile, Trump announced he was scrapping his earlier threat to impose a 20% toll on cargo passing through the Strait of Hormuz, replacing the fee with trade and investment deals with Gulf states. "I have decided to replace the 20% United States Reimbursement Fee with Trade and Investment Deals that the various Gulf States will be making into the United States," Trump posted on Truth Social. "Those investments will be MASSIVE."

Iran Retaliates Across the Region: IRGC Strikes Bahrain, Kuwait, Jordan

Iran responded swiftly and forcefully. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) announced Wednesday it had targeted U.S. Fifth Fleet facilities in Bahrain, claiming to have struck command-and-control centers, logistics bases, fuel depots, and military equipment. Bahrain's Interior Ministry activated air raid sirens and urged citizens and residents to remain calm and head to the nearest safe place.

Iranian state television reported that Iran's army and the Revolutionary Guards carried out separate attacks on U.S. targets in Jordan and Kuwait. The army targeted the Al-Azraq air base in Jordan with drones, while the Guards fired cruise missiles at a U.S. military logistics center at Mina Abdullah in Kuwait.

Jordan's military said Wednesday it had shot down three ballistic missiles that entered Jordanian airspace from Iranian territory, with no casualties or material damage. Kuwait's military confirmed it was intercepting hostile drone attacks, saying "Kuwaiti air defenses are currently engaging hostile drone attacks following the nefarious Iranian aggression."

The IRCG issued a stark warning that if Washington seeks to block the region's oil and gas exports by controlling maritime routes, other export routes serving U.S. and allied interests could also be closed. "Oil and gas exports from the region will either be available for everyone or for no one," the statement said. "The retaliatory operations will continue, and the Strait of Hormuz will remain closed until the United States ends its acts of aggression."

Iran Deputy FM: "No More Talks with the United States"

Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi appeared on state television and declared that Iran would not pursue talks with the United States, nor would it request negotiations. "Iran has not left the negotiating table; rather, it has been obstructed by U.S. actions," he said. "The assumption that increasing pressure or military action can force Iran to seek dialogue is a miscalculation."

He continued: "Iran will not change its position on the strait or ease the restrictions it has imposed there," warning that "Iran's response to U.S. airstrikes will not be proportional; rather, it will make the United States regret its actions."

Iranian analyst Ali Khezrian also said on state television that negotiations with the United States had failed and there was no option other than "force," explaining that "the United States only understands the language of power" and "talks and agreements have produced no results."

Oil Surges Past $86 as Hormuz Crisis Deepens

International oil prices continued their rally as the U.S.-Iran conflict escalated sharply. Brent crude rose 1.72% to $86.19 per barrel in early Wednesday trading, its highest since June 12. U.S. WTI crude gained 1.4% to $80.40 per barrel, its highest since June 15. Oil had already closed up 2% at a one-month high on Tuesday.

Prior to the war, approximately one-fifth of the world's oil and liquefied natural gas transited through the Strait of Hormuz daily. Shipping tracker Kpler reported that only seven cargo ships passed through the strait on Monday, the quietest day since June 13. MarineTraffic data showed vessel activity through the strait declined by about 52% between July 10-12 compared to the previous week.

Tim Waterer, chief market analyst at KCM Trade, noted: "The chances of oil moving back toward $100 in the reasonably near term are still meaningful if hostilities intensify which damages energy infrastructure around the Gulf." However, he added that Brent could stabilize at $75-80 if diplomatic efforts helped reopen the strait.

The U.S. Treasury Department also announced Tuesday it was expanding sanctions targeting Iran's oil sector and froze $130 million held in cryptocurrency linked to Iran's central bank, further tightening economic pressure on Tehran.

Pickaxe Mountain: A Potential Flashpoint for the Next Phase

Trump mentioned in his interview that the U.S. is monitoring suspicious activity at Iran's "Pickaxe Mountain" underground nuclear facility — a heavily fortified site near the Natanz uranium enrichment complex featuring two deep tunnel networks. Experts estimate its burial depth exceeds the penetration capability of the U.S. military's most powerful conventional bunker buster, the GBU-57 MOP.

"Pickaxe is a possible target for a nice, big, fat shot right in the front door," Trump previously said. However, he acknowledged uncertainty about Iranian activity there: "By the way, nobody knows if they even are doing anything in Pickaxe. We have cameras on it. There's very little activity there. But if there's even just a small amount, we'll hit it and we'll hit it hard."

International inspectors have never been permitted to visit the facility, which Iran began constructing in 2020. While there are currently few indications of active nuclear work, analysts warn that the risk of miscalculation is rising sharply as conventional military targets become increasingly depleted.
