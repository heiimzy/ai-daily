⚡ July 23 Tech Earnings Roundup: Alphabet Cloud Revenue Soars 82% to $24.8B, Tesla Net Income Falls 5% with Negative FCF, IBM Cuts Full-Year Outlook

1. Alphabet Q2 Beats Across the Board: Revenue Hits $119.8B, Google Cloud Surges 82%

Google parent Alphabet reported better-than-expected Q2 2026 earnings after market close on Wednesday, delivering total revenue of $119.8 billion, up 24% year-over-year and ahead of analyst estimates. Net profit reached $112.1 billion, a figure heavily inflated by $99 billion in non-operating gains from its stakes in Anthropic and SpaceX. Even excluding these gains, operating performance was robust.

The star of the show was Google Cloud, which posted revenue of $24.8 billion — an 82% year-over-year surge that far exceeded Wall Street's $22.46 billion consensus and accelerated from last quarter's already-impressive 63% growth. This marks Google Cloud's highest growth rate ever. The cloud backlog — contracted work not yet converted into revenue — ballooned to $514 billion, signaling strong future demand.

CEO Sundar Pichai told analysts on the earnings call: "Our AI investments are redefining what's possible across every part of our business. We have exciting momentum across the board." He revealed that Gemini now has 950 million monthly active users, up from 750 million in Q4 2025. The new Antigravity AI coding tool has attracted 2.4 million weekly active users.

Capex Doubles, Sparking Investor Concerns

The capex figure, however, gave some investors pause. Q2 capital expenditures hit $44.9 billion — a 100% year-over-year increase that nearly matches Google Cloud's entire quarterly revenue. More strikingly, Alphabet raised its full-year 2026 capex guidance to $195-205 billion, up from $180-190 billion last quarter and marking the first time annual spend could exceed $200 billion.

When pressed on the timeline for returns, Pichai responded: "I think our compute capacity investments in '27. We are seeing strong demand indicators, including long-term deals. I think, if anything, the dynamics look healthier than where we were about a year ago." Despite his confidence, Alphabet shares slipped in after-hours trading, reflecting ongoing market anxiety about the scale and payback period of AI infrastructure spending.

"Our AI investments are redefining what's possible across every part of our business." — Sundar Pichai, Alphabet CEO

Search advertising revenue grew 17% to approximately $64 billion, in line with expectations. YouTube ad revenue rose ~15%. This marks Alphabet's 12th consecutive quarter of double-digit revenue growth.

2. Tesla Q2: Record Revenue but Profits Under Pressure, FCF Turns Negative

Tesla's Q2 earnings painted a mixed picture. While revenue hit a record high of $28.24 billion and beat expectations, profitability continued to deteriorate on multiple fronts.

Vehicle deliveries rebounded strongly to over 480,000 units — Tesla's best quarter since Q3 2025 — driving the top-line beat. But net income fell 5% year-over-year to just $1.1 billion. Operating expenses surged 47% to $4.3 billion, driven by R&D spending on AI, autonomous driving, and next-generation products.

Gross margin slipped to 16.8% from 17.2% a year ago, well below the 19.4% analysts had expected. Lower average selling prices and declining regulatory credit revenue were the main culprits. Even more alarming, operating margin plunged from 4.1% to 1.4% — meaning Tesla retains just $1.40 in profit for every $100 in revenue after operating costs.

Free cash flow turned sharply negative at -$1.1 billion, a stark reversal from the positive $1.44 billion in Q1 and $146 million in the year-ago period. CFO Vaibhav Taneja had previously warned that cash flow would remain under pressure for the rest of the year, but the speed of deterioration caught many off guard.

Product Delays and Growing AI Bets

The earnings report also revealed further delays in the production timelines for the Cybercab robotaxi, Semi electric truck, and Megapack energy storage products. Tesla is pouring money into scaling these from development to mass production, encountering supply chain and manufacturing challenges along the way. Meanwhile, spending continues on Full Self-Driving (FSD) technology and the Optimus humanoid robot.

On a positive note, Tesla has launched robotaxi pilot programs in Orlando and Tampa, Florida, gathering real-world data for future commercial deployments. However, regulatory approval timelines remain uncertain. Tesla shares fell approximately 3% in after-hours trading.

"Tesla is in a critical transition phase from EV maker to AI and robotics company, and transition costs are eating into short-term profits." — TechCrunch

3. IBM Cuts Full-Year Forecast: Enterprise IT Spending Slowdown Hits Legacy Tech

IBM became the latest traditional tech company to feel the pinch of slowing enterprise IT spending. The company issued an earnings warning on Wednesday, cutting its full-year forecast. IBM cited macroeconomic uncertainty as customers in Europe and Asia delayed large IT infrastructure upgrade contracts. While the company continues to see growth in hybrid cloud and AI consulting, legacy hardware and managed infrastructure services revenue fell short of expectations.

CEO Arvind Krishna stated that the company is pivoting more resources toward AI and hybrid cloud, but noted the transition is taking longer than anticipated. IBM shares fell about 4% after hours.

4. Market Outlook: AI Arms Race Intensifies, Divergence Takes Shape

Collectively, this earnings cycle reveals a clear divergence in the tech landscape: companies owning AI infrastructure and cloud platforms (Google Cloud, AWS, Azure) are reaping the "picks and shovels" rewards of the AI era, while legacy hardware and IT services firms face painful transitions. With Alphabet pushing 2026 capex toward $200 billion, the AI infrastructure arms race among hyperscalers shows no signs of slowing.

For Tesla, the story remains more complex — the company is simultaneously navigating EV price wars, autonomous driving R&D, and long-term AI/robotics ambitions across three fronts. Short-term profitability pressure appears inevitable. The market will closely watch whether Cybercab enters commercial service by 2027 as planned and whether FSD subscription revenue can provide a buffer for the bottom line.

Overall, the Q2 2026 earnings season is validating a key thesis: AI is no longer a "concept story" for tech companies — it is fundamentally reshaping capital allocation and competitive boundaries across the entire industry.
