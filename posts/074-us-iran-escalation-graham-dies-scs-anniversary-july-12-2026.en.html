⚡ July 12 World Politics Update: US Third Wave Hits 140 Iranian Targets, Iran Strikes Back Across 5 Gulf States, Senator Lindsey Graham Dies Suddenly, 14 Nations Reaffirm 2016 South China Sea Ruling

1. US-Iran Conflict Escalates to New Level: Third Wave of Strikes and Regional Retaliation

In the early hours of July 12, US Central Command (CENTCOM) completed its third round of strikes against Iran this week — the largest military operation since the US-Iran Memorandum of Understanding was signed in June. Using fighter jets, drones, and naval vessels, US forces struck approximately 140 Iranian military targets, including missile and drone launch sites, naval capabilities, ammunition storage facilities, communications networks, and coastal surveillance locations across Iran's southern provinces of Bushehr, Hormozgan, Khuzestan, and Sistan and Baluchestan, as well as Lorestan in the west.

"Iran made a poor choice. Now they pay," US Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth posted on social media. President Donald Trump had previously declared the June ceasefire "over."

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) launched a massive retaliation, striking targets across five Gulf states and Jordan:

  • Qatar: Iranian ballistic missiles targeted Doha for the first time. Qatar's air defense system intercepted the missiles, but falling shrapnel wounded three people, including one child. Qatar's Interior Ministry issued a safety alert and advised all vessels to suspend navigation. Doha, which had been a key mediator in US-Iran ceasefire efforts, said it reserves the right to "respond."
  • UAE: The Ministry of Defense activated air defense systems to engage Iranian missiles and drones, later confirming the threats were outside its borders.
  • Bahrain: Missile alert sirens sounded for the third time on Sunday. Bahrain hosts the US Navy's Fifth Fleet.
  • Kuwait: The military reported intercepting aerial targets and condemned the "dangerous escalation" as a grave violation of sovereignty.
  • Oman: Drones targeted sites in the Musandam governorate, an exclave jutting into the Strait of Hormuz. Oman condemned the attack.
  • Jordan: Three Iranian missiles landed without causing casualties. The IRGC claimed to have destroyed a command-and-control center and MQ-9 drone hangars at a US base in Jordan.

Strait of Hormuz Closed Again

The immediate trigger was renewed hostilities in the Strait of Hormuz. The IRGC Navy announced that the Cyprus-flagged container ship M/V GFS Galaxy had traveled through an "unauthorized route" with its navigation systems switched off. After a warning shot struck the vessel, it caught fire, sustained severe engine room damage, and one Indian crew member remains missing. Iran declared the Strait "closed until further notice."

CENTCOM stated that commercial vessels can still transit via the southern route, which has been expanded to two-way traffic. The UK Maritime Trade Operations (UKMTO) maintained the Strait's threat level at "severe," the highest classification. Before the war, approximately one-fifth of the world's oil and LNG passed through this strategic chokepoint.

Iran's chief negotiator and parliament speaker, Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, posted on X: "The era of one-sided deals is OVER. We told you: keep your word or pay the price. Reality is knocking."

Iran's New Supreme Leader Speaks for the First Time

Iran's new Supreme Leader, Ayatollah Mojtaba Khamenei — still unseen in public since the war began — issued his first statement since his father's funeral, vowing revenge for the assassination of former Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei in the war's opening strikes on February 28. "Such revenge is the will of our nation and must certainly be carried out," he said.

Trump responded by threatening on Truth Social: "1,000 missiles are locked and loaded and aimed at the Islamic Republic of Iran, with thousands more to immediately follow... Orders have already been given, and the US military is ready, willing and able, for a one-year period of time, subject to extension, to completely decimate and destroy all areas of Iran."

Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and Lebanon condemned Iran's "destabilizing behavior." India's Ministry of External Affairs confirmed one Indian national missing from the vessel attack and strongly condemned the strike.

2. US Senator Lindsey Graham Dies Suddenly at 71

Republican Senator Lindsey Graham of South Carolina, a key ally of President Donald Trump, died suddenly on July 12 at age 71 after a "brief and sudden illness." His office confirmed the death occurred Saturday evening. NBC News reported that emergency responders had been called to his Capitol Hill residence for a cardiac arrest.

Graham had just returned from Kyiv, where he met Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy on July 10 to discuss Ukraine's air defense needs and sanctions against Russia. During the visit, he suggested China could play a decisive role in pressuring Russia toward peace talks.

Trump posted on social media: "Senator Lindsey Graham, one of the greatest people and Senators I have ever known, is dead! A true American Patriot." Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskyy, and Israeli Defense Minister Israel Katz offered condolences. Zelenskyy called Graham "a true defender of freedom."

Elected to the Senate in 2002, Graham served as chairman of the Senate Budget Committee. A defense hawk, he was a steadfast supporter of Israel, Ukraine, and a hardline opponent of Iran. Notably, Graham was initially a vocal critic of Trump during the 2016 campaign — famously posting "If we nominate Trump, we will get destroyed… and we will deserve it" — before becoming one of his most loyal allies.

3. 10th Anniversary of South China Sea Ruling: 14 Nations + EU Reaffirm Award

July 12, 2026 marks the 10th anniversary of the 2016 South China Sea arbitration ruling. Fourteen countries — the United States, United Kingdom, Philippines, Japan, Australia, New Zealand, Canada, Germany, Italy, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Romania, and Slovenia — issued a joint statement reaffirming the award as "final, legally binding, and definitive." The 27-member European Union released a separate statement calling the ruling a "landmark decision in the peaceful settlement of disputes."

Across the Philippines, nationwide commemorations were held:

  • A "National Peace Walk" at the Quirino Grandstand in Manila
  • The Philippine Navy sounded ship horns simultaneously with 10 long blasts nationwide
  • Over 2,000 people marched in Puerto Princesa, Palawan
  • The country's first "West Philippine Sea Fisherfolk Monument" was unveiled in Quezon City

Philippine Defense Secretary Gilberto Teodoro Jr. declared that the Philippines has become "a convergence hub for upholding international law, particularly UNCLOS," and vowed to "resist and combat any attempt to deny our rights and entitlements in the West Philippine Sea." A recent poll showed 86% of Filipinos support working with like-minded nations to defend the waters.

China's Ministry of Foreign Affairs reiterated that the ruling "seriously contravenes the general practice of international arbitration," is "unjust and unlawful," and that Beijing "neither accepts nor recognizes it."

These three major international stories highlight the intensifying geopolitical tensions across multiple fronts: the US-Iran conflict spiraling from a fragile ceasefire into the heaviest exchanges since the MoU, the sudden loss of a pivotal figure in US politics, and the ongoing South China Sea dispute on the symbolic 10th anniversary of the landmark ruling.
