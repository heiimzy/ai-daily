⚡ July 22 Financial Roundup: Yen Crashes Through 163 (40-Year Low), Brent Oil Surges to $94, Gold Breaks $4,120, Fed Rate Hike Odds Soar

💰 Yen Breaches 163 for First Time Since 1986, Intervention Pressure Mounts

The Japanese yen tumbled to its weakest level in nearly four decades on Wednesday, breaching the 163-per-dollar mark in New York trading and extending its relentless slide. The yen touched 163.24 per dollar, a level not seen since late 1986, before trimming slightly to trade around 163.21 in early Asian trading.

The selloff was driven by a powerful trifecta of pressures: heightened safe-haven demand for the dollar amid the 11th consecutive night of US strikes on Iran; surging crude oil prices (Brent above $94/bbl) worsening Japan's import cost-driven trade deficit; and rising US Treasury yields—the 10-year hit 4.64% (highest since May), while the 30-year yield broke above 5.15%—making dollar-denominated assets more attractive.

Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama told reporters Wednesday that authorities remain ready to "take appropriate and decisive action as needed," but her words failed to stem the yen's decline. Japan has already spent a record 11.73 trillion yen ($719 billion) on intervention between April 28 and May 27, yet those efforts have largely faded. "Without intervention, 165 is the next key level the market will target," said Rinto Maruyama, senior FX and rates strategist at Sumitomo Mitsui Banking Corporation.

HSBC analysts noted that intervention alone is unlikely to have a lasting impact unless the Bank of Japan adopts a more aggressive rate-hiking stance, the Fed pivots back to easing, or investor sentiment toward Japan's fiscal position improves. None of these conditions appear imminent.

🛢️ Brent Crude Surges 4% to $94 as US-Iran Conflict Intensifies

Oil prices continued their relentless climb Wednesday, with Brent crude futures surging 3.64% to $94.33 per barrel—the highest since June 8—while WTI crude rose 3.10% to $86.96. Brent has gained a staggering 22.19% over the past month alone.

The latest leg higher was triggered by the 11th consecutive night of US strikes against Iranian military targets, retaliatory Iranian attacks on US facilities in Bahrain, Kuwait and Jordan, and an announcement by Yemen's Houthi rebels imposing a "naval blockade" on Saudi Arabia. Traffic through the Bab el-Mandeb Strait has plummeted 34% in two weeks.

Goldman Sachs has warned that Brent could surge to $120 per barrel if disruptions through the Strait of Hormuz continue. The bank's base case assumes a gradual easing of tensions, projecting Brent to average $80 in Q4 and $75 next year, but risks remain "tilted to the upside." Kuwait reported its air defenses were intercepting Iranian drones overnight, adding to fears that the conflict is widening.

WTI crude has rallied for four consecutive sessions, up 18.78% month-to-date and 33.27% year-over-year.

🥇 Gold Defies Gravity: Breaks $4,120 Despite Higher Yields and Hawkish Fed

Gold has mounted a remarkable rally this week, climbing approximately 1.5% to the $4,135-4,140 range on Wednesday, extending its winning streak to four consecutive days. Silver has joined the advance, climbing back toward $60 per ounce.

What makes this rally particularly noteworthy is that it is unfolding against a backdrop that should, by conventional logic, be deeply hostile for precious metals: Brent crude above $92, the 10-year Treasury yield at 4.63%, and markets pricing a 71% probability of a Fed rate hike in September. Each of these factors should traditionally weigh on non-yielding safe-haven assets.

ActionForex analysts suggest this divergence may signal a paradigm shift in how markets interpret the oil shock. Investors may be transitioning from an "inflation narrative" to a "stagflation narrative." Persistently elevated oil prices not only fuel inflation but also raise the risk of slower global growth, weaker corporate profitability, and central bank policy mistakes. In this framework, gold regains its appeal not just as an inflation hedge but as protection against geopolitical escalation and stagflation risks.

Technically, gold has broken above its 20-period EMA at $4,094, with the next key resistance at $4,202 (the July 6 high). A decisive move above that level would confirm a double-bottom reversal pattern, potentially opening the path toward $4,300.

📈 Fed September Rate Hike Probability Surges from 58% to 71%

The CME FedWatch Tool showed a dramatic repricing of monetary policy expectations on Wednesday, with the probability of a Fed rate hike in September jumping from approximately 58% last week to 71%. The market continues to price a 97.4% probability that the Fed will hold rates steady at its July meeting.

US Treasury yields surged across the curve. The benchmark 10-year yield climbed to 4.63-4.64%, the highest since May, while the 30-year yield breached 5.15%, a two-month high. Real yields (TIPS) also hit multi-year highs. The dollar strengthened as traders reassessed the chances of tighter policy.

The Fed now faces a classic stagflation dilemma: rising oil prices fuel inflation that demands tighter policy, while the geopolitical shock creates economic headwinds that argue for accommodation. Deutsche Bank's Jim Reid noted that "sovereign bonds had a bad day, but global equities surged thanks to a sharp bounceback in chip stocks—the Philly Semiconductor Index posted its best daily performance in a month, +5.21%."

🇰🇷 KOSPI Surges 5% as Chip Stocks Stage Explosive Rebound

South Korea's KOSPI index surged more than 5% on Wednesday, led by Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix, as the global semiconductor trade staged a dramatic comeback. The rally largely reversed the damage from the "Chinese AI shock" that had gripped the same stocks less than a week earlier.

The catalyst came from Morgan Stanley semiconductor analyst Joseph Moore, who published a report arguing that "the data center memory chip shortage is worsening and is expected to persist through 2028, with memory chip prices rising at least 25% from Q2 to Q3." The call ignited a buying frenzy reminiscent of the peak AI trade.

Storage chip names led the charge: SanDisk surged over 14%, SK Hynix ADRs gained over 13%, Micron Technology jumped over 12% (market cap back above $1.1 trillion), Western Digital rose over 12%, and Seagate Technology climbed over 11%. The Roundhill Storage ETF surged 10.91%. Semiconductor equipment and design stocks also boomed: Intel and AMD rose over 8%, ARM and Applied Materials gained over 7%, and TSMC ADRs added over 5%.

However, BTIG Chief Market Technical Analyst Jonathan Krinsky warned of strong overhead resistance, while Bloomberg macro strategist Michael Ball cautioned that put option demand in semiconductor ETFs remains elevated, making it too early to declare the correction over.
