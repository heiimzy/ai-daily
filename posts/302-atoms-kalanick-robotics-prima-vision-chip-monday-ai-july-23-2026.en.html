⚡ July 23 Tech Roundup: Kalanick's Atoms Raises $1.7B for Robotics, Vision-Restoring Chip Wins EU Approval, Monday.com Lays Off 630 to Pivot to AI

1. Travis Kalanick's Atoms Raises $1.7B Led by a16z to Build the Future of Robotics

Travis Kalanick's robotics company Atoms has raised $1.7 billion in a funding round led by Andreessen Horowitz, with Ben Horowitz joining the company's board. Bain Capital, Fifth Wall, and notably Uber also participated — marking the first reconnection between Kalanick and the company he founded before being ousted as CEO in 2017 amid controversy.

Atoms is essentially a rebranded holding company built atop Kalanick's ghost kitchen project Cloud Kitchens. In March, Kalanick revealed the new name and announced the acquisition of Pronto, the heavy industry automation company run by his former Uber colleague Anthony Levandowski. Kalanick has also expressed interest in expanding into mining, extending Pronto's autonomous vehicle technology further.

"On many levels, this round is a bit of unfinished business. Fuel to complete the bits-to-atoms story arc we started at Uber, continued at CloudKitchens and will now finish at Atoms," Kalanick wrote on X. He described Atoms' vision as building "atoms-based computers" where CPU is manufacturing, storage is real estate, and network is transportation — essentially creating a "wheelbase for robots."

"Travis is Back," declared Ben Horowitz. "It takes a rare kind of entrepreneur to change these old-school, heavy parts of our economy. They need a gritty work ethic, drive, and range that spans across domains, from software architecture to mechanical engineering." Horowitz believes the most valuable application of AI and robotics is to "make everything and everyone more productive" in the physical world.

Industry analysis: The scale of Atoms' funding round is remarkable even by robotics startup standards, reflecting top-tier VC conviction that "physical world AI" represents the next frontier. By extending robotics from delivery to heavy industry and mining, Kalanick is betting on a market arguably larger than autonomous driving alone.

2. Science Corporation's PRIMA Vision-Restoring Chip Wins EU Approval

Science Corporation, the brain-computer interface (BCI) company founded by former Neuralink co-founder Max Hodak, has won approval from Europe's medical device regulator to begin selling PRIMA, a device that restores vision lost from age-related macular degeneration (AMD). The chip has also received a breakthrough device designation from the U.S. FDA, paving the way for expedited review.

Millions of people worldwide suffer from AMD, which destroys light-sensitive cells at the back of the eye, making it difficult to read and recognize faces. PRIMA requires a one-hour outpatient procedure to implant a small chip in the back of the eye. Patients then wear camera-equipped glasses that transmit a view of the world to the implanted chip.

"One of our patients in France finished a 300-page novel a little while ago, and sent us the book," Hodak told TechCrunch. "We have a sketch on the wall that one of our patients drew of the Sydney Opera House. There are videos of patients playing crossword puzzles and filling in Sudoku."

Each PRIMA device is expected to cost in the hundreds of thousands of dollars, with Science Corporation currently in discussions with European healthcare providers over reimbursement. The company plans to launch in Germany first, where its clinical trials were conducted, with the first procedure expected in September.

Science Corporation is also working with Dr. Murat Günel, chair of Yale Medical School's Department of Neurosurgery, to develop procedures for human trials of a directly implanted bio-hybrid brain sensor — a longer-term research project funded by the PRIMA commercialization.

Industry analysis: PRIMA's approval marks a significant milestone for BCI commercialization. Unlike Neuralink's highly invasive full-implant approach, Science Corporation chose a pragmatic "partial implant + external glasses" path, first building a sustainable business in the sizable AMD treatment market.

3. Monday.com Lays Off 630 Employees (20%) to Restructure Around AI

Israeli workplace software company Monday.com announced on July 22 that it is laying off approximately 630 employees — 20% of its global workforce — as part of a major restructuring to pivot its strategy toward an AI-first platform. The company emphasized that the layoffs are not a cost-cutting measure but rather an organizational transformation to "support a leaner, more focused operating model."

"We entered a new era where AI is transforming the role of software, creating the greatest opportunity our industry has ever seen," co-founders Roy Mann and Eran Zinman wrote in a letter to employees. "We have a new market to capture. Without a fundamental change in how we operate, we will not be able to compete and win that market."

Monday.com had already pivoted hard toward AI earlier this year, redesigning its entire product around the belief that enterprise customers increasingly want AI agents working alongside human employees. Its AI Work Platform currently includes a no-code app builder, customizable AI agents, workflow automation tools, and a chatbot capable of generating reports and updating dashboards.

"Over the past nine months, we have shifted our core vision from managing work to doing the work for our customers, with people and AI agents working together in one workspace," the founders stated. The restructuring will reduce management layers, create smaller autonomous teams, and change how the company sells and supports customers.

Monday.com expects to incur between $45 million and $55 million in restructuring charges. The company stressed that the vast majority of savings will be reinvested into talent, products, AI, and future growth. Monday.com's stock has come under pressure over the past year as investors reassess the future of SaaS companies in the age of AI.

Industry context: Monday.com's layoffs are part of a broader trend. According to Layoffs.fyi, a record 78% of tech companies cited the need to refocus around AI as a reason for workforce reductions this year. The shift from "managing work" in the SaaS era to "AI and humans doing work together" is fundamentally reshaping software company organizational structures.
