⚡ July 23 International Politics Roundup: Trump Vows to Destroy a Bridge or Power Plant for Every Ship Attacked in Hormuz, Russia-US-Ukraine Hold Historic Tripartite Talks in Abu Dhabi, US-Saudi Arabia Sign 30-Year Civilian Nuclear Deal

1. Trump Unveils New Deterrence: One Bridge or Power Plant Per Ship

President Donald Trump issued a dramatic new threat against Iran on Wednesday (July 22), declaring on Truth Social that the United States will destroy an Iranian bridge or power plant — including infrastructure in and around Tehran — every time Iran attacks a vessel in the Strait of Hormuz.

"From now on, every time Iran fires a missile, rocket, drone, or uses any other device or weapon to attack any ship in the Strait of Hormuz, the United States will bomb and destroy an Iranian bridge or a power plant," Trump wrote. "Targets also include bridges and power plants located in or near the capital city of Tehran."

The statement follows Trump's warning a day earlier that the U.S. may "soon hit hard" Iran's underground nuclear facility at "Pickaxe Mountain" (Kuh-e Kolang). Israeli intelligence believes Iran has relocated advanced uranium enrichment centrifuges to the fortified site.

Iran Threatens Counterstrikes on US Infrastructure

Iran responded swiftly and forcefully. A military source told Iranian media: "If the United States attacks Iranian bridges or power plants, Iran will strike infrastructure and bridges in this region, including energy facilities in which the United States has interests."

The source reiterated Iran's determination to control passage through the Strait of Hormuz, stating that "ships can only pass through the strait safely if they coordinate with Iran and navigate according to Iranian arrangements."

International law experts have warned that attacks on civilian infrastructure could constitute war crimes under international humanitarian law.

Rubio: Iran Has No Right to Control Hormuz

Earlier Wednesday, Secretary of State Marco Rubio told the ASEAN foreign ministers' meeting that Iran is not "serious" about reaching a deal with the U.S., stressing that Washington remains committed to a diplomatic solution. Rubio identified Iran's demand for control over Hormuz shipping as the core dispute, stating that "under international law, Iran does not have this right."

Brent crude surged 3.7% to $94.33/barrel and WTI rose 3.5% to $87.28/barrel amid escalating tensions. CENTCOM has completed its 11th consecutive night of strikes on Iranian targets, hitting command centers, drone storage facilities, and military logistics infrastructure.

2. Russia-US-Ukraine Hold First Tripartite Talks in Abu Dhabi

Representatives from Russia, the United States, and Ukraine began a trilateral security working group meeting in Abu Dhabi on July 23 — the first-ever tripartite talks involving all three parties since the war began in 2022, according to CNN.

Putin and Witkoff Hold 3.5-Hour Kremlin Meeting

Late the previous night, President Vladimir Putin met with U.S. Special Envoy Steve Witkoff and other American representatives at the Kremlin for 3.5 hours (from 23:35 to 03:04 Moscow time). Kremlin aide Yuri Ushakov called the talks "constructive and extremely candid," confirming the tripartite meeting in Abu Dhabi. The Russian delegation is led by GRU chief Admiral Igor Kostyukov.

Ushakov emphasized that "without resolving the territorial issue according to the parameters agreed in Anchorage, a long-term solution cannot be expected."

"Only One Problem Left"

President Zelensky confirmed from Davos that Ukraine's negotiating team had traveled to Abu Dhabi. "Ukrainian military representatives must be present. We will see the results of the two-day talks," he said. "The United States is much stronger than Russia — that's why I believe Trump can achieve a ceasefire. We are open to different negotiating steps, but both sides must compromise."

Witkoff stated that talks are "solvable" and that there is "only one problem left." European diplomatic sources identified this as the territorial question. Trump warned Zelensky in Davos that Ukraine "could lose more territory" if it refuses a peace deal with Russia.

Zelensky Lashes Out at Europe in Davos

In an unusually fiery address at the World Economic Forum, Zelensky accused European leaders of being "lost" and "trapped in endless failure, unable to protect themselves or effectively support Ukraine." Heeven offered Ukraine's help to "defend Greenland." Meanwhile, rolling blackouts continued across Ukraine on July 23, with Energy Minister German Galushchenko calling it the toughest day for the power grid since 2022.

3. US and Saudi Arabia Sign 30-Year Civilian Nuclear Agreement

The Trump administration is set to announce a landmark civilian nuclear cooperation agreement with Saudi Arabia on Wednesday, July 23, according to the New York Times. The 30-year deal, worth billions to the U.S. nuclear industry, includes a provision for American companies to build a uranium enrichment facility in Saudi Arabia pending a joint feasibility study.

Some U.S. lawmakers from both parties and Israeli officials have voiced concerns that the Saudi civilian program could eventually be converted to develop nuclear weapons. The deal will be submitted to Congress but does not require its approval to take effect. The announcement comes as Houthi rebels this week declared a maritime blockade on Saudi ports, opening a new energy front in the widening Middle East conflict.

4. Lavrov and Rubio to Meet in Manila on July 23

Russian Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov confirmed on July 22 that his meeting with U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio has been scheduled for the morning of July 23 in Manila, on the sidelines of the ASEAN ministerial meetings. "The meeting will be useful in any case," Lavrov said.

Also on July 22, President Zelensky spoke with U.S. envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner about the possibility of restarting negotiations with Russia, according to Ukrainian presidential communications adviser Dmytro Lytvyn.
