⚡ July 21 Tech Deep Dive: US Tech Giants' Hidden AI Debt Hits $1.65 Trillion — The Balance Sheet Risk Behind the AI Arms Race

The AI arms race is reshaping the finances of America's largest technology companies — but much of the borrowing is invisible to investors. A Nikkei Asia investigation published July 21 reveals that hidden debt at five US tech giants (Meta, Google, Oracle, Microsoft, and Amazon) has swelled eightfold in four years to an estimated $1.65 trillion, exceeding their total on-balance-sheet debt and making it significantly harder for investors to assess risk.

The $1.65 Trillion Iceberg Below the Surface

Nikkei's study found that these technology leaders have accumulated staggering hidden liabilities through data center leases, GPU supply contracts, and special-purpose financing vehicles. While these obligations don't appear as "long-term debt" under current accounting standards, they carry the same repayment obligations for investors to consider.

Meta leads the pack with approximately $420 billion in off-balance-sheet debt — nearly triple its transparent debt. The social media giant is building massive AI compute infrastructure across the United States, including its "Hyperion" hyperscale data center in Louisiana. Oracle also carries significant off-balance-sheet exposure, having tapped bond markets for $18 billion while structuring much of its AI cloud buildout through operating leases.

According to the Nikkei investigation, the primary sources of hidden debt include:

  • Long-term data center leases: 10-15 year operating leases for data center space that don't need to be recorded as debt under current accounting rules
  • GPU supply commitments: Multi-year purchasing agreements with NVIDIA, AMD, and others, sometimes structured as take-or-pay contracts
  • Special-purpose financing vehicles: Joint ventures and project-financing structures that keep debt off balance sheets
  • Private credit arrangements: Increasing reliance on private credit markets that operate outside public bond market disclosure frameworks

The Economics Behind the Debt Surge

This debt explosion is occurring against the backdrop of an AI infrastructure super-cycle. Morgan Stanley projects $2.9 trillion in global data center investment between 2025 and 2028, with roughly $900 billion coming from private credit and asset-backed lending. The top five hyperscalers are on track to spend approximately $602 billion on capital expenditures in 2026 alone — a 36% year-over-year increase — with about 75% going directly to AI infrastructure.

According to Bank of America data, hyperscalers added $121 billion in new debt in 2025 alone — more than four times the average annual issuance over the previous five years — with over $90 billion of that concentrated in the final three months. Meta tapped the bond market for $30 billion. Alphabet raised $25 billion. Oracle completed an $18 billion bond sale — making it the largest issuer of investment-grade debt among non-financial US companies, according to Citigroup.

"This AI infrastructure financing frenzy has moved well beyond traditional corporate bond markets, spreading into private credit, asset-backed securities, and off-balance-sheet vehicles." — Nikkei Asia Investigation

Credit Default Swaps Flash Warning Signs

Credit markets are already showing strain. Credit-default swaps on Oracle debt have widened to multi-year highs, with Barclays and Morgan Stanley advising clients to buy protection. A liquid CDS market tied to Meta began actively trading for the first time in late 2025 — the latest sign that investors are rushing to hedge hyperscaler debt exposure.

Analysts warn that when hidden debt accumulates through off-balance-sheet vehicles, traditional credit rating methods may significantly understate true leverage levels. S&P Global Ratings downgraded SoftBank's credit outlook to negative in March 2026, citing concentrated OpenAI risk — the Japanese conglomerate holds a roughly 13% stake worth approximately $65 billion.

Behind the Borrowing: The "Rent vs. Build" AI Compute Dilemma

A key driver of hidden debt is the tech industry's increasing preference for leasing rather than owning AI compute infrastructure. OpenAI CFO Sarah Friar previously revealed the company has reviewed over 800 potential sites across North America for data center development, weighing land, substations, and transmission capacity. OpenAI is preparing to take on debt for the first time to finance expansion.

Japanese markets are feeling the ripple effects. The 10-year Japanese government bond yield has climbed above 2.8% — its highest since 1996 — while 30-year yields touched record highs. South Korea's 10-year yield rose above 4.1%. Analysts warn that AI-driven borrowing is crowding out other long-term investment-grade issuers globally.

The Investor's Dilemma

For investors, the explosion in hidden AI debt presents a complex assessment challenge. On one hand, these tech giants' AI investments could create enormous long-term value. On the other, the hidden accumulation of debt reduces financial transparency. If AI investment returns fall short of expectations — or if interest rates continue rising — the pressure from these hidden obligations could rapidly transmit to public markets.

The Dallas Federal Reserve has noted that AI financing needs are having structural effects on long-term bond markets: "Investors are being asked to give up more compensation for duration, and much of that demand is now tied to one buildout." History suggests that debt-fueled technology investment cycles typically end one of two ways: either the technology breakthrough delivers on its promise, or the debt bubble bursts.

For now, AI commercialization remains strong — OpenAI generated $13 billion in revenue in 2025, and Anthropic posted its first quarterly profit. But the hidden debt dynamic adds a new layer of uncertainty to an otherwise bullish narrative. As one Wall Street analyst put it: "AI may well be the future. But someone has to pay for the road to get there."
