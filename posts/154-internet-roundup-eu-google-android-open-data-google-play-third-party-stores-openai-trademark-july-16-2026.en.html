⚡ July 16 Tech Roundup: EU Forces Google to Open Android and Search Data, Google Play to Host Third-Party Stores from July 22, OpenAI Loses EU Trademark Battle

1. EU Formally Orders Google to Open Android to AI Competitors

On the evening of July 16, 2026, the European Commission published its formal specifications requiring Google to open core capabilities to competitors under the Digital Markets Act (DMA). This represents one of the most interventionist measures imposed on a Big Tech company since the DMA's full implementation in 2024.

Under the EU's requirements, Google must open 11 specific functions in the Android operating system, allowing AI rivals such as OpenAI and other online search engines to access its core system capabilities. This means users will eventually be able to activate competing AI assistants through voice commands similar to "Hey Google" for tasks like ride-hailing and local search.

"Through these measures, we hope to see more alternatives to Google Search and Google AI services (such as Gemini) emerge, giving EU users access to more service choices." — Henna Virkkunen, EU Tech Chief

More significantly, the EU is requiring Google to share its anonymized search data with OpenAI and other AI chatbots that have search functionality — data Google has accumulated over decades to optimize its search engine. Before sharing data, Google can assess competitors for cybersecurity and data protection risks. The data-sharing measures will take effect from January 2027, with a built-in formula for calculating data-sharing pricing.

Google has strongly pushed back. Kent Walker, Google's lawyer, stated in an email: "Today's decision risks undermining important privacy and security protections that millions of European users rely on. We have repeatedly proposed solutions that meet DMA objectives while protecting user safety, but these rulings disregard significant evidence of potential user harm."

Under the timeline, Android-level changes will take effect in the next generation of Android, with users expected to experience the new capabilities from July 2027. The EU emphasized that strict protection mechanisms are in place, and Google only needs to open functions to competitors meeting security and privacy standards.

2. Google Play to Welcome Third-Party App Stores from July 22

In a related landmark development on July 16, Google and Epic Games jointly withdrew their motion to modify a federal court's permanent injunction, clearing the way for a historic transformation of Android's app distribution landscape.

Under the court ruling, starting July 22, 2026 (next Wednesday), Google may no longer block third-party Android app stores from being distributed through Google Play, and must share its app catalog with those stores. The ruling stems from the antitrust lawsuit Epic Games filed against Google in 2020.

Key Implementation Details

  • In-store distribution: From July 22, US users will be able to discover and install third-party app stores directly within Google Play
  • Catalog sharing: Google will automatically provide developer app listings to stores enrolled in the "Play Catalogue Access Programme" unless developers actively opt out
  • Notification process: Google has begun notifying US developers that their apps and games will be shared with participating third-party stores

Importantly, these changes are currently limited to the US market. Outside the US, Google will implement changes through a separate "Registered App Stores" program requiring sideloading rather than direct Google Play distribution, effectively preserving Google's gatekeeper advantage in global markets.

The significance extends far beyond Google Play itself. While Android has long marketed itself as an "open" platform, Google Play has served as the de facto sole mainstream distribution channel, with Google collecting a 30% commission. The arrival of third-party stores will break this monopoly, giving developers more distribution options and lower costs.

"Google Play will never look quite the same after July 22. Years of legal battle between Epic and Google, an $800 million settlement, a federal judge's skepticism about Google's proposed workarounds, and a last-minute withdrawal have converged on a single outcome: rival app stores will live inside Android's dominant marketplace, with access to its full catalog of apps." — PhoneWorld

3. OpenAI Loses EU Trademark Registration Battle

The EU General Court ruled on July 16 that OpenAI cannot register "OPENAI" as a trademark in the European Union. The court upheld a previous decision by the EU Intellectual Property Office (EUIPO), finding that the term "OPENAI" is descriptive and lacks the distinctiveness required for trademark protection.

Court's Rationale

  • Descriptive finding: "Open" signals free accessibility to the relevant audience, and combined with "AI" (Artificial Intelligence), the term suggests "products based on freely accessible AI technology"
  • Not unusual construction: The word combination is not an unusual linguistic construction in English and cannot distinguish the source of goods or services
  • Non-binding foreign registrations: OpenAI argued it has registered the trademark in over 30 countries including the UK and Singapore, but the court ruled other jurisdictions' registrations carry no binding weight for EU trademark law

OpenAI mounted several defenses — arguing that "open" has multiple meanings, that "OPENAI" functions as a compound neologism without a fixed definition, and pointing to EUIPO's previous approvals of similar trademarks. All arguments were dismissed.

The ruling deals a substantial blow to OpenAI's brand protection strategy in the EU market. While OpenAI can still appeal to the European Court of Justice, in the short term it cannot exclusively own the "OPENAI" mark in the EU, potentially complicating its branding strategy and licensing partnerships in the region.

More broadly, this case highlights the unique intellectual property challenges facing AI companies worldwide — when a company's name directly describes its business nature, finding the balance between descriptiveness and distinctiveness becomes a widespread legal puzzle for the AI industry.

4. Analysis: EU Tech Regulation Enters the "Hard Intervention" Era

The three events converging on July 16 point to a clear global trend: tech regulation is shifting from "fine-based deterrence" to "structural intervention."

The EU's demand for Google to open Android and search data is fundamentally about reshaping competition in search and AI assistants. Google has long held over 90% of the European search market and built a formidable AI moat through Gemini and AI Overviews. This intervention is designed to prevent Google from extending its search monopoly into the AI era at its earliest stages.

The Google Play third-party store mandate, meanwhile, represents the US judicial system correcting Android's app distribution monopoly. Though occurring in different jurisdictions, both actions share the same underlying logic — breaking platform "gatekeeper" positions and creating a level playing field for competitors.

For AI companies like OpenAI, while the trademark setback creates short-term challenges, the EU's forced opening of Google's data and Android capabilities actually presents an unprecedented competitive opportunity. As the European Commission emphasized: "In rapidly evolving markets, competition can be lost long before a final decision is adopted." This urgency is driving regulators toward increasingly decisive action.
