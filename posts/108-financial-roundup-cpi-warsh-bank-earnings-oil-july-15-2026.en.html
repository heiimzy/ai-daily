⚡ July 15 Financial Roundup: June CPI Cools to 3.5% in Biggest Drop Since Pandemic, Warsh Debuts Three Task Forces, Big Bank Earnings Crush Estimates, IBM Plunges 24%

1. June CPI Plunges 0.4% MoM — Biggest Drop Since COVID Onset, Core Inflation Also Cools

The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported Tuesday (July 14) that the Consumer Price Index dropped 0.4% month-over-month in June, the largest single-month decline since April 2020 and far below the 0.1% decline economists had expected. The annual inflation rate fell sharply from 4.2% in May to 3.5%, undershooting the 3.8% consensus estimate.

Core CPI (excluding food and energy) was flat month-over-month and rose 2.6% year-over-year — both below forecasts of +0.2% and 2.9% respectively. A 9.7% plunge in gasoline prices was the primary driver, reflecting the brief US-Iran truce in mid-June that temporarily lowered energy costs.

"If you were looking for runaway inflation in this report, you didn't get it," said Jamie Cox, Managing Partner at Harris Financial Group. "It's pretty clear any recent rise in inflation was related to energy prices and wouldn't be long-lasting."

Following the release, the CME FedWatch tool showed the probability of a July rate hike collapse from 37% to just 17%. The 10-year Treasury yield fell to 4.56%, and the US Dollar Index dropped 0.5% to 100.69.

However, analysts cautioned that the relief may be temporary — gasoline prices have already rebounded sharply in July as the US-Iran conflict reignited.

2. Fed Chair Warsh's First Congressional Testimony: Three New Task Forces, "Zero Tolerance" for Inflation

Federal Reserve Chair Kevin Warsh appeared before the House Financial Services Committee at 10:00 AM ET Tuesday for his first semiannual monetary policy testimony since taking office in May.

In prepared remarks, Warsh declared: "The members of our Committee have no tolerance for persistently elevated inflation. And we share a resolute commitment to restoring price stability." He emphasized that getting monetary policy right is the Fed's "number one objective."

Warsh's most notable announcement was the creation of three internal task forces:

  • Data Quality and Innovation Task Force — evaluating new data sources and methodological improvements to ensure policymakers receive accurate, relevant, and actionable economic data
  • Productivity and Jobs Task Force — surveying the pace, reach, and impact of new general-purpose technologies, particularly AI's implications for productive capacity and American workers
  • Inflation Frameworks Task Force — examining the drivers of inflation and weighing approaches for delivering price stability in a dynamic economy

On AI, Warsh noted that "what is now called 'AI investment' will soon be called just 'investment,'" highlighting that high-tech equipment spending has grown nearly 25% over the past four quarters. The market interpreted Warsh's remarks as cautiously hawkish — firm on inflation but measured in tone.

3. Big Bank Q2 Earnings Crush Estimates Across the Board

The Q2 earnings season kicked off with all five major US banks reporting Tuesday morning, each beating analyst expectations by wide margins:

JPMorgan Chase

Q2 EPS of $7.70 crushed the $5.29 consensus, on revenue of $57.35 billion (+28% YoY), setting a record quarterly profit. Commercial and investment banking revenue jumped 27%, with markets revenue soaring 35%. CEO Jamie Dimon cited AI-driven capital investment and a favorable banking environment, while warning that "several risks are shifting below the surface like tectonic plates."

Goldman Sachs

Q2 EPS $20.98 nearly doubled from $10.91 a year ago, far exceeding the $14.51 estimate. Revenue surged 39% to $20.34 billion. Investment banking fees spiked 55%, and asset & wealth management revenue rose 20%. Goldman shares surged 8% on the news, leading the Dow.

Other Banks

Bank of America, Citigroup, and Wells Fargo also delivered above-consensus results. The strong showing across all five banks reflects a resilient US financial sector buoyed by AI-driven investment demand and active capital markets.

4. IBM Crashes 24% on Profit Warning

IBM shares suffered their worst single-day drop in years, plunging approximately 24% on Tuesday. CEO Arvind Krishna warned in a letter to investors that the company's Q2 software and infrastructure results fell well short of expectations, as clients shifted spending "toward servers, storage, and memory purchases to secure supply-constrained infrastructure ahead of expected price increases." IBM's formal earnings call is scheduled for July 22.

The IBM collapse weighed on the Dow, but the broader tech sector recovered. Apple shares retreated over 1% after hitting an all-time high on Monday. SK Hynix's US-listed shares rebounded roughly 11%, recovering some of Monday's 9%+ decline.

5. Oil Surges to One-Month High as Hormuz Crisis Intensifies

Brent crude continued its rally Tuesday, settling in the $86-87/barrel range, extending Monday's 9.6% surge. WTI crude breached the $80/barrel level to $80.80. Oil has gained approximately 12% since Friday.

The catalysts are clear: President Trump announced the reinstatement of a naval blockade on Iran and a 20% toll on all cargo transiting the Strait of Hormuz. Iran retaliated by striking two UAE oil tankers with cruise missiles, killing one Indian crew member and injuring eight others, dramatically escalating supply fears.

Bart Melek, global head of commodity strategy at TD Securities, warned: "I suspect that a move to $100 is quite possible, should it become apparent that physical shortage risks are real and increasingly likely."

Gold rebounded 2% to $4,085/oz after briefly dipping below $4,000 on Monday. Bitcoin recovered to around $63,700 from overnight lows near $61,800.
