1. Google Play Opens to Third-Party App Stores: Six-Year Antitrust Battle Concludes

July 22, 2026 marks a historic shift in Android app distribution. Google Play is officially launching its "Play Catalog Access Program" in the United States, allowing certified third-party Android app stores to list directly inside Google Play and access the full Play Store app catalog.

The change stems from Epic Games' 2020 antitrust lawsuit against Google. In December 2023, a California jury unanimously found Google held an illegal monopoly in Android app distribution. Judge James Donato issued a permanent injunction in October 2024 requiring Google to host rival stores. After appeals to the Ninth Circuit and Supreme Court both failed, Google and Epic reached a settlement in November 2025—but both parties withdrew the joint motion last week, reinstating the original injunction in full.

Requirements and Fees

Participating stores must pay a $5,000 annual security review fee, operate exclusively in the US, keep malware below 1% of all install attempts, and accept all eligible developers without discrimination. Google continues to handle all downloads and collect service fees. Developers can choose between "publish everywhere," "manage individually," or "full opt-out" in Play Console—apps of those who don't opt out will be shared by default.

Epic Games Store, Amazon Appstore, and Samsung Galaxy Store are expected to be among the first enrollees. While Google retains control over download infrastructure and fee collection, the move ends Google Play's monopoly on app discovery—for the first time, US Android users can discover and install competing stores directly within Google Play itself.

2. Block Launches Buzz: Open-Source Workspace Where Humans and AI Agents Collaborate as Equals

Jack Dorsey's fintech company Block released Buzz on July 21—an open-source collaboration platform built on the Nostr protocol that unifies team chat, project management, code hosting, and AI agent collaboration in a single workspace.

Buzz treats AI agents and humans as first-class participants. Built on the decentralized Nostr protocol, every participant—human or agent—holds a cryptographic keypair, making identity portable and independent of any single platform. The project is licensed under Apache 2.0, hosted on GitHub (github.com/block/buzz), and supports self-hosted deployments. Desktop clients are available for macOS, Windows, and Linux, built with Tauri 2 and React 19.

Architecture and Features

The server stack is Rust-heavy, featuring Postgres storage, Redis-based real-time state, Schnorr signature authentication, and a hash-chain audit log. A built-in workflow engine supports YAML-configured automation triggered by messages, schedules, or webhooks. The standout "Buzz Mesh" feature lets team members pool GPU resources, which AI agents can consume through a local OpenAI-compatible endpoint, enabling decentralized AI compute sharing.

Buzz includes channels, direct messages, voice huddles, Git hosting, PR reviews, kanban boards, and MCP agent integration. Block stated Buzz was born from a desire to reduce dependency on Slack and GitHub, with its decentralized architecture ensuring user identity and data aren't locked into any commercial platform. Within hours of launch, the GitHub repository surpassed 2,000 stars.

3. Anthropic Launches "Record a Skill" for Claude Cowork: Teach AI by Demonstration

Anthropic released a groundbreaking feature for its Claude Cowork desktop AI assistant on July 21—"Record a Skill." Users can screen-record themselves performing a task while narrating their actions, and Claude analyzes the click sequences, keystrokes, and voice context to automatically generate a reusable, rerunnable skill.

The core philosophy: "Do it once, and Claude learns it." Previously, setting up automation required writing code or meticulously describing every step. Now, simply performing the task is sufficient. Claude intelligently checks whether API-based connectors exist for any step in the recorded workflow and prefers those over brittle pixel-level UI automation when available.

Early Use Cases

Early adopters report using the feature for precisely the kind of repetitive, low-value tasks that never justified manual scripting: trading alert configurations, LinkedIn engagement workflows, email reply templates, and expense report submissions. Anthropic engineer Lydia Hallie described using it for "workflows I always meant to automate but never did." The feature is rolling out progressively to Pro, Max, and Team subscribers.

The screen recording capability raises privacy considerations—captured footage may inadvertently include credentials, financial data, or other sensitive information. Best practices suggest avoiding exposure of credentials during recording and preferring connector-based integrations for sensitive workflow steps.
