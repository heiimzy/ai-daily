⚡ July 15 Financial Roundup: CPI Cools Sharply, July Rate Hike Odds Plunge to 14%, SK Hynix ADR Surges 27%, Trump Reverses Hormuz Toll Plan

The U.S. June CPI report released Tuesday (July 14) came in well below expectations across the board — the largest disinflationary print in six years, with core CPI monthly rate unexpectedly hitting zero. The data dramatically reshuffled Fed rate hike expectations, pushing all three major U.S. stock indices into positive territory led by tech and semiconductor stocks. Meanwhile, President Trump abruptly scrapped his 20% Hormuz transit fee just hours after announcing it, though oil prices remained elevated near one-month highs.

1. CPI Cools Across the Board: Core CPI Monthly Rate Hits Zero

The Bureau of Labor Statistics reported June headline CPI rose 3.5% year-over-year, sharply below the 3.8% consensus and down from 4.2% in May. On a monthly basis, CPI fell 0.4% — the largest single-month decline since April 2020, handily beating the -0.1% consensus estimate.

Core inflation delivered an even bigger surprise: annual core CPI moderated to 2.6% versus the 2.8% expected, down from 2.9% in May. Monthly core CPI recorded 0%, the smallest increase since January 2021 and below the 0.2% consensus.

Energy Prices Led the Decline

Falling energy prices were the primary driver. Gasoline prices plunged more than 9% month-over-month, and fuel oil declined over 9%, pushing the energy index down 5.7% as a whole. The monthly average price of Brent crude retreated to $84.4/barrel from approximately $103.7/barrel in May, reflecting a brief ceasefire that temporarily reopened the Strait of Hormuz in mid-June.

Beyond energy, prices fell across multiple categories: car insurance, communications, clothing, medical care, and used cars. Core services excluding shelter declined for the first time since the pandemic, and supercore CPI fell to 3.17%. Shelter costs rose at their slowest pace of 0.1%, essentially flat.

2. July Rate Hike Probability Collapses from 41.7% to 14.4%

The CME FedWatch tool showed a dramatic repricing after the CPI release. The probability of the Fed holding rates steady at the July 29 FOMC meeting surged from 58.3% pre-data to 83.4-85.6%, while the odds of a 25-basis-point hike cratered from 41.7% to just 14.4-16.6%.

On Polymarket, the "Fed rate hike in 2026?" contract tumbled from 66.5% to 51.5% — a 15-point drop that put the market back into near-coin-flip territory with $4.09 million matched. The July "no change" contract was pricing at 93.5% with nearly $60 million in volume.

Fed Governor Christopher Waller had stated Monday that he would vote for a rate hike if inflation ran hot. Tuesday's CPI data significantly weakened the case for hawkish action at the July meeting.

3. US Stocks Close Higher: Nasdaq Leads with 0.9% Gain

All three major U.S. indices closed in the green Tuesday, though gains were tempered by Middle East tensions and IBM's historic plunge:

  • Dow Jones Industrial Average: 52,508.66, +0.02% (+10 pts)
  • S&P 500: 7,543.89, +0.38% (+28.55 pts)
  • Nasdaq Composite: 26,107.01, +0.90% (+233.83 pts), best performer of the three

SK Hynix ADR Soars 27% Leading Semiconductor Rebound

Semiconductors were the standout sector. SK Hynix's U.S. ADR surged 27%, making it the single best-performing stock of the session. Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix also rebounded 4.1% and 3.5% respectively in Seoul, reversing Monday's historic selloff. AMD rose over 3%, and cybersecurity stocks CrowdStrike and Palo Alto Networks also posted strong gains. Advancers outnumbered decliners on the Nasdaq by a 1.2-to-1 ratio.

IBM Plunges 24% on Profit Warning

IBM was the biggest drag, crashing 24-25% — its worst single-day loss in years — after warning that Q2 earnings would take a significant hit as clients shift IT spending from software to data-center infrastructure. The stock was the worst performer in the S&P 500.

4. Trump's U-Turn: 20% Hormuz Toll Canceled Hours After Announcement

President Trump announced Monday he would reinstate a naval blockade on Iran and impose a 20% "escort fee" on all cargo transiting the Strait of Hormuz. Within hours, he reversed course, declaring the strait open to all shipping traffic except Iranian vessels — effectively canceling the controversial toll plan.

The reversal provided some relief to markets, though the broader Middle East situation remains volatile. U.S. Central Command launched a third consecutive night of strikes on Iranian targets, while Iran retaliated with ballistic missiles targeting U.S. bases in Bahrain, Jordan, and Kuwait. Two UAE supertankers were hit by Iranian cruise missiles in the southern lane of the Strait of Hormuz, killing one Indian crew member and injuring eight others.

5. Dollar Weakens, Gold Rebounds, Oil Holds Near Highs

Dollar Index Falls to 100.94

The softer CPI data weighed on the greenback. The DXY fell 0.33% to 100.94, while EUR/USD rose 0.35% to 1.1421. USD/JPY edged down 0.13% to 162.21. The broader theme was a repositioning away from dollar longs as rate hike expectations receded.

Gold Stages V-Shaped Recovery Toward $4,100

Spot gold staged a dramatic reversal Tuesday. After falling nearly 3% on Monday — briefly breaking below $4,000 to $3,986 — gold rebounded sharply following the CPI release, with intraday gains exceeding 2.5% as it approached $4,100. It settled at $4,051/oz, up 1.28% on the day.

Central bank buying continues to provide a structural floor. The National Bank of Poland has purchased 82 tonnes of gold so far this year, bringing total holdings to 632.4 tonnes toward a 700-tonne target. The People's Bank of China added 15 tonnes in June — its largest monthly purchase this year and the 20th consecutive month of net buying.

Oil Consolidates Near One-Month Highs

After Monday's 9.6% surge, crude oil gains moderated Tuesday. Brent crude settled at $84.73/barrel, up 1.7% (+$1.43), while WTI closed at $79.34, up 1.5% (+$1.20). Both benchmarks hit their highest closing levels in over a month.

Upside was capped by Trump's canceled toll plan and demand concerns. The U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve has fallen to 316.5 million barrels — the lowest since April 1983. Analysts warn that a prolonged disruption in Hormuz shipping could push Brent toward $100/barrel.

6. Bond Markets and Prediction Markets Repricing

Treasury yields fell across the curve. The 2-year yield dropped 6.13 basis points to 4.202%, while the 10-year yield fell 2.26 bps to 4.587%. Global bond markets followed suit, with UK 10-year yields slipping back below 5%.

Polymarket data shows the "July Fed hold" contract at 93.5% probability with $59.6 million matched. The "zero rate cuts in 2026" contract stands at 80.35% with $42.4 million in volume — suggesting that while July is safe, markets do not expect meaningful easing for the rest of the year.

In Asia, China is set to release Q2 GDP, June industrial production, and retail sales data Wednesday (July 15) at 10:00 CST. The consensus expects GDP growth of 5.1% YoY, slowing from 5.4% in Q1. South Korea's KOSPI has entered bear territory, sliding 25% from its June record high, though it remains up roughly 60% year-to-date — one of the best-performing major indices globally.
