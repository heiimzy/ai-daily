⚡ July 20 International Politics: Iran Seizes British Oil Tanker in Strait of Hormuz, Strikes Saudi Arabia for First Time in 4 Months, US-Iran Exchange Fire for 8th Consecutive Night

Breaking: Iran's Revolutionary Guard Seizes British Oil Tanker in Strait of Hormuz

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) seized the British-flagged oil tanker Stena Impero in the Strait of Hormuz on the evening of July 19 (around 16:00 London time), while briefly detaining a second Liberian-flagged, British-operated vessel, the Mesdar. This marks the first time Iran has directly seized a Western commercial vessel since the US-Israeli coalition launched military strikes against Iran on February 28, escalating the Strait of Hormuz shipping crisis to a dangerous new level.

Stena Bulk and Northern Marine Management, the vessel's owner and manager, confirmed in a joint statement that the Stena Impero was "approached by unidentified small crafts and a helicopter during transit of the Strait of Hormuz while the vessel was in international waters." The company subsequently lost contact with the vessel, which tracking data showed heading north toward Iran. The tanker had 23 crew members on board — Indian, Russian, Latvian, and Filipino nationals — with no British citizens among them.

Iran's semi-official Fars news agency, citing an IRGC statement, said the Stena Impero was seized for "violating international maritime regulations," including shutting down its GPS transponder, entering the Strait through the exit channel, and ignoring Iranian warnings. The Ports and Maritime Organization of Iran's Hormozgan province stated that the tanker had collided with a fishing boat and failed to respond to distress calls. Fars later reported that the Mesdar had been released after receiving "navigational safety instructions and routine environmental inspections."

"Yesterday's action in the Gulf shows worrying signs Iran may be choosing a dangerous path of illegal and destabilizing behavior. These seizures are completely unacceptable... If this situation is not resolved quickly there will be serious consequences." — UK Foreign Secretary Jeremy Hunt

British Foreign Secretary Jeremy Hunt confirmed the seizures late on July 19, convened a government Cobra emergency committee meeting, and warned of "serious consequences" if the situation was not swiftly resolved. The UK government advised all British shipping to "stay out of the area for an interim period." US President Donald Trump said Washington would consult with London on the matter. White House National Security Council spokesperson Garrett Marquis stated this was "the second time in just over a week the UK has been the target of escalatory violence by the Iranian regime."

The tanker seizure came two weeks after British Royal Marines assisted in the seizure of an Iranian oil tanker (Grace 1) off Gibraltar, which the UK said was suspected of carrying oil to Syria in violation of EU sanctions. Iran had consistently characterized that seizure as "piracy" and threatened retaliation.

Iran Directly Strikes Saudi Arabia for First Time in Four Months: Simultaneous Attack on Military Base and Oil Export Corridor

On the morning of July 19, Iran launched its first direct military strikes on Saudi Arabia since early March, simultaneously targeting two of the kingdom's strategic lifelines: the military infrastructure protecting Saudi airspace and the energy infrastructure underpinning Saudi Arabia's economy. This marked the first time in the entire US-Iran conflict that Iran has struck both military and energy targets in Saudi Arabia on the same day.

Ballistic Missile Targets Prince Sultan Air Base

Saudi Arabia's Defense Ministry confirmed that an Iranian ballistic missile targeted Prince Sultan Air Base (PSAB) at Al-Kharj, the primary US air operations hub in Saudi Arabia and home to approximately 2,300 American military personnel. Saudi authorities stated the missile was intercepted. A US official confirmed to Axios that PSAB was directly targeted by an Iranian ballistic missile — the first such direct targeting since the war's early phase.

Shahed Drone Hits SAMREF Refinery at Yanbu

In the same early morning window, an Iranian Shahed-series drone penetrated Saudi air defenses on the Red Sea coast and struck the SAMREF refinery at Yanbu — a 400,000-barrel-per-day joint venture between Saudi Aramco and ExxonMobil. The Saudi Defense Ministry confirmed that a ballistic missile targeting the Yanbu port area was intercepted, but the drone reached the refinery complex. Damage was described as "under assessment."

Yanbu serves as the terminus of Saudi Arabia's East-West Pipeline, which has a capacity of 7 million barrels per day and is the kingdom's primary alternative export route bypassing the Strait of Hormuz. Since the Hormuz crisis began, Saudi Arabia has redirected the majority of its crude exports to the Red Sea ports of Yanbu and Ras Tanura, with Red Sea loadings peaking at 4.7 million barrels per day. The strike on SAMREF demonstrates Iran's ability to reach Saudi Arabia's only functioning alternative to the Strait of Hormuz.

Analysts noted that the July 19 strikes represent a paradigm shift: by simultaneously targeting Saudi Arabia's military host infrastructure (PSAB) and its economic survival corridor (Yanbu), Iran has effectively collapsed whatever remained of Saudi Arabia's "non-belligerence" posture. Riyadh had maintained this position since February 28 — absorbing 38 missiles and 435 drones from Iran while publicly reserving the "option to respond" but refraining from doing so — a diplomatic framework that now faces a fundamentally new factual threshold.

Two US Troops Killed in Jordan: US Completes 8th Night of Strikes as Iran Retaliates Across Gulf

On July 17, Iran launched ballistic missile and drone attacks on a US base in Jordan, killing two American service members, leaving one missing in action, and hospitalizing four others. This marked the first US combat fatalities from direct Iranian fire since March. The total US death toll since the US-Israeli coalition launched military operations against Iran on February 28 now stands at 16, with over 420 wounded.

President Donald Trump called the deaths "a very sad thing" and said the fallen soldiers made "the ultimate sacrifice to prevent Iran from getting a nuclear weapon." Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth wrote on social media: "Godspeed, heroes. Their sacrifice only stiffens our resolve."

In retaliation, US forces began a new wave of airstrikes on Iran at 6 p.m. ET on July 18 (06:00 Beijing time July 19), completing the 8th consecutive night of attacks by 11:30 p.m. ET. US Central Command (CENTCOM) said the strikes were designed to "further degrade Iran's ability to threaten commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz and swiftly punish Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps forces" responsible for the Jordan attack. Targets included Iranian coastal surveillance and air defense facilities, maritime capabilities, missile and drone storage sites, and specific IRGC units. Iranian media reported strikes on Sirik, Qeshm Island, Bandar Abbas, and Shadegan in Hormozgan and Khuzestan provinces, with at least six missiles striking around Qeshm Island.

Iran retaliated swiftly. The IRGC announced a large-scale drone attack on two US military facilities in Kuwait — an ammunition depot at Camp Al-Adiri and air defense radar systems at Ali Al Salem Air Base. Kuwait's military confirmed it was "confronting hostile missile and drone attacks." Kuwait's Ministry of Electricity, Water and Renewable Energy reported that a power generation and desalination facility was struck by Iranian fire for the second consecutive day, causing a fire. Air raid sirens sounded repeatedly in Bahrain, where authorities urged residents to seek shelter.

Israel's military warned that Iranian missiles launched toward the Jordanian port city of Aqaba could cause debris to fall on Israeli territory, activating interception systems over the southern city of Eilat for the first time in weeks.

Iran's Health Ministry reported that 50 people have been killed and over 500 wounded by US strikes over the past three weeks alone. The US reimposed a naval blockade on Iranian ports, redirecting five vessels and disabling one — the Belma tanker — in the first 24 hours.

Tehran Officially Suspends Ceasefire Agreement as Risk of Full-Scale War Surges

Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi announced on state television on July 18 that Tehran has suspended all commitments under the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) signed with the US last month under Pakistani mediation. "The US trampled and stopped fulfilling all its commitments, and we have also stopped fulfilling all our commitments," Gharibabadi stated, adding that Iran is currently "busy defending the country."

Iran's Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei issued a written statement on the same day, accusing the US of repeatedly violating the MoU and declaring that President Trump's signature is "utterly worthless and invalid." He warned that the US would face "unforgettable lessons" and "heavier costs." Since succeeding his father, former Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei — who was killed on the first day of US-Israeli strikes on February 28 — Mojtaba Khamenei has communicated exclusively through written or televised statements and has not appeared in public.

Former IRGC commander-in-chief Mohsen Rezaei, now a senior advisor to the Supreme Leader, had issued an ultimatum warning that if US attacks continued for "two to three more days," Iran would commence "full-scale offensive operations" — adding that "no political border will be secure." The July 19 dual strikes on Saudi Arabia are widely seen as falling within the threshold window described by Rezaei's warning.

President Trump, when informed that Iran had announced its suspension of the ceasefire, responded: "I couldn't care less." Trump had earlier this week threatened to target Iran's power stations and bridges if Tehran refused to return to negotiations.

Gregory Brew, senior geopolitical risk analyst at Eurasia Group, noted that Tehran is seeking to establish a "new normal" in the Strait of Hormuz — where all vessels must register and obtain permission from Iran. The timing of the British tanker seizure demonstrates Tehran is systematically implementing this policy. Todd Huntley, director of Georgetown University's National Security Law Program, warned that the Strait of Hormuz is an international waterway and any attempt at unilateral control would set a "devastating precedent" that could threaten other strategic chokepoints including the Strait of Malacca and the Strait of Gibraltar.

UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres expressed deep concern over the conflict's escalation, particularly attacks on civilian infrastructure. The European Union and the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) issued a joint statement calling on Iran to "immediately and unconditionally cease all attacks and interference with maritime navigation" and maintain the Strait of Hormuz open for transit.

International oil prices rose further following the tanker seizure and Saudi strikes. Data from Kpler showed only 21 vessels passing through the Strait of Hormuz on July 14, compared to the normal average of approximately 150 per day. JPMorgan's latest assessment identified a triple supply shock — collapsed Russian refining capacity, the closed Strait of Hormuz, and threatened Saudi export corridors — as the new defining risk to global energy markets.
